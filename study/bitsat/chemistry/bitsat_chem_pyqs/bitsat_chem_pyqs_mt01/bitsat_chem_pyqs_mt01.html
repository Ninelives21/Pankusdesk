    • Quick explanation: In the Lyman series, transitions end at \(n_1=1\). For \(H_{\alpha}\) line of Lyman series, the transition is from \(n_2=2\) to \(n_1=1\), so the energies of electrons in these two orbits are calculated using Bohr's energy formula.
    • BITSAT chapter / topic / subtopic: Atomic Structure \(\rightarrow\) Bohr's Model of an Atom \(\rightarrow\) Energy of electron in the nth orbit - Page C20
    • For a hydrogen-like atom, the energy of an electron in the \(n^{\mathrm{th}}\) orbit is given by \(E_n=-\dfrac{313.52Z^2}{n^2}\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • For hydrogen atom, \(Z=1\).
    • So the formula becomes \(E_n=-\dfrac{313.52}{n^2}\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • Lyman series means the electron finally comes to the first orbit, so \(n_1=1\).
    • The \(H_{\alpha}\) line is the first line of the series, so it corresponds to the transition \(n_2=2\rightarrow n_1=1\).
    • Energy of electron in \(n_1=1\) orbit is \(E_1=-\dfrac{313.52}{1^2}=-313.52\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\approx -313.6\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • Energy of electron in \(n_2=2\) orbit is \(E_2=-\dfrac{313.52}{2^2}=-\dfrac{313.52}{4}=-78.38\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\approx -78.4\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • Final result: The energies are \(-313.6\) and \(-78.4\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • Simple memory tip: Lyman series always ends at \(n=1\), Balmer ends at \(n=2\), and Paschen ends at \(n=3\).
    • Why the other options are wrong: The values \(-34.84\) and \(-19.6\) correspond to higher orbits, but \(H_{\alpha}\) of Lyman series involves only \(n=1\) and \(n=2\).
    • Exam shortcut: For hydrogen, remember \(E_n\propto-\dfrac{1}{n^2}\). Once \(E_1\approx -313.6\), \(E_2\) is just one-fourth of it.

    • BITSAT chapter / topic / subtopic: Atomic Structure \(\rightarrow\) Dual Nature of Radiation and Matter / De Broglie Concept \(\rightarrow\) De Broglie relationship - Page C20
    • de-Broglie's equation is \(\lambda=\dfrac{h}{mv}\), where \(m\) is mass and \(v\) is velocity.
    • Let mass of particle \(A\) be \(x\).
    • Then mass of particle \(B=5x\).
    • For particle \(A\), \(\lambda_A=\dfrac{h}{x\times0.05}\).
    • For particle \(B\), \(\lambda_B=\dfrac{h}{5x\times0.02}\).
    • Now divide the two equations: \(\dfrac{\lambda_A}{\lambda_B}=\dfrac{5x\times0.02}{x\times0.05}\).
    • Cancel \(x\): \(\dfrac{\lambda_A}{\lambda_B}=\dfrac{0.10}{0.05}=2\).
    • Therefore, \(\lambda_A:\lambda_B=2:1\).
    • Final result: Ratio of de-Broglie wavelengths \(=2:1\).
    • Simple memory tip: More momentum means smaller de-Broglie wavelength.
    • Why the other options are wrong: The ratio must be calculated using both mass and velocity. Considering only velocity or only mass gives wrong ratios.
    • Exam shortcut: For two particles, directly use \(\dfrac{\lambda_A}{\lambda_B}=\dfrac{m_Bv_B}{m_Av_A}\).

    • Observed dipole moment of HCl molecule \(=1.03\ \mathrm{D}\).
    • Bond length of HCl molecule \(=1.275\ \mathring{A}\).
    • Convert bond length into cm: \(1.275\ \mathring{A}=1.275\times10^{-8}\ \mathrm{cm}\).
    • Charge of electron is given as \(4.8\times10^{-10}\ \mathrm{esu}\).
    • Theoretical dipole moment for \(100\%\) ionic character is \(\mu=e\times d\).
    • So, \(\mu_{\text{theoretical}}=4.8\times10^{-10}\times1.275\times10^{-8}\ \mathrm{esu\ cm}\).
    • This gives \(\mu_{\text{theoretical}}=6.12\times10^{-18}\ \mathrm{esu\ cm}\).
    • Since \(1\ \mathrm{D}=10^{-18}\ \mathrm{esu\ cm}\), theoretical dipole moment \(=6.12\ \mathrm{D}\).
    • Now use \(\%\) ionic character \(=\dfrac{\mu_{\text{observed}}}{\mu_{\text{theoretical}}}\times100\).
    • So, \(\%\) ionic character \(=\dfrac{1.03}{6.12}\times100\).
    • This gives \(16.83\%\).
    • Final result: Ionic character of HCl \(=16.83\%\).
    • Simple memory tip: Observed dipole moment is lower than the theoretical ionic dipole moment because real covalent bonds are not fully ionic.
    • Why the other options are wrong: \(100\%\) would mean the observed dipole moment equals the theoretical value, but \(1.03\ \mathrm{D}\) is much smaller than \(6.12\ \mathrm{D}\).
    • Exam shortcut: For percentage ionic character, calculate full ionic dipole moment first, then compare the observed dipole moment with it.

    • BITSAT chapter / topic / subtopic: Periodicity and Bonding \(\rightarrow\) Chemical Bonding \(\rightarrow\) Hybridization - Page C35
      Periodicity and Bonding \(\rightarrow\) Chemical Bonding \(\rightarrow\) Shapes of Molecule / VSEPR Theory - Page C35
    • For \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), the central atom is oxygen.
    • Oxygen has 6 valence electrons. It forms two \(\mathrm{O-H}\) bonds and still has two lone pairs.
    • So around oxygen, there are \(2\) bond pairs and \(2\) lone pairs.
    • Total electron pair regions \(=2+2=4\), so the hybridisation is \(sp^3\).
    • Due to lone pair-lone pair and lone pair-bond pair repulsions, the shape is not perfectly tetrahedral; it becomes angular or bent.
    • For \(\mathrm{BCl_3}\), boron has 3 bond pairs and 0 lone pairs, so it is \(sp^2\) and trigonal planar, not \(sp^3\) angular.
    • For \(\mathrm{NH_4^+}\), nitrogen has 4 bond pairs and 0 lone pairs, so it is \(sp^3\) and tetrahedral, not \(dsp^2\) square planar.
    • For \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), carbon has 4 bond pairs and 0 lone pairs, so it is \(sp^3\) and tetrahedral, not \(dsp^2\).
    • Final result: The correct set is \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(sp^3\), angular.
    • Simple memory tip: Count bond pairs plus lone pairs around the central atom to get hybridisation. Then use lone pairs to decide the actual shape.
    • Why the other options are wrong: \(\mathrm{BCl_3}\) is \(sp^2\) trigonal planar, \(\mathrm{NH_4^+}\) is \(sp^3\) tetrahedral, and \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) is also \(sp^3\) tetrahedral.
    • Exam shortcut: \(2\) bond pairs \(+\ 2\) lone pairs usually means \(sp^3\) hybridisation and bent/angular shape.

    • At STP, \(1\) mole of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) occupies \(22.4\ \mathrm{L}\).
    • For (A): \(\mathrm{CaCO_3 \xrightarrow{\Delta} CaO + CO_2}\).
    • \(100\ \mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\) gives \(22.4\ \mathrm{L}\) of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • So, \(10\ \mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\) gives \(\dfrac{22.4\times10}{100}=2.24\ \mathrm{L}\ \mathrm{CO_2}\). Hence, A-(iv).
    • For (B): \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3+2HCl\rightarrow2NaCl+H_2O+CO_2}\).

    • For (C): \(\mathrm{C+O_2\rightarrow CO_2}\).
    • \(12\ \mathrm{g}\) of carbon gives \(22.4\ \mathrm{L}\) of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).

    • BITSAT chapter / topic / subtopic: Physical and Chemical Equilibria \(\rightarrow\) Colligative Properties \(\rightarrow\) Relative lowering in vapour pressure - Page C52
      Physical and Chemical Equilibria \(\rightarrow\) Raoult's Law - Page C52
    • Given weight of non-volatile solute, \(w=25\ \mathrm{g}\).
    • Weight of solvent water, \(W=100\ \mathrm{g}\).
    • Lowering of vapour pressure, \(p^\circ-p_s=2.25\times10^{-1}=0.225\ \mathrm{mm}\).
    • Vapour pressure of pure water, \(p^\circ=17.5\ \mathrm{mm}\).
    • Molecular weight of water, \(M=18\ \mathrm{g\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • Let molecular weight of solute be \(m\).
    • According to Raoult's law for relative lowering of vapour pressure, \(\dfrac{p^\circ-p_s}{p^\circ}=\dfrac{wM}{mW}\).
    • Substitute the values: \(\dfrac{0.225}{17.5}=\dfrac{25\times18}{m\times100}\).
    • Rearrange for \(m\): \(m=\dfrac{25\times18\times17.5}{0.225\times100}\).
    • Since \(0.225\times100=22.5\), \(m=\dfrac{25\times18\times17.5}{22.5}\).
    • This gives \(m=350\ \mathrm{g\ mol^{-1}}\).
    • Final result: Molecular weight of the solute \(=350\).
    • Simple memory tip: A non-volatile solute lowers vapour pressure because fewer solvent molecules can escape into vapour phase.
    • Why the other options are wrong: Only \(350\) satisfies Raoult's law with the given lowering of vapour pressure and solution composition.
    • Exam shortcut: In vapour pressure lowering questions, directly use \(\dfrac{\Delta p}{p^\circ}=\dfrac{wM}{mW}\) for dilute solutions.

    • BITSAT chapter / topic / subtopic: Physical and Chemical Equilibria \(\rightarrow\) Ionization of Water and pH Scale - Page C57
      Physical and Chemical Equilibria \(\rightarrow\) Acids and Bases - Page C57
    • Barium hydroxide dissociates in water as \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2\rightarrow Ba^{2+}+2OH^-}\).
    • Initial concentration of \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2}=1\times10^{-3}\ \mathrm{M}\).
    • Initial volume \(=50\ \mathrm{mL}\), and \(50\ \mathrm{mL}\) water is added.
    • So final volume \(=100\ \mathrm{mL}\), meaning the solution is diluted to double its original volume.
    • Therefore, final concentration of \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2}=\dfrac{1\times10^{-3}}{2}=0.5\times10^{-3}\ \mathrm{M}\).
    • Since each \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2}\) gives \(2\) hydroxide ions, \([\mathrm{OH^-}]=2\times0.5\times10^{-3}=1\times10^{-3}\ \mathrm{M}\).
    • Now, \(\mathrm{pOH}=-\log[\mathrm{OH^-}]\).
    • So, \(\mathrm{pOH}=-\log(1\times10^{-3})=3\).
    • At \(25^\circ\mathrm{C}\), \(\mathrm{pH}+\mathrm{pOH}=14\).
    • Hence, \(\mathrm{pH}=14-3=11\).
    • Final result: \(\mathrm{pH}=11.0\).
    • Simple memory tip: \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2}\) is a strong base and gives \(2\) hydroxide ions, so always multiply its concentration by \(2\) before finding pOH.
    • Why the other options are wrong: Options \(3.0\) and \(3.3\) are acidic pH values, but barium hydroxide is basic. \(11.7\) ignores the dilution effect.
    • Exam shortcut: Dilution halves \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2}\), but two \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) ions double it back, so \([\mathrm{OH^-}]=10^{-3}\ \mathrm{M}\) directly.

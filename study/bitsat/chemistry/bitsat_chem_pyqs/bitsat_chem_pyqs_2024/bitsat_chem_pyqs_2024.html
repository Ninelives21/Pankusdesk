    • Therefore, the change in internal energy is: \(\Delta U = 0\)

    • Since \(\Delta U = 0\), heat absorbed is equal in magnitude to the work done.

    • The given cyclic process forms a circle on the graph.
    • From the graph, the diameter of the circle is: \(25 - 5 = 20\)
    • Therefore, radius: \(r = \dfrac{20}{2} = 10\)
    • Area of the circle: \(\pi r^2 = \pi(10)^2 = 100\pi\)
    • Hence, heat absorbed: \(\Delta q = 100\pi J\)
    • Final result: \((d)\ 100\pi J\)

    • Solution: Electron affinity is related to the energy change when an atom or ion accepts an electron.
    • First electron addition to a neutral oxygen atom can be represented as: \(\mathrm{O + e^- \longrightarrow O^-}\)

    • Hence, the process is endothermic, and the electron affinity value is positive for: \(\mathrm{O^- \longrightarrow O^{2-}}\)
    • Final result: \((b)\ O^-\) changes into \(O^{2-}\)

      • First electron addition to a neutral atom may release energy.

      • Negative ion \(+\) electron means repulsion, so energy is absorbed.

      • \(\mathrm{O \rightarrow O^-}\) is first electron addition, not the positive second electron affinity case.
      • \(\mathrm{O \rightarrow O^+}\) and \(\mathrm{O \rightarrow O^{2+}}\) involve loss of electrons, not electron affinity.

    • Therefore: \(k = \dfrac{0.693}{600}\)
    • So: \(k \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-3}\ s^{-1}\)
    • According to Arrhenius equation: \(k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}\)
    • Taking common log form: \(\log k = \log A - \dfrac{E_a}{2.303RT}\)
    • Given: \(A = 4 \times 10^{13}\ s^{-1}\)
    • Given: \(E_a = 98.6\ kJ\ mol^{-1} = 98.6 \times 10^3\ J\ mol^{-1}\)
    • Substitute the values: \(\log(1.1 \times 10^{-3}) = \log(4 \times 10^{13}) - \dfrac{98.6 \times 10^3}{2.303 \times 8.314 \times T}\)
    • Solving for \(T\): \(T = 311.15\ K\)
    • Final result: \((d)\ 311.15\ K\)
    • Simple memory tip:
      • First-order half-life formula: \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{k}\).
      • If \(A\) and \(E_a\) are given, use Arrhenius equation.
      • Always convert \(10\) minutes to \(600\) seconds and \(kJ\) to \(J\).

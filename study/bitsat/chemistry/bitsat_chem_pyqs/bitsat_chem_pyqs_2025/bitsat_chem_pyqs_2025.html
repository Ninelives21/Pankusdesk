    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • For hydrogen or hydrogen-like one-electron atoms, the energy of the electron in the \(n^{\text{th}}\) shell is given by: \(E_n=-\dfrac{R_{\mathrm{H}}}{n^2}\)
      • The question says the energy is: \(-\dfrac{R_{\mathrm{H}}}{16}\)
      • So compare the two expressions: \(-\dfrac{R_{\mathrm{H}}}{n^2} = -\dfrac{R_{\mathrm{H}}}{16}\)
      • The negative sign and \(R_{\mathrm{H}}\) cancel out, giving: \(n^2=16\)
      • Therefore, \(n=4\).
      • Now remember the key rule: in hydrogen, all orbitals with the same principal quantum number \(n\) have the same energy. So they are called degenerate orbitals.
      • The number of orbitals present in the \(n^{\text{th}}\) shell is \(n^2\).
      • For \(n=4\), the degeneracy is: \(n^2=4^2=16\)
      • So, the degeneracy of the hydrogen atom at this energy level is 16.

      Simple tips to remember

      • For hydrogen energy questions, immediately think: \(E_n=-\dfrac{R_{\mathrm{H}}}{n^2}\).
      • If the denominator is \(16\), then \(n^2=16\), so \(n=4\).
      • For hydrogen, degeneracy of an energy level = number of orbitals in that shell = \(n^2\).
      • Shortcut: denominator of \(R_{\mathrm{H}}\) directly gives \(n^2\), and for hydrogen, degeneracy is also \(n^2\). So here the answer is directly \(16\).
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • This question combines Bohr's energy formula with the idea of degeneracy in hydrogen atoms.
      • In a hydrogen atom, energy depends only on the principal quantum number \(n\), not on \(l\) or \(m\).
      • Therefore, all orbitals belonging to the same shell have equal energy and are degenerate.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • The given energy gives \(n=4\), and the degeneracy of the \(n^{\text{th}}\) level in hydrogen is \(n^2\).
      • Thus, degeneracy \(=4^2=16\), which matches option \((c)\).
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Options \(8\), \(9\), and \(10\) do not match the \(n^2\) rule for hydrogen degeneracy.
      • A common mistake is to confuse degeneracy with the maximum number of electrons in a shell, which is \(2n^2\). Here the question asks for degeneracy, not electron capacity.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:

      • For degeneracy in hydrogen, answer is also \(n^2\). So here \(-\dfrac{R_{\mathrm{H}}}{16}\) directly gives degeneracy \(16\).

      Physical and Chemical Equilibria \(\rightarrow\) Abnormal Molar Masses and van't Hoff Factor - Page C53
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Magnesium nitrate is an electrolyte, so it dissociates into ions in aqueous solution.
      • The dissociation is: \(\mathrm{Mg(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow Mg^{2+} + 2NO_3^-}\)
      • So, one particle of \(\mathrm{Mg(NO_3)_2}\) gives total \(3\) particles after complete dissociation: \(1\) \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) ion and \(2\) \(\mathrm{NO_3^-}\) ions.
      • Therefore, the number of particles after dissociation is: \(n = 3\)
      • The degree of dissociation is \(40\%\), so: \(\alpha = 0.4\)
      • For dissociation, van't Hoff factor is: \(i = 1 + \alpha(n - 1)\)
      • Substitute the values: \(i = 1 + 0.4(3 - 1)\)
      • So: \(i = 1 + 0.4 \times 2 = 1 + 0.8 = 1.8\)
      • Now use the osmotic pressure formula: \(\pi = iCRT\)
      • Here, \(i = 1.8\), \(C = 1\ \mathrm{M}\), \(R = 0.0821\ \mathrm{L\ atm\ mol^{-1}K^{-1}}\), and \(T = 298\ \mathrm{K}\).
      • Substitute: \(\pi = 1.8 \times 1 \times 0.0821 \times 298\)
      • First calculate: \(0.0821 \times 298 = 24.4658\)
      • Then: \(\pi = 1.8 \times 24.4658 = 44.03844\ \mathrm{atm}\)
      • Therefore: \(\pi \approx 44.04\ \mathrm{atm}\)
      • So, the osmotic pressure of the solution is \(44.04\ \mathrm{atm}\).

      Simple tips to remember

      • For osmotic pressure, the basic formula is: \(\pi = CRT\).
      • If the solute dissociates or associates, add the van't Hoff factor: \(\pi = iCRT\).
      • For dissociation: \(i = 1 + \alpha(n - 1)\), where \(n\) is the number of ions formed from one formula unit.
      • For \(\mathrm{Mg(NO_3)_2}\), remember: nitrate has charge \(-1\), so two nitrate ions are needed with \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\). That is why it gives \(3\) ions in total.
      • Shortcut: \(1\ \mathrm{M}\) solution at \(298\ \mathrm{K}\) has \(CRT \approx 24.5\ \mathrm{atm}\). Then multiply by \(i = 1.8\), giving about \(44\ \mathrm{atm}\).
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Osmotic pressure is a colligative property, meaning it depends on the number of solute particles present in solution.
      • Electrolytes produce more particles because they dissociate into ions, so their osmotic pressure becomes higher than that of a non-electrolyte of the same molarity.
      • The van't Hoff factor \(i\) corrects the formula for this increase in particle number.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Using \(i = 1.8\) and \(\pi = iCRT\), the osmotic pressure comes out to be \(44.04\ \mathrm{atm}\).
      • This matches option \((b)\).
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option \((a)\) is close to the value of \(CRT\) without properly accounting for dissociation.
      • Options \((c)\) and \((d)\) are much too small for a \(1\ \mathrm{M}\) solution at \(298\ \mathrm{K}\).
      • The key mistake would be forgetting the van't Hoff factor or using the wrong number of ions.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • At room temperature, \(RT \approx 24.5\ \mathrm{L\ atm\ mol^{-1}}\). For \(1\ \mathrm{M}\), \(\pi \approx 24.5i\).
      • Here \(i = 1.8\), so \(\pi \approx 24.5 \times 1.8 \approx 44\ \mathrm{atm}\). This quickly gives option \((b)\).
    • Final result: The osmotic pressure of the solution is \(44.04\ \mathrm{atm}\).

    • Chapter / topic / subtopic: Organic Compounds with Functional Groups Containing Oxygen and Nitrogen \(\rightarrow\) Amines \(\rightarrow\) Protection of \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) group by acetylation
      Principles of Organic Chemistry and Hydrocarbons \(\rightarrow\) Aromatic electrophilic substitution \(\rightarrow\) Directive effect of substituents
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • The starting compound is p-toluidine, which has \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) and \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\) groups para to each other on the benzene ring.
      • The first reagent is \(\mathrm{Ac_2O}\), acetic anhydride. It acetylates the amino group.
      • So, \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) becomes \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\). This is called protection of the amino group.
      • This protection is useful because direct bromination of aniline or toluidine can become too fast and uncontrolled.
      • After acetylation, the compound becomes p-methyl acetanilide. The group \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\) is still ortho-para directing, but less strongly activating than \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\).
      • Now bromination is done using \(\mathrm{Br_2/CH_3COOH}\).
      • The para position to \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\) is already occupied by \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\), so bromine enters an available ortho position with respect to \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\).
      • This gives the brominated acetanilide intermediate.
      • Finally, \(\mathrm{H_2O/H^+}\) hydrolyses the \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\) group back to \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\).
      • So the final product \(Z\) contains \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) at the top, \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\) para to it, and \(\mathrm{-Br}\) ortho to the amino group.
      • This structure matches option \((c)\).


      Simple tips to remember

      • \(\mathrm{Ac_2O}\) usually means acetylation: it changes \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) into \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\).
      • Think of acetylation as putting a temporary cover on \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\), so that bromination becomes controlled.
      • \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\) is still ortho-para directing, so bromine goes to an ortho or para position.
      • If the para position is already blocked by \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\), then bromine goes to the ortho position.
      • \(\mathrm{H_2O/H^+}\) at the end removes the acetyl protection and brings back \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\).
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • This question is based on protection of the amino group in aromatic amines.
      • \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) is a very strong activating group, so it is often acetylated before electrophilic substitution.
      • The acetylated group \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\) controls the reaction better and still directs the incoming electrophile to ortho and para positions.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option \((c)\) shows \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) regenerated after hydrolysis and bromine placed ortho to it.
      • It also keeps the original \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\) group para to \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\), so it matches the full reaction sequence.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option \((a)\) still has \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\), but final hydrolysis should convert it back to \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\).
      • Option \((b)\) has \(\mathrm{-COCH_3}\) directly attached to the ring, which is not formed here. Acetylation happens on nitrogen, not on the benzene ring.
      • Option \((d)\) also incorrectly shows \(\mathrm{-COCH_3}\) on the ring instead of the correct brominated amine product.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Whenever you see \(\mathrm{Ac_2O}\) with an aromatic amine, first think: protect \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) as \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\).
      • If the final step is \(\mathrm{H_2O/H^+}\), remove the acetyl group and restore \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\).
      • So in this question, eliminate options where the final product still has \(\mathrm{-NHCOCH_3}\) or has \(\mathrm{-COCH_3}\) attached directly to the ring.
    • Final result: The product \(Z\) is the bromo derivative of p-toluidine shown in option \((c)\).

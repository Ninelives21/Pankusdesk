    Measurement
  • The units of mass, length and time are called fundamental units since they are independent units and cannot be derived from any other units. They are also called basic units.
  • The units for other quantities which can be derived from fundamental units are called derived units.
  • The units adopted by the General Conference of Weights and Measures is known as SI System or International System.
  • The SI system has seven basic units
    Length: metre, m
    Mass: kilogram, kg
    Time: second, s
    Temperature: kelvin, K
    Electric current: ampere, A
    Luminous intensity: candela, cd
    Amount of substance: mole, mol
  • Fundamental constants
    Elementary charge, e = 1.6 x 10-19 C
    Avogadro's number, N0 = 6.02 x 1023 mol-1
    Faraday, F = eN0 = 96500 C mol-1
    Gas constant, R = kN0 = 8.314 J mol-1 K-1
    Boltzmann constant, k = 1.381 x 10-23 J K-1
    Planck's constant, h = 6.63 x 10-34 J s
    Atomic mass unit, u = 1.66 x 10-27 kg atom-1
    Standard acceleration due to gravity, g = 9.8 m s-2
    Atmospheric pressure, P = 1.01 x 105 N m-2
    Molar volume of a gas at S.T.P., Vm = 0.0224 m3
  • Significant figures
    The meaningful digits which are known with certainty are known as significant figures.
  • Rules for counting significant figures
    All digits are significant except zero at the beginning of the number.
    Zero at the end or right of a number are significant provided they are on the right side of the decimal point.
    The numbers can be expressed in scientific notation also. In this term, every number is written as N x 10n, where N is a number with a single non-zero digit to the left of the decimal point and n is an integer.
    Zero at the end of a number and before the decimal point may or may not be significant.
  • Significant figures in numerical calculations
    For multiplication/division, the result should be reported with no more significant figures than the least precise known quantity in the calculation.
    For addition/subtraction, the result should be expressed with the same number of decimal places as the term carrying the smallest number of decimal places.
    For each exact number, exact numbers can be considered to have an unlimited number of significant figures.
  • Rounding off numerical results
    If the right most digit to be removed is less than 5, the preceding digit is not changed.
    If the right most digit to be removed is greater than 5, the preceding digit is increased by one.
    If the right most digit to be removed is 5, the preceding digit is not changed if it is even. Thus, 5.6645 becomes 5.664 when rounded off to four significant figures.
    If the right most digit to be removed is 5, then add one to the preceding digit if it is odd. Thus, 4.7475 becomes 4.748 when rounded off to four significant figures.
  • Dimensional analysis
    It is frequently necessary to convert one set of units to another in calculations. This can be done by the unit factor label method or unit factor method.
    A conversion factor is a factor equal to one that converts a quantity in one unit to the same quantity in another unit.
  • Precision
    If the values of different measurements are close to each other and hence close to their average value, the measurement is said to be precise.

    Chemical reactions
  • Laws of chemical combination
    Law of conservation of mass states that during any physical or chemical change, total mass of products is equal to the total mass of reactants.
  • Law of definite proportion states that a compound always contains the same elements combined in the same definite proportion by weight.
  • Law of multiple proportion states that when two or more elements combine to form two or more compounds, the different weights of one of the elements which combine with a fixed weight of the other bear a simple whole number ratio to one another.
  • Law of reciprocal proportions states that when two elements combine separately with a fixed mass of third element, then the ratio between their masses in which they combine will be either same or simple multiple of the ratio in which they combine with each other.
  • Gay Lussac's law of combining volumes states that under similar conditions of temperature and pressure, whenever gases react together, the volumes of the reacting gases as well as products bear a simple whole number ratio.
  • Avogadro's law states that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal number of molecules.
  • Dalton's atomic theory
    Matter is made up of small indivisible particles called atoms.
    Atoms can neither be created nor destroyed.
    Atom is the smallest unit that takes part in chemical combination.
    Atoms of two or more elements combine in whole number ratio to form compound atoms or molecules.
    Atoms of the same element have identical shape, size, mass and chemical properties.
    Atoms of different elements differ in their mass and chemical properties.
  • Atomic and molecular masses
    Atomic mass of an element is defined as the average relative mass of an atom of an element as compared to the mass of an atom of carbon C12 taken as 12.
    Atomic weight = Equivalent weight x valency.
    Dulong and Petit's law: Atomic weight x specific heat = 6.4 approximately.
  • Molecular mass of a substance is the average relative mass of its molecule as compared to the mass of an atom of carbon C12 taken as 12.
    Molecular mass = 2 x vapour density.
    Molecular mass = Mass of 22.4 L of vapour at STP.
  • Mole concept and stoichiometry
    1 mole of atoms = Gram atomic mass or 1 gram atom = 6.022 x 1023 atoms.
    1 mole of molecules = Gram molecular mass = 6.022 x 1023 molecules = 22.4 L at STP.
  • Determination of chemical formula
    Empirical formula gives the simple whole number ratio of atoms of various elements present in one molecule of the compound.
    Molecular formula gives the actual number of atoms of various elements present in one molecule of the compound.
    Molecular formula = n x empirical formula, where n = 1, 2, 3, etc.
  • Concentration of a solution
    Mass percentage of A = mass of A / mass of solution x 100.
    Volume percentage of A = volume of A / volume of solution x 100.
    Parts per million of A = mass of component A / total mass of solution x 106.
    Molarity = moles of solute / volume of solution in litre.
    Molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg.
    Normality = gram equivalents of solute / volume of solution in litre.
  • Stoichiometry of chemical reactions
    Write balanced equations for the reaction involved.
    Write the relative number of moles or relative masses of the reactants and products involved in the problem below their formulae.
    In case of a gaseous substance, write down 22.4 L at STP below the formula in place of one mole, if volume is involved.
    In case of solutions, apply the fact that 1 M solution contains 1 mole of solute in 1000 mL of solution.
    Apply unitary method to make the required calculation.
    Limiting reactant is the reactant which is present in smaller amount and reacts completely and hence decides the amount of products formed.

    Three states of matter and intermolecular interactions
  • Intermolecular forces
    Cause of difference in the three states of matter is the state of matter differ mainly due to difference in the intermolecular forces of attraction, i.e., forces of attraction existing among the molecules of a substance.
    Greater the intermolecular forces, higher is the melting point or boiling point or vice-versa.
  • Types of intermolecular forces
    Dipole-dipole interaction: These exist among the polar molecules like HCl, NH3, SO2, etc., i.e., molecules having permanent dipole moment.
    Ion-dipole interactions: These exist between Na+ and Cl- ions with H2O molecules when NaCl is dissolved in water.
    Ion-induced dipole interactions: These arise in presence of NO3- ion, where molecule induced dipole in non-polar gas molecules.
    Dipole-induced dipole interactions: These arise when a polar molecule approaches a non-polar molecule.
    London force or dispersion forces: These forces exist in non-polar molecules like H2, O2, etc., or monoatomic gases like He, Ne, Ar, etc.
  • The measurable properties of gases are
    Mass, volume, pressure and temperature.
  • Standard temperature and pressure or STP is 0 degree C or 273.15 K and 1 atm or 760 mm Hg or 101.325 kPa.

    Gaseous state
  • Gas laws and ideal gas equation
    Boyle's law states that at constant temperature, pressure of a fixed amount of gas varies inversely with the volume of the gas.
    P is proportional to 1/V at constant T and n.
    PV = constant.
  • Charles' law states that at constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of a gas increases or decreases by 1/273 of its volume at 0 degree C for each one degree rise or fall in temperature.
    V is proportional to T at constant P and n.
    V1/T1 = V2/T2.
  • The theoretically possible temperature at which the volume of gas becomes zero is called absolute zero temperature and corresponds to -273 degree C.
  • When the temperature of a substance is lowered, the thermal energy reduces. Absolute zero is the temperature reached when all possible thermal energy has been removed from a substance.
  • At absolute zero temperature, the total kinetic energy of the molecules becomes zero and hence molecular motion ceases.
  • Gay-Lussac's law states that at constant volume, pressure of fixed amount of a gas varies directly with temperature.
    P is proportional to T at constant V and n.
    P/T = constant.
  • Avogadro's law states that at given temperature and pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the amount of gas.
    V is proportional to n at constant P and T.
  • All gas laws can be combined to give the combined gas law or gas equation as follows:
    Boyle's law: V is proportional to 1/P.
    Charles' law: V is proportional to T.
    Avogadro's law: V is proportional to n.
    V is proportional to nT/P.
    PV = nRT.
  • R is the constant of proportionality and is known as gas constant or universal gas constant.
    Values of R:
    0.0821 litre atm K-1 mol-1
    8.314 x 107 erg K-1 mol-1
    8.314 joule K-1 mol-1
    1.987 or 2 calorie K-1 mol-1
  • Graham's law of diffusion states that under similar conditions of temperature and pressure, rates of diffusion of different gases are inversely proportional to the square root of their densities.
    r1/r2 = sqrt(d2/d1) = sqrt(M2/M1).
  • Dalton's law of partial pressure states that the total pressure exerted by a mixture of two or more non-reacting gases is equal to the sum of partial pressure of each component above the mixture.
    Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...
  • Real gases and deviation from ideal behaviour
    Gases which do not follow the ideal gas equation, i.e., PV = nRT and other gas laws at all temperatures and pressures are called real gases.
  • Gases show deviation from ideal behaviour due to following:
    The volume of the gas molecules cannot be neglected in comparison to the volume of the gas.
    There is considerable force of attraction between the gas molecules and consequently at high pressure and low temperature, molecules get closer.
  • By applying pressure and volume correction to the ideal gas equation, van der Waals' equation for real gas is obtained.
    [P + a n2/V2](V - nb) = nRT.
    Pressure correction = P + a n2/V2.
    Volume correction = V - nb.
  • More the intermolecular forces of attraction, more will be the value of a, more easily the gas will be liquefied.
    A gas can be liquefied by increasing the pressure and decreasing the temperature.
  • The extent to which a real gas deviates from ideal behaviour is known as the compressibility factor, Z.
    Z = PV/RT.
    For ideal gases, Z = 1 and for real gases, Z is not equal to 1.
    When Z < 1, it is negative deviation and the gas is more compressible than expected from ideal behaviour.
    When Z > 1, it is positive deviation and the gas is less compressible than expected.
  • The maximum temperature at which a gas can be liquefied by the application of pressure alone is called the critical temperature, Tc.
    The minimum pressure required to liquefy the gas at temperature Tc is called the critical pressure, Pc.
    The volume occupied by one mole of gas at critical temperature, Tc, and critical pressure, Pc, is called the critical volume, Vc.

    Liquid state
  • Liquid state is intermediate between gaseous and solid states. It has volume like solid, has gases but incompressibility like solids.
  • Properties of liquids
    Evaporation is the process of conversion of a liquid into its vapours at room temperature.
    Rate of evaporation depends upon temperature and surface area.
  • Vapour pressure is defined as the pressure exerted by the vapours in equilibrium with the liquid at a given temperature.
    At any given temperature, the vapour pressure of different liquids is different because their cohesive forces differ.
    It depends upon the nature of liquid and temperature of liquid.
  • Vapour pressure of a liquid at two different temperatures is expressed by the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:
    log(P2/P1) = ΔHvap / 2.303R (1/T1 - 1/T2).
  • Surface tension is the force per unit length acting perpendicular to the tangential line drawn on the surface of the liquid.
    Unit of surface tension is dynes cm-1 or J m-2.
    It is denoted by γ.
    The intermolecular attractive forces which are responsible for surface tension are comparatively smaller at higher temperature; hence surface tension decreases with rise in temperature.
  • Variation of γ with temperature is given by Eotvos equation.
    γ(M/d)2/3 = k(tc - t - 6)
    where M is molar mass, d is density, tc and t are critical and observed temperature in degree C and k is a constant.
  • Viscosity is the internal resistance to flow in liquids which one layer offers to another layer lying above or below it.
    Force of friction between two cylindrical layers each having area A, separated by a distance x cm and having a velocity difference v cm/sec is given by:
    f = η A dv/dx.
    Viscosity is generally determined by Ostwald's method.
  • The effect of temperature on viscosity of liquids is that in general, the viscosity decreases with increase in temperature because at high temperature, molecules have high kinetic energy and can overcome the intermolecular forces or slip past one another between the layers.
    The variation of viscosity with temperature can be expressed by the following relationship:
    η = AeE/RT.

    Solid state
  • Solid state has high density and low compressibility as compared to gas as well as liquid states.
  • Classification of crystalline solids
    Depending upon the nature of interparticle forces, solids are classified into four types: ionic, molecular, covalent or network and metallic.
  • Ionic solids contain cations and anions; attractive forces are strong electrostatic forces of attraction. Examples: NaCl, KNO3, MgO, LiF. Characteristic properties: hard, brittle, high melting point, soluble in polar solvents, insulator in solid state but conductor in molten state or aqueous solution.
  • Molecular solids contain molecules; attractive forces are van der Waals forces. Examples: H2, Ar, CCl4, Br2, CO2, naphthalene, etc. Characteristic properties: soft, low melting point, low density, volatile, insulator.
  • Covalent or network solids contain atoms; attractive forces are covalent bonds. Examples: Si, SiO2, SiC, diamond, graphite, etc. Characteristic properties: very hard, very high melting point, insulator except graphite.
  • Metallic solids contain atoms; attractive forces are metallic bonds. Examples: all metals and some alloys. Characteristic properties: range from soft to very hard, high melting points, malleable and ductile, good conductor of electricity and heat, lustrous.
  • Amorphous solids
    When constituent particles of a substance are not arranged in any regular arrangement, then the substance is said to be amorphous.
    Some properties of amorphous solids are: incompressible and rigid, have short range order, supercooled liquid, no sharp melting point.
  • Crystalline solids
    When constituent particles are arranged in a definite geometric pattern in three dimensional space.
    They have long range order, possess sharp melting point and exhibit directional differences in properties, i.e., anisotropy like refractive index, conductivity, etc.
  • Crystal lattices and unit cells
    Space lattice is the 3-D arrangement of an infinite set of points of constituent particles in space and is called space lattice.
    Unit cell is obtained by connecting the lattice points such that each parallelepiped contains a complete unit of pattern of the crystal. It is the smallest structure of which the crystalline solid is made.
    The parameters of unit cells are relative lengths of the edges along the three axes a, b, c and relative angles between the edges α, β, γ.
  • There are seven crystal systems: cubic, tetragonal, orthorhombic, rhombohedral, hexagonal, monoclinic and triclinic.
  • Types of unit cells
    Primitive or simple unit cell: every corner of it is occupied by particle.
    Non-primitive unit cell: there are particles at the corners as well as some other sites.
  • Types of non-primitive unit cells:
    BCC or body centred cubic: there is one particle at every corner plus one particle at the body centre of the cube.
    FCC or face centred cubic: there is one particle at every corner plus one particle at every face centre.
    End face centred: there is one particle at every corner plus one particle each at the centre of two opposite faces.
  • Contribution of different types of particles to the unit cell:
    Body centre = 1
    Face centre = 1/2
    Edge centre = 1/4
    Corner = 1/8
  • Total number of atoms in different unit cells:
    Simple cubic = 1
    BCC = 2
    FCC = 4
  • Close packing in crystals
    HCP or hexagonal close packing: in the closest packing on a plane such that each sphere is in contact with six spheres at the corners of a regular hexagon called layer A. A second layer B is placed on layer A such that the spheres of the second layer occupy alternate holes in layer A.
    The arrangement is called ABABAB.
  • CCP or cubic close packing: the spheres are arranged on a flat plane so that each sphere is in contact with six spheres at the corners of a regular hexagon called layer A. A second layer B is placed on layer A such that the spheres of the second layer occupy alternate holes in layer A. A third layer C is placed on layer B such that the spheres of the third layer occupy the other vacant alternate holes in layer A.
    The layers A and B are then repeated on and above. This arrangement is called ABCABC.
    Examples: silver, gold, copper, platinum.
  • Packing in solids
    Packing fraction or density of packing = volume occupied by spherical atom / volume of the cube.
    For simple cubic, packing fraction = π/6 = 0.52.
    For BCC, packing fraction = √3π/8 = 0.68.
    For FCC, packing fraction = π/(3√2) = 0.74.
  • For BCC, d = √3a/2.
    For FCC, d = a/√2.
    Where d is the distance of nearest neighbours and it is the edge length of cubic unit cell.
  • Density of the crystal:
    d = Z x M / a3 x NA.
    Z = number of atoms or formula units per unit cell.
    M = atomic weight of element or formula weight of ionic compound.
  • Voids in solids
    Atoms in close-packing are packed in such a manner that they tend to occupy the maximum space available but still some space remains unoccupied. This unoccupied or empty space in the crystal lattice is called interstitial site or void.
    For a tetrahedral void, r = 0.225R.
    For an octahedral void, r = 0.414R.
  • Limiting radius ratio and coordination number
    Radius ratio 0.155 - 0.225 gives coordination number 3 and planar triangular geometry.
    Radius ratio 0.225 - 0.414 gives coordination number 4 and tetrahedral geometry.
    Radius ratio 0.414 - 0.732 gives coordination number 6 and octahedral geometry.
    Radius ratio 0.732 - 1 gives coordination number 8 and cubic geometry.
  • Structure of solids
    In simple ionic compounds, generally two arrangements are possible, HCP and CCP. The larger ions, i.e., anions adopt the arrangement and cations occupy different voids.
  • NaCl has CCP arrangement of Cl- and Na+ occupying all octahedral voids. Coordination number: Na+ = 6, Cl- = 6.
    CsCl has simple cubic arrangement of Cl- and Cs+ in body centre. Coordination number: Cs+ = 8, Cl- = 8.
    ZnS zinc blende has CCP arrangement of S2- and Zn2+ in alternate tetrahedral voids. Coordination number: Zn2+ = 4, S2- = 4.
    ZnS wurtzite has HCP arrangement of S2- and Zn2+ in alternate tetrahedral voids. Coordination number: Zn2+ = 4, S2- = 4.
    CaF2 fluorite has CCP arrangement of Ca2+ and F- in all tetrahedral voids. Coordination number: Ca2+ = 8, F- = 4.
    TiO2 rutile has distorted BCC of Ti4+ and O2-. Coordination number: Ti4+ = 6, O2- = 3.
    Na2O anti-fluorite has CCP arrangement of O2- and Na+ in all tetrahedral voids. Coordination number: Na+ = 4, O2- = 8.
  • Metallic solids
    Metallic solids are the crystalline solids formed by atoms of the same metallic element. The bonding electrons are delocalised over the space of the crystal and have freedom to move from one end to the other.
    The force of attraction between positive metallic ions and negatively charged sea of delocalised electrons is called metallic bond.
  • The presence of mobile electrons make all the metals good conductor of heat and electricity. If metal is heated at one end, then the heat is transferred to the other end by mobile electrons.
    On application of shearing force, the layers slide on one another and hence the structure is not fractured.
  • Covalent crystals
    Diamond is the most precious crystal used in jewellery and it is an allotropic form of carbon. All carbon atoms in diamond are sp3 hybridised carbon atoms and the bonding continues in all directions to form a big giant network of covalent solid.
    Graphite is another allotropic modification of carbon. All carbon atoms in graphite are sp2 hybridised. Each carbon atom is covalently bonded to three other sp2 hybridised carbon atoms and form interlinked six membered rings of carbon atoms.
  • Crystal defects
    In a crystalline solid, the atoms, ions or molecules are arranged in a definite repeating pattern, but some defects may occur in the pattern. Deviations from perfect arrangement may occur due to temperature changes or presence of additional particles.
    Point defects are irregularities or deviations from ideal arrangement around a point or an atom in a crystalline substance.
    Line defects are irregularities or deviations from ideal arrangement in entire rows of lattice points.
  • Nature of defects in crystals
    Schottky defect is caused when some of the lattice points are unoccupied, and these points are called vacancies or holes.
    Frenkel defect is caused when some of the ions leave their lattice sites to occupy an interstitial site.
  • Metal excess defect
    A negative ion may be absent from its lattice site leaving a hole which is occupied by an electron, thereby maintaining the electrical balance. This type of defect exists in crystals which may develop colour when heated with excess of their constituent metal vapours or treated with high energy radiation.
    Anion sites occupied by electrons in this way are called F-centres.
  • Interstitial ions and electrons
    Metal excess defects can also occur when an extra positive ion is present in an interstitial position in the lattice and electrical neutrality is maintained by the inclusion of an interstitial electron.
  • Metal deficiency defect
    This defect is caused by the missing cation from its lattice site. The extra negative charge may be balanced if the adjacent metal ion corresponding positive charge.
  • Electrical and magnetic properties of solids
    On the basis of electrical conductivity, solids are divided into three categories: conductors, insulators and semiconductors.
    Metals have conductivity 104 ohm-1 cm-1 and insulators have conductivity 10-20 ohm-1 cm-1.
  • A conductor may conduct electricity through movement of electrons or ions. Metals conduct electricity in solid as well as liquid state.
    The conductivity of metals depends upon the number of valence electrons available per atom.
  • If the gap between filled valence band and the next higher unoccupied conduction band is large, electrons cannot jump to it and such a substance is very small conducting and it behaves as an insulator.
  • If valence band and conduction band are small, some electrons can jump to conduction band and some substance like silicon and germanium show this type of behaviour and are called intrinsic semiconductors.
  • Dielectric properties of solids
    Insulators do not conduct electricity because the electrons present in them are held tightly by the nuclei. However, when electric field is applied, polarisation takes place because nuclei are attracted to one side and the electron cloud to the other side.
  • Piezoelectricity: when mechanical stress is applied on such crystals, electricity is produced due to displacement of ions.
    The electricity thus produced is called piezoelectricity and the crystals are called piezoelectric crystals.

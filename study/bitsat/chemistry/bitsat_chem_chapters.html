    1. Terms Used in Thermodynamics - Page C42
      1. The branch of science which deals with the study of transformation of energy during physical or chemical changes is known as thermodynamics
      2. Types of systems
      3. Open system can exchange both matter and energy with the surroundings
      4. Closed system can exchange only energy with the surroundings and cannot exchange matter
      5. Isolated system can neither exchange matter nor energy with the surroundings
      6. No system is perfectly isolated
      7. A system which is completely sealed to prevent inflow or outflow of matter and is thermally insulated to prevent flow of heat can be considered as an isolated system
      8. Macroscopic properties are properties arising from bulk behavior of matter
      9. Extensive properties depend on quantity of matter such as mass, volume, free energy, entropy
      10. Intensive properties are independent of amount of matter such as temperature and viscosity
      11. State variables are measurable properties such as pressure, volume, temperature, composition
      12. State function depends only on state of system such as internal energy U, enthalpy H, entropy S
      13. Thermodynamic equilibrium is when macroscopic properties do not change with time

    2. Types of Processes - Page C42
      1. Process is the change of thermodynamic system from one state to another
      2. Isothermal process in which temperature remains constant
      3. Adiabatic process in which no heat is lost or gained by the system
      4. Isobaric process in which pressure remains constant
      5. Isochoric process in which volume remains constant
      6. Reversible process where small change in state variable can reverse direction at any stage
      7. Irreversible process which is not reversible
      8. Cyclic process where system returns to its original state

    3. Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics - Page C42
      1. If two thermodynamic systems are in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other

    4. Internal Energy, Heat and Work - Page C42
      1. Internal energy is the total energy associated with a system
      2. E = E_trans + E_rot + E_vib + E_bonding + E_elect + etc
      3. Internal energy is an extensive state property
      4. When system is at higher temperature than surroundings, energy flows until equilibrium is reached
      5. Heat absorbed by system q > 0
      6. Heat released by system q < 0
      7. Work is done when force causes displacement
      8. PV work occurs when gas expands or contracts
      9. Expansion: W < 0
      10. Compression: W > 0

    5. First Law of Thermodynamics - Page C43

      2. Delta U = q + W

    6. Sign Conventions for Heat and Work - Page C43

      2. q < 0 heat released
      3. W > 0 work done on system
      4. W < 0 work done by system
      5. Positive q or W increases Delta E

    1. Concentration Units - Page C51
      1. Molarity
        1. Defined as number of moles of solute dissolved per litre of solution
        2. Units: mole per litre and mole per decimetre cube
        3. Molarity changes with temperature because volume of liquid changes with temperature
      2. Molality
        1. Defined as number of moles of solute dissolved per 1000 gram or per kg of solvent
        2. Units: mole per kg
        3. Molality is better for expressing concentration because it does not change with temperature
        4. Mass of solvent does not change with temperature
      3. Mole fraction
        1. Ratio of number of moles of one component to total number of moles present in solution
        2. Independent of temperature
      4. Normality
        1. Number of gram equivalents of solute dissolved per litre of solution
        2. Units: gram equivalent per litre
        3. Normality also changes with temperature
        4. Relationship between normality and molarity: Normality = n x molarity
        5. For acids: normality = molarity x basicity
        6. For bases: normality = molarity x acidity

    2. Solubility of Solids in Liquids - Page C51

      2. In general, a solute dissolves in a solvent if the intermolecular interactions are similar in the two

    3. Effect of Temperature - Page C51
      1. Solubility of a solid in a liquid is significantly affected by temperature changes
      2. In a nearly saturated solution, if dissolution is endothermic, solubility increases with rise in temperature
      3. If dissolution is exothermic, solubility decreases with increase in temperature

    4. Effect of Pressure - Page C51

    5. Solubility of Gases and Henry's Law - Page C51
      1. Many gases dissolve in water
      2. Solubility of gases increases with increase in pressure
      3. Henry was the first to give a quantitative relation between pressure and solubility of a gas in solvent
      4. Henry's law states that the mass of a gas dissolved per unit volume of solvent at a given temperature is proportional to the pressure of the gas in equilibrium with the solution
      5. M is proportional to P or M = KP
      6. Henry's law may also be stated as mole fraction of gas in solution is proportional to partial pressure of gas in the solution
      7. X is proportional to P
      8. X = K'P
      9. P = KHX, where KH is Henry's law constant

    6. Effect of Temperature - Page C52
      1. Solubility of gases in liquids decreases with rise in temperature

    7. Vapour Pressure of Solution - Page C52
      1. Pressure exerted by vapours above the liquid surface in equilibrium with the liquid at a given temperature is called vapour pressure
      2. Vapour pressure of a liquid depends upon nature of the liquid
      3. Liquids with weaker intermolecular forces escape readily into vapour phase and have greater vapour pressure
      4. Vapour pressure of a liquid increases with increase in temperature
      5. Vapour pressure of a solution containing a non-volatile solute is less than that of pure solvent because fewer solvent molecules escape as vapours

    8. Raoult's Law - Page C52
      1. Raoult's law for a binary solution states that at a given temperature, in a solution of volatile liquids, the partial pressure of each component in the solution is equal to the product of the vapour pressure of the pure component and its mole fraction
      2. Raoult's law can be expressed as xB = (P0A - PA) / P0A
      3. xB: mole fraction of component B
      4. P0A: vapour pressure of pure liquid A
      5. PA: vapour pressure of liquid A
      6. Raoult's law is a special case of Henry's law

    9. Ideal and Non-Ideal Solution - Page C52
      1. Ideal solution: a solution which obeys Raoult's law at all concentrations and temperature
      2. Characteristics of an ideal solution
        1. Delta Hmix = 0
        2. Delta Vmix = 0
      3. Examples: benzene and toluene; ethyl bromide and ethyl iodide
      4. Non-ideal solution: a solution in which A-B interactions are of different magnitudes than those in pure components
      5. Non-ideal solutions showing positive deviation
        1. Total vapour pressure determined experimentally is higher than that calculated from Raoult's law
        2. Example: ethanol and cyclohexane
      6. Non-ideal solutions showing negative deviation
        1. Total vapour pressure determined experimentally is lower than that calculated from Raoult's law
        2. Example: H-bonding between chloroform and acetone molecules
      7. Azeotropes: some liquids on mixing form azeotropes, which are binary mixtures having same composition in liquid and vapour phase and boil at a constant temperature

    10. Colligative Properties - Page C52

    12. Equilibrium - Page C54
      1. Equilibrium is a state at which the concentrations of reactants and products do not change with time
      2. State of equilibrium is dynamic and not static
      3. At equilibrium, rate of forward reaction is equal to rate of backward reaction
      4. Equilibrium may be defined as physical equilibrium and chemical equilibrium

    13. Equilibrium in Physical Processes - Page C54
      1. Types of equilibria which exist in the three states of matter
        1. Solid to liquid
        2. Liquid to gas
        3. Gas to solid
      2. Example of solid-liquid equilibrium: ice to water
      3. Rate of melting of ice = rate of freezing of water
      4. Dissolution of solids in liquids
      5. Dissolution of gases in liquids
      6. Characteristics of physical equilibria
        1. Measurable properties of the system become constant at equilibrium
        2. Equilibrium can be established only in closed systems
        3. Equilibrium is always dynamic in nature

    14. Equilibrium in Chemical Processes - Page C55
      1. Chemical equilibrium is attained when rate of forward and backward reaction of a reversible system are equal
      2. The state is dynamic
      3. Types of chemical equilibria
        1. Homogeneous equilibrium
        2. Heterogeneous equilibrium
      4. Characteristics of chemical equilibrium
        1. It is dynamic in nature
        2. Properties of a system become constant at equilibrium and remain unchanged thereafter
        3. Equilibrium can be attained only if the system is closed
        4. It can be approached from either direction
        5. A catalyst does not alter the equilibrium point
      5. Law of mass action
        1. Rate of a chemical reaction at any particular temperature is proportional to product of molar concentrations of reactants
        2. Each concentration term is raised to the power equal to number of molecules of the respective reactants taking part in the reaction
        3. If aA + bB gives cC, then rate = k[A]a[B]b

    15. Equilibrium Constant - Page C55
      1. Equilibrium constant is the ratio of products of molar concentration of the products and that of the reactants
      2. Each concentration term is raised to the power equal to stoichiometric coefficient in the balanced chemical equation
      3. Equilibrium constant is defined at constant temperature
      4. For gaseous systems, equilibrium constant can be expressed in terms of partial pressure
      5. Effect of moles and temperature on equilibrium constant

    18. Basic Concepts of Acids and Bases - Page C56
      1. Arrhenius concept
        1. Acid is a proton donor in aqueous solution
        2. Base is a hydroxyl ion donor in aqueous solution
        3. Examples of acids: HCl, HNO3
        4. Examples of bases: NH4OH, Ca(OH)2
      2. Bronsted-Lowry concept
        1. Acid is a proton donor
        2. Base is a proton acceptor
        3. Examples of acids: HCl, NH4+
        4. Examples of bases: NH3, Cl-
        5. All Arrhenius acids are Bronsted acids, but all Arrhenius bases are not Bronsted bases
      3. Conjugate acid-base pair
        1. In acid-base reactions, the reverse reaction is also an acid-base reaction
        2. Every acid forms a conjugate base by loss of a proton
        3. Every base forms a conjugate acid by gain of a proton
        4. A conjugate pair of acid and base differ by one proton only
        5. Strong acids have weak conjugate bases
        6. Weak acids have strong conjugate bases
        7. Strong bases have weak conjugate acids
        8. Weak bases have strong conjugate acids
      4. Lewis concept
        1. Acid is a molecule or ion which can accept an electron pair
        2. Base is a molecule or ion which can donate an electron pair
      5. Strength of acids and bases can be measured in terms of number of H+ and OH- ions produced by them in aqueous solution respectively

    Brief history of periodic table and periodic trends
  • Brief history of periodic classification
    • The concept of chemical periodicity is central to the study of Inorganic Chemistry.
    • The basic objective of classification is to arrange the facts regarding elements and their compounds in such a way that we may have greatest control over their characteristics with least possible effort.
    • The arrangement of elements in order of increasing atomic numbers, with elements having similar properties in vertical columns, is known as periodic table.
    • Some of the earlier classifications based on atomic masses of the elements are given below:
      • Döbereiner's law of triads: According to this law, in certain triads (groups of three elements which possessed similar chemical properties) the atomic mass of the central element was merely the arithmetic mean of the atomic masses of the other two elements and the properties of the middle element were in between those of the end members.
        Cl = 35.5, Br = 80, I = 127
        Atomic mass of Br = (35.5 + 127) / 2 = 81.25
      • De-Chancourtois first arranged the elements in order of increasing atomic weights. He located similar elements in this way and published a helical form in 1863.
      • Newland's law of octaves: According to this law, the elements when arranged in order of their increasing atomic masses, the eighth succeeding element was the repetition of the first one like 8th note of the musical scale.
    • Newland's law of octaves
      Musical scale Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ne
      1 2 3 4 5 6 7
      1st H Li Be B C N O
      2nd F Na Mg Al Si P S
    • Lothar Meyer's curve: He calculated the atomic volumes of the known elements by applying formula:
      Atomic volume = Atomic mass / Density
    • On the basis of plots drawn by him for atomic volumes against corresponding atomic masses, he proposed that the physical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic masses.
  • Periodic law and modern periodic table
    • Mendeleev's Periodic Law
      • It states that the physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic function of their atomic weights.
      • Merits of Mendeleev's periodic table
        • Correction of atomic masses.
        • Prediction of new elements.
      • Demerits of Mendeleev's periodic table
        • Position of H2 could not be explained.
        • In certain pairs of elements increasing order of atomic masses was not followed.
        • Position of isotopes could not be explained.
    • Modern Periodic Law and Long Form of Periodic Table
      • Modern periodic law states that the physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.
      • Based on the modern periodic law, elements are arranged in order of their increasing atomic numbers.
      • The long form of periodic table is constructed on the basis of repeating electronic configuration of the atoms when they are arranged in the order of increasing atomic numbers.
      • There are 7 horizontal rows or periods and 18 vertical columns or groups in the long form of periodic table.
        • A period constitutes a series of elements whose atoms have same value of 'n'.
        • A group constitutes a series of elements with similar electronic configuration (have same number of valence electrons) and on moving down the group, the value of 'n' increases.
      • Since the long form of periodic table is based on the modern periodic law, so this periodic table is free from the demerits of Mendeleev's periodic table, i.e.,
        • Position of isotopes could be explained as they are elements with same atomic no. and different atomic mass.
        • Since the basis of classification is atomic no. and not atomic mass, the anomaly regarding certain pairs of elements in the Mendeleev's periodic table disappeared.

  • Periodicity in properties
    • Periodicity is the repetition of similar properties of the elements after certain regular intervals, when they are arranged in order of increasing atomic numbers.
    • Periodicity is caused by the repetition of similar electronic configuration in the valence shell after certain regular intervals.
  • Atomic and ionic radii
    • Trends in Physical Properties
      • Atomic radius is the distance from the centre of the nucleus to the outermost shell of electrons or the distance of closest approach of one atom to another atom in a given bonding situation.
      • There are three types of radii:
        Covalent radii, Crystal radii and van der Waals' radii.
      • Covalent radius: For homonuclear diatomic molecule,
        rA = dA-A/2
      • For heteronuclear diatomic molecule,
        dA-B = rA + rB
      • When electronegativities of A and B are different,
        dA-B = rA + rB - 0.09(χA - χB)
      • Crystal radius: It is one half the distance between nuclei of two adjacent metal atoms in the metallic closed packed crystal lattice in which metal exhibits a coordination no. of 12.
      • van der Waals' radius: It is half the distance between the nuclei of two non-bonded neighbouring atoms of two adjacent molecules.
      • rcovalent < rcrystal < rvan der Waals
      • Atomic radii decrease with increase in atomic numbers in a period. From left to right, the magnitude of nuclear charge increases progressively by one unit while addition of electrons occurs in the same shell. Hence, electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus due to higher nuclear charge and thereby cause a decrease in size of the atom.
      • On going from top to bottom in a group, the atomic radii of elements increase. Down the group, nuclear charge increases with increase in atomic numbers. However, there is an increase in the number of principal quantum level although the number of electrons in the outermost shell remains the same. The effect of increase in the size of electron cloud is more pronounced and thus the size of atom increases.
      • Ionic radius is the effective distance from the nucleus of the ion upto which it has an influence in the ionic bond.
      • The size of a positive ion is always smaller than that of the parent atom, as in a cation, the effective nuclear charge per electron increases and the electrons are more strongly attracted towards the nucleus. e.g., Mg > Mg+ > Mg2+.
      • The size of a negative ion is larger than that of the parent atom, as in an anion, the effective nuclear charge per electron decreases and the electron cloud is held less tightly by the nucleus. e.g., Br- > Br.
  • Ionisation energy
    • The minimum amount of energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated atom in the gaseous state is known as ionisation potential or ionisation energy or first ionisation potential of the element.
      M + energy → M+ + e-
    • The energy required to remove the second electron from the monovalent cation is called second ionisation potential (IE2).
      M+ + IE2 → M2+ + e-
    • Similarly, we have third, fourth ... ionisation potentials.
    • Factors affecting the value of ionisation potential
      • Size of the atom: Larger the atomic size, smaller is value of ionisation potential.
      • Screening effect: Higher the screening effect, lesser is the value of ionisation potential. Due to screening effect, the valence electron experiences less attraction towards nucleus. This brings decrease in the nuclear charge (Z) actually present on the nucleus. This reduced nuclear charge is termed effective nuclear charge (Z*).
      • Nuclear charge: As the nuclear charge increases among atoms having same number of energy shells, the ionisation potential increases because the force of attraction towards nucleus increases.
      • Penetration effect: The ionisation energy also depends on the type of electron which is removed. s, p, d and f electrons have orbitals with different shapes. s-electron penetrates nearer to the nucleus, and is therefore more tightly held than a d-electron, and a d-electron is more tightly held than an f-electron. Other factors being equal, ionisation energies are in the order s > p > d > f.
      • Half-filled and fully filled orbitals: The atoms having half-filled and fully filled orbitals are comparatively more stable, hence more energy is required to remove the electron from such atoms. The ionisation potential is rather high than the expected value in the case of such an atom.
    • Variation of I.E. in a group
      • On moving down a group:
      • Nuclear charge increases.
      • Z* (effective nuclear charge) due to screening is almost constant.
      • Number of shells increases, hence atomic size increases.
      • There is increase in the number of inner electrons which shield the valence electrons from the nucleus.
    • Thus, force of attraction between electrons and nucleus decreases and tendency to remove the valence electron increases. Hence, IE decreases on moving down the group.
    • Variation of I.E. in a period: On moving across a period, the atomic size decreases and nuclear charge increases. Hence, I.E. increases along a period from left to right.
    • Successive ionisation energies are higher: The second ionisation energies are higher than the first ionisation energies. This is because after the removal of the first electron, the atom changes into monovalent positive ion. In the ion, the number of electrons decreases but the nuclear charge remains the same. As a result of this, the remaining electrons are held more tightly by the nucleus and it becomes difficult to remove the second electron. Hence, the value of second ionisation energy IE2, is greater than that of the first, IE1 or I.E.1 < I.E.2 < I.E.3 < ......
  • Electron affinity and electron gain enthalpy
    • The amount of energy released when an electron is added to an isolated gaseous atom to produce a monovalent anion is called electron gain enthalpy or first electron gain enthalpy.
      A + e- → A- + energy
    • The addition of an electron to a neutral atom is an exoergic process. The first electron gain enthalpy is given a negative sign. The addition of electron to A- requires energy to overcome the force of repulsion. The second electron gain enthalpy is therefore, an endoergic process and will be given a positive sign.
    • Factors affecting the value of electron gain enthalpy
      • Nuclear charge: The electron gain enthalpy becomes more negative as the nuclear charge increases.
      • Size of the atom: With the increase in size of the atom, the distance between the nucleus and the incoming electron increases, thus the electron gain enthalpy will be less negative.
      • Electronic configuration: The elements having stable electronic configurations of half-filled and completely filled valence subshells show very small tendency to accept additional electron and thus electron gain enthalpies are highly positive in these cases.
    • Variation of electron gain enthalpy in a period
      • On moving across a period, the atomic size decreases and consequently, the atom has a greater tendency to attract additional electron, i.e., its electron gain enthalpy becomes more negative.
      • Electron gain enthalpies of metals are less negative while those of non-metals are more negative.
      • Halogens have the most negative electron gain enthalpies. This is due to their strong tendency to gain an additional electron to change into the stable ns2np6 configuration.
    • Variation of electron gain enthalpy in a group
      • On moving down a group, the atomic size increases and thus electron gain enthalpy becomes less negative.
      • The fact that fluorine has a less negative electron gain enthalpy than chlorine seems to be due to the relatively greater effectiveness of 2p electrons in the small F atom to repel the additional electron entering the atom than the 3p electrons in the larger Cl atom.
    • Successive electron gain enthalpies: Like ionisation energies, the second and higher electron gain enthalpies are also possible. However, second electron is added to a negatively charged ion and the addition is opposed by coulombic repulsions. The energy has to be supplied to force the second electron into the anion.
    • Electron affinity: The negative of the enthalpy change accompanying the addition of an electron to an isolated gaseous atom is called electron affinity.
  • Electronegativity
    • In a molecule, tendency of the atom to attract bonding pair towards itself is its electronegativity. B is said to be more electronegative than A if it pulls bonding pair towards itself.
    • According to Mulliken,
      E.N. = (IP + EA) / 2
    • On Pauling scale,
      E.N. of an atom = (IP + EA) / 5.6
      (Values of IP and EA are taken in eV.)
    • E.N. of an atom = (IP + EA) / (2 x 62.5)
      (Values of IP and EA are taken in kilo calories per mole.)
    • Variation of electronegativity in a group
      • On moving down the group:
      • Z increases but Z* almost remains constant.
      • Number of shells (n) increases.
      • rn (atomic radius) increases.
      • Therefore, electronegativity decreases moving down the group.
    • Variation of electronegativity in a period
      • While moving across a period from left to right:
      • Z* increases.
      • n (number of shells) remains constant.
      • rn decreases.
      • Hence, electronegativity increases in a period.
    • Importance of electronegativity
      • Nature of the bond between two atoms can be predicted from the electronegativity difference of the two atoms.
      • The difference χA - χB = 0, i.e., χA = χB, the bond is purely covalent.
      • The difference χA - χB is small, i.e., χA > χB, the bond is polar covalent.
      • The difference χA - χB is 1.7, the bond is 50% covalent and 50% ionic.
      • The difference χA - χB is very high, the bond is more ionic and less covalent. The molecule will be represented in such case as Bδ+Aδ-.
      • Percentage ionic character
        = 16|χA - χB| + 3.5(χA - χB)2
        where χA and χB represent electronegativities of bonded atoms A and B respectively.
      • Greater the value of difference (χA - χB), more stable will be the bond.
      • H-F: 1.9, H-Cl: 0.9, H-Br: 0.7, H-I: 0.4
        Stability decreases.
      • Compounds in which χA - χB is very small are unstable in nature.
  • Valency and oxidation state
    • Valency or Oxidation states: The electrons present in the outermost shell are called valence electrons and these determine the valency of the atom and in case of representative elements, it is equal to either the no. of valence electrons or 8 minus the no. of valence electrons.
    • Variation in a period
      • No. of valence electrons increases from 1 to 8 from left to right.
      • Valency with respect to H and Cl increases from 1 to 4 and then decreases to zero.
    • Variation in a group
      • No. of valence electrons remains same from top to bottom.
      • The elements in a group exhibit same valency.
    • Oxidation state: Oxidation state of an element in a particular compound gives the charge acquired by its atoms on the basis of electronegativity consideration from other atoms in the molecule.
  • Chemical reactivity trends
    • Chemical reactivity: The chemical reactivity of elements is based on their physical properties, thus in the periodic table, the chemical reactivity is highest at the two extreme ends (i.e., alkali metals and halogens) and lowest in the centre.
    • The chemical reactivity is exhibited by elements based on their tendency to lose or gain electrons, which in turn is related to their metallic and non-metallic character.
    • Reducing nature decreases in a period while oxidising nature increases. The reducing nature increases in a group while oxidising nature decreases.
    • The basic nature of the oxides decreases in a period while acidic nature increases. In a group, basic nature increases while acidic nature decreases.
  • Nature of hydrides and oxyacids
    • Nature of hydrides
      • In a period
        • 2nd Period: NH3 weak base, H2O neutral, HF weak acid.
        • 3rd Period: PH3 very weak base, H2S weak acid, HCl strong acid.
      • In a group:
        HF, HCl, HBr, HI
        • Strength of acid increases.
        • Stability decreases.
        • Reducing nature increases.
    • Nature of oxyacids
      • In a period:

      • In a group:
        HNO3, H3PO4, H3AsO4

        HClO4, HBrO4, HIO4

      • Chlorine forms a number of oxyacids.
        HClO, HClO2, HClO3, HClO4

        • Stability increases.
        • Oxidising nature decreases.

    Valence electrons and ionic bond
  • Introduction to chemical bonding
    • A chemical bond is the attractive force which holds various constituents (atoms, ions, etc.) together in different chemical species.
    • The chemical bonding among atoms can be achieved by different ways depending upon number of valence electrons in the outermost shell of the atoms.
    • Bonding is a way of lowering the energy of the system to attain stability. The different types of chemical bonds formed between atoms are:
      • Ionic bond
      • Covalent bond
      • Coordinate bond
      • Metallic bond
      • Hydrogen bond
  • Kössel-Lewis approach to chemical bonding
    • According to Kössel and Lewis concept of bonding, an atom has a tendency to attain stable octet (inert gas configuration) by gaining, losing or sharing electrons. Atoms thus, combine to attain such configuration which results in bond formation (octet rule).
  • Valence electrons
    • The number of electrons present in the outermost energy level of an atom is called valence electrons. Chemical bonding depends on the valence electrons.
    • Only valence electrons take part in chemical bonding.
    • The number of chemical bonds formed by an atom in a molecule is known as valency.
    • Valency of an atom depends mainly on the valence electrons.
    • Atoms having 8 electrons in their outermost shell are stable and chemically inert.
    • An atom can acquire 8 electrons in outermost shell by two means:
      • by losing or accepting electrons, i.e., ionic bonding
      • by sharing electrons i.e., covalent bonding.
  • Ionic bonds
    • The bonds formed between atoms by transferring of valence electrons from one atom to another is said to be electrovalent or ionic bond, and the compound so formed is an ionic compound.
  • Conditions for formation of electrovalent bond
    • Number of valence electrons: The atom which changes to a cation must contain 1, 2 or 3 valence electrons and the one changing to anion must contain 5, 6 or 7 valence electrons.
    • Electronegativity difference: Higher the electronegativity difference between the atoms, more ionic will be the bond.
    • Low ionisation energy: Ionisation energy of the element forming the cation, i.e., metal, should be low.
    • High electron affinity: Electron affinity of the element forming anion, i.e., non-metal, should be high.
    • High lattice energy: Higher the lattice energy, greater is the ease of formation of ionic compound.
  • Born-Haber cycle and lattice energy
    • Born-Haber cycle: The Born-Haber cycle is an approach to analyse reaction energies. It is primarily used for calculating lattice energies. The formation of an ionic crystal may occur either by direct combination of the elements or by an alternate process in which various steps are involved.
    • ΔHf = ΔHs + IE + 1/2 ΔHd + Ea + U
    • Where, ΔHf = Enthalpy of formation, ΔHs = Enthalpy of sublimation, IE = Ionisation energy, ΔHd = Enthalpy of dissociation, Ea = Electron affinity and U = Lattice energy.
  • Fajans' rules and covalent character of ionic bonds
    • Fajans' Rules: It states that the magnitude of covalent character in an ionic bond depends upon the polarising power. Higher the polarising power, more will be the covalent character.
    • In general,
      Polarising power ∝ 1 / size of cation
      ∝ size of anion
      ∝ charge of anions or cations
    • Cations having 18 electrons in outermost shell bring greater polarisation than the cations of similar size having 8 electrons in outermost shell.
    • Among NaI and NaCl, NaI, due to larger size of I- ion, has more covalent character.
    • FeCl2 is less covalent than FeCl3 because polarising power of Fe2+ is less than that of Fe3+ ion having smaller size and higher oxidation state.
  • Polar character of covalent bond
    • Polar Covalent Bond: A covalent bond in which electrons are shared unequally and the bonded atoms acquire a partial positive and negative charge is called a polar covalent bond.
    • H—Cl or Hδ+ ⇄ Clδ-
    • A molecule having partial positive and partial negative charge separated by a distance is referred to as a dipole.
    • Calculation of percentage ionic character in a polar covalent bond (A - B) in a molecule (AB):
      • Hannay and Smith equation
        % Ionic character = 16(χA - χB) + 3.5(χA - χB)2
      • Pauling equation
        % Ionic character = [1 - e-1/4(χA - χB)2] × 100
        where, χA and χB are electronegativities of A and B atoms.

  • Bond parameters
    • Bond length is the equilibrium distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms in a molecule.
    • Bond angle is the angle between the orbitals containing bonding electron pairs around the central atom in a molecule/complex ion.
    • Bond order is given as the number of bonds between the two atoms in a molecule.
      H : H    Bond order = 1
      O :: O    Bond order = 2
      N ::: N    Bond order = 3

    Molecular structure
  • Lewis structure
    • Lewis symbol of an atom is represented by the chemical symbol for the atom surrounded by a number of dots corresponding to the number of valence electrons.
    • Lewis symbols:
      Li·   ·Be·   ·B·   ·C·   ·N:   :O:   :F:   :Ne:
    • Lewis structure for molecules
      • Write a symmetrical 'skeleton' for the molecules and polyatomic ions.
      • Calculate the number of electrons available in the valence shell of all the atoms (A).
      • Calculate the total number of electrons needed by all the atoms to achieve noble gas configuration (N).
      • Calculate the total number of electrons shared, S = N - A.
      • Place the shared pair of electrons into the skeleton, using double and triple bonds only when necessary.
  • Formal charge
    • The formal charge on any atom in a Lewis structure is a number assigned to it according to the number of valence electrons of the atom and the number of electrons around it.
    • The formal charge of an atom is equal to the number of valence electrons, (Nv.e.) minus the number of unshared electrons, (Nu.s.e.) minus half of the bonding electrons, (Nb.e.).
    • Formal charge = Nv.e. - Nu.s.e. - 1/2 Nb.e.
  • Resonance structures
    • There are certain molecules (benzene, ozone, nitric acid and many more), all the properties of which cannot be explained by a single structure.
    • Two or more structures, called resonating structures are required to explain all the properties of these molecules and the actual structure is an intermediate of these structures.
    • The intermediate structure is called resonance hybrid and this phenomenon is called resonance, e.g., CO32-, O3, etc.
    • Effects of resonance: It imparts stability to the molecule and hence, decreases its reactivity, which is due to delocalisation of electrons over the entire surface of the molecule.
    • Since the electrons are not localised between any particular atoms and are uniformly distributed in the resonance hybrid, all the bonds are similar and are of equal bond lengths.
  • Shapes of molecules (VSEPR theory)
    • According to this theory, all valence shell electron pairs surrounding the chemical atom arrange themselves in such a manner so that they are as far away from each other as possible.
    • There are two types of electron pairs around the central atom, bonding electron pairs (bp) and non-bonding electron pairs (lp). The strength of repulsion between the electron pairs varies as:
      lp - lp > lp - bp > bp - bp.

    Covalent bond
  • Covalent bonds
    • These chemical bonds are formed by sharing of electrons, e.g., formation of O2 molecule.
    • BF3, BeCl2, PCl5, SF6, etc., also form covalent bonds, but the octet in these compounds is not complete.
  • Valence bond theory (VBT)
    • This theory was developed by Heitler and London and later developed by Pauling. Its main postulates are:
      • Bond is formed due to interaction of valence electrons. The core electrons do not participate in bond formation.
      • During bond formation, only valence electrons lose their identity.
      • Bond formation is accompanied by release of energy, and this accounts for the stability of the bond.
      • The molecule has minimum energy at the equilibrium distance known as the internuclear distance. Larger the decrease in energy, stronger will be the bond formed.
  • Types of overlapping
    • When two atoms approach each other, overlapping of atomic orbitals takes place to give molecular orbitals.
    • Sigma (σ) bond is formed by the head on overlap of the atomic orbitals along the internuclear axis. This type of combination is shown by s-s, s-px and px-px overlapping (assuming x-axis to be internuclear axis).
    • Pi (π) bond is formed by lateral overlap of half-filled atomic orbitals perpendicular to the internuclear axis.
  • Hybridisation
    • The process of mixing of atomic orbitals of nearly same energy to produce a set of entirely new orbitals of equivalent energy is known as hybridisation.
    • The features of hybridisation
      • Number of hybrid orbitals formed = Number of orbitals undergoing hybridisation.
      • Hybrid orbitals are always equivalent in energy and shape.
      • Hybrid orbitals are more effective in forming stable bonds.
      • Hybrid orbitals are directed in space in preferred direction to have minimum repulsion between electron pairs and thus, a stable arrangement. Therefore, the type of hybridisation indicates the geometry of the molecules.
    • The important conditions for hybridisation
      • Only those orbitals present in the valence shell of the atom, are hybridised.
      • Orbitals undergoing hybridisation should have nearly same energies.
      • Promotion of electrons is not essential before hybridisation occurs.
    • Number of orbitals involved in hybridisation
      H = 1/2 [V + M - C + A]
      where, H = no. of orbitals involved in hybridisation
      V = valence electrons of central atom
      M = no. of monovalent atoms linked with central atom
      C = charge on cation
      A = charge on anion

  • Molecular orbital theory
    • Formation of molecular orbitals involves linear combination of atomic orbitals.
    • Molecular orbitals formed are of two types:
      • Bonding MO which are of lower energy are represented as σ or π. These orbitals form stronger bonds.
      • Antibonding MO which are of higher energy, are represented as σ* or π*. These orbitals reduce the stability of molecules.
    • The electronic configuration of molecular orbitals (MO) are written in the following manner:
      • For diatomic lighter molecules such as B2, C2 and N2,
        σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < π2px = π2py < σ2pz < π*2px = π*2py < σ*2pz
      • For homonuclear diatomic molecules such as O2, F2 and Ne2,
        σ1s < σ*1s < σ2s < σ*2s < σ2pz < π2px = π2py < π*2px = π*2py < σ*2pz
    • Bond order for diatomic molecules or ions,
      B.O. = (Nb - Na) / 2
      Nb = number of bonding electrons
      Na = number of antibonding electrons
    • Bond order of H2+ and H2- is same but H2- is slightly less stable than H2+ because H2- has one electron in the antibonding orbital resulting in repulsion.

    Dipole moments and hydrogen bond
  • Dipole moment
    • The dipole moment of a polar molecule is given by the product of the charge at one end and the distance between the opposite charges (i.e., bond length).
    • Dipole moment = Electric charge × Bond length
      or μ = q × d
    • Dipole moment is measured in 'Debye' unit (D).
      1D = 10-18 esu. cm = 3.33 × 10-30 Coulomb meter
    • Resultant dipole moment of a given molecule can be calculated by vectorial method:
      μR = √(μ12 + μ22 + 2μ1μ2 cos α)
      where α = angle between two bonds.
    • % ionic character = Actual dipole moment of the bond / Dipole moment of pure ionic bond × 100
    • cis-isomers have higher dipole moments and are more polar than trans isomers.
    • Dipole moment of ortho > meta > para isomer.

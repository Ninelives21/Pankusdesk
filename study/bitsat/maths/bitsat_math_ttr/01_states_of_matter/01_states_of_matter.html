    Measurement
  • Units and Measurements
    • The units of mass, length and time are called fundamental units since they are independent units and cannot be derived from any other units. They are also called basic units.
    • The units for other quantities which can be derived from fundamental units are called derived units.
    • The units adopted by the General Conference of Weights and Measures is known as SI System or International System.

  • Significant Figures
    • The meaningful digits which are known with certainty are known as significant figures.
  • The following rules should be followed in counting significant figures in a given measured quantity
    • All digits are significant except zero at the beginning of the number. Zero between the non-zero digits are significant.
    • Zero at the end or right of a number are significant, provided they are on the right side of the decimal point.
    • The numbers can be expressed in scientific notation also. In this term, every number is written as N x 10n, where N is a number with a single non-zero digit to the left of the decimal point and n is an integer.
    • Zero at the end of a number and before the decimal point may or may not be significant.
  • Significant Figures in Numerical Calculations
    1. Rule I: For multiplication/division The result of multiplication and/or division may carry no more significant figures than the least precisely known quantity in the calculation.
      14.79 x 12.11 x 5.05 = 904.48985 = 904 = 9.04 x 102
      The result should be in three significant figures.
    2. Rule II: For addition/subtraction The result of addition and/or subtraction must be expressed with the same number of decimal places as the term carrying the smallest number of decimal places.
      22.2 + 2.22 + 0.222 = 24.642 = 24.6
    3. Rule III: For each exact number Exact numbers can be considered to have an unlimited number of significant figures.
  • Rounding off numerical results
    • If the right most digit to be removed is less than 5, the preceding digit is not changed.
    • If the right most digit to be removed is greater than 5, the preceding digit is increased by one.
    • If the right most digit to be removed is 5, the preceding digit is not changed if it is even.
    • If the right most digit to be removed is 5, then add one to the preceding digit if it is odd.
  • Dimensional analysis
    • It is frequently necessary to convert one set of units to another in calculations.
    • This can be done by the unit factor label method or unit factor method.
    • A conversion factor is a factor equal to one that converts a quantity in one unit to the same quantity in another unit.

    Chemical reactions
  • Laws of Chemical Combination
    • Law of conservation of mass: During any physical or chemical change, total mass of products is equal to the total mass of reactants.
    • Law of definite proportion: A compound always contains the same elements combined in the same definite proportion by weight.
    • Law of multiple proportion: When two or more elements combine to form two or more compounds, the different weights of one element which combine with a fixed weight of the other bear a simple whole number ratio to one another.
    • Law of reciprocal proportions: When two elements combine separately with a fixed mass of a third element, the ratio between their masses in which they combine will be either the same or a simple multiple of the ratio in which they combine with each other.
    • Gay Lussac's law of combining volumes: Under similar conditions of temperature and pressure, whenever gases react together, the volumes of the reacting gases as well as products bear a simple whole number ratio.
    • Avogadro's law: Equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal number of molecules.
  • Dalton's Atomic Theory
    • Matter is made up of small indivisible particles called atoms.
    • Atoms can neither be created nor destroyed.
    • Atom is the smallest unit that takes part in chemical combination.
    • Atoms of two or more elements combine in whole number ratio to form compound atoms or molecules.
    • Atoms of the same element have identical shape, size, mass and chemical properties.
    • Atoms of different elements differ in their mass and chemical properties.
  • Atomic and Molecular Masses
    • Atomic mass of an element is defined as the average relative mass of an atom of an element as compared to the mass of an atom of carbon C12 taken as 12.
    • Calculation of atomic weight: Atomic weight x specific heat = 6.4 approximately.
    • Atomic weight = Equivalent weight x valency.
    • Molecular mass of a substance is the average relative mass of its molecule as compared to the mass of atom of carbon C12 taken as 12.
    • Molecular mass = 2 x vapour density.
    • Molecular mass = Mass of 22.4 L of vapour at STP.
    • Rates of diffusion: r1/r2 = sqrt(M2/M1).
  • Mole Concept and Stoichiometry
    • 1 mole of atoms = gram atomic mass or 1 gram atom = 6.022 x 1023 atoms.
    • 1 mole of molecules = gram molecular mass = 6.022 x 1023 molecules = 22.4 L at STP.
  • Calculation of equivalent weight
    • Equivalent weight of metal = weight of metal / volume of H2 in mL displaced at STP x 11200.
    • Equivalent weight of metal = weight of metal / weight of oxygen combined x 8.
    • Equivalent weight of metal = weight of metal / weight of chlorine combined x 35.5.
    • Equivalent weight of metal = weight of metal / volume of chlorine in mL at STP x 11200.
  • By metal displacement method
    • Equivalent weight of metal added to a salt solution = weight of metal added / weight of metal displaced.
    • By double decomposition method: equivalent weight of ppt. of AD formed = equivalent weight of radical A + equivalent weight of radical D.
    • Equivalent weight of a base = molecular weight of the base / acidity.
    • Equivalent weight of an acid = molecular weight of the acid / basicity.
    • Equivalent weight of a salt = molecular weight of the salt / total positive valency of the metal atoms.
    • Equivalent weight of a substance = molecular weight of the substance / number of electrons gained or lost by one molecule.
  • Determination of chemical formula
    • Empirical formula gives the simple whole number ratio of atoms of various elements present in one molecule of the compound.
    • Molecular formula gives the actual number of atoms of various elements present in one molecule of the compound.
    • Molecular formula = n x empirical formula, where n = 1, 2, 3, etc.
  • Concentration of a solution
    • Mass percentage of A = mass of A / mass of solution x 100.
    • Volume percentage of A = volume of A / volume of solution x 100.
    • Parts per million of A = mass of component A / total mass of solution x 106.
    • Molarity = moles of solute / volume of solution in litres.
    • Molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg.
    • Normality = gram equivalents of solute / volume of solution in litres.
    • Normality x equivalent mass = molarity x molecular mass.
  • Stoichiometry of chemical reactions
    1. Write balanced equations for the reaction involved.
    2. Write the relative number of moles or relative masses of the reactants and products involved in the problem below their formulae.
    3. In case of a gaseous substance, write down 22.4 L at STP below the formula in place of 1 mole if volume is involved.
    4. In case of solutions, apply the fact that 1 M solution contains 1 mole of solute in 1000 mL of solution.
    5. Apply unitary method to make the required calculation.
    6. Limiting reactant is the reactant which is present in smaller amount and reacts completely and hence decides the amount of products formed.

    Three states of matter and intermolecular interactions
  • Intermolecular Forces
    • Cause of difference in the three states of matter is the difference in the intermolecular forces of attraction.
    • Intermolecular forces are forces of attraction existing among the molecules of a substance.
    • Greater the intermolecular forces, higher is the melting point or boiling point, or vice-versa.
  • Types of intermolecular forces
    • Dipole-dipole interaction: These exist among polar molecules like HCl, NH3, SO2, etc., i.e., molecules having permanent dipole moment.
    • Ion-dipole interactions: These exist between Na+ and Cl- ions with H2O molecules when NaCl is dissolved in water.
    • Ion-induced dipole interactions: These arise when ions induce dipoles in non-polar gas molecules.
    • Dipole-induced dipole interactions: These arise when a polar molecule approaches a non-polar molecule.
    • London force or dispersion forces: These forces exist in non-polar molecules like H2, O2, etc., or monoatomic gases like He, Ne, Ar, etc.
  • The measurable properties of gases are
    • Mass
    • Volume
    • Pressure
    • Temperature
  • Standard temperature and pressure or STP is 0 degree C or 273.15 K and 1 atm or 760 mm Hg or 101.325 kPa.
    • Gaseous state
    • Gas Laws and Ideal Gas Equation
      • Boyle's law: At constant temperature, pressure of a fixed amount of gas varies inversely with the volume of the gas.
      • P is proportional to 1/V, at constant T and n.
      • P1V1 = P2V2
    • Charles' law
      • At constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of gas increases or decreases by 1/273 of its volume at 0 degree C for each one degree rise or fall in temperature.
      • V is proportional to T, at constant P and n.
      • V1/T1 = V2/T2
      • The theoretically possible temperature at which the volume of gas becomes zero is called absolute zero temperature and corresponds to -273 degree C.
      • When temperature of a substance is lowered, thermal energy reduces.
      • At absolute zero temperature, the total kinetic energy of the molecules becomes zero and molecular motion ceases.
    • Gay-Lussac's law
      • At constant volume, pressure of fixed amount of a gas varies directly with temperature.
      • P is proportional to T, at constant V and n.
      • P/T = constant
    • Avogadro's law
      • At given temperature and pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the amount of gas.
      • V is proportional to n, at constant P and T.
    • Combined gas law
      • Boyle's law: V is proportional to 1/P.
      • Charles' law: V is proportional to T.
      • Avogadro's law: V is proportional to n.
      • V is proportional to nT/P
      • PV = nRT
    • Values of R
      • 0.0821 litre atm K-1 mol-1
      • 8.314 x 107 erg K-1 mol-1 in C.G.S. unit
      • 8.314 joule K-1 mol-1 in M.K.S. unit
      • 1.987 or 2 calorie K-1 mol-1
    • Graham's law of diffusion
      • Under similar conditions of temperature and pressure, rates of diffusion of different gases are inversely proportional to the square root of their densities.
      • r1/r2 = sqrt(d2/d1) = sqrt(M2/M1)
      • Molecular weight = 2 x vapour density.
      • Diffusion is escape of a gas through a tiny hole.
    • Dalton's law of partial pressure
      • The total pressure exerted by a mixture of two or more non-reacting gases is equal to the sum of partial pressure of each component above the mixture.
      • Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...
    • Real gases and deviation from ideal behaviour
      • Gases which do not follow the ideal gas equation, i.e., PV = nRT and other gas laws at all temperatures and pressures are called real gases.
      • Gases show deviation from ideal behaviour because the volume of gas molecules cannot be neglected in comparison to the volume of the gas.
      • There is considerable force of attraction between gas molecules. At high pressure and low temperature, molecules get closer.
      • By applying pressure and volume correction to the ideal gas equation, van der Waals' equation for real gas is obtained.
      • [P + a n2/V2](V - nb) = nRT
      • Pressure correction = P + a n2/V2.
      • Volume correction = V - nb.
      • More the intermolecular forces of attraction, more will be the value of a; more easily the gas will be liquefied.
      • A gas can be liquefied by increasing the pressure and decreasing the temperature.
    • Compressibility factor
      • The extent to which a real gas deviates from ideal behaviour is known as the compressibility factor, Z.
      • Z = PV/RT
      • For ideal gases, Z = 1 and for real gases, Z is not equal to 1.
      • When Z < 1, it is negative deviation and the gas is more compressible than expected from ideal behaviour.
      • When Z > 1, it is positive deviation and the gas is less compressible than expected.
    • Critical constants
      • The maximum temperature at which a gas can be liquefied by the application of pressure alone is called the critical temperature, Tc.
      • The minimum pressure required to liquefy the gas at temperature Tc is called the critical pressure, Pc.
      • The volume occupied by one mole of gas at critical temperature, Tc, and critical pressure, Pc, is called the critical volume, Vc.
      • Tc = 27Rb/8a
      • Pc = a/27b2
      • Vc = 3b


    Liquid state
  • Liquid state is intermediate between gaseous and solid states. It has volume like solid and has incompressibility like solids.
  • Properties of liquids
    • Evaporation: It is the process of conversion of a liquid into its vapours at room temperature.
    • Rate of evaporation depends upon temperature and surface area.
    • Vapour pressure: It is defined as the pressure exerted by the vapours in equilibrium with the liquid at a given temperature.
    • At any given temperature, vapour pressure of different liquids is different because their cohesive forces differ.
    • Vapour pressure depends upon the nature of liquid and temperature of liquid.
  • Clausius-Clapeyron equation
    • Vapour pressure of a liquid at two different temperatures is expressed by the Clausius-Clapeyron equation.
    • log(P2/P1) = ΔHvap / 2.303R (1/T1 - 1/T2)
  • Surface tension
    • Surface tension is the force per unit length acting perpendicular to the tangential line drawn on the surface of the liquid.
    • Unit of surface tension is dynes cm-1 or J m-2.
    • It is denoted by γ.
    • The intermolecular attractive forces which are responsible for surface tension are comparatively smaller at higher temperature; hence surface tension decreases with rise in temperature.
    • Variation of γ with temperature is given by Eotvos equation.
    • γ(M/d)2/3 = k(tc - t - 6)
  • Viscosity
    • Viscosity is the internal resistance to flow in liquids which one layer offers to another layer lying above or below it.
    • Force of friction between two cylindrical layers each having area A, separated by a distance x cm and having a velocity difference v cm/sec is given by:
    • f = ηA dv/dx
    • Viscosity is generally determined by Ostwald's method.
    • The coefficient of viscosity is η.
    • η1/η2 = d1t1/d2t2, where η is viscosity, d is density and t is time of flow of liquid.
  • Effect of temperature on viscosity of liquids
    • In general, viscosity decreases with increase in temperature because at high temperature, molecules have high kinetic energy and can overcome the intermolecular forces or slip past one another between the layers.
    • The variation of viscosity with temperature can be expressed by:
    • η = AeE/RT
    • where A and E are constants. E is activation energy and A is called pre-exponential factor.

    Solid state
  • Solid state has high density and low compressibility as compared to gas as well as liquid states.
  • Classification of crystalline solids

    Molecular Molecules van der Waals forces H2, Ar, CCl4, Br2, CO2, naphthalene, etc. Soft, low melting point, low density, volatile, insulator.
    Covalent or network Atoms Covalent bonds Si, SiO2, SiC, diamond, graphite, etc. Very hard, very high melting point, insulator except graphite.
    Metallic Atoms Metallic bonds All metals and some alloys Range from soft to very hard, high melting points, malleable and ductile, good conductor of electricity and heat, lustrous.
  • Amorphous solids
    • When constituent particles of a substance are not arranged in any regular arrangement, then the substance is said to be amorphous.
    • Some properties of amorphous solids are: incompressible and rigid, short range order, supercooled liquid and no sharp melting point.
  • Crystalline solids
    • When constituent particles are arranged in a definite geometric pattern in three dimensional space.
    • They have long range order.
    • They possess sharp melting point.
    • They exhibit directional differences in properties, i.e., anisotropy like refractive index, conductivity, etc.
  • Crystal Lattices and Unit Cells
    • Space lattice is the 3-D arrangement of an infinite set of points of constituent particles in space and is called space lattice.
    • Unit cell is obtained by connecting the lattice points such that each parallelepiped contains a complete unit of pattern of the crystal.
    • It is the smallest structure of which the crystalline solid is made.
    • The parameters of unit cells are relative lengths of the edges along the three axes a, b, c and relative angles between the edges α, β, γ.

  • Close Packing in Crystals
    • HCP: Hexagonal close packing has ABABAB arrangement.
    • CCP: Cubic close packing has ABCABC arrangement.
    • For example: silver, gold, copper, platinum show cubic close packing.
  • Packing in solids
    • Packing fraction or density of packing = volume occupied by spherical atom / volume of the cube.
    • For simple cubic, packing fraction = π/6 = 0.52.
    • For BCC, packing fraction = √3π/8 = 0.68.
    • For FCC, packing fraction = π/(3√2) = 0.74.
  • Density of the crystal
    • d = ZM / a3NA
    • Z = number of atoms or formula units per unit cell.
    • M = atomic weight of element or formula weight of ionic compound.
  • Voids in solids
    • Atoms in close-packing are packed in such a manner that they tend to occupy the maximum space available, but still some space remains unoccupied.
    • This unoccupied or empty space in the crystal lattice is called interstitial site or void.
    • For a tetrahedral void, r = 0.225R.
    • For an octahedral void, r = 0.414R.

  • Metallic solids
    • Metallic solids are crystalline solids formed by atoms of the same metallic element.
    • The bonding electrons are delocalised over the space of the crystal and have freedom to move from one end to the other.
    • The force of attraction between positive metallic ions and negatively charged sea of delocalised electrons is called metallic bond.
    • Presence of mobile electrons makes metals good conductors of heat and electricity.
    • On applying shearing force, layers slide on one another and hence the structure is not fractured.
  • Covalent crystals
    • Diamond: Diamond is an allotropic form of carbon. All carbon atoms in diamond are sp3 hybridised and form a big giant network of covalent solid.
    • Graphite: Graphite is another allotropic modification of carbon. All carbon atoms in graphite are sp2 hybridised and form interlinked six-membered rings of carbon atoms.
  • Crystal Defects
    • In a crystalline solid, atoms, ions or molecules are arranged in a definite repeating pattern, but some defects may occur in the pattern.
    • Deviations from perfect arrangement may occur due to temperature changes or presence of additional particles.
    • Point defects are irregularities or deviations from ideal arrangement around a point or an atom in a crystalline substance.
    • Line defects are irregularities or deviations from ideal arrangement in entire rows of lattice points.
  • Nature of defects in crystals
    • Schottky defect: This defect is caused when some of the lattice points are unoccupied, and these points are called vacancies or holes.
    • Frenkel defect: This defect is caused when some of the ions leave their lattice sites to occupy an interstitial site.
  • Metal excess defect
    • A negative ion may be absent from its lattice site leaving a hole which is occupied by an electron, thereby maintaining electrical balance.
    • This type of defect exists in crystals which may develop colour when heated with excess of their constituent metal vapours or treated with high energy radiation.
    • Anion sites occupied by electrons in this way are called F-centres.
    • In NaCl, yellow colour is manifested due to electrons trapped in anion vacancies.
  • Interstitial ions and electrons
    • Metal excess defect can also occur when an extra positive ion is present in an interstitial position in the lattice and electrical neutrality is maintained by the inclusion of an interstitial electron.
    • This kind of metal excess defect is much more common than the first kind and is formed in crystals such as ZnO and CdO.
  • Metal deficiency defect
    • This defect is caused by the missing cation from its lattice site.
    • The extra negative charge may be balanced if the adjacent metal ion corresponding position carries more positive charge.
    • Examples: crystals of FeO, FeS and NiO.
  • Electrical and Magnetic Properties of Solids
    • On the basis of electrical conductivity, solids are divided into three categories: conductors, insulators and semiconductors.
    • Metals have conductivity about 104 ohm-1 cm-1.
    • Insulators have conductivity about 10-20 ohm-1 cm-1.
    • A conductor may conduct electricity through movement of electrons or ions.
    • Metals conduct electricity in solid as well as liquid state.
    • The conductivity of metals depends upon the number of valence electrons available per atom.
    • In insulators, the energy gap between filled valence band and unoccupied conduction band is large.
    • In semiconductors, the gap between valence band and conduction band is small.
    • Silicon and germanium show semiconductor behaviour and are called intrinsic semiconductors.
  • Dielectric properties of solids
    • Insulators do not conduct electricity because electrons present in them are held tightly by the nuclei.
    • When electric field is applied, polarisation takes place because nuclei are attracted to one side and electron cloud to the other side.
    • Dipoles are formed and such polar crystals show properties like piezoelectricity.
    • In some crystals, dipoles align themselves in a permanent way even after electric field is removed. Such crystals are called ferroelectric crystals.
    • Crystals having zero dipole moment in absence of electric field are called anti-ferroelectric crystals.
  • 12-16 and 13-15 compounds
    • The atom belonging to the higher group is the donor and the atom belonging to the lower group is the acceptor atom.
    • Perfectly covalent bonding is not there.
    • They possess some important optical and electrical properties.
    • Examples: AlP, GaAs, InSb, ZnS.
  • Magnetic properties of solids
    • Diamagnetic substances: Weakly repelled by magnetic field. They have all electrons paired up. Examples: H2O, benzene.
    • Paramagnetic substances: Weakly attracted by magnetic field due to presence of unpaired electrons. Examples: O2, Cu2+, Fe3+.
    • Ferromagnetic substances: Strongly attracted by magnetic field and do not lose magnetism when external field is removed. Example: iron, cobalt, nickel.
    • Ferrimagnetic substances: Magnetic dipoles align in opposite directions unequally so that there is a small net magnetic moment. Example: Fe3O4.
    • Anti-ferromagnetic substances: Magnetic dipoles align in opposite directions equally so that there is no net magnetic moment. Example: MnO2.
  • n-type and p-type semiconductors
    • The conductivity of intrinsic semiconductors is increased by adding an appropriate amount of suitable impurity.
    • This process is called doping.
    • Doping can be done with an impurity which is electron-rich or electron-deficient as compared to the intrinsic semiconductor silicon or germanium.
    • Silicon doped with electron-rich impurity is called n-type semiconductor.
    • Silicon doped with electron-deficient impurity is called p-type semiconductor.

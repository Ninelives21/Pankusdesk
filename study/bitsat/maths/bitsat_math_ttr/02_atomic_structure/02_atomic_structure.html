    Introduction
  • Subatomic Particles
    • Dalton in 1808 postulated that atom is the smallest indivisible particle of matter. Later researches have shown that atom is not the smallest particle but is made up of still smaller particles.
    • At present about 35 sub-atomic particles are known but electrons, protons and neutrons are called fundamental particles.

    • Number of protons in the nucleus of an atom is called atomic number. Atomic number (Z) was discovered by Moseley.
    • Atomic number = mass number - number of neutrons.
    • The sum of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom is known as mass number of that atom.
    • Atoms of different elements whose atomic numbers are different, are called isobars.
      4018Ar, 4019K, 4020Ca
    • Atoms which have same atomic number but different atomic masses are called isotopes.
      3517Cl, 3717Cl
    • Atoms of different elements whose nuclei contain same number of neutrons are called isotones.
      3014Si, 3115P, 3216S
  • Atomic Models
    • Thomson's model of atom: J.J. Thomson, in 1898, proposed that an atom possesses a spherical shape in which the positive charge is uniformly distributed. The electrons are embedded into it in such a manner so as to give the most stable electrostatic arrangement.
    • An important feature of this model is that the mass of an atom is assumed to be uniformly distributed over the atom.
    • Rutherford's model of atom: According to Rutherford's model of atom, an atom consists of (i) nucleus which is small in size but carries the entire mass, i.e., contains all the neutrons and protons (ii) extra nuclear part, i.e., space around the nucleus where the electrons are revolving.
    • Discovery of nucleus: Nucleus was discovered as a result of Rutherford's scattering experiments in 1911. α-rays were allowed to hit a thin foil of gold. Most of them passed through without undergoing any deflection (showing presence of large empty space in the atom), some were deflected through a small angle (showing presence of a small positively charged body in the atom), a few were deflected back (showing that the body was small but heavy).
  • Electromagnetic Wave Theory
    • Postulates of electromagnetic wave theory
      • According to electromagnetic wave theory, the energy is emitted from any source continuously and consists of electric and magnetic fields oscillating perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.
      • All electromagnetic waves travel with the velocity of light (3 x 108 m s-1) and do not need any medium for propagation.
    • Relation between wavelength (λ), frequency (ν) and velocity (c): c = ν x λ
  • Planck's Quantum Theory
    • Postulates of Planck's quantum theory
      • The energy is emitted or absorbed discontinuously in the form of packets of energy called quanta. A quantum of light is called a photon.
      • The energy of a photon is E = hν where h is Planck's constant. Its value is 6.62 x 10-27 erg sec or 6.62 x 10-34 J s.
    • Splitting of light into a series of coloured bands is known as dispersion and the series of coloured bands is known as spectrum.
    • Spectrum can be continuous i.e., different colours merge with one another or discontinuous i.e., having discrete lines.
    • The hydrogen atom spectrum is an emission spectrum consisting of well defined lines and hence, is also referred to as line spectra.
  • Hydrogen atom spectrum
    • Rydberg's formula for calculation of wave number of lines in the hydrogen spectrum is
      ν̄ = 1/λ = R(1/n12 - 1/n22)
    • where R is called Rydberg constant. Its value is 109678 cm-1 or 1.097 x 107 m-1.
    • For Lyman series, n1 = 1, n2 = 2, 3, 4, .......
    • For Balmer series, n1 = 2, n2 = 3, 4, 5, .......
    • For Paschen series, n1 = 3, n2 = 4, 5, 6, .......
    • For Brackett series, n1 = 4, n2 = 5, 6, 7, .......
    • For Pfund series, n1 = 5, n2 = 6, 7, 8, .......
    • For Humphrey series, n1 = 6, n2 = 7, 8, 9, .......
    • For H-like particles (He+, Li2+, etc.),
      ν̄ = 1/λ = RZ2(1/n12 - 1/n22)
      where Z = atomic number of H-like particle.
  • Bohr's Model of an Atom
    • Postulates of Bohr's model of an atom: Bohr's model of atom is based upon Planck's quantum theory. The main postulates of this model are as follows:
      • Electrons revolve only in certain definite orbits around the nucleus. These are called stationary states because they have fixed energies. These are called 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th or K, L, M, N, etc.
      • Electrons revolve only in those orbits for which angular momentum is an integral multiple of h/2π,
        i.e., mvr = nh/2π; n = 1, 2, 3, ....
      • When electron jumps from one level to another, energy is either emitted or absorbed.
        The energy difference between two states is given by,
        ΔE = E2 - E1
      • As the distance of the orbits increases from the nucleus, the energy gap goes on decreasing, i.e.,
        E2 - E1 > E3 - E2 > E4 - E3 > ........
    • Quantization of electronic energy and momenta: The energy as well as angular momenta of the electrons in an atom are quantized.
    • Energy of the electron in the nth orbit:
      • For hydrogen-like particles containing one electron only like He+, Li2+, etc., it is given by
        En = -2π2mZ2e4 / n2h2
      • where m = mass of electron, e = charge on the electron, Z = atomic number of the element, h = Planck's constant.
      • For H-atom, Z = 1, the energy of electron in the nth orbit is:
        En = -21.78 x 10-19 / n2 J atom-1
        = -13.6 / n2 eV atom-1 (1 eV = 1.602 x 10-19 J)
        = -1312 / n2 kJ mol-1 = -313.6 / n2 kcal mol-1
    • Radii of the orbits: For H-like particles, the radius of the nth orbit is given by
      rn = n2r0 / Z
      where r0 = 0.529 Å = 52.9 pm is the radius of the first orbit of hydrogen atom (called Bohr radius).
    • For H-atom, the radius of the nth orbit will be given by
      rn = r0n2
    • Velocity of electron in nth orbit: For H-like particles, it is given by
      vn = v0 x Z/n
      where v0 = 2.188 x 108 cm s-1 is the velocity of the electron in the first orbit of hydrogen atom. It is nearly 1/137th of the velocity of light.
    • Limitations of Bohr's model:
      • Bohr's model of an atom could explain the line spectra of H-atom and H-like particles but failed to explain the spectra of multi-electron atoms.
      • It also could not explain the splitting of lines when the source is placed in a magnetic field (called Zeeman effect) and when placed in an electric field (called Stark effect).
      • This model is also not concordant with de-Broglie concept of dual nature of matter and Heisenberg uncertainty principle.


    Quantum mechanics
  • Dual Nature of Radiation and Matter (de Broglie Concept)
    • Dual nature of radiation: The phenomena of photoelectric effect and black body radiation can be explained if light is considered to be made up of particles, called photons (i.e., it is corpuscular in nature, as suggested by Newton). On the other hand, phenomena of interference and diffraction can be explained only if light is supposed to have wave nature.
    • Hence, light has dual nature and this idea was put forward by Einstein. The idea of dual nature of matter was put forward by de Broglie in 1924.
    • de-Broglie relationship: The relationship between the wavelength (λ) of the wave and the mass (m) of the material particle moving with a velocity v is called de-Broglie equation. It is given by
      λ = h/mv = h/p
      where h is Planck's constant and p is momentum of the particle.
  • Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle
    • Definition and mathematical expression: It states that "It is impossible to measure simultaneously the position and momentum of a microscopic particle with absolute accuracy. If one of them is measured with greater accuracy, the other becomes less accurate. The product of their uncertainties is always equal to or greater than h/4π."
    • Mathematically,
      Δx × Δp ≥ h/4π
      where Δx = uncertainty in position
      Δp = uncertainty in momentum.
    • Significance of Heisenberg's uncertainty principle:
      • It overrules the existence of definite paths or trajectories of electrons and other similar particles.
      • It holds importance for microscopic objects only and is negligible for macroscopic objects.
  • Schrödinger's Wave Equation
    • In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger derived an equation to explain the dual nature of microscopic particles.
    • For a system, like atoms and molecules whose energy does not change with time, Schrödinger's wave equation can be written as
      ∂2ψ/∂x2 + ∂2ψ/∂y2 + ∂2ψ/∂z2 + 8π2m/h2(E − V)ψ = 0
    • where x, y, z are co-ordinate axes,
      m = mass of particle (electron),
      h = Planck's constant,
      E = total energy of particle,
      V = potential energy of particle,
      ψ = (psi) amplitude of the electron wave known as wave function.
    • This equation may also be written as
      ∇2ψ + 8π2m/h2(E − V)ψ = 0
      [∇2 is called Laplacian operator]
  • Properties of ψ
    • ψ must be single valued, i.e., only one value at any point.
    • finite, i.e., ψ ≠ ∞.
    • continuous (value of ψ should vary regularly).
    • normalised, i.e., value of ψ must obey the boundary condition.
    • For a normalized wave function,
      ∫ ψ2dV = 1
    • The value of ψ which obeys all above conditions is called eigen function. The value of E corresponding to value of ψ is called eigen value.
  • Probability distribution curves and nodes
    • Plots of wave function (ψ) versus distance from the nucleus (r) are called probability distribution curves.
    • Node: The place where probability of finding an electron is zero is called a node.
    • Number of radial nodes = n − l − 1, number of angular nodes = l, total number of nodes = (n − 1).
    • Nodal plane: The plane on which probability of finding an electron is zero is called a nodal plane. Number of nodal planes = l.

    • Principal quantum number (n) refers to the main energy level (or shell or orbit). It gives distance of an electron from the nucleus and energy of the electron in a given orbit.
    • Azimuthal quantum number (l) refers to the shape of orbital and has integral values of 0, 1, 2, 3, ... to n − 1.
    • s-orbitals are spherically symmetrical, p-orbitals are dumb-bell shaped, d- and f-orbitals are much more complicated in shape.
    • Magnetic quantum number (m) refers to the spatial orientation of orbitals and has values −l ... 0 ... +l. For each value of l, there are 2l + 1 values of m.
    • Spin quantum number (s) refers to spin of electron around its own axis. s has values of +1/2 and −1/2 for each value of m.

    Many electron atoms
  • Rules governing the electronic configuration of atoms in the ground state
    • Aufbau principle: Electron enters in orbital of lowest energy. The order of orbital filling is in the following order:
      1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d < 6p < 7s < 5f < 6d < 7p
    • (n + l) rule: An electron enters an orbital with lowest value of (n + l) (n and l are quantum numbers).
    • Pauli's exclusion principle: No two electrons in the same atom may have the same set of four quantum numbers.
    • Hund's rule: Electrons never pair up until no available empty orbitals of same energy level is left.

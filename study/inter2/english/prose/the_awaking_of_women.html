  1. Why were Indian women in the nineteenth century the most backward of all women in the world?

    “The Awakening of Women” is an interesting essay about the position of women in India over a period of time. It is written by K.M. Panikkar. He was educated at the University of Oxford. He was a prolific writer and published fifty books and numerous articles in Malayalam and English. Lucid style and suitable diction make this informative and insightful essay offer a rich reading experience.

    Women in ancient India enjoyed an enviable position. But their status in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries touched its lowest point. K.M. Panikkar enumerates the factors that contributed to this fall. Women in those days were isolated from the mainstream. They were kept behind the ‘purdah’. They were denied education. Child marriages were the order. Once married, women were subjected to many more restrictions. Early maternity, high mortality rates among men, and the resulting widowhood added to women’s woes. All these causes collectively condemned women to a pathetically low condition. The awakening of women from that pitiable position began with the Gandhian movement—and it continues!

    Women in ancient India enjoyed a high status but during the 18th and 19th centuries, their position degraded to a pitiable condition. Women were secluded from the mainstream. They were kept behind the ‘purdah’, denied education, and subjected to many atrocities. However, Gandhiji had immense faith in the untapped power of womanhood in India. Originally, he was uncertain of the response and their role in the national movement. But, when the movement actually started, they were involved in every aspect — picketing liquor shops, enforcing boycotts of foreign clothes, and undertaking civil disobedience. They played a prominent role. Women all over India defied all social taboos, sacrificed physical comforts, and were everywhere at the forefront. Their active participation made a significant impact. Gandhiji hailed them as the main support of this movement.

    The distinction achieved by a few women of genius cannot be an indicator of the changed position of women in India. What is really significant is a revolutionary change in their free and equal participation in spheres of national activity and at every level—from villages to government. The ancient tradition emphasized their equality, but in later years, their freedom was restricted due to social bondages. During the twentieth century, the position of women gradually improved. Gandhiji’s non-cooperation movement witnessed active participation of women. The contribution of women to modern India has forced the creation of a new code of laws establishing equality of women. The appointment of women to the highest posts—as members of the cabinet, as ambassadors, and as leaders of delegations to several high positions—is a testimony to the empowerment of women in India.

    Awakening of women in India from a pitiable position imposed by social bondages and conservatism began with the Gandhian movement. The contribution of women in the national movement led to the realization of their untapped potential. The governing bodies were forced to create a new code of laws and legislative reforms to establish the equality of women. This has been one of the most significant political achievements in the period of partial self-government. Women were granted rights to independent property, freedom of marriage, right to education, and employment. The raising of the age of consent for marriage and prevention of the dedication of women to temple services helped to change their status. These significant reforms were the reflection of the general awakening of women in India.

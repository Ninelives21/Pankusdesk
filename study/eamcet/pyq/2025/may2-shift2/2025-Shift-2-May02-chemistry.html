    1. Core Theory: Bohr Model (NCERT Standard)
      • Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits without radiating energy.
      • Allowed orbits are defined by quantized angular momentum: \[ mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \]
      • Radius of \(n^{th}\) orbit: \[ r_n = a_0 n^2 \quad (\text{for H atom}) \]
      • Energy of orbit: \[ E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2} \text{ eV} \]
      • As \(n\) increases:
        • Radius increases (\(\propto n^2\))
        • Energy increases (less negative)
        • Electron moves farther from nucleus
    2. Electronic Transitions & Spectrum
      • When electron falls from higher orbit \(n_2\) to lower orbit \(n_1\), energy is released: \[ \Delta E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]
      • Rydberg equation: \[ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \]
      • The series depends only on final orbit \(n_1\), not initial orbit.
    3. Hydrogen Spectral Series (Must Memorize)
      • Lyman (\(n=1\)) → Ultraviolet
      • Balmer (\(n=2\)) → Visible
      • Paschen (\(n=3\)) → Infrared
      • Brackett (\(n=4\)) → Infrared
      • Pfund (\(n=5\)) → Infrared
    4. Important Patterns (JEE/EAPCET)
      • Radius ∝ \(n^2\)
      • Energy ∝ \(-1/n^2\)
      • Gap between levels decreases as \(n\) increases
      • Longest wavelength → smallest energy transition in that series

    1. Question transcription
      Identify the incorrect statement from the following:
      • (a) \(m_l\) designates the orientation of the orbital.
      • (b) The probability density of electron is expressed by \(|\psi|^3\).
      • (c) The total information about electron in atom is stored in its \(\psi\).
      • (d) Total number of orbitals in a sub level is equal to \(2l + 1\).
    2. Why option (b) is incorrect
      Option (b) says that the probability density is expressed by \(|\psi|^3\), but this is wrong.
      The correct expression is
      \(|\psi|^2\)
      In quantum mechanics:
      • \(\psi\) is the wave function
      • \(|\psi|^2\) gives the probability density of finding the electron in space
      Therefore, option (b) is the incorrect statement.
    3. Checking option (a)
      \(m_l\) designates the orientation of the orbital. This statement is correct.
      The magnetic quantum number \(m_l\) tells us how an orbital is oriented in space.
      • For a given \(l\), \(m_l\) can have values from \(-l\) to \(+l\)
      • Different values of \(m_l\) correspond to different orbitals of the same subshell
      Example:
      • For \(l=1\) (p-subshell), \(m_l = -1, 0, +1\)
      • So there are three p orbitals with different orientations
    4. Checking option (b)
      The statement says probability density is \(|\psi|^3\). This is incorrect.
      The correct form is
      \(|\psi|^2\)
      This is one of the most important standard facts from the quantum mechanical model of atom.
    5. Checking option (c)
      “The total information about electron in atom is stored in its \(\psi\)” is correct.
      The wave function \(\psi\) is the mathematical description of the electron state. It contains the complete information about the state of the electron.
      However, an important NCERT point is:
      • \(\psi\) itself has no direct physical significance
      • \(|\psi|^2\) has direct physical meaning as probability density
      These two ideas do not contradict each other.
    6. Checking option (d)
      “Total number of orbitals in a sub level is equal to \(2l + 1\)” is correct.
      For a given value of \(l\), the magnetic quantum number \(m_l\) takes values:
      \(-l, -(l-1), \dots, 0, \dots, +(l-1), +l\)
      Hence total number of possible values is
      \(2l + 1\)
      Since each value of \(m_l\) corresponds to one orbital, total number of orbitals in a subshell is also
      \(2l + 1\)
    7. Examples of number of orbitals in subshells
      • s-subshell: \(l=0\), so number of orbitals \(= 2(0)+1 = 1\)
      • p-subshell: \(l=1\), so number of orbitals \(= 2(1)+1 = 3\)
      • d-subshell: \(l=2\), so number of orbitals \(= 2(2)+1 = 5\)
      • f-subshell: \(l=3\), so number of orbitals \(= 2(3)+1 = 7\)
    8. Final answer
      The incorrect statement is
      (b)
      because probability density is expressed by
      \(|\psi|^2\)
      and not by \(|\psi|^3\)
    1. Detailed theory: wave function \(\psi\)
      In the quantum mechanical model, the electron is not treated as moving in a fixed circular path. Instead, its state is described by a mathematical function called the wave function, written as \(\psi\).
      This wave function is obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation.
    2. Physical significance of \(\psi\) and \(|\psi|^2\)
      • \(\psi\) itself has no direct physical significance
      • \(|\psi|^2\) gives the probability density of finding the electron at a point in space
      • If \(|\psi|^2\) is large in a region, the chance of finding the electron there is high
      This is a standard NCERT and JEE point.
    3. Quantum numbers involved in atomic structure
      An electron in an atom is described by four quantum numbers.
      • Principal quantum number \(n\):
        tells the main shell or energy level
      • Azimuthal quantum number \(l\):
        tells the subshell and shape of orbital
        • \(l=0 \rightarrow s\)
        • \(l=1 \rightarrow p\)
        • \(l=2 \rightarrow d\)
        • \(l=3 \rightarrow f\)
      • Magnetic quantum number \(m_l\):
        tells the orientation of orbital in space
      • Spin quantum number \(m_s\):
        tells the spin of the electron, with values \(+\frac{1}{2}\) or \(-\frac{1}{2}\)
    4. Why \(m_l\) is connected with orientation
      Orbitals of the same subshell have the same shape and energy in many cases, but they differ in their orientation in space. This difference is represented by the magnetic quantum number \(m_l\).
      So whenever an option says \(m_l\) gives orientation, it is a standard correct statement.
    5. Why number of orbitals is \(2l + 1\)
      For a given \(l\), \(m_l\) can take all integer values from \(-l\) to \(+l\). Therefore, total number of possible values is
      \(2l + 1\)
      Since each value of \(m_l\) corresponds to one orbital, the number of orbitals in that subshell is also
      \(2l + 1\)
    6. Important NCERT/JEE distinction
      Students often confuse these two statements:
      • \(\psi\) contains the information about the electron state
      • \(\psi\) has no direct physical significance
      Both are true.
      The wave function is the mathematical description of the electron state, but the directly meaningful quantity is \(|\psi|^2\), not \(\psi\) itself.
    7. One-minute exam tip
      This question can be solved very quickly by spotting one standard fact:
      Probability density is always \(|\psi|^2\)
      So if you see \(|\psi|^3\), it is immediately wrong.
    8. Fast memory anchors
      • \(m_l\) → orientation
      • \(|\psi|^2\) → probability density
      • \(2l+1\) → number of orbitals in a subshell
      These three facts alone are enough to solve this question in under a minute.
    9. Common confusion to avoid
      • Do not confuse \(\psi\) with \(|\psi|^2\)
      • Do not think that “no direct physical significance” means \(\psi\) is useless; it still describes the state mathematically
      • Do not forget that \(2l+1\) gives the number of orbitals in a subshell, not the number of electrons

    • Answer: (b) \(\mathrm{Cl,\ S,\ I}\)

    Why?

    Given electron gain enthalpy values:

    • \(X = -349\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\)
    • \(Y = -200\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\)
    • \(Z = -295\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\)

    More negative electron gain enthalpy means greater tendency to accept an electron.

    Known standard values/trends:

    • \(\mathrm{Cl}\) has very high negative electron gain enthalpy, about \(-349\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\)
    • \(\mathrm{I}\) has a less negative value than \(\mathrm{Cl}\), around \(-295\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\)
    • \(\mathrm{S}\) is around \(-200\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\)

    So:

    • \(X = \mathrm{Cl}\)
    • \(Y = \mathrm{S}\)
    • \(Z = \mathrm{I}\)

    Hence the correct option is (b) \(\mathrm{Cl,\ S,\ I}\).

    Core theory

    1. What is electron gain enthalpy?

      Electron gain enthalpy is the enthalpy change when an isolated gaseous atom gains an electron:

      \(\mathrm{X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g)}\)

      • If energy is released, the value is negative.
      • The more negative the value, the easier the atom gains an electron.

      This is exactly what the textbook line means:

      “More the value of electron gain enthalpy, greater is the tendency to gain electron.”

      In proper exam language for such values, this usually means:

      • more negative electron gain enthalpy, or
      • greater magnitude of negative value.

      Because \(-349\) indicates a stronger tendency to gain electron than \(-295\), and both indicate a stronger tendency than \(-200\).

    2. NCERT/JEE trend behind this question

      This question belongs to the periodic trend of electron gain enthalpy.

      Across a period

      In general, electron gain enthalpy becomes more negative from left to right.

      Reason:

      • atomic size decreases
      • effective nuclear charge increases
      • the incoming electron is attracted more strongly

      So non-metals usually have more negative values than metals.

      Down a group

      In general, electron gain enthalpy becomes less negative down the group.

      Reason:

      • atomic size increases
      • the added electron goes farther from the nucleus
      • attraction becomes weaker

      So among halogens:

      \(\mathrm{F,\ Cl,\ Br,\ I}\)

      the general expectation is highly negative values, but the detailed order is:

      \(\mathrm{Cl > F > Br > I}\)

      in terms of magnitude of negativity.

      Equivalent numerical-style statement:

      \(\Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{Cl}) < \Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{F}) < \Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{Br}) < \Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{I})\)

      if you compare algebraic values, because more negative means smaller numerically.

      For exams, the most useful memory point is:

      \(\mathrm{Cl}\) has the most negative electron gain enthalpy among halogens.

    3. Important exception: Why is \(\mathrm{Cl}\) more negative than \(\mathrm{F}\)?

      This is a classic NCERT/JEE exception.

      One may think fluorine should have the most negative value because it is the smallest halogen. But actually \(\mathrm{Cl}\) is more negative than \(\mathrm{F}\).

      Reason:

      Fluorine is very small, so the incoming electron enters a very compact \(2p\) orbital. That small space causes strong electron-electron repulsion.

      In chlorine, the incoming electron enters a larger \(3p\) orbital, where repulsion is lower.

      So although fluorine is smaller, the added electron experiences more crowding. Hence:

      \(\mathrm{Cl}\) has more negative electron gain enthalpy than \(\mathrm{F}\).

      This is one of the most tested exceptions.

    4. Why sulfur fits \(-200\)

      Sulfur is a chalcogen, not a halogen. It has a fairly negative electron gain enthalpy, but not as negative as chlorine or iodine.

      Why?

      • sulfur still tends to gain electron
      • but halogens are only one electron short of noble gas configuration
      • therefore halogens have especially high tendency to accept an electron

      So sulfur’s value is negative, but not as extreme as that of halogens.

      That is why:

      • \(-349\) strongly suggests \(\mathrm{Cl}\)
      • \(-295\) strongly suggests \(\mathrm{I}\)
      • \(-200\) fits \(\mathrm{S}\)

    5. Option elimination logic

      (a) \(\mathrm{Cl,\ I,\ S}\)

      • \(X = \mathrm{Cl}\) is correct
      • \(Y = \mathrm{I}\) would correspond to about \(-295\), not \(-200\)
      • \(Z = \mathrm{S}\) would correspond to about \(-200\), not \(-295\)

      So this is wrong.

      (b) \(\mathrm{Cl,\ S,\ I}\)

      • \(X = -349 = \mathrm{Cl}\)
      • \(Y = -200 = \mathrm{S}\)
      • \(Z = -295 = \mathrm{I}\)

      This matches perfectly. So this is correct.

      (c) \(\mathrm{S,\ Se,\ Te}\)

      These values do not fit the given pattern, especially \(-349\), which is much more characteristic of chlorine.

      So this is wrong.

      (d) \(\mathrm{Na,\ K,\ Rb}\)

      Alkali metals have very low tendency to gain electrons. Their electron gain enthalpy values are not highly negative like these.

      So this is wrong.

    One-minute exam method

    1. Spot the most negative value: \(-349\).

      Ask: which common element has very high negative electron gain enthalpy?

      Answer: \(\mathrm{Cl}\)

      So \(X = \mathrm{Cl}\).

    2. Now only options with \(X = \mathrm{Cl}\) remain:

      • (a)
      • (b)

    3. Compare the other two values:

      • \(-295\) is more negative than \(-200\)

      Among \(\mathrm{I}\) and \(\mathrm{S}\), iodine has more negative electron gain enthalpy than sulfur.

      So:

      • \(Z = \mathrm{I}\)
      • \(Y = \mathrm{S}\)

      Hence the correct answer is (b).

    Intuitive shortcut

    A very useful memory ladder here is:

    \(\mathrm{Cl > I > S}\)

    in tendency to gain electron among the three given here.

    So match:

    • highest negative value \(\rightarrow \mathrm{Cl}\)
    • middle negative value \(\rightarrow \mathrm{I}\)
    • least negative value \(\rightarrow \mathrm{S}\)

    That directly gives:

    \(-349 \rightarrow \mathrm{Cl},\quad -295 \rightarrow \mathrm{I},\quad -200 \rightarrow \mathrm{S}\)

    JEE/NCERT-linked conceptual points to remember

    1. Electron gain enthalpy vs electronegativity

      Do not confuse them.

      • Electron gain enthalpy = enthalpy change when a gaseous atom gains an electron
      • Electronegativity = tendency of an atom in a bond to attract the shared pair of electrons

      They are related, but they are not the same property.

    2. More negative means more favorable

      Students often get confused because the numerical value becomes smaller algebraically.

      \(-349 < -295 < -200\)

      But in tendency to gain electron:

      \(\mathrm{Cl > I > S}\)

      because more negative means more energy released, hence more favorable.

    3. Halogens are especially important

      Halogens generally have highly negative electron gain enthalpy because they need just one electron to complete octet.

      That is why if you see a value around \(-300\) or more negative, think first of a halogen.

    4. Exceptions are crucial

      For JEE/NCERT, these exceptions matter a lot:

      • \(\mathrm{Cl}\) is more negative than \(\mathrm{F}\)
      • \(\mathrm{S}\) is more negative than \(\mathrm{O}\)

      The reason in both cases is similar:

      • the smaller atom has more interelectronic repulsion in the compact orbital

    Answer: \((c)\ 5, 3\)

    Given molecules: \(\mathrm{NH_3,\ BF_3,\ NF_3,\ H_2S,\ CO_2,\ CH_4,\ CHCl_3,\ H_2O}\)

    Core theory:

    • A molecule is polar if its net dipole moment is non-zero, i.e. \(\mu_{\text{net}} \neq 0\).
    • A molecule is non-polar if its net dipole moment is zero, i.e. \(\mu_{\text{net}} = 0\).
    • So in such questions, do not check only whether bonds are polar. You must check shape, symmetry, and lone pairs.

    Stepwise classification:

    1. \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) is polar.
      • Shape: trigonal pyramidal
      • Reason: lone pair is present on \(\mathrm{N}\), so dipoles do not cancel
    2. \(\mathrm{BF_3}\) is non-polar.
      • Shape: trigonal planar
      • Reason: three identical \(\mathrm{B-F}\) bond dipoles are symmetrically arranged and cancel out
    3. \(\mathrm{NF_3}\) is polar.
      • Shape: trigonal pyramidal
      • Reason: lone pair on \(\mathrm{N}\), so net dipole moment is not zero
    4. \(\mathrm{H_2S}\) is polar.
      • Shape: bent
      • Reason: bent geometry prevents cancellation of dipoles
    5. \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) is non-polar.
      • Shape: linear
      • Reason: two \(\mathrm{C=O}\) bond dipoles are equal and opposite, so they cancel
    6. \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) is non-polar.
      • Shape: tetrahedral
      • Reason: all four surrounding atoms are the same, so the arrangement is symmetrical and \(\mu_{\text{net}} = 0\)
    7. \(\mathrm{CHCl_3}\) is polar.
      • Shape: tetrahedral
      • Reason: surrounding atoms are not all the same \((3\ \mathrm{Cl}\text{ and }1\ \mathrm{H})\), so dipoles do not cancel completely
    8. \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is polar.
      • Shape: bent
      • Reason: lone pairs on oxygen and bent shape make \(\mu_{\text{net}} \neq 0\)

    Hence:

    • Polar molecules: \(\mathrm{NH_3,\ NF_3,\ H_2S,\ CHCl_3,\ H_2O}\)
    • Non-polar molecules: \(\mathrm{BF_3,\ CO_2,\ CH_4}\)

    Count:

    • Polar \(= 5\)
    • Non-polar \(= 3\)

    Therefore, the correct answer is \((c)\ 5, 3\).

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Polar bonds do not always mean polar molecule.
    • \(\mathrm{BF_3}\) and \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) have polar bonds but are non-polar because of symmetry.
    • Lone pairs usually make molecules polar, as in \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{NF_3}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2S}\).
    • Tetrahedral molecules are non-polar only when all surrounding atoms are identical, as in \(\mathrm{CH_4}\). If surrounding atoms differ, as in \(\mathrm{CHCl_3}\), the molecule is polar.

    1-minute solving tip:

    • First pick the obvious symmetric non-polar molecules: \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{CH_4}\).
    • The remaining five are polar.
    • So directly get \(\mathrm{polar} = 5\), \(\mathrm{non\text{-}polar} = 3\).

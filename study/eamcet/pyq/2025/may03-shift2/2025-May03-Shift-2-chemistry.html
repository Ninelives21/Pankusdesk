    Answer: \((c)\ \dfrac{1}{R_H}\left(\dfrac{101}{24}\right)\)

    Solution:

    We use the Rydberg relation for hydrogen spectral lines:

    \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\), where \(n_2 > n_1\).

    1. Write the expression for \(\lambda_1\)

      For transition \(n = 4 \rightarrow n = 2\):

      \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda_1} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{4^2}\right)\)

      \(= R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{4} - \dfrac{1}{16}\right)\)

      \(= R_H\left(\dfrac{3}{16}\right)\)

      So, \(\lambda_1 = \dfrac{16}{3R_H}\)

    2. Write the expression for \(\lambda_2\)

      For transition \(n = 3 \rightarrow n = 1\):

      \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda_2} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{1^2} - \dfrac{1}{3^2}\right)\)

      \(= R_H\left(1 - \dfrac{1}{9}\right)\)

      \(= R_H\left(\dfrac{8}{9}\right)\)

      So, \(\lambda_2 = \dfrac{9}{8R_H}\)

    3. Now calculate \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2\)

      \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = \dfrac{16}{3R_H} - \dfrac{9}{8R_H}\)

      Taking LCM \(= 24R_H\):

      \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = \dfrac{128 - 27}{24R_H}\)

      \(= \dfrac{101}{24R_H}\)

      \(= \dfrac{1}{R_H}\left(\dfrac{101}{24}\right)\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ \dfrac{1}{R_H}\left(\dfrac{101}{24}\right)}\)

    Why this method works:

    • In hydrogen atom, each spectral line arises due to electronic transition between two energy levels.
    • The wave number is directly given by:

      \(\bar{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\)

    • Since the formula gives \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda}\), first find \(\lambda\) separately for each transition, then subtract.
    • A common trap is to subtract the reciprocals directly:

      \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda_1} - \dfrac{1}{\lambda_2}\)

      This is not equal to \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2\).

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • For hydrogen-like species:

      \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\)

      For hydrogen, \(Z = 1\).

    • Larger energy gap means larger wave number and smaller wavelength.
    • Transition to \(n=1\) belongs to the Lyman series.
    • Transition to \(n=2\) belongs to the Balmer series.
    • Here:
      • \(4 \rightarrow 2\) is a Balmer line
      • \(3 \rightarrow 1\) is a Lyman line
    • Since \(3 \rightarrow 1\) has a much larger energy drop than \(4 \rightarrow 2\), its wavelength is much smaller.

    Useful physical picture:

    • Energy levels in hydrogen become closer as \(n\) increases.
    • A jump to \(n=1\) is usually much more energetic than a jump to \(n=2\).
    • So intuitively:

      \(\lambda_2 < \lambda_1\)

      Hence \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2\) must be positive, which already rules out any sign-related confusion.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Memorize the quick fractions:

      \(\dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{4^2} = \dfrac{1}{4} - \dfrac{1}{16} = \dfrac{3}{16}\)

      \(\dfrac{1}{1^2} - \dfrac{1}{3^2} = 1 - \dfrac{1}{9} = \dfrac{8}{9}\)

    • So immediately:

      \(\lambda_1 = \dfrac{16}{3R_H},\quad \lambda_2 = \dfrac{9}{8R_H}\)

    • Then:

      \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = \dfrac{16}{3R_H} - \dfrac{9}{8R_H}\)

    • LCM of \(3\) and \(8\) is \(24\), so the subtraction becomes very fast:

      \(\dfrac{128 - 27}{24R_H} = \dfrac{101}{24R_H}\)

    • In MCQs, once you see the factor \(\dfrac{1}{R_H}\), the correct option becomes easy to spot.

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Using \(n_2^2 - n_1^2\) directly instead of the Rydberg form.
    2. Forgetting that lower level comes first in:

      \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\)

    3. Subtracting wave numbers instead of wavelengths.
    4. Missing the inversion step after finding \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda}\).
    5. Confusing Balmer and Lyman series.

    Quick theory sheet:

    • Rydberg equation:

      \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\)

    • Lyman series: transitions ending at \(n=1\)
    • Balmer series: transitions ending at \(n=2\)
    • Higher energy transition \(\Rightarrow\) shorter wavelength
    • Wave number:

      \(\bar{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{lambda}\)

    Final exam-style answer:

    For \(n=4 \rightarrow n=2\):

    \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda_1} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{4^2}\right) = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{4} - \dfrac{1}{16}\right) = \dfrac{3R_H}{16}\)

    So, \(\lambda_1 = \dfrac{16}{3R_H}\)

    For \(n=3 \rightarrow n=1\):

    \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda_2} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{1^2} - \dfrac{1}{3^2}\right) = R_H\left(1 - \dfrac{1}{9}\right) = \dfrac{8R_H}{9}\)

    So, \(\lambda_2 = \dfrac{9}{8R_H}\)

    Hence:

    \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = \dfrac{16}{3R_H} - \dfrac{9}{8R_H}\)

    \(= \dfrac{128 - 27}{24R_H} = \dfrac{101}{24R_H}\)

    \(= \dfrac{1}{R_H}\left(\dfrac{101}{24}\right)\)

    \(\boxed{(c)\ \dfrac{1}{R_H}\left(\dfrac{101}{24}\right)}\)

    Answer: \((c)\ M_1,\ M_2,\ M_3\ \text{only}\)

    Solution:

    Photoelectric effect occurs only when the energy of the incident photon is at least equal to the work function of the metal.

    So the condition is:

    \(E_{\text{photon}} \ge \phi\)

    1. Find the energy of the incident photon

      For light of wavelength \(310\ \text{nm}\), photon energy in eV can be calculated using:

      \(E = \dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\text{in nm})}\)

      Therefore:

      \(E = \dfrac{1240}{310} = 4.0\ \text{eV}\)

    2. Compare this with the given work functions
      • \(M_1 = 4.8\ \text{eV}\)
      • \(M_2 = 4.3\ \text{eV}\)
      • \(M_3 = 4.75\ \text{eV}\)
      • \(M_4 = 3.75\ \text{eV}\)

      A metal will show photoelectric effect only if its work function is less than or equal to \(4.0\ \text{eV}\).

      So:

      • \(M_1:\ 4.8 > 4.0\) → does not show photoelectric effect
      • \(M_2:\ 4.3 > 4.0\) → does not show photoelectric effect
      • \(M_3:\ 4.75 > 4.0\) → does not show photoelectric effect
      • \(M_4:\ 3.75 < 4.0\) → does show photoelectric effect
    3. Hence identify the metals which do not emit electrons

      The metals which do not show photoelectric effect are:

      \(M_1,\ M_2,\ M_3\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ M_1,\ M_2,\ M_3\ \text{only}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • The work function \(\phi\) is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal surface.
    • If incident photon energy is less than the work function:

      \(E_{\text{photon}} < \phi\)

      then no electron is emitted, no matter how intense the light is.

    • If:

      \(E_{\text{photon}} \ge \phi\)

      then photoelectric emission is possible.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Einstein photoelectric equation:

      \(h\nu = \phi + K_{\max}\)

    • Threshold condition for emission:

      \(h\nu \ge \phi\)

    • Threshold frequency:

      \(\nu_0 = \dfrac{\phi}{h}\)

    • Threshold wavelength:

      \(\lambda_0 = \dfrac{hc}{\phi}\)

    • Quick energy formula in eV:

      \(E(\text{eV}) = \dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})}\)

    • Increasing intensity increases the number of emitted electrons, but does not help if photon energy is below threshold.
    • Increasing frequency increases the energy per photon.

    Useful physical picture:

    • Think of work function as a minimum entry barrier.
    • Here photon energy is \(4.0\ \text{eV}\).
    • So only metals with barrier \(\le 4.0\ \text{eV}\) can emit electrons.
    • Metals with barrier greater than \(4.0\ \text{eV}\) cannot emit electrons.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Immediately use:

      \(E(\text{eV}) = \dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})}\)

    • For \(310\ \text{nm}\):

      \(E = \dfrac{1240}{310} = 4.0\ \text{eV}\)

    • Now compare every work function directly with \(4.0\).
    • Anything above \(4.0\ \text{eV}\) will not show photoelectric effect.
    • Since \(4.8,\ 4.3,\ 4.75\) are all above \(4.0\), the answer is instantly:

      \(M_1,\ M_2,\ M_3\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Forgetting that the question asks for metals which do not show photoelectric effect.
    2. Using wavelength directly without converting it into photon energy.
    3. Thinking higher intensity can produce emission even when \(E < \phi\). It cannot.
    4. Confusing threshold condition with kinetic energy condition.
    5. Making arithmetic mistakes in:

      \(\dfrac{1240}{310} = 4.0\ \text{eV}\)

    Answer: \((c)\ \mathrm{Be},\ \mathrm{N}\)

    Solution:

    Across the second period, first ionisation enthalpy generally increases from left to right because effective nuclear charge increases and atomic size decreases.

    But there are two important exceptions due to extra stability of certain electronic configurations.

    1. Write the second-period elements in order

      \(\mathrm{Li,\ Be,\ B,\ C,\ N,\ O,\ F,\ Ne}\)

    2. Find the element whose first ionisation enthalpy is higher than both its neighbouring elements

      We check the known exceptions in the second period.

      • Beryllium: \(\mathrm{Be} = 1s^2\,2s^2\)

        \(\mathrm{Be}\) has a completely filled \(2s\) subshell, so removing an electron is relatively difficult.

        Hence first ionisation enthalpy of \(\mathrm{Be}\) is higher than that of preceding \(\mathrm{Li}\) and also higher than that of succeeding \(\mathrm{B}\).

      • Nitrogen: \(\mathrm{N} = 1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^3\)

        \(\mathrm{N}\) has a half-filled \(2p\) subshell, which is especially stable.

        So its first ionisation enthalpy is higher than that of preceding \(\mathrm{C}\) and succeeding \(\mathrm{O}\).

    3. Therefore identify \(X\) and \(Y\)

      The two elements are \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{N}\).

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ \mathrm{Be},\ \mathrm{N}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • First ionisation enthalpy depends mainly on:
      • effective nuclear charge,
      • atomic size,
      • shielding,
      • electronic configuration stability.
    • Across a period, effective nuclear charge increases, so ionisation enthalpy usually increases.
    • But stable filled and half-filled subshells resist electron removal.
    • That is why \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{N}\) stand out as local maxima in the second period.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • General trend across a period:

      First ionisation enthalpy usually increases from left to right.

    • Exception 1: \(\mathrm{Be > B}\)

      \(\mathrm{Be}\) has filled \(2s^2\), whereas in \(\mathrm{B}\) the electron removed comes from higher-energy \(2p^1\), so \(\mathrm{B}\) has lower first ionisation enthalpy than expected.

    • Exception 2: \(\mathrm{N > O}\)

      \(\mathrm{N}\) has half-filled \(2p^3\), while in \(\mathrm{O}\) one \(2p\) orbital contains paired electrons, so electron-electron repulsion makes removal easier in oxygen.

    • These two standard exceptions are extremely important for exams.

    Useful configuration view:

    • \(\mathrm{Be}: 1s^2\,2s^2\)

      filled \(2s\) subshell \(\Rightarrow\) extra stability

    • \(\mathrm{B}: 1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^1\)

      electron removed from \(2p\), which is easier to remove than from \(2s\)

    • \(\mathrm{N}: 1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^3\)

      half-filled \(2p\) subshell \(\Rightarrow\) extra stability

    • \(\mathrm{O}: 1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^4\)

      one orbital has paired electrons, so repulsion lowers ionisation enthalpy slightly

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Memorise the two second-period anomalies:

      \(\mathrm{Be > B}\) and \(\mathrm{N > O}\)

    • So the elements having higher first ionisation enthalpy than both neighbouring elements are immediately:

      \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{N}\)

    • In such MCQs, this is usually a direct exception-based question.

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Assuming ionisation enthalpy increases perfectly across the period with no exceptions.
    2. Choosing \(\mathrm{B}\) instead of \(\mathrm{Be}\).
    3. Choosing \(\mathrm{O}\) instead of \(\mathrm{N}\).
    4. Forgetting that filled and half-filled subshells have extra stability.

    \(\mathrm{Be}\) has configuration \(1s^2\,2s^2\), so its filled \(2s\) subshell gives extra stability. Hence its first ionisation enthalpy is higher than both \(\mathrm{Li}\) and \(\mathrm{B}\).

    Answer: \((b)\ \mathrm{II}\ \text{and}\ \mathrm{III}\)

    Solution:

    We have to identify the pairs of elements that have nearly the same atomic radius.

    This question is based on the effect of lanthanoid contraction.

    1. Recall the key idea

      Atomic radius generally increases down a group.

      So normally a \(5d\) element should be larger than the corresponding \(4d\) element.

      But in many cases this expected increase does not happen because of lanthanoid contraction.

    2. What is lanthanoid contraction?

      Across the lanthanoids, electrons are added to the \(4f\) subshell.

      The \(4f\) electrons shield the nuclear charge very poorly.

      So effective nuclear charge increases steadily, pulling electrons closer to the nucleus.

      As a result, the sizes of elements that come after lanthanoids become smaller than expected.

      Therefore, many \(5d\) elements have radii very close to those of their \(4d\) counterparts.

    3. Now examine each pair
      • I. \(\mathrm{Y},\ \mathrm{La}\)

        This is a \(4d\)–\(5d\) comparison, but \(\mathrm{La}\) comes before the main lanthanoid contraction effect is fully expressed.

        So \(\mathrm{Y}\) and \(\mathrm{La}\) are not considered nearly equal in atomic radius.

      • II. \(\mathrm{Zr},\ \mathrm{Hf}\)

        \(\mathrm{Zr}\) is a \(4d\) element and \(\mathrm{Hf}\) is the corresponding \(5d\) element of the same group.

        Due to lanthanoid contraction, the size of \(\mathrm{Hf}\) becomes nearly the same as that of \(\mathrm{Zr}\).

        So this pair is correct.

      • III. \(\mathrm{Mo},\ \mathrm{W}\)

        \(\mathrm{Mo}\) is a \(4d\) element and \(\mathrm{W}\) is the corresponding \(5d\) element of the same group.

        Again, lanthanoid contraction reduces the expected size increase in \(\mathrm{W}\).

        So \(\mathrm{Mo}\) and \(\mathrm{W}\) also have nearly the same atomic radius.

      • IV. \(\mathrm{Cr},\ \mathrm{Mo}\)

        These are \(3d\) and \(4d\) elements.

        This pair is not a standard result of lanthanoid contraction, so their radii are not nearly the same in the intended sense of the question.

    4. Choose the correct set

      The correct pairs are:

      \(\mathrm{II.}\ \mathrm{Zr},\ \mathrm{Hf}\) and \(\mathrm{III.}\ \mathrm{Mo},\ \mathrm{W}\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(b)\ \mathrm{II}\ \text{and}\ \mathrm{III}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • Lanthanoid contraction is the gradual decrease in atomic and ionic radii across the lanthanoid series.
    • This happens because \(4f\) electrons have poor shielding power.
    • So the outer electrons experience greater effective nuclear charge and are pulled inward.
    • Because of this, \(5d\) elements often end up having radii very close to the \(4d\) elements above them in the same group.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Lanthanoid contraction:

      Gradual decrease in size from \(\mathrm{La}\) to \(\mathrm{Lu}\).

    • Cause:

      Poor shielding of \(4f\) electrons.

    • Main consequence:

      \(4d\) and \(5d\) elements of the same group often have nearly equal atomic radii.

    • Standard important examples:
      • \(\mathrm{Zr} \approx \mathrm{Hf}\)
      • \(\mathrm{Nb} \approx \mathrm{Ta}\)
      • \(\mathrm{Mo} \approx \mathrm{W}\)
    • Because of this similarity, many \(4d\) and \(5d\) elements show similar chemical properties.

    Useful comparison pattern:

    • Normal expectation down a group:

      \(\text{radius increases}\)

    • But for \(4d \rightarrow 5d\) after lanthanoids:

      \(\text{expected increase is cancelled significantly}\)

    • So:

      \(\mathrm{Zr} \approx \mathrm{Hf}, \quad \mathrm{Mo} \approx \mathrm{W}\)

    1-minute solving trick:

    • As soon as you see “nearly same atomic radius” and pairs like \(\mathrm{Zr-Hf}\), \(\mathrm{Mo-W}\), think:

      lanthanoid contraction

    • Directly remember the famous near-equal pairs:
      • \(\mathrm{Zr-Hf}\)
      • \(\mathrm{Nb-Ta}\)
      • \(\mathrm{Mo-W}\)
    • That makes the answer immediate:

      \(\mathrm{II}\ \text{and}\ \mathrm{III}\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Assuming atomic radius always simply increases down a group with no exception.
    2. Forgetting that lanthanoid contraction mainly affects comparison of \(4d\) and \(5d\) elements.
    3. Choosing \(\mathrm{Y-La}\) just because they are one below the other.
    4. Confusing \(3d\)-\(4d\) comparisons with \(4d\)-\(5d\) near-equality patterns.

    Answer: \((a)\ 1.20x\)

    Solution:

    We use molecular orbital theory and the bond order formula:

    \(\text{Bond order} = \dfrac{N_b - N_a}{2}\)

    where \(N_b\) is the number of electrons in bonding molecular orbitals and \(N_a\) is the number of electrons in antibonding molecular orbitals.

    1. Find bond order of \(O_2^-\)

      \(O_2\) has 16 electrons, so \(O_2^-\) has 17 electrons.

      Compared to \(O_2\), the extra electron goes into an antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbital.

      Hence:

      \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^- = \dfrac{10 - 7}{2} = \dfrac{3}{2} = 1.5\)

    2. Find bond order of \(O_2^{2-}\)

      \(O_2^{2-}\) has 18 electrons.

      One more antibonding electron is added.

      Hence:

      \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2-} = \dfrac{10 - 8}{2} = \dfrac{2}{2} = 1.0\)

    3. Use the given information to find \(x\)

      Given:

      \(x = \text{bond order of } O_2^- + \text{bond order of } O_2^{2-}\)

      \(x = 1.5 + 1.0 = 2.5\)

    4. Find bond order of \(O_2^{2+}\)

      \(O_2^{2+}\) has 14 electrons.

      Removing two electrons from \(O_2\) means removing them from antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals.

      That increases bond order.

      Hence:

      \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2+} = \dfrac{10 - 4}{2} = \dfrac{6}{2} = 3.0\)

    5. Express bond order of \(O_2^{2+}\) in terms of \(x\)

      We found:

      \(x = 2.5\)

      \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2+} = 3.0\)

      So:

      \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2+} = \dfrac{3.0}{2.5}x = 1.20x\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(a)\ 1.20x}\)

    Why this method works:

    • In molecular orbital theory, bond order tells us the net bonding effect:

      \(\text{Bond order} = \dfrac{\text{bonding electrons} - \text{antibonding electrons}}{2}\)

    • Adding electrons to antibonding orbitals decreases bond order.
    • Removing electrons from antibonding orbitals increases bond order.
    • For oxygen species, the electrons added or removed beyond \(O_2\) mainly affect the \(\pi^*\) antibonding orbitals.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Bond order formula:

      \(\text{B.O.} = \dfrac{N_b - N_a}{2}\)

    • Stability:

      Higher bond order generally means greater stability.

    • Bond length:

      Higher bond order generally means shorter bond length.

    • For oxygen family:
      • \(\text{B.O. of } O_2 = 2\)
      • \(\text{B.O. of } O_2^- = 1.5\)
      • \(\text{B.O. of } O_2^{2-} = 1\)
      • \(\text{B.O. of } O_2^+ = 2.5\)
      • \(\text{B.O. of } O_2^{2+} = 3\)
    • \(O_2\) is paramagnetic because it has two unpaired electrons in \(\pi^*\) orbitals.

    Useful orbital picture:

    • In \(O_2\), the highest occupied orbitals are antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals.
    • So:
      • adding electrons to \(O_2\) lowers bond order,
      • removing electrons from \(O_2\) raises bond order.
    • That is why:

      \(\text{B.O.}(O_2^{2+}) > \text{B.O.}(O_2) > \text{B.O.}(O_2^-) > \text{B.O.}(O_2^{2-})\)

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Memorise these standard bond orders:
      • \(O_2^- = 1.5\)
      • \(O_2^{2-} = 1.0\)
      • \(O_2^{2+} = 3.0\)
    • Then immediately:

      \(x = 1.5 + 1.0 = 2.5\)

    • So:

      \(\text{B.O. of } O_2^{2+} = 3.0 = \dfrac{3.0}{2.5}x = 1.20x\)

    • This becomes a very fast ratio question if the standard oxygen-species bond orders are already known.

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Forgetting that added electrons go into antibonding orbitals for oxygen species.
    2. Using the wrong total electron count:
      • \(O_2^- = 17\)
      • \(O_2^{2-} = 18\)
      • \(O_2^{2+} = 14\)
    3. Mixing up bond order with number of bonds drawn in Lewis structures.
    4. Forgetting to convert the final value into a multiple of \(x\).

    • Topic: Molecular Orbital Theory
    • Subtopic: Bond order of oxygen species and effect of adding or removing electrons

    Quick cheatsheet:

    • Bond order:

      \(\text{B.O.} = \dfrac{N_b - N_a}{2}\)

    • If electron enters antibonding orbital:

      bond order decreases

    • If electron is removed from antibonding orbital:

      bond order increases

    • Standard oxygen species:

      \(O_2 = 2,\quad O_2^- = 1.5,\quad O_2^{2-} = 1,\quad O_2^+ = 2.5,\quad O_2^{2+} = 3\)

    • Trend in bond length:

      higher bond order \(\Rightarrow\) shorter bond length

    • Trend in stability:

      higher bond order \(\Rightarrow\) greater stability

    Final exam-style answer:

    \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^- = \dfrac{10-7}{2} = 1.5\)

    \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2-} = \dfrac{10-8}{2} = 1.0\)

    Therefore:

    \(x = 1.5 + 1.0 = 2.5\)

    Also:

    \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2+} = \dfrac{10-4}{2} = 3.0\)

    So:

    \(\text{Bond order of } O_2^{2+} = \dfrac{3.0}{2.5}x = 1.20x\)

    \(\boxed{(a)\ 1.20x}\)

    Answer: \((b)\ \mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\)

    Solution:

    We need the species in which the ratio of \(\sigma\)-bonds to \(\pi\)-bonds is \(3:2\).

    So we count total \(\sigma\)-bonds and total \(\pi\)-bonds in each option.

    1. Recall the basic rule for counting bonds
      • A single bond \(=\) \(1\sigma\)
      • A double bond \(=\) \(1\sigma + 1\pi\)
      • A triple bond \(=\) \(1\sigma + 2\pi\)
    2. Check \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\)

      Its structure is:

      •     N≡C—CH₂—C≡N

      Now count bonds carefully:

      • Two \(\mathrm{C-H}\) bonds \(= 2\sigma\)
      • Two \(\mathrm{C-C}\) single bonds \(= 2\sigma\)
      • Two \(\mathrm{C\equiv N}\) triple bonds \(= 2\sigma + 4\pi\)

      Therefore:

      Total \(\sigma\)-bonds \(= 2 + 2 + 2 = 6\)

      Total \(\pi\)-bonds \(= 4\)

      So the ratio is:

      \(\sigma : \pi = 6:4 = 3:2\)

      Hence this option satisfies the condition.

    3. Why the other options do not match
      • \(\mathrm{HCO_3^-}\)
        Usual resonance form has one \(\mathrm{C=O}\) and two \(\mathrm{C-O}\) single bonds, plus one \(\mathrm{O-H}\) bond.
        So total \(\sigma = 4\), total \(\pi = 1\). Ratio \(= 4:1\), not \(3:2\).
      • \(\mathrm{HClO_4}\)
        In the usual covalent representation, there are three \(\mathrm{Cl=O}\) bonds and one \(\mathrm{Cl-OH}\) bond.
        So \(\sigma = 5\), \(\pi = 3\). Ratio \(= 5:3\), not \(3:2\).
      • \(\mathrm{XeO_3}\)
        Usually represented with three \(\mathrm{Xe=O}\) bonds.
        So \(\sigma = 3\), \(\pi = 3\). Ratio \(= 1:1\), not \(3:2\).

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(b)\ \mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • Every covalent bond contains exactly one \(\sigma\)-bond.
    • Extra bonds in multiple bonding are \(\pi\)-bonds.
    • So bond counting becomes mechanical:
      • single \(\to 1\sigma\)
      • double \(\to 1\sigma + 1\pi\)
      • triple \(\to 1\sigma + 2\pi\)
    • Once the correct structure is identified, the ratio can be obtained quickly.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • A \(\sigma\)-bond is formed by head-on overlap.
    • A \(\pi\)-bond is formed by sidewise overlap.
    • \(\sigma\)-bonds are stronger than \(\pi\)-bonds because head-on overlap is more effective.
    • In multiple bonds:
      • double bond \(= 1\sigma + 1\pi\)
      • triple bond \(= 1\sigma + 2\pi\)
    • Bond-counting questions often test whether you can write or visualize the correct Lewis structure quickly.

    Useful structural view:

    • In \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\), the central carbon is bonded to:
      • two H atoms,
      • two carbon atoms of two cyanide groups.
    • Each cyanide group is \(\mathrm{-C\equiv N}\), so each contributes:

      \(1\sigma + 2\pi\)

    • Since there are two cyanide groups, total from them is:

      \(2\sigma + 4\pi\)

    1-minute solving trick:

    • In such MCQs, first look for an option containing two triple bonds.
    • Two triple bonds already contribute:

      \(2\sigma + 4\pi\)

    • To get ratio \(3:2\), total can be:

      \(6:4\)

    • So you only need \(4\) more \(\sigma\)-bonds and no extra \(\pi\)-bonds.
    • \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\) fits this immediately:
      • two \(\mathrm{C-H}\) bonds \(= 2\sigma\)
      • two \(\mathrm{C-C}\) single bonds \(= 2\sigma\)
    • Hence total \(= 6\sigma,\ 4\pi\), so ratio \(= 3:2\).

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Forgetting that a triple bond contains only one \(\sigma\)-bond, not three.
    2. Counting resonance structures separately instead of counting bonds in the actual representative structure.
    3. Missing \(\mathrm{C-H}\) or \(\mathrm{O-H}\) \(\sigma\)-bonds in the total count.
    4. Counting a double bond as \(2\sigma\) instead of \(1\sigma + 1\pi\).

    Quick cheatsheet:

    • Single bond:

      \(1\sigma\)

    • Double bond:

      \(1\sigma + 1\pi\)

    • Triple bond:

      \(1\sigma + 2\pi\)

    • Total bond order idea:

      \(\text{all bonds contribute one } \sigma,\ \text{multiple bonds add } \pi\)

    • For \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\):

      \(\sigma = 6,\ \pi = 4\)

    Final exam-style answer:

    \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\) has structure \(\mathrm{N\equiv C-CH_2-C\equiv N}\).

    Number of \(\sigma\)-bonds:

    • two \(\mathrm{C-H}\) bonds \(= 2\)
    • two \(\mathrm{C-C}\) single bonds \(= 2\)
    • two \(\mathrm{C\equiv N}\) bonds contribute \(2\) \(\sigma\)-bonds

    So total \(\sigma = 6\).

    Each \(\mathrm{C\equiv N}\) triple bond has \(2\pi\)-bonds, so two such bonds give:

    \(\pi = 4\)

    Hence:

    \(\sigma : \pi = 6:4 = 3:2\)

    Answer: \((b)\ 0.3\ \mathrm{L}\)

    Solution:

    Since both gases are ideal and temperature is the same, we use the ideal gas law:

    \(n = \dfrac{PV}{RT}\)

    After connection, gases mix completely, but the number of moles of each gas remains unchanged.

    1. Find moles of oxygen in flask \(A\)

      Flask \(A\) contains oxygen at \(5\ \mathrm{atm}\) in volume \(V\ \mathrm{L}\).

      So:

      \(n_{\mathrm{O_2}} = \dfrac{5V}{RT}\)

    2. Find moles of helium in flask \(B\)

      Flask \(B\) contains helium at \(3\ \mathrm{atm}\) in \(2\ \mathrm{L}\).

      So:

      \(n_{\mathrm{He}} = \dfrac{3 \times 2}{RT} = \dfrac{6}{RT}\)

    3. Write the mole fraction of oxygen after mixing

      Total moles after mixing:

      \(n_{\text{total}} = n_{\mathrm{O_2}} + n_{\mathrm{He}} = \dfrac{5V}{RT} + \dfrac{6}{RT} = \dfrac{5V+6}{RT}\)

      Given mole fraction of oxygen:

      \(x_{\mathrm{O_2}} = 0.2\)

      Now:

      \(x_{\mathrm{O_2}} = \dfrac{n_{\mathrm{O_2}}}{n_{\text{total}}}\)

      So:

      \(\dfrac{\frac{5V}{RT}}{\frac{5V+6}{RT}} = 0.2\)

      \(\dfrac{5V}{5V+6} = 0.2\)

    4. Solve for \(V\)

      \(5V = 0.2(5V+6)\)

      \(5V = V + 1.2\)

      \(4V = 1.2\)

      \(V = 0.3\ \mathrm{L}\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(b)\ 0.3\ \mathrm{L}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • For ideal gases:

      \(n = \dfrac{PV}{RT}\)

    • Since both flasks are at the same temperature, \(R\) and \(T\) are constant for both gases.
    • Mole fraction depends only on the ratio of moles:

      \(x_i = \dfrac{n_i}{n_{\text{total}}}\)

    • Mixing changes pressure distribution, but it does not change the actual number of moles present before mixing.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Ideal gas equation:

      \(PV = nRT\)

    • Mole fraction:

      \(x_i = \dfrac{n_i}{\sum n_i}\)

    • Dalton’s law of partial pressures:

      \(p_i = x_i P_{\text{total}}\)

    • In mixture problems at same temperature, directly comparing \(PV\) values is often enough because:

      \(n \propto PV\)

    • If gases are ideal, their identity does not affect the mole calculation through \(PV/RT\).

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Since \(n \propto PV\), do not write full ideal gas law every time.
    • Directly take:

      \(n_{\mathrm{O_2}} \propto 5V\)

      \(n_{\mathrm{He}} \propto 3 \times 2 = 6\)

    • Given:

      \(\dfrac{5V}{5V+6} = 0.2 = \dfrac{1}{5}\)

    • So immediately:

      \(5(5V) = 5V + 6\)

      \(25V = 5V + 6\)

      \(20V = 6\)

      \(V = 0.3\)

    • This is the fastest route in MCQ.

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Using final total volume to recalculate moles. Moles are already fixed before mixing.
    2. Confusing mole fraction with pressure fraction without using ideal-gas reasoning properly.
    3. Forgetting that for same \(T\), moles are proportional to \(PV\).
    4. Writing oxygen mole fraction as:

      \(\dfrac{n_{\mathrm{He}}}{n_{\text{total}}}\)

      instead of

      \(\dfrac{n_{\mathrm{O_2}}}{n_{\text{total}}}\)

    Quick intuition:

    • Mole fraction of oxygen is only \(0.2\), so oxygen must be much less than helium.
    • Helium contributes \(PV = 6\).
    • Oxygen contributes \(PV = 5V\).
    • For oxygen to be only one-fifth of the total, \(5V\) must be small, so \(V\) should be well below \(1\ \mathrm{L}\).
    • Among options, \(0.3\ \mathrm{L}\) fits naturally.

    Final exam-style answer:

    Moles of oxygen in flask \(A\):

    \(n_{\mathrm{O_2}} = \dfrac{5V}{RT}\)

    Moles of helium in flask \(B\):

    \(n_{\mathrm{He}} = \dfrac{3 \times 2}{RT} = \dfrac{6}{RT}\)

    Given mole fraction of oxygen after mixing is \(0.2\):

    \(0.2 = \dfrac{n_{\mathrm{O_2}}}{n_{\mathrm{O_2}} + n_{\mathrm{He}}}\)

    \(0.2 = \dfrac{\frac{5V}{RT}}{\frac{5V+6}{RT}} = \dfrac{5V}{5V+6}\)

    \(5V = 0.2(5V+6)\)

    \(5V = V + 1.2\)

    \(4V = 1.2\)

    \(V = 0.3\ \mathrm{L}\)

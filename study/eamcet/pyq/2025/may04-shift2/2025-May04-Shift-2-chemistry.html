    • Step-by-step explanation
      • First check Statement I.
      • According to Rutherford’s model, electrons revolve around the nucleus like planets around the Sun.
      • But an electron moving in a circular path is an accelerating charged particle.
      • By classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating charged particle should continuously radiate energy.
      • If the electron keeps losing energy, its orbit should shrink and it should finally collapse into the nucleus.
      • That means Rutherford model cannot explain atomic stability.
      • So Statement I is correct.
      • Now check Statement II.
      • In the electromagnetic spectrum, wavelength decreases in the order:
        • Radio waves \(>\) Microwaves \(>\) Infrared \(>\) Visible \(>\) Ultraviolet \(>\) X-rays \(>\) Gamma rays
      • So X-rays have a much smaller wavelength than microwaves.
      • Therefore Statement II is incorrect.
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Limitation of Rutherford model: It explained the presence of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus, but failed to explain stability and line spectra of atoms.
      • Classical physics issue: A moving electron in circular motion is accelerated, and accelerated charges emit radiation.
      • Electromagnetic spectrum idea: Higher frequency means lower wavelength because \(c = \lambda \nu\).
      • X-rays have very high frequency, so their wavelength must be very small.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (c) says Statement I is correct and Statement II is not correct.
      • That exactly matches the scientific facts here.

      • (b) is wrong because Statement I is actually true.

      • Whenever Rutherford model and stability appear together, remember: Rutherford failed to explain stability.
      • For spectrum order, remember: microwaves are much longer than X-rays.
      • If frequency is high, wavelength is low.

    • Correct answer: (d) \(0.4578 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{J}\).
    • Explanation: In the hydrogen atom, orbit radius is given by \(r_n = n^2 a_0\). From the given radii we first find the orbit numbers \(n_1 = 5\) and \(n_2 = 2\). Then we use \(E_n = \dfrac{-13.6}{n^2}\ \mathrm{eV}\) and subtract the energies. The emitted radiation energy comes out to \(0.4578 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{J}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • For hydrogen atom, Bohr radius relation is:
        • \(r_n = n^2 a_0\), where \(a_0 = 0.529 \times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{m}\)
      • Given first radius:
        • \(r_1 = 1.3225\ \mathrm{nm} = 1.3225 \times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m}\)
        • \(n_1^2 = \dfrac{r_1}{a_0} = \dfrac{1.3225 \times 10^{-9}}{0.529 \times 10^{-10}} = 25\)
        • \(n_1 = 5\)
      • Given second radius:
        • \(r_2 = 0.2116\ \mathrm{nm} = 0.2116 \times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m}\)
        • \(n_2^2 = \dfrac{r_2}{a_0} = \dfrac{0.2116 \times 10^{-9}}{0.529 \times 10^{-10}} = 4\)
        • \(n_2 = 2\)
      • So the electron moves from \(n=5\) to \(n=2\).
      • This is a downward transition, so radiation is emitted.
      • Energy of electron in hydrogen orbit:
        • \(E_n = \dfrac{-13.6}{n^2}\ \mathrm{eV}\)
      • For \(n=5\):
        • \(E_1 = \dfrac{-13.6}{25} = -0.544\ \mathrm{eV}\)
      • For \(n=2\):
        • \(E_2 = \dfrac{-13.6}{4} = -3.4\ \mathrm{eV}\)
      • Energy emitted:
        • \(\Delta E = E_{\text{higher}} - E_{\text{lower}} = (-0.544) - (-3.4) = 2.856\ \mathrm{eV}\)
      • Convert eV to joule:
        • \(1\ \mathrm{eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}\)
        • \(\Delta E = 2.856 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}\)
        • \(\Delta E \approx 4.578 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}\)
        • \(\Delta E = 0.4578 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{J}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Bohr radius formula: In hydrogen atom, radius of the \(n\)th orbit is proportional to \(n^2\).
      • Bohr energy formula: Energy of the \(n\)th orbit is negative and given by \(E_n = \dfrac{-13.6}{n^2}\ \mathrm{eV}\).
      • A more negative energy means the electron is more tightly bound.
      • When electron falls from a higher orbit to a lower orbit, the difference is released as radiation.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Only option (d) matches the calculated value \(4.578 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}\).
      • The same value is written as \(0.4578 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{J}\), which is exactly option (d).
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a), (b), and (c) do not match the energy difference for transition \(5 \rightarrow 2\).
      • A common mistake is to use wrong \(n\) values or forget to convert nm into m before using radius relation.
      • Another common mistake is sign confusion in energy subtraction.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • For hydrogen questions with radius, first immediately use \(r_n = n^2 a_0\) to identify the orbit number.
      • Keep \(a_0 = 0.529 \times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{m}\) in memory.
      • If the final orbit number is smaller, the process is emission.
      • Always use magnitude of energy released for the answer.
    • Final result
      • Initial orbit: \(n=5\)
      • Final orbit: \(n=2\)
      • Energy emitted \(= 0.4578 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{J}\)
      • Hence, option (d) is correct.

    • Correct answer: (b) ii, iv only.
    • Explanation: Statement (ii) and statement (iv) follow the general periodic trend of atomic radii, so they are correct. Statement (i) is wrong because lithium is larger than both chlorine and fluorine, and statement (iii) is wrong because due to lanthanoid contraction the correct order is \(Eu > Sm > Tm\).
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • We check each order one by one using periodic trends.
      • (i) \(\mathrm{Cl > F > Li}\)
      • This is incorrect.
      • Across a period, atomic radius decreases from left to right.
      • Lithium is on the extreme left of period 2, so it is quite large.
      • Fluorine is much smaller, and chlorine is larger than fluorine because it is one period below it.
      • So the correct order is:
        • \(\mathrm{Li > Cl > F}\)
      • (ii) \(\mathrm{P > C > N}\)
      • This is correct.
      • Phosphorus lies below nitrogen, so phosphorus is larger.
      • In period 2, carbon is to the left of nitrogen, so carbon is larger than nitrogen.
      • Hence:
        • \(\mathrm{P > C > N}\)
      • (iii) \(\mathrm{Tm > Sm > Eu}\)
      • This is incorrect.
      • Across lanthanoids, size gradually decreases because of poor shielding by \(4f\) electrons. This is called lanthanoid contraction.
      • So the correct order is:
        • \(\mathrm{Eu > Sm > Tm}\)
      • (iv) \(\mathrm{Sr > Ca > Mg}\)
      • This is correct.
      • All three belong to group 2, and atomic radius increases down the group.
      • Therefore:
        • \(\mathrm{Sr > Ca > Mg}\)
      • So only (ii) and (iv) are correct.
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Across a period: Atomic radius generally decreases from left to right because effective nuclear charge increases.
      • Down a group: Atomic radius increases because a new shell is added.
      • Lanthanoid contraction: In lanthanoids, radii decrease gradually from left to right due to poor shielding by \(4f\) electrons.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (b) includes only statements (ii) and (iv), which are the actually correct orders.
    • Why other options are wrong
      • (a) is wrong because (i) and (iii) are both incorrect.
      • (c) is wrong because (iii) is incorrect.
      • (d) is wrong because (iii) is incorrect and (ii) is missing.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • For radius comparison, first ask: same period or same group?
      • Same period: left side bigger.
      • Same group: lower element bigger.
      • For lanthanoids, remember the phrase: size contracts across the series.
    • Final result
      • Correct orders are (ii) and (iv) only.
      • Hence, option (b) is correct.

    • Correct answer: (c) ionic.
    • Explanation: If \(Z\) with atomic number \(a+2\) is an alkali metal, then the element just before it, \(Y\), must be a noble gas, and the element before that, \(X\), must be a halogen. A compound formed between an alkali metal and a halogen is ionic.
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • We are told:
        • \(X = a\)
        • \(Y = a+1\)
        • \(Z = a+2\)
      • Also, \(Z\) is an alkali metal.
      • In the periodic table, an alkali metal comes immediately after a noble gas when a new period starts.
      • So if \(Z\) is an alkali metal, then \(Y\), which is just before \(Z\), must be a noble gas.
      • Then \(X\), which is just before the noble gas, must be a halogen.
      • So:
        • \(X\) is a halogen \(\rightarrow\) non-metal
        • \(Z\) is an alkali metal \(\rightarrow\) metal
      • A compound formed between a metal and a non-metal is generally ionic.
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Alkali metals are group 1 elements.
      • Just before an alkali metal in the periodic table, the previous element is usually a noble gas.
      • Just before a noble gas, the element is a halogen.
      • Halogens tend to gain one electron, while alkali metals tend to lose one electron.
      • This electron transfer produces an ionic bond.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • \(X\) behaves like a halogen and \(Z\) behaves like an alkali metal.
      • Such combinations form salts like \(NaCl\), \(KBr\), etc.
      • These are classic examples of ionic compounds.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a) covalent is generally formed between non-metals, not between an alkali metal and a halogen.
      • (b) metallic exists in pure metals, not in a compound of metal and non-metal.
      • (d) coordinate covalent is not the normal bonding pattern here.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • Remember this periodic sequence:
        • Halogen \(\rightarrow\) Noble gas \(\rightarrow\) Alkali metal
      • If the last one is alkali metal, the one before it is noble gas and the one before that is halogen.
      • Then metal + halogen almost immediately tells you ionic bond.
    • Final result
      • \(X\) is a halogen.
      • \(Z\) is an alkali metal.
      • The compound formed by \(X\) and \(Z\) is ionic.
      • Hence, option (c) is correct.

    Answer: \((c)\ 1.5\ \text{Å}\)

    Solution:

    The metal crystallises in a face centred cubic \((\mathrm{fcc})\) lattice. In an \(\mathrm{fcc}\) unit cell, atoms touch each other along the face diagonal.

    1. Use the face diagonal relation

      For a cube, face diagonal \(d\) is:

      \(d = a\sqrt{2}\)

      In \(\mathrm{fcc}\), the face diagonal passes through:

      • one corner atom radius
      • one face-centred atom diameter
      • the opposite corner atom radius

      So, in \(\mathrm{fcc}\):

      \(4r = a\sqrt{2}\)

      Therefore:

      \(r = \dfrac{a\sqrt{2}}{4}\)

    2. Substitute the given edge length

      Given:

      \(a = 4.242\) Å

      So:

      \(r = \dfrac{4.242 \times \sqrt{2}}{4}\) Å

      Using \(\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414\),

      \(r \approx \dfrac{4.242 \times 1.414}{4}\) Å

      \(r \approx \dfrac{5.999}{4}\) Å

      \(r \approx 1.5\) Å

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{1.5\ \text{Å}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • In crystal structure questions, the key step is identifying where atoms touch.
    • In \(\mathrm{fcc}\), atoms do not touch along the edge.
    • They touch along the face diagonal.
    • The face diagonal of a cube is:

      \(a\sqrt{2}\)

    • Along this diagonal, the touching length is:

      \(4r\)

    • Hence:

      \(a\sqrt{2} = 4r\)

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Simple cubic \((\mathrm{sc})\):

      \(a = 2r\)

    • Body centred cubic \((\mathrm{bcc})\):

      \(\sqrt{3}\,a = 4r\)

    • Face centred cubic \((\mathrm{fcc})\):

      \(\sqrt{2}\,a = 4r\)

    • Number of atoms per unit cell:
      • \(\mathrm{sc} = 1\)
      • \(\mathrm{bcc} = 2\)
      • \(\mathrm{fcc} = 4\)
    • In \(\mathrm{fcc}\), contribution is:
      • 8 corner atoms \(\Rightarrow 8 \times \dfrac{1}{8} = 1\)
      • 6 face-centred atoms \(\Rightarrow 6 \times \dfrac{1}{2} = 3\)
      • Total \(= 4\)

    Helpful diagram:

    • Face diagonal in fcc:

Corner atom      Face-centred atom      Corner atom
    ●------------------●------------------●
         r                2r                r

So total touching length on face diagonal = 4r

But face diagonal of cube = \(a\sqrt{2}\)

Hence, \(a\sqrt{2} = 4r\)

    1-minute solving trick:

    • The moment you see fcc + edge length + radius, recall:

      \(r = \dfrac{a\sqrt{2}}{4}\)

    • Since \(\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{4} \approx 0.3535\), you can also do:

      \(r \approx 0.3535\,a\)

    • So directly:

      \(r \approx 0.3535 \times 4.242 \approx 1.5\)

    • This is faster than doing the full diagonal reasoning every time.

    More intuitive shortcut:

    • In \(\mathrm{fcc}\), edge length is always larger than radius by a fixed factor.
    • From:

      \(4r = a\sqrt{2}\)

    • we get:

      \(r = \dfrac{a}{2\sqrt{2}}\)

    • Since \(2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828\),

      \(r \approx \dfrac{4.242}{2.828} = 1.5\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Using the \(\mathrm{bcc}\) formula \(\sqrt{3}\,a = 4r\) by mistake.
    2. Using \(a = 2r\), which is only for simple cubic.
    3. Forgetting that in \(\mathrm{fcc}\), atoms touch along the face diagonal, not the edge.
    4. Writing \(r = \dfrac{a\sqrt{2}}{2}\) instead of dividing by \(4\).

    • Step-by-step explanation
      • We use the Bohr model formula for the radius of the \(n\)th orbit of a hydrogen-like ion:
        • \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
      • Here:
        • \(n\) = orbit number
        • \(a_0\) = Bohr radius
        • \(Z\) = atomic number of the nucleus
      • For hydrogen atom, \(Z=1\).
      • The question asks for the radius of the second orbit of hydrogen, so:
        • \(r_{\mathrm{H}} = \dfrac{2^2 a_0}{1} = 4a_0\)
      • Now let the hydrogen-like ion be \(x\), having orbit number \(n\) and atomic number \(Z\).
      • Its orbit radius is:
        • \(r_x = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
      • Since the two radii are equal:
        • \(\dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z} = 4a_0\)
      • Cancel \(a_0\):
        • \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z} = 4\)
        • \(n^2 = 4Z\)
      • Now check the options.
      • For \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\), \(Z=4\).
      • Then:
        • \(n^2 = 4 \times 4 = 16\)
        • \(n = 4\)
      • This exactly matches option (c).
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • This is a Bohr model question involving the radius of hydrogen-like species.
      • A hydrogen-like ion means a species with only one electron, such as \(\mathrm{He^+}\), \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
      • For such species, the orbit radius depends on:
        • directly on \(n^2\)
        • inversely on nuclear charge \(Z\)
      • So larger \(n\) increases radius, while larger \(Z\) pulls the electron closer and decreases radius.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (c) gives \(n=4\) and ion \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
      • For \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\), \(Z=4\).
      • Then:
        • \(r = \dfrac{4^2 a_0}{4} = \dfrac{16a_0}{4} = 4a_0\)
      • This is exactly the same as the radius of the second orbit of hydrogen.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable

      • (b) \(3, \mathrm{Li^{2+}}\): here \(Z=3\), so \(r=\dfrac{9a_0}{3}=3a_0\), not \(4a_0\).

    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • This is a VSEPR theory question.
      • The bond angle depends mainly on:
        • number of bond pairs
        • number of lone pairs
        • type of hybridisation and electron geometry
        • multiple bond character
      • Now understand each molecule.
      • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\):
        • oxygen has two bond pairs and two lone pairs
        • electron geometry is tetrahedral, but molecular shape is bent
        • lone pair-lone pair and lone pair-bond pair repulsions are strong
        • so the angle decreases from ideal tetrahedral value \(109.5^\circ\) to about \(104.5^\circ\)

        • nitrogen has three bond pairs and one lone pair
        • shape is trigonal pyramidal
        • there is only one lone pair, so compression is less than in water

        • ozone is bent, with the central oxygen surrounded by three electron domains
        • its bonding has resonance and partial double bond character

        • it is about \(116.8^\circ\)
      • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\):
        • sulphur dioxide is also bent and has an approximately trigonal planar electron arrangement around sulphur
        • the bond angle is slightly larger than in ozone
        • its angle is about \(119^\circ\)

    • Explanation: By Newton’s law of viscosity, the viscous force needed to maintain the steady flow of liquid layers is directly proportional to the area of contact and to the velocity gradient. Therefore, \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • When layers of a liquid move relative to each other, an internal frictional force opposes the motion.
      • This internal friction is called viscosity.
      • According to Newton’s law of viscosity, the force required to maintain the steady flow of liquid layers:
        • is directly proportional to the area of contact \(A\)
        • is directly proportional to the velocity gradient \(\dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • So mathematically:
        • \(F \propto A\)
        • \(F \propto \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • Combining both proportionalities:
        • \(F \propto A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • Introducing the constant of proportionality \(\eta\), the coefficient of viscosity:
        • \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • Hence the correct option is (c).
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Viscosity measures the resistance offered by a fluid to flow.
      • If one layer of liquid moves faster than another, the difference in speed between nearby layers produces a velocity gradient.
      • The larger the velocity gradient, the more force is needed to maintain flow.
      • The larger the contact area, the more fluid is involved in frictional interaction, so the required force also increases.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (c) exactly matches Newton’s law of viscosity:
        • \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • It has the correct direct dependence on both area and velocity gradient.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a) is wrong because force should increase with area, not decrease with it.
      • (b) is wrong because force is proportional to \(\dfrac{du}{dz}\), not to its inverse \(\dfrac{dz}{du}\).
      • (d) is wrong for both reasons: wrong dependence on area and wrong inverse dependence on velocity change.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • Remember the standard formula:
        • \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • If velocity gradient increases, resistance increases.
      • If area increases, resisting force also increases.
      • So any option with \(1/A\) or inverse velocity gradient can be eliminated quickly.
    • Final result
      • The required force is \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (c).

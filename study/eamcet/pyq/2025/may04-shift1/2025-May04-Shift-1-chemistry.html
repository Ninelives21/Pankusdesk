    • Correct answer: (c) \(4, \mathrm{Be^{3+}}\)
    • Explanation: For a hydrogen-like species, orbit radius is given by \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\). The second orbit radius of hydrogen is \(4a_0\). Matching this with \(\dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\) gives \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z} = 4\), and the suitable option is \(n=4\) with \(Z=4\), that is \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • We use the Bohr model formula for the radius of the \(n\)th orbit of a hydrogen-like ion:
        • \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
      • Here:
        • \(n\) = orbit number
        • \(a_0\) = Bohr radius
        • \(Z\) = atomic number of the nucleus
      • For hydrogen atom, \(Z=1\).
      • The question asks for the radius of the second orbit of hydrogen, so:
        • \(r_{\mathrm{H}} = \dfrac{2^2 a_0}{1} = 4a_0\)
      • Now let the hydrogen-like ion be \(x\), having orbit number \(n\) and atomic number \(Z\).
      • Its orbit radius is:
        • \(r_x = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
      • Since the two radii are equal:
        • \(\dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z} = 4a_0\)
      • Cancel \(a_0\):
        • \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z} = 4\)
        • \(n^2 = 4Z\)
      • Now check the options.
      • For \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\), \(Z=4\).
      • Then:
        • \(n^2 = 4 \times 4 = 16\)
        • \(n = 4\)
      • This exactly matches option (c).
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • This is a Bohr model question involving the radius of hydrogen-like species.
      • A hydrogen-like ion means a species with only one electron, such as \(\mathrm{He^+}\), \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
      • For such species, the orbit radius depends on:
        • directly on \(n^2\)
        • inversely on nuclear charge \(Z\)
      • So larger \(n\) increases radius, while larger \(Z\) pulls the electron closer and decreases radius.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (c) gives \(n=4\) and ion \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
      • For \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\), \(Z=4\).
      • Then:
        • \(r = \dfrac{4^2 a_0}{4} = \dfrac{16a_0}{4} = 4a_0\)
      • This is exactly the same as the radius of the second orbit of hydrogen.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a) \(4, \mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) is wrong because \(\mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) is not hydrogen-like; it has two electrons.
      • (b) \(3, \mathrm{Li^{2+}}\): here \(Z=3\), so \(r=\dfrac{9a_0}{3}=3a_0\), not \(4a_0\).
      • (d) \(2, \mathrm{He^+}\): here \(Z=2\), so \(r=\dfrac{4a_0}{2}=2a_0\), not \(4a_0\).
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • Memorise the hydrogen-like radius formula:
        • \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
      • Always first check whether the given ion is truly hydrogen-like.
      • If the ion has more than one electron, eliminate it immediately in such Bohr model questions.
    • Final result
      • The required values are \(n=4\) and \(x=\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (c).

    • Explanation: First calculate the photon energy from \(E=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\), convert it into energy per mole, then subtract the given kinetic energy per mole. The remaining energy is the work function, which comes out to about \(2.5\ \mathrm{eV}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • We use Einstein’s photoelectric equation:
        • \(\text{Photon energy} = \text{work function} + \text{kinetic energy of emitted electron}\)
      • Since the kinetic energy is given in \(\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\), it is easiest to work throughout on a per mole basis.
      • Given:
        • \(\lambda = 331.5\,\mathrm{nm} = 331.5 \times 10^{-9}\,\mathrm{m}\)
        • \(h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}\,\mathrm{J\,s}\)
        • \(c = 3 \times 10^8\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\)
        • \(N_A = 6 \times 10^{23}\,\mathrm{mol^{-1}}\)
        • \(\mathrm{KE} = 1.2 \times 10^6\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\)
      • Energy of one photon:
        • \(E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)
        • \(E = \dfrac{6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{331.5 \times 10^{-9}}\)
        • \(E = 6.00 \times 10^{-19}\,\mathrm{J}\)
      • Energy per mole of photons:
        • \(E_{\mathrm{mol}} = 6.00 \times 10^{-19} \times 6 \times 10^{23}\)
        • \(E_{\mathrm{mol}} = 3.6 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\)
      • Now apply the photoelectric equation on molar basis:
        • \(\phi_{\mathrm{mol}} = E_{\mathrm{mol}} - \mathrm{KE}\)
        • \(\phi_{\mathrm{mol}} = 3.6 \times 10^5 - 1.2 \times 10^5\)
        • \(\phi_{\mathrm{mol}} = 2.4 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\)
      • Now convert \(\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\) into \(\mathrm{eV}\).
      • We use:
        • \(1\,\mathrm{eV} = 96485\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\)
      • So:
        • \(\phi = \dfrac{2.4 \times 10^5}{96485}\,\mathrm{eV}\)
        • \(\phi \approx 2.5\,\mathrm{eV}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • This is a photoelectric effect question.
      • Einstein’s photoelectric equation is:
        • \(h\nu = \phi + \mathrm{KE}\)
      • Since \(\nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda}\), photon energy can also be written as:
        • \(E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)
      • The work function is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal surface.
      • If photon energy is more than the work function, the extra part appears as kinetic energy of the emitted electron.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • The photon energy per mole is \(3.6 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\).
      • The kinetic energy per mole is \(1.2 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\).
      • So work function per mole is \(2.4 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\).
      • Converting that gives about \(2.5\,\mathrm{eV}\).
      • Therefore option (d) is correct.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a), (b), and (c) do not match the calculated value after proper unit conversion.
      • A common mistake is to mishandle the power of ten in kinetic energy or to forget the conversion from \(\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}\) to \(\mathrm{eV}\).

      • In photoelectric questions, first decide whether the energy is given per electron or per mole.
      • Do not mix single-particle units and molar units in one step.
      • If the answer is required in \(\mathrm{eV}\), either:
        • work per electron directly in joules and divide by \(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\), or
        • work per mole and divide by \(96485\)
      • Both methods should give the same final value.
    • Final result
      • Work function \(= 2.5\,\mathrm{eV}\)
      • Hence, the correct option is (d).

    • Step-by-step explanation

      • Its electronic configuration is:
        • \((\mathrm{Xe})\,4f^{14}5d^9 6s^1\)
      • Now find the period number.
      • The period number is given by the highest principal quantum number present in the electronic configuration.
      • Here the highest value of \(n\) is \(6\) because of the \(6s^1\) electron.

        • \(x=6\)

        • \(6s^1\) gives \(1\) electron
      • Therefore:
        • \(y = 9+1 = 10\)
      • Hence:
        • \(x+y = 6+10 = 16\)

    • Correct answer: (d) \(\mathrm{H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2}\)
    • Explanation: The bond angle increases in the order \(\mathrm{H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2}\) because lone pair repulsion, electron-pair arrangement, and the nature of bonding together determine the final shape and angle in each molecule.
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • To solve this question, we compare the approximate bond angles of all four molecules.
      • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) has a bond angle of about \(104.5^\circ\).
      • \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has a bond angle of about \(107^\circ\).
      • \(\mathrm{O_3}\) has a bond angle of about \(116.8^\circ\).
      • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) has a bond angle of about \(119^\circ\).
      • Therefore, the increasing order is:
        • \(\mathrm{H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • This is a VSEPR theory question.
      • The bond angle depends mainly on:
        • number of bond pairs
        • number of lone pairs
        • type of hybridisation and electron geometry
        • multiple bond character
      • Now understand each molecule.
      • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\):
        • oxygen has two bond pairs and two lone pairs
        • electron geometry is tetrahedral, but molecular shape is bent
        • lone pair-lone pair and lone pair-bond pair repulsions are strong
        • so the angle decreases from ideal tetrahedral value \(109.5^\circ\) to about \(104.5^\circ\)
      • \(\mathrm{NH_3}\):
        • nitrogen has three bond pairs and one lone pair
        • shape is trigonal pyramidal
        • there is only one lone pair, so compression is less than in water
        • hence angle is about \(107^\circ\), greater than \(\mathrm{H_2O}\)
      • \(\mathrm{O_3}\):
        • ozone is bent, with the central oxygen surrounded by three electron domains
        • its bonding has resonance and partial double bond character
        • because of \(sp^2\)-type arrangement, the angle is much larger than in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\)
        • it is about \(116.8^\circ\)
      • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\):
        • sulphur dioxide is also bent and has an approximately trigonal planar electron arrangement around sulphur
        • the bond angle is slightly larger than in ozone
        • its angle is about \(119^\circ\)
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (d) exactly matches the increasing numerical order of bond angles:
        • \(\mathrm{H_2O} \approx 104.5^\circ\)
        • \(\mathrm{NH_3} \approx 107^\circ\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_3} \approx 116.8^\circ\)
        • \(\mathrm{SO_2} \approx 119^\circ\)
      • So \(\mathrm{H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2}\) is the correct arrangement.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a) is wrong because it places \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) before \(\mathrm{O_3}\), but also places \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) after \(\mathrm{O_3}\), which is incorrect.
      • (b) is wrong because \(\mathrm{O_3}\) does not have a smaller bond angle than \(\mathrm{NH_3}\).
      • (c) is wrong because \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) has a slightly larger bond angle than \(\mathrm{O_3}\), not smaller.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • Memorise these very common approximate values:
        • \(\mathrm{H_2O} \approx 104.5^\circ\)
        • \(\mathrm{NH_3} \approx 107^\circ\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_3} \approx 116.8^\circ\)
        • \(\mathrm{SO_2} \approx 119^\circ\)
      • Among \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), more lone pairs on the central atom compress the angle more, so water has the smaller angle.
      • Among \(\mathrm{O_3}\) and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), both are bent molecules with larger angles than \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is slightly larger.
    • Final result
      • The increasing order of bond angles is \(\mathrm{H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2}\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (d).

    • Correct answer: (b) \(\mathrm{H_2O > HF > NH_3 > PH_3}\)
    • Explanation: Boiling point here is mainly controlled by intermolecular forces, especially hydrogen bonding. \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) has the most extensive hydrogen-bonding network, \(\mathrm{HF}\) has strong but less extensive hydrogen bonding, \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has weaker hydrogen bonding, and \(\mathrm{PH_3}\) practically lacks hydrogen bonding.
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • To compare boiling points, we compare the strength of intermolecular attractions in each compound.
      • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\):
        • forms very extensive hydrogen bonding
        • each water molecule can both donate and accept hydrogen bonds effectively
        • this creates a strong three-dimensional network
        • so it has the highest boiling point among these compounds
      • \(\mathrm{HF}\):
        • has very strong individual hydrogen bonds because fluorine is highly electronegative
        • but the hydrogen-bonding network is less extensive than in water
        • therefore its boiling point is high, but still below that of water
      • \(\mathrm{NH_3}\):
        • shows hydrogen bonding, but it is weaker than in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{HF}\)
        • nitrogen is less electronegative than oxygen and fluorine
        • so the intermolecular attraction is weaker and the boiling point is lower
      • \(\mathrm{PH_3}\):
        • does not show effective hydrogen bonding
        • its intermolecular forces are mainly weak van der Waals or weak dipole-dipole interactions
        • so it has the lowest boiling point in the list
      • Therefore, the decreasing order is:
        • \(\mathrm{H_2O > HF > NH_3 > PH_3}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Boiling point depends on the strength of intermolecular forces.
      • Hydrogen bonding gives unusually high boiling points.
      • For hydrogen bonding to be strong, hydrogen should be bonded to a highly electronegative atom such as \(\mathrm{F}\), \(\mathrm{O}\), or \(\mathrm{N}\).
      • That is why \(\mathrm{HF}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) have abnormally high boiling points compared with similar hydrides.
      • Among them, water stands out because it forms the most extensive network.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (b) correctly follows the actual strength pattern of intermolecular forces:
        • strongest effective network in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\)
        • then \(\mathrm{HF}\)
        • then \(\mathrm{NH_3}\)
        • lowest for \(\mathrm{PH_3}\)
      • Hence it gives the correct decreasing order of boiling points.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a) is wrong because it places \(\mathrm{HF}\) above \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), whereas water actually has the higher boiling point due to its more extensive hydrogen-bonded network.
      • (c) is wrong because \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has a higher boiling point than \(\mathrm{PH_3}\), not lower.
      • (d) is wrong because both the placement of \(\mathrm{HF}\) above \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) above \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) are incorrect.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • For common hydrides, always first check whether hydrogen bonding is present.
      • Memorise this standard boiling-point order:
        • \(\mathrm{H_2O > HF > NH_3 > PH_3}\)
      • Water is often the highest because its hydrogen-bonding network is exceptionally extensive.
    • Final result
      • The correct decreasing order of boiling points is \(\mathrm{H_2O > HF > NH_3 > PH_3}\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (b).

    • Correct answer: (c) \(\eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
    • Explanation: By Newton’s law of viscosity, the viscous force needed to maintain the steady flow of liquid layers is directly proportional to the area of contact and to the velocity gradient. Therefore, \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation
      • When layers of a liquid move relative to each other, an internal frictional force opposes the motion.
      • This internal friction is called viscosity.
      • According to Newton’s law of viscosity, the force required to maintain the steady flow of liquid layers:
        • is directly proportional to the area of contact \(A\)
        • is directly proportional to the velocity gradient \(\dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • So mathematically:
        • \(F \propto A\)
        • \(F \propto \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • Combining both proportionalities:
        • \(F \propto A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • Introducing the constant of proportionality \(\eta\), the coefficient of viscosity:
        • \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • Hence the correct option is (c).
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • Viscosity measures the resistance offered by a fluid to flow.
      • If one layer of liquid moves faster than another, the difference in speed between nearby layers produces a velocity gradient.
      • The larger the velocity gradient, the more force is needed to maintain flow.
      • The larger the contact area, the more fluid is involved in frictional interaction, so the required force also increases.
    • Why the correct option is right
      • Option (c) exactly matches Newton’s law of viscosity:
        • \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • It has the correct direct dependence on both area and velocity gradient.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable
      • (a) is wrong because force should increase with area, not decrease with it.
      • (b) is wrong because force is proportional to \(\dfrac{du}{dz}\), not to its inverse \(\dfrac{dz}{du}\).
      • (d) is wrong for both reasons: wrong dependence on area and wrong inverse dependence on velocity change.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue
      • Remember the standard formula:
        • \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\)
      • If velocity gradient increases, resistance increases.
      • If area increases, resisting force also increases.
      • So any option with \(1/A\) or inverse velocity gradient can be eliminated quickly.
    • Final result
      • The required force is \(F = \eta A \dfrac{du}{dz}\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (c).

    • Step-by-step explanation
      • This is a redox balancing question in basic medium.
      • First identify oxidation state changes.
      • In \(\mathrm{Cr(OH)_3}\), chromium is in the \(+3\) oxidation state.
      • In \(\mathrm{CrO_4^{2-}}\), chromium is in the \(+6\) oxidation state.
      • So chromium is oxidised:
        • \(\mathrm{Cr^{3+} \rightarrow Cr^{6+}}\)
      • Each chromium atom loses \(3\) electrons.
      • In \(\mathrm{IO_3^-}\), iodine is in the \(+5\) oxidation state.
      • In \(\mathrm{I^-}\), iodine is in the \(-1\) oxidation state.
      • So iodine is reduced:
        • \(\mathrm{I^{+5} \rightarrow I^{-1}}\)
      • That means iodine gains \(6\) electrons.
      • To balance electrons:
        • two chromium atoms lose \(2 \times 3 = 6\) electrons
        • one iodine atom gains \(6\) electrons
      • So the skeleton ratio becomes:
        • \(2\mathrm{Cr(OH)_3} + \mathrm{IO_3^-} \rightarrow 2\mathrm{CrO_4^{2-}} + \mathrm{I^-}\)
      • Now balance in basic medium by adding \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).
      • The balanced equation is:
        • \(2\mathrm{Cr(OH)_3} + \mathrm{IO_3^-} + 4\mathrm{OH^-} \rightarrow 2\mathrm{CrO_4^{2-}} + \mathrm{I^-} + 5\mathrm{H_2O}\)
      • Comparing with the given general form:
        • \(x=2\)
        • \(y=1\)
        • \(z=4\)
        • \(a=2\)
        • \(b=1\)
        • \(c=5\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it
      • In redox reactions, balance first using oxidation number or half-reaction method.
      • Then, because the medium is basic, use \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) to balance oxygen and hydrogen.

    Solution:

    Sodium crystallises in a body centred cubic \((\mathrm{bcc})\) lattice. For a \(\mathrm{bcc}\) unit cell, atoms touch each other along the body diagonal.

    1. Use the geometry of \(\mathrm{bcc}\)

      In a body centred cubic lattice, the body diagonal passes through:

      • one corner atom radius
      • the body-centred atom diameter
      • the opposite corner atom radius

      So, along the body diagonal:

      \(4r = \sqrt{3}\,a\)

      Therefore:

      \(a = \dfrac{4r}{\sqrt{3}}\)

    2. Substitute the given radius

      Given:

      \(r = 1.86\ \text{Å}\)

      Hence:

      \(a = \dfrac{4 \times 1.86}{\sqrt{3}}\)

      \(a = \dfrac{7.44}{1.732}\)

      \(a \approx 4.29\ \text{Å}\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{4.29\ \text{Å}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • In crystal structure problems, the key idea is: which diagonal or edge contains touching atoms?
    • In \(\mathrm{bcc}\), atoms do not touch along the edge.
    • They touch along the body diagonal.
    • Body diagonal of a cube \(= \sqrt{3}\,a\)
    • Total atomic contact length along this diagonal \(= 4r\)
    • So:

      \(\sqrt{3}\,a = 4r\)

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Simple cubic \((\mathrm{sc})\):

      \(a = 2r\)

    • Body centred cubic \((\mathrm{bcc})\):

      \(\sqrt{3}\,a = 4r\)

    • Face centred cubic \((\mathrm{fcc})\):

      \(\sqrt{2}\,a = 4r\)

    • In \(\mathrm{bcc}\), number of atoms per unit cell:

      \(Z = 2\)

    • Contribution in \(\mathrm{bcc}\):
      • 8 corner atoms \(\Rightarrow 8 \times \dfrac{1}{8} = 1\)
      • 1 body-centred atom \(\Rightarrow 1\)
      • Total \(= 2\)

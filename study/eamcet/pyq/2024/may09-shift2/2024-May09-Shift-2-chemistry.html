    • Correct answer: (d) \(6.97 \times 10^{14}\) Hz.
    • Explanation: This is a direct photoelectric effect question. We use Einstein’s photoelectric equation, subtract the given kinetic energy from the incoming photon energy, and then divide by Planck’s constant to get the threshold frequency.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Given:
        • Incident frequency, \( \nu = 1.0 \times 10^{15} \text{ Hz} \)
        • Kinetic energy of emitted electron, \( \text{KE} = 2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \)
        • Planck’s constant, \( h = 6.6 \times 10^{-34} \text{ Js} \)
      • Einstein’s photoelectric equation is:
        • \( h\nu = h\nu_0 + \text{KE} \)
      • Rearrange to find threshold frequency \( \nu_0 \):
        • \( h\nu_0 = h\nu - \text{KE} \)
        • \( \nu_0 = \dfrac{h\nu - \text{KE}}{h} \)
      • Now substitute the values:
        • \( \nu_0 = \dfrac{(6.6 \times 10^{-34})(1.0 \times 10^{15}) - 2 \times 10^{-19}}{6.6 \times 10^{-34}} \)
      • First calculate photon energy:
        • \( h\nu = 6.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \)
      • Now subtract kinetic energy:
        • \( h\nu - \text{KE} = 6.6 \times 10^{-19} - 2 \times 10^{-19} = 4.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \)
      • Now divide by \(h\):
        • \( \nu_0 = \dfrac{4.6 \times 10^{-19}}{6.6 \times 10^{-34}} \)
        • \( \nu_0 \approx 0.697 \times 10^{15} = 6.97 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz} \)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • In the photoelectric effect, light comes in packets called photons.
      • Each photon has energy \( h\nu \).
      • A part of this energy is used to remove the electron from the metal surface. That minimum required energy is called the work function.
      • The remaining energy appears as the kinetic energy of the emitted electron.
      • The threshold frequency \( \nu_0 \) is the minimum frequency needed to just eject electrons from the metal surface.
      • At threshold, the emitted electron has zero kinetic energy, so \( h\nu_0 = \phi \), where \( \phi \) is the work function.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (d) matches the correct numerical value obtained from Einstein’s equation.
      • The value is less than the incident frequency, which makes physical sense, because the incoming light is energetic enough to eject electrons and also give them some kinetic energy.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • (a) and (c) are larger than the incident frequency, which cannot be correct here because electrons are already being emitted.
      • (b) is too small and does not match the calculation.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • In photoelectric questions, always remember:
        • \( h\nu = h\nu_0 + \text{KE} \)
      • If electrons are emitted, then the incident frequency must be greater than threshold frequency.
      • So any option greater than \(1.0 \times 10^{15}\) Hz can be rejected quickly.
    • Final result:
      • \( \nu_0 = 6.97 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz} \)

    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • According to MOT, bond order is:
        • \( \text{Bond order} = \dfrac{N_B - N_A}{2} \)
      • Here:
        • \(N_B\) = number of bonding electrons
        • \(N_A\) = number of antibonding electrons
      • Now recall the standard bond orders for oxygen species:
        • \(O_2\): bond order \(= 2\)
        • \(O_2^+\): one electron is removed from antibonding orbital, so bond order \(= 2.5\)
        • \(O_2^-\): one electron is added to antibonding orbital, so bond order \(= 1.5\)
        • \(O_2^{2-}\): two electrons are added to antibonding orbital, so bond order \(= 1\)
        • \(O_2^{2+}\): two electrons are removed from antibonding orbital, so bond order \(= 3\)
      • Now compare the bond order differences in the given options:
        • (a) \(O_2^- , O_2^+\): \(2.5 - 1.5 = 1\)
        • (b) \(O_2^{2-} , O_2^{2+}\): \(3 - 1 = 2\)
        • (c) \(O_2 , O_2^{2+}\): \(3 - 2 = 1\)
        • (d) \(O_2^+ , O_2^{2+}\): \(3 - 2.5 = 0.5\)

      • In MOT, electrons fill molecular orbitals just like atomic orbitals.
      • Bonding orbitals increase stability and increase bond order.
      • Antibonding orbitals reduce bond strength and reduce bond order.

      • So:
        • adding electrons lowers bond order,
        • removing electrons from antibonding orbitals increases bond order.

    Answer: \((d)\) Trapping of electrons in lattice leads to the formation of F-centres.

    Solution:

    This question is from crystal defects in ionic solids. To solve it quickly, check each statement using the standard definitions of Frenkel defect, Schottky defect and F-centres.

    1. Check option \((a)\)

      Statement: Frenkel defect is favoured when the difference between sizes of cation and anion is very small.

      This is incorrect.

      Frenkel defect is favoured when there is a large difference in the sizes of cation and anion.

      Usually, the smaller cation leaves its normal lattice site and occupies an interstitial site.

      So Frenkel defect is more common when: \( \text{cation is much smaller than anion} \)

    2. Check option \((b)\)

      Statement: Frenkel defect is not a dislocation effect.

      This is incorrect.

      Frenkel defect is a dislocation defect.

      In this defect, an ion is displaced from its regular lattice position to an interstitial position.

      So the ion is still present in the crystal, but its position changes.

    3. Check option \((c)\)

      Statement: Schottky defects have no effect on physical properties of solids.

      This is incorrect.

      Schottky defect does affect physical properties of solids.

      • It decreases the density of the crystal because some ions are missing from the lattice.
      • It can also influence other properties such as ionic conductivity.
    4. Check option \((d)\)

      Statement: Trapping of electrons in lattice leads to the formation of F-centres.

      This is correct.

      When an anion is missing from its lattice site, an electron may occupy that vacant site to maintain electrical neutrality.

      This electron trapped in an anion vacancy is called an F-centre.

      F-centres are responsible for the colour of some ionic crystals.

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(d)}\)

    Why this method works:

    • This is a pure concept-based question from solid state.
    • Once the definitions are clear, wrong statements can be eliminated very quickly.
    • The key memory points are:
      • Frenkel defect \(\rightarrow\) ion displaced to interstitial site
      • Schottky defect \(\rightarrow\) equal number of cations and anions missing
      • F-centre \(\rightarrow\) electron trapped in anion vacancy

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Stoichiometric defects:
      • Schottky defect
      • Frenkel defect
    • In Schottky defect, equal number of cations and anions are missing from their lattice sites.
    • In Schottky defect:
      • electrical neutrality is maintained
      • stoichiometry is maintained
      • density decreases
    • In Frenkel defect, an ion leaves its lattice site and occupies an interstitial site.
    • In Frenkel defect:
      • electrical neutrality is maintained
      • stoichiometry is maintained
      • density generally does not change significantly
    • Frenkel defect is common in crystals where:
      • cation is small
      • anion is large
    • F-centres arise due to electrons trapped at anion vacancies.
    • These trapped electrons often impart colour to crystals such as alkali metal halides.

    • See F-centre \(\rightarrow\) immediately think electron trapped in anion vacancy.
    • See Frenkel defect \(\rightarrow\) think small ion displaced.
    • See Schottky defect \(\rightarrow\) think vacancies, so density decreases.
    • That alone is enough to reject \((a)\), \((b)\), and \((c)\).

    More intuitive memory trick:

    • Frenkel sounds like “free to move”: an ion shifts from its place to an interstitial site.
    • Schottky means sites are empty: ions are missing from the crystal.
    • F-centre means electron-filled vacancy.

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Thinking Frenkel defect occurs when cation and anion sizes are similar.
    2. Forgetting that Frenkel defect is a dislocation defect.
    3. Assuming Schottky defect has no effect on density.
    4. Confusing F-centre with missing electron instead of trapped electron.

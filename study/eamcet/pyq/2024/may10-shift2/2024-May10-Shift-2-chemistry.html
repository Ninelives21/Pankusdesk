    Solution:

    We need the number of lone pairs on the central atom in \(\mathrm{ClF_3,\ NF_3,\ SF_4,\ XeF_4}\), respectively.

    1. Count lone pairs on the central atom one by one

      A quick rule is:

      \(\text{Lone pairs on central atom} = \dfrac{\text{valence electrons of central atom} - \text{number of sigma bonds}}{2}\)

      Applying this:

      Compound Structure No. of lone pairs on central atom
      \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\) \(2\)
      \(\mathrm{NF_3}\) \(1\)
      \(\mathrm{SF_4}\) \(1\)
      \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\) \(2\)
    2. \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\)

      Chlorine has \(7\) valence electrons and forms \(3\) sigma bonds with fluorine.

      So:

      \(\dfrac{7-3}{2} = 2\) lone pairs

    3. \(\mathrm{NF_3}\)

      Nitrogen has \(5\) valence electrons and forms \(3\) sigma bonds.

      So:

      \(\dfrac{5-3}{2} = 1\) lone pair

    4. \(\mathrm{SF_4}\)

      Sulfur has \(6\) valence electrons and forms \(4\) sigma bonds.

      So:

      \(\dfrac{6-4}{2} = 1\) lone pair

    5. \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\)

      Xenon has \(8\) valence electrons and forms \(4\) sigma bonds.

      So:

      \(\dfrac{8-4}{2} = 2\) lone pairs

    6. Hence the required sequence is

      \(\mathrm{ClF_3,\ NF_3,\ SF_4,\ XeF_4 = 2,\ 1,\ 1,\ 2}\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ 2,1,1,2}\)

    Why this method works:

    • Lone pairs are the valence electron pairs on the central atom that are not used in bonding.
    • In VSEPR theory, both bond pairs and lone pairs are counted as electron domains.
    • Once lone pairs are known, shape prediction becomes much easier.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • \(\mathrm{NF_3}\) has \(1\) lone pair and is trigonal pyramidal.
    • \(\mathrm{SF_4}\) has \(1\) lone pair and is see-saw.
    • \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\) has \(2\) lone pairs and is T-shaped.
    • \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\) has \(2\) lone pairs and is square planar.
    • Molecules like \(\mathrm{ClF_3,\ SF_4,\ XeF_4}\) are classic examples of expanded octet.

    • Use directly:

      \(\mathrm{ClF_3}: \dfrac{7-3}{2}=2\)

      \(\mathrm{NF_3}: \dfrac{5-3}{2}=1\)

      \(\mathrm{SF_4}: \dfrac{6-4}{2}=1\)

    Answer: \((a)\ 12.5\%,\ 50\%\)

    Solution:

    Given crystal formula:

    \(AB_2O_4\)

    Oxygen atoms form a ccp lattice.

    1. First count the number of oxygen atoms in one ccp unit cell

      In a ccp arrangement, the effective number of atoms per unit cell is:

      \(4\)

      So, in one unit cell, the number of oxygen atoms is \(4\).

    2. Use the formula \(AB_2O_4\) to find how many \(A\) and \(B\) atoms are present

      The ratio is:

      \(A:B:O = 1:2:4\)

      Since there are \(4\) oxygen atoms in one unit cell, the corresponding numbers are:

      • \(A = 1\)
      • \(B = 2\)
      • \(O = 4\)
    3. Now count the voids in ccp

      If the number of close-packed particles is \(n\), then:

      • Number of tetrahedral voids \(= 2n\)
      • Number of octahedral voids \(= n\)

      Here \(n = 4\), so:

      • Number of tetrahedral voids \(= 8\)
      • Number of octahedral voids \(= 4\)
    4. Find \(x\%\): fraction of tetrahedral voids occupied by \(A\)

      Number of \(A\) atoms \(= 1\)

      Total tetrahedral voids \(= 8\)

      Therefore,

      \(x = \dfrac{1}{8}\times 100 = 12.5\%\)

    5. Find \(y\%\): fraction of octahedral voids occupied by \(B\)

      Number of \(B\) atoms \(= 2\)

      Total octahedral voids \(= 4\)

      Therefore,

      \(y = \dfrac{2}{4}\times 100 = 50\%\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{x = 12.5\%,\ y = 50\%}\)

    \(\boxed{(a)\ 12.5\%,\ 50\%}\)

    Why this method works:

    • In close packing questions, the whole idea is to connect:

      \(\text{number of host atoms} \rightarrow \text{number of voids} \rightarrow \text{fraction occupied}\)

    • Since oxygen forms ccp, oxygen is the host lattice.
    • Once the number of oxygen atoms per unit cell is known, the formula \(AB_2O_4\) immediately tells us how many \(A\) and \(B\) atoms must be present.
    • Then we compare those numbers with the available tetrahedral and octahedral voids.

      In fact, the \(\mathrm{C-O-H}\) angle in alcohol is close to \(108.9^\circ\), which is slightly less than tetrahedral.

    2. Bond angle in B: \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) in ether

      In ether \(\mathrm{CH_3-O-CH_3}\), oxygen again has two lone pairs, but now both sides contain alkyl groups.

      These two \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\) groups are bulkier than hydrogen and repel each other. This steric / bond-pair repulsion tends to open up the \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) angle.

      So in ethers, the \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) angle becomes slightly greater than the tetrahedral angle.

      Therefore for B:

      \(Y > 109^\circ 28'\)

      A typical \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) angle in ether is about \(111.7^\circ\).

    3. Hence

      \(X < 109^\circ 28'\) and \(Y > 109^\circ 28'\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ X < 109^\circ 28',\ Y > 109^\circ 28'}\)

    Why this method works:

    • Oxygen in alcohols and ethers generally has tetrahedral electron-pair arrangement.
    • But actual bond angles depend on the relative repulsions:

      \(\text{lone pair-lone pair} > \text{lone pair-bond pair} > \text{bond pair-bond pair}\)

    • In alcohol, lone pairs compress the \(\mathrm{C-O-H}\) angle.
    • In ether, the two bulky alkyl groups push apart and increase the \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) angle.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • The oxygen atom in alcohols and ethers is approximately \(\mathrm{sp^3}\)-hybridised.
    • Ideal tetrahedral angle:

      \(109^\circ 28'\)

    • Lone pairs compress bond angles because they occupy more space.
    • Bulky substituents can increase bond angle because of steric repulsion.
    • Therefore:

      \(\mathrm{C-O-H}\) in alcohol is usually slightly less than tetrahedral, while \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) in ether is usually slightly greater.

    Helpful comparison:

    • Alcohol: one alkyl group + one hydrogen

      \(\Rightarrow\) angle slightly compressed

    • Ether: two alkyl groups around oxygen

      \(\Rightarrow\) angle slightly expanded

    1-minute solving trick:

    • See oxygen with two lone pairs \(\Rightarrow\) start from tetrahedral \(109^\circ 28'\).
    • For \(\mathrm{C-O-H}\) in alcohol, lone-pair compression dominates \(\Rightarrow\) angle becomes less.
    • For \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\) in ether, two bulky carbon groups repel \(\Rightarrow\) angle becomes more.
    • So directly:

      \(X < 109^\circ 28',\quad Y > 109^\circ 28'\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Assuming every \(\mathrm{sp^3}\) centre has exactly \(109^\circ 28'\).

      That is only the ideal angle; lone pairs and steric effects modify it.

    2. Treating alcohol and ether oxygen as identical in bond angle behavior.
    3. Forgetting that hydrogen is much smaller than an alkyl group.
    4. Ignoring lone-pair repulsion while comparing bond angles.

    Oxygen in both alcohol and ether is approximately \(\mathrm{sp^3}\)-hybridised, so the ideal angle is \(109^\circ 28'\).

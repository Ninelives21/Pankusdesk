    • Correct answer: (c) 1 : 1
    • Explanation: We use Heisenberg uncertainty principle and the relation between uncertainty in momentum and uncertainty in velocity. After substitution, the numerical ratio of uncertainty in position to uncertainty in momentum becomes \(1:1\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Given:
        • \(\Delta v = \dfrac{1}{2m}\sqrt{\dfrac{h}{\pi}}\)
      • From Heisenberg uncertainty principle:
        • \(\Delta x \cdot \Delta p = \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\)
      • Also,
        • \(\Delta p = m\Delta v\)
      • So,
        • \(\dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta p} = \dfrac{h}{4\pi \Delta p^2}\)
      • Now substitute \(\Delta p = m\Delta v\):
        • \(\dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta p} = \dfrac{h}{4\pi m^2 (\Delta v)^2}\)
      • Given \(\Delta v = \dfrac{1}{2m}\sqrt{\dfrac{h}{\pi}}\), so
        • \((\Delta v)^2 = \dfrac{1}{4m^2}\dfrac{h}{\pi}\)
      • Substitute this:
        • \(\dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta p} = \dfrac{h}{4\pi m^2 \left(\dfrac{1}{4m^2}\dfrac{h}{\pi}\right)}\)
        • \(= \dfrac{h}{h} = 1\)
      • Therefore,
        • \(\Delta x : \Delta p = 1 : 1\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • This question is based on Heisenberg uncertainty principle.
      • The standard relation is:
        • \(\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\)
      • For such exam questions, we usually use the equality form.
      • Also remember:
        • \(p = mv\)
        • so uncertainty in momentum is \(\Delta p = m\Delta v\)
      • Once \(\Delta v\) is given, convert it into \(\Delta p\), then compare with \(\Delta x\).
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (c) matches the derived ratio exactly:
        • \(\Delta x : \Delta p = 1 : 1\)
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (a), (b), and (d) do not satisfy the uncertainty relation after proper substitution of the given \(\Delta v\).
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Whenever \(\Delta v\) is given, first convert it into \(\Delta p\) using:
        • \(\Delta p = m\Delta v\)
      • Then use:
        • \(\Delta x = \dfrac{h}{4\pi \Delta p}\)
      • That makes the ratio calculation much faster.
    • Final result:
      • The ratio of uncertainty in position and momentum is 1 : 1.
      • Hence, the correct option is (c).

    • Correct answer: (b) \(3d^5\ 4s^1;\ 3d^{10}\ 4s^1\)
    • Explanation: Chromium and copper are well-known exceptions to the usual Aufbau filling order. They show extra stability in half-filled and fully-filled \(d\)-subshells. So chromium has \(3d^5 4s^1\) and copper has \(3d^{10} 4s^1\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Expected configuration by simple Aufbau idea:
        • Cr should have been \(3d^4 4s^2\)
        • Cu should have been \(3d^9 4s^2\)
      • But actually, one electron from \(4s\) shifts into the \(3d\) subshell.
      • This happens because:
        • half-filled \(d^5\) configuration is especially stable
        • fully-filled \(d^{10}\) configuration is also especially stable
      • Therefore:
        • Cr \(= 3d^5 4s^1\)
        • Cu \(= 3d^{10} 4s^1\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Orbital filling generally follows Aufbau principle.
      • However, some atoms show exceptional stability when they achieve:
        • half-filled subshells
        • fully-filled subshells
      • The reasons usually quoted are:
        • better symmetry
        • greater exchange energy
        • extra stability of half-filled and fully-filled arrangements
      • Cr and Cu are the most frequently asked exceptions.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (b) gives the actual valence shell configurations of chromium and copper correctly.
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (a) gives the expected simple Aufbau configuration for Cr, but not the actual one.
      • (c) gives correct Cr but wrong Cu.
      • (d) gives wrong configurations for both Cr and Cu.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Memorize these two exceptions exactly:
        • Cr \(= 3d^5 4s^1\)
        • Cu \(= 3d^{10} 4s^1\)
      • These are among the most repeated electronic configuration questions in exams.
    • Final result:
      • The valence shell configurations are:
        • Cr \(= 3d^5 4s^1\)
        • Cu \(= 3d^{10} 4s^1\)
      • Hence, the correct option is (b).

    • Explanation: Due to poor shielding by \(4f\) electrons, the size of lanthanoids decreases steadily from La to Lu. This is called lanthanoid contraction. Because of this, covalent character increases, basic character of hydroxides decreases, reactivity decreases, and Zr and Hf end up having nearly the same radius. Separation is not easy, so statement (E) is false.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Lanthanoid contraction means:
        • gradual decrease in size of lanthanoid atoms and ions from La to Lu
      • The reason is poor shielding by \(4f\) electrons.
      • Now test each statement.
      • (A) Covalent properties of lanthanoid metal hydroxides increase from La to Lu.
        • As cation size decreases, polarising power increases.
        • Greater polarising power increases covalent character.
        • So (A) is correct.
      • (B) Chemical reactivity decreases from La to Lu.
        • Smaller size means electrons are held a little more strongly.
        • So reactivity generally decreases across the series.
        • Thus (B) is correct.
      • (C) \(\mathrm{La(OH)_3}\) is more basic than \(\mathrm{Lu(OH)_3}\).
        • As covalent character increases from La to Lu, basic character of hydroxides decreases.
        • So \(\mathrm{La(OH)_3}\) is indeed more basic than \(\mathrm{Lu(OH)_3}\).
        • Hence (C) is correct.
      • (D) Zr and Hf have about the same radius.
        • Normally size should increase down a group.
        • But due to lanthanoid contraction before Hf, its size becomes nearly equal to that of Zr.
        • So (D) is correct.
      • (E) Separation of lanthanoids from one another is easy.
        • This is false.
        • The lanthanoids have very similar sizes and very similar chemical properties.
        • That makes their separation difficult, not easy.
      • Therefore, the correct statements are A, B, C and D only.
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • The key cause of lanthanoid contraction is the weak shielding effect of \(4f\) electrons.
      • As effective nuclear charge increases across the series, the size decreases.
      • This contraction affects many properties:
        • size and basicity
        • covalent character
        • reactivity
        • similarity of Zr and Hf
        • difficulty in separation of lanthanoids
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (b) includes all the true statements:
        • A, B, C, and D
      • It correctly excludes E, which is false.
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (a) is wrong because E is false.
      • (c) is wrong because D is also true, so it is incomplete.
      • (d) is wrong because A is true but omitted.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • For lanthanoid contraction, remember this effect chain:
        • size decreases
        • polarising power increases
        • covalent character increases
        • basicity decreases
        • separation becomes difficult
      • Also memorize:
        • Zr and Hf have nearly same size
    • Final result:
      • The correct statements are A, B, C and D only.
      • Hence, the correct option is (b).

    • Correct answer: (a) O > Se > S
    • Explanation: Electron gain enthalpy is usually discussed by magnitude here. In group 16, sulphur has more negative electron gain enthalpy than oxygen because oxygen is very small and the incoming electron faces stronger repulsion in its compact orbital. Going down from sulphur to selenium, the value becomes less negative. Therefore, in the given style of comparison, the order is \(O > Se > S\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • First recall the general trend:
        • as we move down a group, electron gain enthalpy usually becomes less negative
      • So between sulphur and selenium:
        • \(S\) has more negative electron gain enthalpy than \(Se\)
      • Now compare oxygen and sulphur:
        • Normally one may expect oxygen to have more negative value because it is above sulphur.
        • But oxygen is exceptionally small.
        • The added electron enters a very compact \(2p\) orbital.
        • This causes strong electron-electron repulsion.
        • So oxygen has less negative electron gain enthalpy than sulphur.
      • The approximate values support this:
        • \(S \approx -208.8\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\)
        • \(Se \approx -195.5\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\)
        • \(O \approx -141.4\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\)
      • Thus, in increasing negativity:
        • \(O < Se < S\)
      • But the options are written in the convention used in the question key, so the correct marked order is:
        • \(O > Se > S\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Electron gain enthalpy is the enthalpy change when an isolated gaseous atom gains an electron.
      • More negative value means stronger tendency to accept an electron.
      • Oxygen behaves anomalously because of:
        • very small size
        • high electron-electron repulsion in compact \(2p\) orbital
      • So sulphur ends up having more negative electron gain enthalpy than oxygen.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Using the option convention given in the paper, option (a) matches the accepted answer key.
      • It reflects that oxygen has the least negative value, selenium is intermediate, and sulphur has the most negative value among the three.
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (b), (c), and (d) do not correctly capture both the oxygen anomaly and the trend from S to Se as presented in the key.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Memorize this exception:
        • \(S\) has more negative electron gain enthalpy than \(O\)
      • Also remember:
        • down the group from \(S\) to \(Se\), electron gain enthalpy becomes less negative
      • That immediately fixes the relative positions of all three elements.
    • Final result:
      • The correct order as asked in the paper is O > Se > S.
      • Hence, the correct option is (a).

    • Correct answer: (b) \(A = O_2^+,\ B = O_2^{2-}\)
    • Explanation: This is a molecular orbital theory question. We must compare the bond orders of the given oxygen species and also identify which one is diamagnetic. Among them, \(O_2^+\) has the highest bond order, while \(O_2^{2-}\) is diamagnetic.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Let us start from \(\mathrm{O_2}\).
      • For \(\mathrm{O_2}\), the molecular orbital configuration in the valence region gives:
        • bond order \(= 2\)
        • two unpaired electrons
        • so \(\mathrm{O_2}\) is paramagnetic
      • Now for \(\mathrm{O_2^+}\):
        • one electron is removed from an antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbital
        • removing an antibonding electron increases bond order by \(0.5\)
        • so bond order becomes \(2.5\)
        • it still has one unpaired electron, so it is paramagnetic
      • For \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\):
        • one extra electron enters an antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbital
        • this decreases bond order by \(0.5\)
        • so bond order becomes \(1.5\)
        • it remains paramagnetic
      • For \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\):
        • two extra electrons enter antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals
        • bond order becomes \(1\)
        • all electrons become paired
        • so \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic
      • Thus:
        • highest bond order \(= \mathrm{O_2^+}\)
        • diamagnetic species \(= \mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Bond order is given by:
        • \(\text{Bond order} = \dfrac{N_b - N_a}{2}\)
      • Here, \(N_b\) is the number of bonding electrons and \(N_a\) is the number of antibonding electrons.
      • Very important idea:
        • removing electrons from antibonding orbitals increases bond order
        • adding electrons to antibonding orbitals decreases bond order
      • Magnetic nature depends on whether unpaired electrons are present or not.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • \(\mathrm{O_2^+}\) has bond order \(2.5\), which is the highest among the given species.
      • \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) has all electrons paired, so it is diamagnetic.
      • That matches option (b).
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (a) is wrong because \(\mathrm{O_2}\) does not have the highest bond order, and \(\mathrm{O_2^+}\) is not diamagnetic.
      • (c) is wrong because \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) does not have the highest bond order, and \(\mathrm{O_2}\) is paramagnetic.
      • (d) is wrong because \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) has the lowest bond order among these, not the highest, and \(\mathrm{O_2^+}\) is paramagnetic.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Memorize this order directly for oxygen species:
        • \(\mathrm{O_2^+}: 2.5\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_2}: 2\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_2^-}: 1.5\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}: 1\)
      • Also remember:
        • only \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic among these four
    • Final result:
      • The species with highest bond order is \(\mathrm{O_2^+}\).
      • The diamagnetic species is \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (b).

    • Correct answer: (a) \(a : 0,\ b : 0,\ c : -1\)
    • Explanation: In the correct Lewis structure of carbonate ion, carbon is the central atom. One oxygen is double-bonded and two oxygens are single-bonded in any one resonance form. Using the formal charge formula on the marked atoms gives \(a = 0,\ b = 0,\ c = -1\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • First recall the formal charge formula:
        • \(\text{Formal charge} = \text{valence electrons} - \text{lone pair electrons} - \dfrac{1}{2}(\text{bond pair electrons})\)
      • In the shown resonance form of \(\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}\):
        • \(a\) is the central carbon atom
        • \(b\) is the double-bonded oxygen
        • \(c\) is a single-bonded oxygen
      • Now calculate each formal charge.
      • For atom \(a\) (carbon):
        • valence electrons \(= 4\)
        • lone pair electrons \(= 0\)
        • bond pair electrons around carbon \(= 8\)
        • \(\text{FC} = 4 - 0 - \dfrac{1}{2}(8) = 4 - 4 = 0\)
      • For atom \(b\) (double-bonded oxygen):
        • valence electrons \(= 6\)
        • lone pair electrons \(= 4\)
        • bond pair electrons \(= 4\)
        • \(\text{FC} = 6 - 4 - \dfrac{1}{2}(4) = 6 - 4 - 2 = 0\)
      • For atom \(c\) (single-bonded oxygen):
        • valence electrons \(= 6\)
        • lone pair electrons \(= 6\)
        • bond pair electrons \(= 2\)
        • \(\text{FC} = 6 - 6 - \dfrac{1}{2}(2) = 6 - 6 - 1 = -1\)
      • So the formal charges are:
        • \(a = 0\)
        • \(b = 0\)
        • \(c = -1\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Formal charge is a bookkeeping device used in Lewis structures.
      • For carbonate ion:
        • there are three equivalent resonance structures
        • in each one, one oxygen is double-bonded and two are single-bonded
      • The actual structure is a resonance hybrid, but formal charge is calculated using one valid Lewis structure at a time.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (a) correctly gives the calculated formal charges for the marked atoms in the shown Lewis form.
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (b) wrongly gives \(-2\) on oxygen \(b\), which is impossible for the double-bonded oxygen in this structure.
      • (c) wrongly gives \(-2\) on carbon, but carbon here has formal charge \(0\).
      • (d) wrongly gives \(-1\) on both \(b\) and \(c\), whereas only the single-bonded oxygen has \(-1\) in this form.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • In carbonate ion, remember one standard resonance form:
        • carbon \(= 0\)
        • double-bonded oxygen \(= 0\)
        • single-bonded oxygen \(= -1\)
      • This helps solve such questions almost instantly.
    • Final result:
      • The formal charges are \(a : 0,\ b : 0,\ c : -1\).
      • Hence, the correct option is (a).

    • Explanation: For real gases, the compressibility factor \(z\) becomes less than 1 when attractive forces dominate. \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) is more easily liquefiable and has stronger intermolecular attractions than gases like \(\mathrm{N_2}\), but weaker than \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).     • Step-by-step explanation:
      • The compressibility factor is defined as:
        • \(z = \dfrac{pV}{nRT}\)
      • For an ideal gas:
        • \(z = 1\)
      • For real gases:
        • \(z < 1\) means attractive forces are dominant
        • \(z > 1\) means repulsive forces or finite molecular volume effect is dominant
      • Now compare the gases qualitatively.
      • \(\mathrm{H_2}\) and He:
        • they show very weak attractions
        • their curves do not dip much below 1
      • \(\mathrm{CO_2}\):
        • it is a larger molecule
        • it has stronger van der Waals attractions than \(\mathrm{N_2}\)
        • so its \(z\) value dips more below 1
      • A stronger dip means stronger attractive forces at moderate pressures.
      • In the graph, curve D shows the lowest dip among the labeled curves \(A, B, C,\) and \(D\).
      • Therefore, curve D represents \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Compressibility factor measures deviation from ideal behaviour.
      • Real gases deviate because of:
        • intermolecular attractions
        • finite molecular size
      • At low to moderate pressure, attraction often dominates, causing \(z < 1\).
      • Gases with stronger attractions show a greater negative deviation.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Among the curves shown, D has the deepest dip below \(z=1\), indicating stronger attractive forces.
      • This matches \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) in the given comparison.
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • A, B, and C show smaller deviations than D.
      • They correspond to gases with weaker attractive interactions compared with \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) in this graph-based comparison.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • In \(z\) vs \(p\) graphs:
        • deeper dip below 1 \(\rightarrow\) stronger attraction \(\rightarrow\) more easily liquefiable gas
      • So identify the gas with stronger intermolecular attraction and match it to the curve with the larger downward deviation.
    • Final result:
      • The plot for \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) gas is D.
      • Hence, the correct option is (d).

    • Correct answer: (d) 28.8
    • Explanation: The average molecular weight of a gas mixture is found by taking the weighted average of the molecular masses using their percentage composition. Here, air contains \(79\%\ \mathrm{N_2}\) and \(21\%\ \mathrm{O_2}\), so the average comes out to \(28.84\), which is approximately \(28.8\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Given:
        • \(\mathrm{N_2} = 79\%\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_2} = 21\%\)
      • Molecular masses:
        • \(\mathrm{N_2} = 28\)
        • \(\mathrm{O_2} = 32\)
      • Average molecular weight:
        • \(\overline{M} = \dfrac{79 \times 28 + 21 \times 32}{100}\)
      • Now calculate:
        • \(79 \times 28 = 2212\)
        • \(21 \times 32 = 672\)
        • Total \(= 2212 + 672 = 2884\)
      • Therefore,
        • \(\overline{M} = \dfrac{2884}{100} = 28.84\)
      • So the nearest option is:
        • \(28.8\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • For a mixture of gases, average molar mass is the weighted mean of molar masses.
      • If percentages are given directly, use:
        • \(\overline{M} = \dfrac{\sum (\%\ \text{of gas}) \times (\text{molar mass})}{100}\)
      • This works because the percentages act like mole fractions here in the standard exam treatment.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (d) matches the calculated value \(28.84 \approx 28.8\).
    • Why other options are wrong:
      • (a) 44.8 is far too high for a mixture mainly made of \(\mathrm{N_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\).
      • (b) 100 is impossible here.
      • (c) 14.4 is too low and is about half the expected value.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Since air is mostly \(\mathrm{N_2}\) with some \(\mathrm{O_2}\), the answer must lie between 28 and 32, and closer to 28.
      • That alone quickly rules out 44.8, 100, and 14.4 even before full calculation.
    • Final result:
      • The average molecular weight of air is 28.8.
      • Hence, the correct option is (d).

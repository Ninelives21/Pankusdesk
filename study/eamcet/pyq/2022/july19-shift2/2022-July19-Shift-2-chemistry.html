    • Correct answer: (c) \(\dfrac{9}{4} r_2, \dfrac{4}{9} E_2\)
    • Explanation:
      • For hydrogen atom, Bohr radius varies as \(n^2\) and energy varies as \(\dfrac{1}{n^2}\). So when we go from second orbit to third orbit, radius increases by the factor \(\dfrac{3^2}{2^2} = \dfrac{9}{4}\), while energy becomes \(\dfrac{2^2}{3^2} = \dfrac{4}{9}\) times the previous value.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • For hydrogen-like species, radius of the \(n\)-th orbit is:
        • \(r_n \propto \dfrac{n^2}{Z}\)
      • For hydrogen, \(Z = 1\), so:
        • \(r_n \propto n^2\)
      • Therefore,
        • \(\dfrac{r_3}{r_2} = \dfrac{3^2}{2^2} = \dfrac{9}{4}\)
        • \(r_3 = \dfrac{9}{4} r_2\)
      • Now use the energy relation:
        • \(E_n \propto \dfrac{1}{n^2}\)
      • So,
        • \(\dfrac{E_3}{E_2} = \dfrac{1/3^2}{1/2^2} = \dfrac{4}{9}\)
        • \(E_3 = \dfrac{4}{9} E_2\)
      • Hence, third orbit has:
        • radius \(= \dfrac{9}{4} r_2\)
        • energy \(= \dfrac{4}{9} E_2\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • According to Bohr model for hydrogen-like atoms:
        • \(r_n = a_0 \dfrac{n^2}{Z}\)
        • \(E_n = -13.6 \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \, \text{eV}\)
      • This means radius increases with \(n^2\), but energy magnitude decreases with \(n^2\).
      • For this type of exam question, the sign of energy is usually ignored in the option pattern, and only proportional value is compared.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (c) matches both correct proportional changes:
        • \(r_3 = \dfrac{9}{4} r_2\)
        • \(E_3 = \dfrac{4}{9} E_2\)
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Options (a) and (b) wrongly make the third orbit radius smaller than the second orbit.
      • Option (d) wrongly makes energy increase with orbit number, which is opposite to Bohr theory.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Remember this very fast pair:
        • \(r_n \propto n^2\)
        • \(E_n \propto \dfrac{1}{n^2}\)
      • So bigger orbit number means bigger radius but smaller energy value in magnitude comparison questions.
    • Final result:
      • The radius and energy of the third Bohr orbit are \(\dfrac{9}{4} r_2\) and \(\dfrac{4}{9} E_2\), respectively.

    • Explanation:
      • \(Na^+\), \(Mg^{2+}\), and \(Al^{3+}\) are isoelectronic species. In an isoelectronic series, ionic radius decreases as nuclear charge increases. So among these three, \(Na^+\) is largest and \(Al^{3+}\) is smallest. Also, \(K^+\) has one more electron shell than them, so it is أكبر in size than all of them.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • First identify the electronic configurations:
        • \(Na^+\): 10 electrons
        • \(Mg^{2+}\): 10 electrons
        • \(Al^{3+}\): 10 electrons
      • So these three are isoelectronic.
      • In an isoelectronic series, the species with higher nuclear charge pulls the same electron cloud more strongly, so its radius becomes smaller.
      • Therefore:
        • \(Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}\)
      • Now compare \(K^+\) with them.
      • \(K^+\) has electron configuration of argon, so it has more shells than \(Na^+\), \(Mg^{2+}\), and \(Al^{3+}\).
      • Hence \(K^+\) is larger than all of them.
      • So the final correct order is:
        • \(K^+ > Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Ionic radius depends mainly on:
        • number of electron shells
        • effective nuclear charge
      • For isoelectronic species, the number of electrons is the same, so the deciding factor becomes nuclear charge.
      • Higher positive charge on the nucleus means stronger pull and smaller ionic size.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (b) correctly places \(K^+\) as the largest and correctly orders the isoelectronic species as:
        • \(Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}\)
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option (a) is wrong because \(K^+\) cannot be the smallest here.
      • Option (c) is wrong because among the isoelectronic trio, \(Mg^{2+}\) must be larger than \(Al^{3+}\).
      • Option (d) completely reverses the correct trend.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • For isoelectronic species, use this rule:
        • more protons \(\Rightarrow\) smaller size
      • So for 10-electron species:
        • \(Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}\)
      • Then place \(K^+\) before them because it has an extra shell.
    • Final result:
      • The correct order of ionic radii is \(K^+ > Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}\).

    • Correct answer: (b) Metallic character and atomic radii
    • Explanation:
      • Across a period, effective nuclear charge increases. Because of this, electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus, so atomic radius decreases. As atoms become less willing to lose electrons, metallic character also decreases. Therefore, both metallic character and atomic radii generally decrease along a period.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • When we move from left to right across a period, protons are added to the nucleus.
      • But the added electrons enter the same main shell, so shielding does not increase very much.
      • This makes the effective nuclear charge increase.
      • As a result:
        • atomic radius decreases because electrons are pulled inward
        • metallic character decreases because the tendency to lose electrons becomes smaller
      • So the pair of properties that generally decreases is:
        • metallic character and atomic radii
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Periodic trends across a period usually follow increasing effective nuclear charge.
      • Because of this:
        • atomic radius decreases
        • metallic character decreases
        • electronegativity generally increases
        • electron affinity generally becomes more negative
        • ionisation enthalpy generally increases
      • This question is a classic trend-recognition problem from periodicity.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (b) is correct because both metallic character and atomic radius move downward in value as we go from left to right in a period.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option (a) is wrong because ionisation energy generally increases across a period, while atomic radius decreases.
      • Option (c) is wrong because electronegativity generally increases, not decreases.
      • Option (d) is not a generally valid pair for steady decrease across a period.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Across a period, remember this quick summary:
        • size decreases
        • metallic nature decreases
        • non-metallic nature increases
      • That immediately points to option (b).
    • Final result:
      • The set of properties that generally decreases along a period is metallic character and atomic radii.

    • Correct answer: (d) \(BeCl_2 > BF_3 > SiCl_4 > SF_6\)
    • Explanation:
      • This is a direct geometry-and-bond-angle question. We first identify the shape of each molecule, then write its standard bond angle, and finally arrange them in decreasing order.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • \(BeCl_2\)
        • \(Be\) has two bond pairs and no lone pair around the central atom.
        • Its geometry is linear.
        • So bond angle \(= 180^\circ\).
      • \(BF_3\)
        • \(B\) has three bond pairs and no lone pair.
        • Its geometry is trigonal planar.
        • So bond angle \(= 120^\circ\).
      • \(SiCl_4\)
        • \(Si\) has four bond pairs and no lone pair.
        • Its geometry is tetrahedral.
        • So bond angle \(= 109.5^\circ\).
      • \(SF_6\)
        • \(S\) has six bond pairs and no lone pair.
        • Its geometry is octahedral.
        • The standard adjacent bond angle is \(90^\circ\).
      • Now arrange the angles in decreasing order:
        • \(180^\circ > 120^\circ > 109.5^\circ > 90^\circ\)
      • So:
        • \(BeCl_2 > BF_3 > SiCl_4 > SF_6\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • This question is based on VSEPR theory.
      • When there are only bond pairs and no lone pairs on the central atom, the ideal molecular shape gives the standard bond angle directly.
      • Important geometries to remember:
        • linear \(\rightarrow 180^\circ\)
        • trigonal planar \(\rightarrow 120^\circ\)
        • tetrahedral \(\rightarrow 109.5^\circ\)
        • octahedral \(\rightarrow 90^\circ\)
      • These are among the most commonly asked memory-based geometry facts in entrance exams.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (d) is correct because it follows the exact decreasing order of standard bond angles:
        • \(BeCl_2 (180^\circ)\)
        • \(BF_3 (120^\circ)\)
        • \(SiCl_4 (109.5^\circ)\)
        • \(SF_6 (90^\circ)\)
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option (a) is wrong because \(BeCl_2\) must have the largest angle, not \(BF_3\).
      • Option (b) is wrong because \(SF_6\) cannot have larger bond angle than \(SiCl_4\) or \(BF_3\).
      • Option (c) is wrong because tetrahedral angle \(109.5^\circ\) is smaller than trigonal planar angle \(120^\circ\).
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Memorize this quick order of ideal bond angles:
        • linear \(> \) trigonal planar \(> \) tetrahedral \(> \) octahedral
      • In degree values:
        • \(180 > 120 > 109.5 > 90\)
      • That directly solves this question in one step.
    • Final result:
      • The correct decreasing order of bond angles is \(BeCl_2 > BF_3 > SiCl_4 > SF_6\).

    • Correct answer: (b) \(Li_2^{2-}\), \(Ne_2\), \(He_2\) only
    • Explanation:
      • By molecular orbital theory, a species exists only if its bond order is greater than zero. So we calculate bond order for each species. The ones with bond order zero do not exist.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • The bond order formula is:
        • \(\text{Bond order} = \dfrac{N_b - N_a}{2}\)
      • Here \(N_b\) is the number of bonding electrons and \(N_a\) is the number of antibonding electrons.
      • \(H_2^+\)
        • Configuration: \(\sigma_{1s}^1\)
        • Bond order \(= \dfrac{1 - 0}{2} = 0.5\)
        • So it exists.
      • \(He_2^{2+}\)
        • Total electrons = 2
        • Configuration: \(\sigma_{1s}^2\)
        • Bond order \(= \dfrac{2 - 0}{2} = 1\)
        • So it exists.
      • \(Li_2^{2-}\)
        • Total electrons = 8
        • Configuration effectively fills both bonding and antibonding 2s orbitals equally.
        • Bond order \(= \dfrac{4 - 4}{2} = 0\)
        • So it does not exist.
      • \(Ne_2\)
        • All bonding and antibonding molecular orbitals are fully balanced.
        • Bond order \(= 0\)
        • So it does not exist.
      • \(Be_2^-\)
        • Extra electron gives a positive bond order.
        • Bond order \(= 0.5\)
        • So it exists.
      • \(He_2\)
        • Configuration: \(\sigma_{1s}^2 \sigma_{1s}^{*2}\)
        • Bond order \(= \dfrac{2 - 2}{2} = 0\)
        • So it does not exist.
      • Hence the species that do not exist are:
        • \(Li_2^{2-}\), \(Ne_2\), and \(He_2\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Molecular orbital theory says stability depends on net bonding.
      • If bond order is:
        • \(> 0\), the species can exist
        • \(= 0\), the species does not exist
      • This is one of the most tested shortcuts in diatomic species problems.
      • Even species that are unusual, like ions, can exist if bond order is positive.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (b) is correct because only \(Li_2^{2-}\), \(Ne_2\), and \(He_2\) have bond order zero.
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option (a) is incomplete because it misses \(Li_2^{2-}\).
      • Option (c) is wrong because \(Be_2^-\) has bond order \(0.5\), so it can exist.
      • Option (d) is wrong because \(H_2^+\) has bond order \(0.5\), so it exists.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Never guess existence from “common sense.” Always calculate bond order.
      • Two famous non-existing species to remember are:
        • \(He_2\)
        • \(Ne_2\)
      • Also remember that \(H_2^+\) does exist because its bond order is \(0.5\).
    • Final result:
      • The species that do not exist are \(Li_2^{2-}\), \(Ne_2\), and \(He_2\).

    • Correct answer: (d) \(1 + \dfrac{Pb}{RT}\)
    • Explanation:
      • At high pressure, volume correction becomes more important than attractive-force correction in the van der Waals equation. Under that condition, the real gas compressibility factor becomes \(Z = 1 + \dfrac{Pb}{RT}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Start with van der Waals equation for 1 mole:
        • \(\left(P + \dfrac{a}{V^2}\right)(V - b) = RT\)
      • At high pressure, gas is highly compressed.
      • In this situation, the volume correction term \(b\) becomes very significant.
      • Also, compared to very large pressure, the attraction correction \(\dfrac{a}{V^2}\) is often neglected in the standard exam treatment.
      • So the equation becomes:
        • \(P(V - b) = RT\)
      • Expand:
        • \(PV - Pb = RT\)
      • Rearrange:
        • \(PV = RT + Pb\)
      • Now compressibility factor is:
        • \(Z = \dfrac{PV}{RT}\)
      • Therefore:
        • \(Z = \dfrac{RT + Pb}{RT} = 1 + \dfrac{Pb}{RT}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Compressibility factor is defined as:
        • \(Z = \dfrac{PV}{RT}\)
      • For an ideal gas, \(Z = 1\).
      • For real gases, deviations occur because of:
        • intermolecular attractions, represented by \(a\)
        • finite molecular size, represented by \(b\)
      • At high pressure, the excluded volume effect dominates, making \(Z\) greater than 1.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (d) is correct because the high-pressure form of the van der Waals equation leads directly to:
        • \(Z = 1 + \dfrac{Pb}{RT}\)
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • Option (a) is only for an ideal gas.
      • Options (b) and (c) have the wrong dependence on pressure and \(b\).
      • At high pressure, \(Z\) is generally greater than 1, which matches option (d), not option (c).
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Remember:
        • high pressure \(\Rightarrow\) excluded volume effect dominates
        • \(Z > 1\)
      • So among the options, choose the one greater than 1 in the physically meaningful form, which is:
        • \(1 + \dfrac{Pb}{RT}\)
    • Final result:
      • The compressibility factor of a real gas at high pressure is \(1 + \dfrac{Pb}{RT}\).

Since \(NH_3\) and \(CO_2\) have stronger attractions than \(N_2\), their \(a\) values are larger, and hence their \(Z\) values are lower.
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Compressibility factor is:
        • \(Z = \dfrac{PV}{RT}\)
      • If \(Z < 1\), it means the gas is more compressible than an ideal gas.
      • This usually happens when attractive forces between molecules are significant.
      • Now recall the meaning of van der Waals constant \(a\):
        • \(a\) measures the strength of intermolecular attractions.
      • \(NH_3\) has strong intermolecular attraction because it can form hydrogen bonding.
      • \(CO_2\) also has stronger attractive forces than \(N_2\), mainly due to higher polarizability and stronger van der Waals interactions.
      • \(N_2\) is comparatively less attractive in intermolecular interaction.
      • Therefore:
        • \(a(NH_3) > a(N_2)\)
        • \(a(CO_2) > a(N_2)\)
      • Hence \(NH_3\) and \(CO_2\) have lower \(Z\) values than \(N_2\).
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Real gas deviation depends on two opposite effects:
        • attractive forces decrease pressure and tend to make \(Z < 1\)
        • finite molecular size increases effective volume and tends to make \(Z > 1\)
      • The constant \(a\) in van der Waals equation is the attraction term.
      • The constant \(b\) is the excluded volume term.
      • So if a gas has stronger attractions, its \(a\) value is larger and it is more likely to show \(Z < 1\) under suitable conditions.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (a) is correct because both \(NH_3\) and \(CO_2\) have stronger intermolecular attractions than \(N_2\), so both have larger \(a\) values.

      • Whenever \(Z\) is lower, think:
        • stronger attraction
        • larger \(a\)
      • Also remember:
        • \(NH_3\) has hydrogen bonding
        • \(CO_2\) is more polarizable than \(N_2\)
      • So both must have larger \(a\) than \(N_2\).
    • Final result:
      • The compressibility factor is lower for \(NH_3\) and \(CO_2\) than for \(N_2\) because their van der Waals constant \(a\) values are greater than that of \(N_2\).

    • Correct answer: (d) \(C_4H_{10}\)
    • Explanation:
      • For gases measured under the same conditions of temperature and pressure, volume is proportional to the number of moles. So the volume ratio directly gives the mole ratio in the combustion equation. From the given data, the hydrocarbon produces 4 molecules of \(CO_2\) and 5 molecules of \(H_2O\), which leads to the formula \(C_4H_{10}\).
    • Step-by-step explanation:
      • Let the hydrocarbon be \(C_aH_b\).
      • Its combustion equation is:
        • \(C_aH_b + \left(a + \dfrac{b}{4}\right) O_2 \rightarrow aCO_2 + \dfrac{b}{2}H_2O\)
      • Given:
        • 10 mL hydrocarbon produces 40 mL \(CO_2\)
        • 10 mL hydrocarbon produces 50 mL water vapour
      • Since gas volumes are proportional to moles, we get:
        • \(1 : 4\) ratio for hydrocarbon : \(CO_2\)
        • \(1 : 5\) ratio for hydrocarbon : \(H_2O\)
      • Now compare with the general equation:
        • 1 mole \(C_aH_b\) gives \(a\) moles of \(CO_2\)
        • 1 mole \(C_aH_b\) gives \(\dfrac{b}{2}\) moles of \(H_2O\)
      • From \(CO_2\) ratio:
        • \(a = 4\)
      • From water vapour ratio:
        • \(\dfrac{b}{2} = 5\)
        • \(b = 10\)
      • Therefore the hydrocarbon is:
        • \(C_4H_{10}\)
    • Concept used / theory behind it:
      • Avogadro’s law says that equal volumes of gases under the same conditions contain equal numbers of molecules.
      • So in gaseous-reaction stoichiometry, volume ratios can be treated exactly like mole ratios.
      • For hydrocarbon combustion:
        • number of carbon atoms = volume ratio of hydrocarbon to \(CO_2\)
        • number of hydrogen atoms = 2 \(\times\) volume ratio of hydrocarbon to \(H_2O\)
      • This is one of the quickest ways to solve gaseous hydrocarbon-combustion questions.
    • Why the correct option is right:
      • Option (d) is correct because the data give:
        • 4 carbon atoms from 40 mL \(CO_2\)
        • 10 hydrogen atoms from 50 mL \(H_2O\)
      • So the formula must be \(C_4H_{10}\).
    • Why other options are wrong or less suitable:
      • \(C_4H_6\) and \(C_4H_8\) have too few hydrogen atoms.
      • \(C_3H_4\) has the wrong number of carbon atoms as well as hydrogen atoms.
    • Exam tip / shortcut / elimination clue:
      • Use this very fast rule for gaseous hydrocarbon combustion:
        • \(C_xH_y \rightarrow xCO_2 + \dfrac{y}{2}H_2O\)
      • So directly:
        • 10 mL hydrocarbon \(\rightarrow\) 40 mL \(CO_2\) means \(x = 4\)
        • 10 mL hydrocarbon \(\rightarrow\) 50 mL \(H_2O\) means \(\dfrac{y}{2} = 5\), so \(y = 10\)
      • No long calculation is needed.
    • Final result:
      • The molecular formula of the hydrocarbon is \(C_4H_{10}\).

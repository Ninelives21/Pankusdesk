Environmental Chemistry

    Atmospheric pollution and pollutant classification
  • Pollutant-effect map
    • Environmental Chemistry questions often ask for the most characteristic effect of each pollutant.
    • \(\mathrm{CO}\) is mainly a blood poison.
    • \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) is a lung irritant.
    • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is linked with classical smog and plant injury.
    • \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) and \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) are mainly greenhouse gases.
  • \(\mathrm{NO_2}\): respiratory effect
    • \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) is a strong lung irritant and can lead to acute respiratory disease in children.
    • In moist respiratory passages it may form acidic species such as \(\mathrm{HNO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\).
    • So “acute respiratory disease in children” points to \(\mathrm{NO_2}\).
  • \(\mathrm{CO}\): blood-poison effect
    • \(\mathrm{CO}\) is highly toxic, but its main action is formation of carboxyhaemoglobin.
    • Carboxyhaemoglobin reduces oxygen transport in blood.
    • Do not select \(\mathrm{CO}\) for a question specifically asking for a lung irritant.
  • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\): plant injury
    • Higher concentration of \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) in air damages vegetation and can cause stiffness of flower buds.
    • This plant-injury fact is often tested along with smog and acid-rain concepts.

    Smog: classical vs photochemical
  • Classical smog
    • Classical smog is reducing smog.
    • It is mainly associated with smoke, fog and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).
    • It is typically connected with cool, humid conditions and sulphur pollution.
  • Photochemical smog
    • Photochemical smog is oxidising smog.
    • It forms when sunlight acts on nitrogen oxides and hydrocarbons, producing oxidising and irritating pollutants.
    • Common components include \(\mathrm{O_3}\), \(\mathrm{NO}\), \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), PAN, aldehydes, formaldehyde/methanal and acrolein.
    • The standard component set in PYQs is \(\mathrm{O_3,\ NO,\ PAN}\).
  • Photochemical smog formation idea
    • \(\mathrm{NO_2 \xrightarrow{sunlight} NO + O}\), followed by \(\mathrm{O + O_2 \rightarrow O_3}\).
    • This explains why ozone is a key oxidising component of photochemical smog.
  • Odd-one-out and component traps
    • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is not a common component of photochemical smog; it belongs mainly with classical smog.
    • If \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) appears as the odd one out in a photochemical-smog question, it is usually the answer.
  • Carbon-containing smog components
    • Carbon-containing components of photochemical smog include acrolein, methanal and PAN.
    • Acrolein is acrylic aldehyde, \(\mathrm{CH_2=CH-CHO}\), so it must contain both a double bond and an aldehyde group.
    • Do not confuse acrolein with acrylonitrile \(\mathrm{CH_2=CH-CN}\).
    • Do not confuse acrolein with PAN-type structures such as \(\mathrm{CH_3CO(O)NO_2}\).
    • For acrolein, remember \(\mathrm{CH_2=CH-CHO}\).

    Greenhouse effect and global warming
  • Greenhouse-gas definition and counted examples
    • Greenhouse gases absorb infrared radiation and contribute to atmospheric warming.
    • In the PYQ list, \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O(g)}\) are counted as greenhouse gases.
  • Not counted in the given greenhouse list
    • \(\mathrm{H_2O(l)}\), \(\mathrm{O_2}\) and \(\mathrm{NO}\) should not be counted as greenhouse gases in the given list.
    • The count in that PYQ is \(3\), not \(5\).
  • Greenhouse effect vs ozone depletion
    • Methane, \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), is mainly remembered as a greenhouse gas.
    • It is not a primary ozone-depleting substance in the standard stratospheric ozone-depletion question.
    • Keep greenhouse effect and ozone depletion separate: \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), and water vapour are greenhouse-effect keywords.
    • CFCs, chlorine radicals and nitrogen oxides are ozone-depletion keywords.

    Acid rain and normal rain-water pH
  • Why normal rain water is acidic
    • Normal rain water is slightly acidic because atmospheric \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) dissolves in water to form weak carbonic acid.
    • \(\mathrm{CO_2 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons H_2CO_3}\).
  • pH value and acid-rain clue
    • The pH of normal rain water is about \(5.6\), not \(7\) or \(7.5\).
    • Values much below this are associated with acid rain.
  • Acid-rain gases and trap
    • Oxides of sulphur and nitrogen are important in acid-rain chemistry because they can form strong acids in the atmosphere.
    • In statement questions, do not confuse normal rain-water pH with neutral pure-water pH.

    Stratospheric ozone depletion
  • Ozone layer and causes of depletion
    • The ozone layer is present in the stratosphere and absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation.
    • Ozone depletion is mainly caused by reactive species such as chlorine radicals and nitrogen oxides.
  • CFC role
    • CFCs such as \(\mathrm{CF_2Cl_2}\) are important ozone-depleting substances.
    • They are stable in the lower atmosphere but release chlorine radicals in the stratosphere under UV light.
  • Chlorine radical catalytic cycle
    • \(\mathrm{Cl\cdot + O_3 \rightarrow ClO\cdot + O_2}\).
    • \(\mathrm{ClO\cdot + O \rightarrow Cl\cdot + O_2}\).
    • Net effect: \(\mathrm{O_3 + O \rightarrow 2O_2}\).
  • Nitric oxide catalytic cycle
    • Nitric oxide can also catalytically destroy ozone: \(\mathrm{NO + O_3 \rightarrow NO_2 + O_2}\).
    • \(\mathrm{NO_2 + O \rightarrow NO + O_2}\).
    • Again, the net effect is conversion of ozone to oxygen.
  • Odd-one-out ozone-depletion trap
    • In “not primarily responsible for ozone depletion” questions, \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) is the odd one out when compared with \(\mathrm{NO}\), \(\mathrm{CF_2Cl_2}\) and chlorine species.

    Water pollution and drinking-water standards
  • Drinking-water standard values
    • Drinking-water standard questions are often direct memory checks.
    • The maximum prescribed concentration of copper is \(1\,\mathrm{mg\,dm^{-3}}\).
    • The maximum prescribed concentration of zinc is \(15\,\mathrm{mg\,dm^{-3}}\).
    • For copper and zinc, remember the pair \(\mathrm{Cu}=1\), \(\mathrm{Zn}=15\).
    • If copper is represented as \(x\) in a symbolic option, match zinc with \(\dfrac{15}{x}\) as given in the PYQ format.
  • Nitrate contamination
    • Excess nitrate in drinking water can cause methemoglobinemia, especially in infants.
    • It interferes with oxygen transport.
    • This should not be confused with fluoride, sulphate or lead contamination effects.
  • Eutrophication
    • Eutrophication occurs when nutrient enrichment causes excessive algal growth.
    • Decomposition of algal biomass consumes dissolved oxygen.
    • The final effect is a decrease in dissolved oxygen and harm to aquatic life.
  • Water-softening method comparison
    • For water softening, ion-exchange process is the most effective method among lime-soda, permutit, ion-exchange and boiling/filtration.
    • Boiling removes only temporary hardness.
    • Ion exchange gives more complete removal of hardness-causing ions.

    Soil pollution, pesticides and industrial wastes
  • DDT identity and chlorine count
    • DDT is dichloro diphenyl trichloroethane, an organochlorine pesticide.
    • Its name itself gives the chlorine count: “dichloro” gives \(2\) chlorine atoms on the phenyl rings.
    • “Trichloroethane” gives \(3\) chlorine atoms on the ethane part.
    • Total chlorine atoms in DDT \(=2+3=5\).
    • Do not count only the “dichloro” part and ignore the “trichloroethane” part.
  • Industrial non-biodegradable waste
    • Thermal power plants generate fly ash and related solid residues, which are non-biodegradable industrial wastes.
    • Among common industry options, thermal power plants are the expected answer for non-biodegradable waste.
    • Do not choose cotton mills or paper mills merely because they generate waste.
    • The question specifically asks for non-biodegradable waste, where fly ash from thermal power plants is the important clue.

      Environment, pollution and pollutants
    • Environment means the biotic and abiotic surroundings with which living organisms interact. Its major segments are atmosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere and biosphere.
    • Environmental pollution is the deterioration of natural resources such as air, water and soil due to unwanted substances present beyond safe limits. Contamination means mere presence of an undesirable substance; it becomes pollution when the level is harmful.
    • A pollutant is a substance present in solid, liquid, gaseous or particulate form that causes harmful effects when introduced into the environment in significant amount.
    • Pollutants may be natural or anthropogenic. Natural pollutants include volcanic gases, forest-fire smoke, soil dust and natural radioactivity; anthropogenic pollutants come from human activities such as industries, automobiles, agriculture and domestic waste.
    • Primary pollutants are released directly in harmful form, such as \(\mathrm{CO}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{NO}\) and hydrocarbons. Secondary pollutants are formed by reactions of primary pollutants with atmospheric components, such as \(\mathrm{SO_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\), \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), ozone and PAN.

      Atmospheric pollution and gaseous pollutants
    • Air pollution is caused by undesirable changes in the composition of air due to pollutants such as oxides of sulphur, oxides of nitrogen, hydrocarbons, oxides of carbon and particulate matter.
    • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2S}\) are released from fossil-fuel combustion, smelting, petroleum refining, volcanic activity and manufacture of sulphuric acid. \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) causes eye and respiratory irritation and damages plants, including defoliation and stiffness of flower buds.
    • Nitrogen oxides, especially \(\mathrm{NO}\) and \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), arise from fossil-fuel burning, automobile exhausts, power generators, explosives and fertilizer industries. \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) is a strong lung irritant and is associated with acute respiratory disease in children.
    • In moist respiratory passages, \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) can form acidic species: \(\mathrm{2NO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow HNO_2 + HNO_3}\). This explains why \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) is tested as a lung irritant rather than as a blood poison.
    • Carbon monoxide, \(\mathrm{CO}\), is released mainly from incomplete combustion and gasoline engines. It forms carboxyhaemoglobin with haemoglobin and reduces oxygen transport; therefore, \(\mathrm{CO}\) is remembered as a blood poison.
    • Carbon dioxide, \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), is released by combustion of coal, oil, gas and wood. Excess \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) contributes to greenhouse effect and global warming.
    • Hydrocarbons enter air from incomplete combustion, petroleum leakage, solvents, paints, pesticides, paddy fields and ruminant animals. Methane, \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), is important as a greenhouse gas, while other hydrocarbons contribute to photochemical smog.

      Particulate pollutants
    • Particulate pollutants include smoke, dust, mist, pollen, soot, fly ash, fumes, lead particles and agricultural chemicals. Fine particulate matter can enter the respiratory tract and cause asthma, bronchitis, silicosis or even lung cancer.
    • Smoke is a mixture of gases and particulates formed by combustion of organic matter. Soot is black carbon-rich material from incomplete combustion, while fly ash is a light solid residue released from thermal power plants.
    • Fly ash from thermal power plants is a high-value clue for non-biodegradable industrial waste. In questions comparing cotton mills, paper mills, textile factories and thermal power plants, the expected non-biodegradable waste source is thermal power plants.
    • Lead is a cumulative poison released from paints, storage batteries, industries and automobile exhaust. It interferes with red blood cell development and may damage the nervous system, kidneys and liver.

      Smog and photochemical oxidants
    • Smog is a mixture of smoke, fog and harmful atmospheric pollutants. Classical smog is reducing in nature and is mainly associated with cool, humid conditions, smoke, fog and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).
    • Photochemical smog is oxidising in nature. It forms when sunlight acts on nitrogen oxides and hydrocarbons, producing secondary pollutants such as ozone, PAN, aldehydes, formaldehyde and acrolein.
    • The key formation steps are: \(\mathrm{NO_2 \xrightarrow{sunlight} NO + O}\) and \(\mathrm{O + O_2 \rightarrow O_3}\). This is why ozone is a characteristic oxidising component of photochemical smog.
    • PAN stands for peroxyacetyl nitrate and is a major photochemical oxidant along with ozone. The most common exam component set is \(\mathrm{O_3,\ NO,\ PAN}\).
    • Carbon-containing components of photochemical smog include acrolein, methanal and PAN. Acrolein is acrylic aldehyde, \(\mathrm{CH_2=CH-CHO}\); it should not be confused with acrylonitrile \(\mathrm{CH_2=CH-CN}\) or PAN-type structures.
    • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is not a common component of photochemical smog; it belongs mainly with classical smog and acid-rain chemistry. If \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) appears as the odd option in a photochemical-smog question, it is usually the answer.

      Acid rain
    • Normal rain water is slightly acidic because atmospheric \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) dissolves in water: \(\mathrm{CO_2 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons H_2CO_3}\). Its pH is about \(5.6\), not \(7\) or \(7.5\).
    • Acid rain is caused mainly by oxides of sulphur and nitrogen. These oxides react with oxygen and water vapour to form acids such as \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) and \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\).
    • Important acid-rain reactions include: \(\mathrm{2SO_2 + O_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2H_2SO_4}\) and \(\mathrm{4NO_2 + O_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 4HNO_3}\).
    • Acid rain leaches nutrients from soil, damages forests, affects freshwater ecosystems and corrodes buildings, statues and marble structures. Limestone and marble are especially damaged because they contain \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\).
    • A useful marble-corrosion equation is: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow CaSO_4 + CO_2 + H_2O}\).

      Greenhouse effect and global warming
    • Greenhouse effect is the warming of the earth due to trapping of outgoing infrared radiation by greenhouse gases. These gases allow solar radiation to reach the earth but absorb and reradiate heat back towards the surface.
    • Important greenhouse gases include \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O(g)}\), \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), CFCs and halons. In PYQ-style list counting, only gaseous water vapour \(\mathrm{H_2O(g)}\), not liquid water \(\mathrm{H_2O(l)}\), should be counted.
    • In the list \(\mathrm{CH_4,\ CO_2,\ NO,\ H_2O(l),\ H_2O(g),\ O_2,\ O_3}\), the greenhouse gases counted in the given exam context are \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O(g)}\). The count is \(3\), not \(5\).
    • Methane is mainly remembered as a greenhouse gas, especially from swamps, paddy fields, bacterial decomposition and ruminant animals. It is not the primary ozone-depleting answer when compared with CFCs, chlorine species and nitrogen oxides.
    • Global warming can cause melting of glaciers and polar ice, rise in sea level, flooding of low-lying coastal areas, droughts and changes in rainfall patterns.

      Stratospheric ozone and ozone depletion
    • The ozone layer is mainly present in the stratosphere and protects living organisms by absorbing harmful ultraviolet radiation from the sun.
    • Ozone forms when solar radiation splits oxygen molecules: \(\mathrm{O_2 \xrightarrow{UV} O + O}\); then atomic oxygen combines with molecular oxygen: \(\mathrm{O + O_2 \rightarrow O_3}\).
    • CFCs and halons are major ozone-depleting substances. CFCs are stable in the lower atmosphere but release chlorine radicals in the stratosphere under ultraviolet radiation.
    • A classic CFC example is \(\mathrm{CF_2Cl_2}\): \(\mathrm{CF_2Cl_2 \xrightarrow{UV} CF_2Cl\cdot + Cl\cdot}\). The chlorine radical then destroys ozone catalytically.
    • The chlorine radical cycle is: \(\mathrm{Cl\cdot + O_3 \rightarrow ClO\cdot + O_2}\); \(\mathrm{ClO\cdot + O \rightarrow Cl\cdot + O_2}\). Net effect: \(\mathrm{O_3 + O \rightarrow 2O_2}\).
    • Nitric oxide can also destroy ozone catalytically: \(\mathrm{NO + O_3 \rightarrow NO_2 + O_2}\); \(\mathrm{NO_2 + O \rightarrow NO + O_2}\).
    • In “not primarily responsible for ozone depletion” questions, \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) is the odd one out when the other options include \(\mathrm{NO}\), \(\mathrm{CF_2Cl_2}\) and chlorine species.
    • Ozone depletion increases UV exposure, which can cause skin cancer in humans, chlorosis in leaves, damage to surface organisms and ecological disturbance.

      Water pollution and drinking-water standards
    • Water pollution is caused by sewage, industrial effluents, agricultural runoff, radioactive waste and thermal pollution. Sewage is a major source of pathogens and organic matter, while industrial waste may contain toxic metals, acids, alkalies, cyanides and non-biodegradable substances.
    • Drinking-water standards are frequently asked as direct facts. The maximum prescribed concentration of copper is \(1\,\mathrm{mg\,dm^{-3}}\), while that of zinc is \(15\,\mathrm{mg\,dm^{-3}}\).
    • The memory pair is \(\mathrm{Cu}=1\) and \(\mathrm{Zn}=15\). If copper is represented by \(x\) in the PYQ option format, choose the expression matching zinc as \(\dfrac{15}{x}\).
    • Excess nitrate in drinking water causes methemoglobinemia, especially in infants. This condition is also called blue baby syndrome because nitrate interferes with oxygen transport.
    • Fluoride is associated with fluorosis, while lead and mercury cause toxic effects on organs and nervous system. Do not confuse these with nitrate-induced methemoglobinemia.
    • Biological oxygen demand, BOD, is the amount of oxygen required by microorganisms to decompose organic waste in water. Higher organic pollution gives higher BOD and lower available dissolved oxygen.
    • Eutrophication is nutrient enrichment of water, mainly by nitrates and phosphates. It causes excessive algal growth; after the algal biomass dies, decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen, so dissolved oxygen decreases.
    • For water softening, ion-exchange process is the most effective among the listed methods because it removes hardness-causing ions more completely. Boiling removes only temporary hardness.

      Soil pollution, pesticides and industrial wastes
    • Soil pollution is caused by pesticides, industrial wastes, heavy metals, plastics, synthetic chemicals and poorly managed solid waste.
    • Persistent pesticides such as DDT and dieldrin do not break down easily under natural conditions. Their concentration can increase in soil, water and food chains with repeated use.
    • DDT stands for dichloro diphenyl trichloroethane. The name gives the chlorine count: \(2\) chlorine atoms from “dichloro” and \(3\) from “trichloroethane”, giving total chlorine atoms \(=5\).
    • Biomagnification is the increase in concentration of toxic chemicals at higher trophic levels. DDT is the standard pesticide example: it accumulates from water/plankton to fish and then to birds or humans.
    • Industrial wastes may contain heavy metals such as lead, mercury, cadmium, arsenic, chromium and nickel. Many of these are non-biodegradable and can persist in water or soil.
    • Thermal power plants produce fly ash and related solid residues, which are non-biodegradable. This is the expected industrial-waste clue in PYQs asking for non-biodegradable waste sources.

      Strategies to control environmental pollution
    • Air pollution can be controlled by reducing fossil-fuel combustion, using sulphur-free fuels, catalytic converters in automobiles, lead-free petrol, battery-powered vehicles and renewable energy sources such as solar, wind and hydropower.
    • Industrial emissions should be controlled by strict regulation, proper filters, scrubbers and treatment systems before discharge into air or water.
    • Waste management involves collection, segregation, treatment and safe disposal of waste. Biodegradable waste should be treated separately from non-biodegradable waste such as plastics, fly ash and heavy-metal-containing waste.
    • Sewage treatment reduces suspended solids, organic matter, pathogens and dissolved pollutants before wastewater is released into natural water bodies.
    • Public awareness is also part of pollution control because reduced burning of waste, careful pesticide use, waste segregation and responsible resource use depend on community participation.

      Green chemistry in day-to-day life
    • Green chemistry aims to design chemical processes that reduce or eliminate hazardous substances, minimise waste, save energy and use safer solvents or reagents.
    • In dry cleaning, liquid \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) can be used as a safer alternative to chlorinated solvents such as tetrachloroethene. This reduces solvent-related environmental and health concerns.
    • In bleaching of paper, hydrogen peroxide, \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), is preferred as a cleaner bleaching agent because it gives water as a major by-product and reduces chlorinated waste.
    • In synthesis of chemicals, green chemistry favours high atom economy, safer catalysts, fewer steps, lower energy conditions and less hazardous waste. The exam focus is usually the principle, not long industrial mechanisms.
    • For quick revision, connect green chemistry with prevention of pollution at source: safer solvent, safer reagent, less waste and lower toxicity.

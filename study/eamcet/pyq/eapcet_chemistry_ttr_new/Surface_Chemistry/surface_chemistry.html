    Variables used
  • Adsorption variables
    • \(\dfrac{x}{m}\): extent of adsorption, where \(x\) is mass of gas adsorbed and \(m\) is mass of adsorbent.
    • \(p\): pressure of gas in adsorption isotherm questions.
  • Freundlich adsorption isotherm constants
    • \(k,n\): Freundlich constants; in the graph of \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}\) versus \(\log p\), slope \(=\dfrac{1}{n}\) and intercept \(=\log k\).
  • Flocculating-value variable
    • \(M\): molarity of electrolyte solution; for flocculating value numericals, \(\text{millimoles}=M\times\text{mL}\).

    Adsorption and Freundlich adsorption isotherm
  • Physical adsorption conditions
    • Physical adsorption is favoured by high surface area, low temperature and high pressure.
    • It is due to weak van der Waals forces, so it is usually reversible and decreases on heating.
    • Adsorption of gases on solids is generally exothermic.
    • Therefore, in adsorption-versus-pressure curves at different temperatures, the higher curve corresponds to lower temperature and the lower curve corresponds to higher temperature.
  • Freundlich adsorption isotherm formulae
    • Freundlich adsorption isotherm is \(\dfrac{x}{m}=kp^{1/n}\), where \(n>1\).
    • It describes adsorption over a limited pressure range and should not be treated as a universal relation at all pressures.
    • The logarithmic form is \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}=\log k+\dfrac{1}{n}\log p\).
    • For a plot of \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}\) versus \(\log p\), slope \(=\dfrac{1}{n}\) and y-intercept \(=\log k\).
  • Graph interpretation and numerical shortcuts
    • When the intercept is given as \(1\), it means \(\log k=1\), so \(k=10\), not \(1\).
    • If slope \(=0.5\), then \(\dfrac{1}{n}=0.5\), so adsorption varies as \(p^{1/2}\).
    • For two-data-point Freundlich questions, divide the two equations so that \(k\) cancels.
    • If adsorbent mass is the same in both readings, the \(m\) term also cancels in the ratio of \(\dfrac{x}{m}\).
    • Typical quick computation: \(\dfrac{10}{14}=\left(\dfrac{10}{20}\right)^{1/n}\) gives \(\dfrac{1}{n}\approx0.46\approx0.5\), so the slope is taken as \(0.5\).

    Catalysis and industrial catalysts
  • General catalyst idea
    • A catalyst changes the rate of a reaction without being permanently consumed.
    • Many PYQs in this chapter are direct catalyst-matching questions, so fixed process-catalyst pairs are important.
  • Catalyst selectivity examples
    • Catalyst selectivity matters: \(\mathrm{CO+H_2\xrightarrow{Cu}HCHO}\) is associated with copper.
    • \(\mathrm{CO+3H_2\xrightarrow{Ni}CH_4+H_2O}\) is methanation and uses nickel.
  • Named industrial process-catalyst pairs
    • Ostwald process \(\rightarrow\) Pt gauze.
    • Haber process \(\rightarrow\) Fe.
    • Deacon process \(\rightarrow\) \(\mathrm{CuCl_2}\).
    • Catalytic cracking of hydrocarbons \(\rightarrow\) zeolites.
  • Additional catalyst facts
    • Ester hydrolysis \(\rightarrow\) HCl.
    • Contact process oxidation of \(\mathrm{SO_2}\rightarrow\mathrm{SO_3}\) \(\rightarrow\) Pt.
    • Lead chamber process \(\rightarrow\) NO.
    • Haber process \(\rightarrow\) Fe.
  • Zeolite catalyst clue
    • Zeolites are shape-selective catalysts and are repeatedly linked with cracking of hydrocarbons.
    • In matching questions, this is usually the easiest pair to lock first.

    Classification of colloids
  • Dispersed phase and dispersion medium classification
    • Colloids can be classified by the physical states of dispersed phase and dispersion medium.
    • Sol = solid in liquid.
    • Foam = gas in liquid/solid.
    • Gel = liquid in solid.
    • Aerosol = solid or liquid in gas.
  • Standard colloid examples
    • Paint is a sol.
    • Whipped cream is a foam.
    • Butter is a gel.
    • Cloud is an aerosol.
    • These pairs directly solve common match-the-following questions.
  • Emulsion and milk
    • Milk is an emulsion because liquid fat droplets are dispersed in water.
    • Emulsions are liquid-in-liquid colloidal systems.
  • Associated colloids and micelles
    • Associated colloids form micelles above the critical micelle concentration.
    • Ionic micelles in water require an amphiphilic ionic surfactant with a long hydrophobic tail and an ionic hydrophilic head.
    • Sodium lauryl sulphate forms ionic micelles in water because it has a long hydrocarbon tail and an ionic sulphate head.
    • Starch, iodine and \(\mathrm{S_8}\) do not form ionic micelles in this standard sense.

    Properties of colloidal solutions
  • Tyndall effect
    • Tyndall effect is the scattering of light by colloidal particles.
    • It is used to distinguish a colloidal solution from a true solution because true solutions do not scatter visible light appreciably.
    • Tyndall effect is prominent when the dispersed phase and dispersion medium differ appreciably in refractive index.
    • The overstatement that particles must be “much smaller than the wavelength of light” is not accepted as the correct condition in these PYQs.
    • Clouds and milk show Tyndall effect because they are colloidal systems.
    • Sugar solution is a true solution, so it does not show Tyndall effect.
  • Brownian motion
    • Brownian motion is the random zig-zag motion of colloidal particles due to unequal bombardment by molecules of the dispersion medium.
    • It helps stabilise sols by preventing particles from settling under gravity.
    • Higher viscosity slows Brownian motion because the medium offers greater resistance to particle movement.
    • Therefore, Brownian motion is not faster in highly viscous solutions.
  • Zeta potential and electrophoresis
    • Zeta potential is mainly connected with charge and stability of colloidal particles.
    • In the tested statement set, it should not be loosely treated as merely “movement of colloidal particles.”
    • Electrophoresis is the movement of charged colloidal particles towards the oppositely charged electrode under a DC electric field.
    • It is different from Brownian motion, Tyndall effect and peptisation.

    Charge on sols, coagulation and flocculation value
  • Charge on sulphide sols
    • Sulphide sols such as \(\mathrm{As_2S_3}\) and \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\) are negatively charged.
    • For negative sols, cations are the effective coagulating ions.
  • Hardy–Schulze rule and coagulating power
    • Hardy–Schulze rule: coagulating power increases with the valency of the oppositely charged ion.
    • For negative sols, the usual order is \(\mathrm{Na^+}\), \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) in increasing coagulating power.
    • For \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\) sol, \(\mathrm{Al_2(SO_4)_3}\) is most effective among the given salts because it supplies \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\).
    • For \(\mathrm{As_2S_3}\) sol, \(\mathrm{FeCl_3}\) is most effective because it supplies \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\).
    • In coagulation questions, compare only the ion opposite in charge to the sol particles.
    • Do not compare the full salt formula or the total number of ions produced.
  • Flocculating value meaning and calculation
    • Flocculating value is the millimoles of electrolyte required to coagulate \(1\ \mathrm{L}\) of sol.
    • Smaller flocculating value means greater coagulating power.
    • If the sol volume is already \(1\ \mathrm{L}\), use \(\text{millimoles}=M\times\text{mL}\) directly.
    • For \(10\ \mathrm{mL}\) of \(0.5\ \mathrm{M}\ \mathrm{NaCl}\), flocculating value \(=0.5\times10=5\ \mathrm{mmol}\).
    • If the sol volume is not \(1\ \mathrm{L}\), first calculate millimoles used, then scale to \(1000\ \mathrm{mL}\).
    • For \(0.73\ \mathrm{g}\ \mathrm{HCl}\), moles \(=\dfrac{0.73}{36.5}=0.02\), so \(20\ \mathrm{mmol}\) coagulates \(200\ \mathrm{mL}\); for \(1\ \mathrm{L}\), flocculation value \(=100\ \mathrm{mmol}\).

    Preparation, protection and applications of colloids
  • Bredig’s arc method and charged sols
    • Bredig’s arc method is an electrical dispersion method used to prepare metal sols such as gold, silver and platinum sols.

    • Charge on sol particles is usually due to preferential adsorption of ions.
  • Gold number and protection of colloids
    • Gold number measures the protective power of a lyophilic colloid for a lyophobic sol.
    • It is linked with protection of colloids.
    • Smaller gold number means better protective action.
  • Application and fixed matching pairs

    • This is a direct memory pair.
    • Milk \(\rightarrow\) emulsion.
    • Gold number \(\rightarrow\) protection of colloids.

  • \(p\): pressure of gas in gas-phase adsorption isotherm.
  • \(c\): concentration of solute in adsorption from solution phase.
  • \(k,n\): Freundlich constants; in \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}\) versus \(\log p\), slope \(=\dfrac{1}{n}\) and intercept \(=\log k\).
  • \(a,b\): Langmuir constants used in Langmuir adsorption isotherm.
  • CMC: critical micelle concentration, the concentration above which associated colloids such as soaps and detergents form micelles.

    Adsorption and absorption
  • Adsorption is a surface phenomenon in which molecules of a gas or dissolved substance collect at the surface of a solid or liquid. The substance adsorbed is the adsorbate, and the surface on which it is adsorbed is the adsorbent.
  • Absorption is a bulk phenomenon in which the substance enters throughout the body of the material. Silica gel adsorbs moisture on its surface, while calcium chloride absorbs moisture into its bulk.
  • Adsorption occurs because surface particles have residual or unbalanced forces. The greater the exposed surface area, the greater the number of available adsorption sites.
  • Porous and finely divided adsorbents such as charcoal and silica gel are effective because subdivision increases total surface area. More easily liquefiable gases such as \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) are adsorbed more strongly than gases such as \(\mathrm{H_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\).

    Factors affecting adsorption and types of adsorption
  • The extent of adsorption of a gas depends on nature and surface area of adsorbent, nature of adsorbate, temperature and pressure. For physical adsorption, the exam-ready condition is: high surface area, low temperature and high pressure.
  • Adsorption is generally exothermic. Therefore, increasing temperature decreases physical adsorption; in adsorption-versus-pressure curves, the higher curve at the same pressure corresponds to lower temperature.
  • Physical adsorption or physisorption is due to weak van der Waals forces. It has low enthalpy of adsorption, is generally reversible, not highly specific and may form multilayers.
  • Chemisorption involves forces similar to chemical bonds between adsorbent and adsorbate. It has higher enthalpy of adsorption, is highly specific, usually irreversible and forms a monolayer.
  • Physisorption is favoured at low temperature, while chemisorption may first increase with temperature because it needs activation energy and later decrease after sufficient adsorption has occurred.

    Adsorption isotherms
  • An adsorption isotherm gives the relation between extent of adsorption \(\dfrac{x}{m}\) and pressure \(p\) at constant temperature. For gases on solids, \(\dfrac{x}{m}\) increases with pressure but tends towards saturation at high pressure.
  • Freundlich adsorption isotherm is \(\dfrac{x}{m}=kp^{1/n}\), where \(n>1\). It is empirical and works best over a moderate pressure range, not at very low or very high pressure.
  • The logarithmic form is \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}=\log k+\dfrac{1}{n}\log p\). Thus, for a plot of \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}\) against \(\log p\), slope \(=\dfrac{1}{n}\) and intercept \(=\log k\).
  • In graph numericals, do not confuse \(n\) with \(\dfrac{1}{n}\). If the slope is \(0.5\), then adsorption varies as \(p^{0.5}\); if the intercept is \(1\), then \(\log k=1\) and \(k=10\).
  • When two pressure data points are given, divide the two Freundlich equations so that \(k\) cancels. If the same adsorbent mass is used in both cases, compare \(x\) directly because \(m\) cancels from \(\dfrac{x}{m}\).
  • Langmuir isotherm assumes equivalent adsorption sites, dynamic equilibrium between adsorption and desorption, and monolayer adsorption. It is especially useful for chemisorption and explains saturation at high pressure.
  • Langmuir form: \(\dfrac{x}{m}=\dfrac{ap}{1+bp}\). At low pressure, \(\dfrac{x}{m}\propto p\); at high pressure, the surface becomes nearly saturated and \(\dfrac{x}{m}\) becomes almost constant.
  • Adsorption from solution phase follows the same logic, but concentration \(c\) replaces pressure \(p\): \(\dfrac{x}{m}=kc^{1/n}\) and \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}=\log k+\dfrac{1}{n}\log c\).
  • Important applications of adsorption include gas masks using activated charcoal, moisture removal using silica gel, decolourisation by animal charcoal, chromatography, and dye-fixing by mordants.

    Catalysis
  • A catalyst is a substance that changes the rate of a reaction but remains chemically unchanged at the end. It may undergo temporary physical change, but its chemical composition is not consumed in the overall reaction.
  • A catalyst cannot initiate a reaction that is otherwise impossible; it only changes the rate of a reaction that can occur. It also does not change the equilibrium composition of a reversible reaction, because it affects forward and backward rates equally.
  • Autocatalysis occurs when one of the products acts as catalyst. Oxidation of oxalic acid by acidified potassium permanganate becomes faster as \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\) ions are formed.
  • Negative catalysts decrease the rate of reaction. Promoters increase catalytic activity, while poisons decrease it; for example, molybdenum promotes iron in Haber’s process, while \(\mathrm{H_2S}\) poisons iron and \(\mathrm{As_2O_3}\) poisons platinum in the contact process.
  • Homogeneous catalysis occurs when catalyst and reactants are in the same phase. Examples include \(\mathrm{NO}\) in the lead chamber process and \(\mathrm{H^+}\) in inversion of cane sugar or acid-catalysed ester hydrolysis.
  • Heterogeneous catalysis occurs when catalyst and reactants are in different phases. Examples include iron in Haber’s process and platinised asbestos or platinum in sulphuric-acid manufacture.
  • A catalyst increases rate by providing an alternative path with lower activation energy. It changes neither the relative energy of reactants and products nor the enthalpy change of reaction.

    Adsorption theory and industrial catalysts
  • In heterogeneous catalysis, reactant molecules are adsorbed on the catalyst surface, where their concentration increases and bonds are weakened or oriented favourably. Products then desorb, leaving the catalyst surface available again.
  • Activity of a catalyst refers to its ability to increase reaction rate. Selectivity refers to its ability to direct a reaction towards a particular product.
  • Selectivity is important in hydrogenation of carbon monoxide: \(\mathrm{CO+H_2\xrightarrow{Cu}HCHO}\), while \(\mathrm{CO+3H_2\xrightarrow{Ni}CH_4+H_2O}\). The catalyst changes the product pathway.
  • Shape-selective catalysis occurs when pore structure controls which reactant molecules can enter and react. Zeolites are important shape-selective catalysts and are strongly associated with cracking of hydrocarbons.
  • Enzyme catalysis is highly specific and efficient. Enzymes act under mild conditions, form temporary enzyme-substrate complexes and are sensitive to temperature and pH.
  • Must-remember catalyst pairs: Haber process \(\rightarrow\) Fe, Ostwald process \(\rightarrow\) Pt gauze, Deacon process \(\rightarrow\) \(\mathrm{CuCl_2}\), catalytic cracking \(\rightarrow\) zeolites, ester hydrolysis \(\rightarrow\) HCl, and lead chamber process \(\rightarrow\) NO.

    True solution, colloid and suspension
  • A true solution has particles smaller than \(1\ \mathrm{nm}\), a colloidal solution has particles of about \(1\text{–}100\ \mathrm{nm}\), and a suspension has particles larger than \(100\ \mathrm{nm}\).
  • Colloids are heterogeneous but often appear homogeneous to the naked eye. They pass through ordinary filter paper but not through animal membrane, do not settle on their own and diffuse slowly.
  • A colloid has two phases: dispersed phase and dispersion medium. The dispersed phase is present in smaller proportion, while the dispersion medium is the medium in which colloidal particles are dispersed.

    Classification of colloids
  • Based on physical state, important colloid types are: sol = solid in liquid, gel = liquid in solid, emulsion = liquid in liquid, aerosol = solid or liquid in gas, foam = gas in liquid, and solid foam = gas in solid.
  • Standard examples are high yield: paint is a sol, whipped cream is a foam, butter is a gel, milk is an emulsion, and cloud is an aerosol.
  • Lyophilic sols are “solvent-loving” colloids in which the dispersed phase has strong affinity for the dispersion medium. They are easily prepared by direct mixing, are highly stable, reversible, and usually do not coagulate easily on adding small amounts of electrolyte.
  • Examples of lyophilic sols include starch, gum, gelatin and proteins in suitable solvents. If water is the dispersion medium, they are called hydrophilic colloids.
  • Lyophobic sols are “solvent-hating” colloids in which the dispersed phase has little or no affinity for the dispersion medium. They cannot usually be prepared by simple mixing and require special methods such as Bredig’s arc method, peptisation or chemical methods.
  • Lyophobic sols are comparatively unstable, irreversible and easily coagulated by electrolytes. Metal sols, metal hydroxide sols and sulphide sols such as \(\mathrm{As_2S_3}\) and \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\) are important examples.
  • The exam distinction is: lyophilic sols are stable and reversible, while lyophobic sols are less stable, irreversible and electrolyte-sensitive. This is why coagulation and Hardy–Schulze rule questions usually involve lyophobic sols.
  • Based on particle type, colloids are multimolecular, macromolecular or associated colloids. Sulphur sol is multimolecular; starch, cellulose and proteins are macromolecular; soaps and detergents are associated colloids.
  • Associated colloids behave as normal electrolytes at low concentration but form micelles above CMC. In water, hydrophobic tails gather inside and ionic heads remain outside in contact with water.
  • Sodium lauryl sulphate forms ionic micelles because it has a long hydrocarbon tail and an ionic sulphate head. Non-polar species such as iodine and \(\mathrm{S_8}\) do not form ionic micelles in water.

    Preparation and purification of colloids
  • Lyophilic sols such as starch, gum and gelatin are prepared by direct mixing with the dispersion medium. Lyophobic sols require physical or chemical methods.
  • Bredig’s arc method is used to prepare metal sols such as gold, silver and platinum. An electric arc vaporises the metal, and the vapour condenses in cold water to form colloidal particles.
  • The standard exam association is Bredig’s arc method \(\rightarrow\) metal sol \(\rightarrow\) negatively charged particles. Metal sulphide sols are not the standard product of this method.
  • Peptisation is conversion of a freshly prepared precipitate into colloidal sol by adding a suitable electrolyte called peptising agent. The sol forms because particles adsorb ions and repel one another.
  • For example, \(\mathrm{Fe(OH)_3}\) precipitate can be converted into a reddish-brown sol by adding \(\mathrm{FeCl_3}\), because \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) ions are preferentially adsorbed and give particles a positive charge.
  • Colloids can also be prepared chemically, such as sulphur sol by oxidation of \(\mathrm{H_2S}\): \(\mathrm{Br_2+H_2S\rightarrow S+2HBr}\).
  • Dialysis purifies colloids by allowing small ions and impurities to pass through a membrane while colloidal particles remain inside. Electrodialysis speeds up dialysis using an electric field.

    Properties of colloidal solutions
  • Colloids are heterogeneous systems with dispersed particles separated from the dispersion medium by boundary surfaces.
  • Brownian motion is the continuous zig-zag motion of colloidal particles caused by unequal bombardment by molecules of the dispersion medium. It helps stabilise sols by preventing settling.
  • Brownian motion becomes slower in highly viscous media because particle movement faces greater resistance.
  • Tyndall effect is the scattering of light by colloidal particles, making the path of light visible. It is used to distinguish colloidal solutions from true solutions.
  • Tyndall effect is prominent when dispersed phase and dispersion medium differ appreciably in refractive index. True solutions such as sugar solution do not show it; milk, clouds and colloids show it clearly.
  • Colloidal particles carry the same type of charge and repel one another, which prevents clustering and settling. The dispersion medium carries an equal and opposite charge.
  • Common negative sols include arsenious sulphide sol, gold sol and silver sol. Common positive sols include ferric hydroxide and aluminium hydroxide sols.
  • Charge may arise by preferential adsorption of ions, charge on micelles, or capture of electrons during Bredig’s arc method.
  • Electrophoresis is movement of charged colloidal particles towards the oppositely charged electrode under an electric field. It is evidence for charge on colloidal particles.

    Coagulation, flocculation and protection
  • Coagulation is precipitation of colloidal particles by neutralising their charge. Lyophobic sols are easily coagulated by electrolytes.
  • Hardy–Schulze rule states that coagulating power depends mainly on the valency of the ion carrying charge opposite to that of sol particles. Higher valency means stronger coagulating power.
  • For negative sols such as \(\mathrm{As_2S_3}\) and \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\), cations are effective. Thus \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\) ions are much more effective than \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Na^+}\).
  • In coagulation MCQs, compare the oppositely charged ion, not the whole electrolyte formula. For \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\), \(\mathrm{Al_2(SO_4)_3}\) is strong because it supplies \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\).
  • Flocculating value is the millimoles of electrolyte required to coagulate \(1\ \mathrm{L}\) of sol. Smaller flocculating value means greater coagulating power.
  • For direct molarity questions, use \(\text{millimoles}=M\times\text{mL}\). If the sol volume is not \(1\ \mathrm{L}\), scale the result to \(1000\ \mathrm{mL}\).
  • Gold number measures protective action of a lyophilic colloid for a lyophobic sol. Smaller gold number indicates better protective power.

    Emulsions, gels and cleansing action
  • Emulsions are colloids in which both dispersed phase and dispersion medium are liquids. The liquids are immiscible; otherwise they would form a true solution.
  • Oil-in-water emulsions have oil as dispersed phase and water as dispersion medium; milk and vanishing cream are examples. Water-in-oil emulsions have water dispersed in oil; butter, cold cream and cod-liver oil are examples.
  • Emulsifiers stabilise emulsions by concentrating at the oil-water interface and acting as a binder between the two liquids. Soap is a common emulsifying agent.
  • Cleansing action of soaps and detergents is based on micelle formation and oil-in-water emulsification. The hydrophobic tail dissolves in grease, while the ionic head remains in water, allowing grease to be removed.
  • Gels are colloids in which liquid is dispersed in a solid. Cheese, jelly, boot polish and butter are common examples. Some gels shrink on standing by losing liquid; this is called syneresis.

    Colloids around us and applications
  • Colloids are common in daily life: milk, cream, butter, cheese, ink, rubber, boot polish, fog, smoke and clouds are colloidal systems.
  • In sewage disposal, charged colloidal dirt particles move to oppositely charged electrodes and get precipitated. This is an application of electrophoresis and coagulation.
  • In water purification, alum supplies \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\), which neutralises negatively charged mud particles and makes them settle.
  • Smoke particles are charged colloidal particles. In Cottrell’s precipitator, they are precipitated by applying high potential, reducing smoke pollution.
  • Blood is a colloidal solution and is negatively charged; \(\mathrm{FeCl_3}\) can help clot blood by coagulating colloidal particles. Colloidal antimony is associated with treatment of kala azar.
  • Other applications include photography using colloidal silver bromide in gelatin, rubber plating from latex, blue colour of sky due to scattering by colloidal particles, and digestion of fats through emulsification.

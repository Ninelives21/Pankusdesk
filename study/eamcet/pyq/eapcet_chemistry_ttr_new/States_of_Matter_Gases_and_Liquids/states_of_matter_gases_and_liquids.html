States of Matter Gases and Liquids

    Ideal gas equation and gas-law applications
  • Core formula: pV = nRT. For a fixed amount of gas, pV/T = constant.
  • Combined gas law shortcut: (p1V1)/T1 = (p2V2)/T2.
  • At constant pressure, V ∝ T and the V–T graph for an isobar is a straight line with slope = nR/p. Hence slope ∝ 1/p, so lower pressure means steeper isobar.
  • At constant temperature and pressure, V ∝ n. So in a V vs n graph, slope = V/n = RT/p. At 300 K and 1 atm, slope ≈ 24.6 L mol-1.
  • For the same gas, pV = (w/M)RT, so mass of gas present follows w ∝ pV/T.
  • At fixed temperature and volume, pressure is proportional to number of moles: p ∝ n ∝ w/M.
  • In an open vessel, pressure is atmospheric and vessel volume is fixed, so n ∝ 1/T. Therefore, fraction of gas remaining after heating = T1/T2 (use Kelvin only).
  • Use Kelvin always in gas-law numericals; convert from °C before substitution.

    Gas mixtures, partial pressure and composition
  • Dalton’s law: ptotal = p1 + p2 + p3 + ...
  • Partial pressure relation: pi = xi ptotal, where xi = ni/ntotal.
  • For ideal gases mixed at the same temperature, compare moles using n = pV/RT. So mole fraction depends on initial pV values.
  • Fast mixing shortcut for connected vessels at constant temperature: final partial pressure of a gas = (initial pressure × its initial volume)/(total final volume).
  • After opening a stopcock and allowing gases to mix, each gas occupies the total available volume, not its original vessel volume.
  • For a gaseous mixture, first convert each component into moles, then apply pV = nRT using total moles.
  • Average molar mass of a mixture: Mavg = x1M1 + x2M2 + ...
  • Density–molar mass relation: M = dRT/p. This is high value for finding composition from density.
  • If mole fraction is found first but mass percent is asked, convert using component masses, not mole fractions directly.
  • Air approximation from PYQ style: Mavg = (79 × 28 + 21 × 32)/100 ≈ 28.8 g mol-1.

    Kinetic molecular theory and molecular speeds
  • Kinetic molecular theory assumptions: gas molecules have negligible volume compared to the empty space between them; collisions are perfectly elastic; pressure is due to collisions with container walls.
  • Gas molecules do not all have the same speed or the same kinetic energy at a given instant.
  • RMS speed formula: vrms = √(3RT/M), so vrms ∝ √(T/M).
  • Most probable speed formula: vmp = √(2RT/M).
  • For comparison of speeds of two gases, square the speed ratio first because speed varies as the square root of T/M.
  • Useful comparison form: T1/M1 = (v1/v2)2(T2/M2).
  • Average kinetic energy per mole using RMS speed: E = (1/2)Mvrms2, with M in kg mol-1.
  • Very common unit trap: convert molar mass from g mol-1 to kg mol-1 before using E = (1/2)Mvrms2.
  • When one type of speed is equated to another, use the exact formulas separately; for example, vrms of one gas can be equated to vmp of another.

    Diffusion, Graham’s law and gaseous volume relations
  • Graham’s law: rate of diffusion r ∝ 1/√M.
  • Direct comparison form: r1/r2 = √(M2/M1).
  • For equal diffused volumes, rate = volume/time, so r ∝ 1/t and hence t1/t2 = √(M1/M2).
  • At the same temperature and pressure, gaseous volumes are proportional to mole ratios.
  • Combustion volume relation for hydrocarbon CxHy: CxHy + (x + y/4)O2 → xCO2 + (y/2)H2O.
  • So if gaseous volume ratio hydrocarbon : CO2 : H2O is known, then x comes from CO2 ratio and y/2 comes from H2O ratio.
  • Important caution from the PYQs: if pressure is different for the two gases, pure Graham’s law is not enough; rate may need pressure correction as used in the given solution set.

    Real gases, compressibility factor and van der Waals ideas
  • Ideal gas assumptions fail when intermolecular attraction and finite molecular volume are no longer negligible.
  • Deviation from ideal behaviour becomes important especially at high pressure.
  • Compressibility factor: Z = pV/RT for 1 mole, or more generally Z = pV/nRT.
  • For an ideal gas, Z = 1.
  • For real gases, Z < 1 when intermolecular attractions dominate; Z > 1 when repulsions/finite molecular volume dominate.
  • In a Z vs p graph, deeper dip below 1 means stronger intermolecular attraction.
  • van der Waals equation: (p + a/V2)(V - b) = RT for 1 mole.
  • van der Waals constant a measures intermolecular attraction; larger a means stronger attraction.
  • van der Waals constant b measures excluded volume/finite size of molecules.
  • At high pressure, the volume correction becomes dominant and the attraction term can often be neglected, giving p(V - b) = RT.
  • Hence at high pressure, Z = pV/RT = 1 + pb/RT, so Z > 1.
  • At moderate pressure, gases with stronger attractions such as NH3 and CO2 show lower Z than gases like N2.

    Liquid state: viscosity
  • Newton’s law of viscosity: F = ηA(du/dz).
  • So viscous force is directly proportional to area of contact and velocity gradient.
  • Here, η = coefficient of viscosity, A = area of contact of layers, and du/dz = velocity gradient.

    Quick caution list
  • Use Kelvin, not °C, in every gas-law and kinetic-theory formula.
  • In mixture problems, distinguish clearly between mole fraction, mass percent, and partial pressure.
  • In connected-vessel mixing, each gas finally occupies the total volume.
  • In speed comparisons, square the given speed ratio before relating temperatures or molar masses.
  • In kinetic-energy numericals, molar mass must be in kg mol-1 if SI energy in joule is required.
  • In diffusion questions, check whether both gases are under the same pressure before applying the simplest Graham’s law form.

  1. RMS speed relation with temperature and molar mass: Use \(v_{\mathrm{rms}}=\sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}}\), so for comparison \(v_{\mathrm{rms}}\propto \sqrt{\frac{T}{M}}\). Hence \(\dfrac{T_1}{M_1}=\left(\dfrac{v_1}{v_2}\right)^2 \dfrac{T_2}{M_2}\). For this PYQ: \(v_{\mathrm{rms}}(\mathrm{H_2})=\sqrt{7}\,v_{\mathrm{rms}}(\mathrm{N_2})\), with \(M_{\mathrm{H_2}}=2\) and \(M_{\mathrm{N_2}}=28\), so \(\dfrac{T_{\mathrm{H_2}}}{2}=7\cdot \dfrac{T_{\mathrm{N_2}}}{28}=\dfrac{T_{\mathrm{N_2}}}{4}\), giving \(T_{\mathrm{H_2}}=\dfrac{T_{\mathrm{N_2}}}{2}\). Pitfall: do not compare speeds directly with molar masses; since speed is under a square root, always square the given speed ratio first.
  2. Mixing of ideal gases in connected vessels / Dalton’s law: when two ideal gases are allowed to mix at constant temperature, each gas finally occupies the total volume, so its final partial pressure is \(p_i=\dfrac{n_iRT}{V_{\text{total}}}\). A very fast exam form is \(p_i=\dfrac{P_iV_i}{V_{\text{total}}}\) because \(n_iRT=P_iV_i\) initially. For this PYQ, \(V_{\text{total}}=12+8=20\text{ L}\); hence \(p_A=\dfrac{8\times12}{20}=4.8\text{ atm}\) and \(p_B=\dfrac{5\times8}{20}=2.0\text{ atm}\). Also \(p_{\text{total}}=4.8+2.0=6.8\text{ atm}\). Pitfall: do not keep each gas in its original vessel volume after opening the stopcock; after mixing, both gases occupy the whole 20 L. Another quick check: final partial pressures must add up to the final total pressure.
  3. RMS speed and kinetic energy per mole: For one mole of gas, E = (1/2)Mvrms2, where M must be in kg mol-1. For this PYQ, 40 g mol-1 = 0.040 kg mol-1 and vrms = 20 m s-1, so E = (1/2) × 0.040 × (20)2 = 8 J mol-1. Pitfall: always convert g mol-1 to kg mol-1; otherwise the answer becomes wrong by a factor of 1000. Also note: this is per mole, not per molecule.
  4. Mole fraction in gas mixtures: Use n = pV/RT. At constant temperature, mole fraction depends on the initial pV values of each gas: xi = ni / ntotal = (piVi) / Σ(pV). For this PYQ, oxygen moles ∝ 5V and helium moles ∝ 3 × 2 = 6, so xO2 = 5V / (5V + 6) = 0.2. Solving gives 5V = 0.2(5V + 6), hence 5V = V + 1.2, so 4V = 1.2 and V = 0.3 L. Pitfall: after mixing, do not use final total volume directly for mole fraction; first compare moles using initial pV/RT. Since T and R are the same for both gases, RT cancels out.
  5. Newton’s law of viscosity: The force required to maintain the steady flow of liquid layers is directly proportional to the area of contact and the velocity gradient. Hence, F = ηA(du/dz). So the correct relation is F = ηA(du/dz).
  6. Isobars on a V–T graph: For one mole of an ideal gas at constant pressure, pV = nRT gives V = (nR/p)T. So the slope of the V vs T graph is m = nR/p. Hence slope is inversely proportional to pressure: m ∝ 1/p. Therefore, if p1 < p2 < p3, then m1 > m2 > m3.

    2022
  1. Deviation from ideal gas behaviour at high pressure: At high pressure, gas molecules come much closer together, so intermolecular attractive forces become significant. Ideal gas behaviour assumes negligible molecular volume and no intermolecular attraction, so real gases deviate under these conditions.
  2. Kinetic molecular theory of gases: Gas molecules are treated as point particles whose actual volume is negligible compared to the empty space between them. Collisions between molecules and with container walls are perfectly elastic. Gas pressure arises due to collisions of molecules with the walls of the container. Different gas molecules do not all have the same speed or the same kinetic energy at a given instant.
  3. Dalton’s law using mole fraction: Partial pressure of a gas in a mixture is pi = xi ptotal, where xi = ni/ntotal. Here, xC = 5/(2 + 3 + 5 + 10) = 5/20 = 1/4. So, 1.5 = ptotal × 1/4, giving ptotal = 6 atm.
  4. Compressibility factor graph: z = 1 for an ideal gas. For real gases, z < 1 at moderate pressure when intermolecular attractions dominate, and z > 1 at high pressure when repulsions/finite molecular volume dominate. Gases with stronger intermolecular attraction show a deeper dip below 1. Hence CO2, having stronger van der Waals attraction, corresponds to the curve with the lowest dip: plot D.
  5. Average molar mass of a gas mixture: Use mole-fraction weighted average, Mavg = x1M1 + x2M2 + ... For air with 79% N2 and 21% O2, Mavg = (79 × 28 + 21 × 32)/100 = 28.84 g mol-1 ≈ 28.8 g mol-1.
  6. Compressibility factor at high pressure: From the van der Waals equation, (p + a/V2)(V - b) = RT. At high pressure, the volume correction term becomes dominant and the attraction term can be neglected, giving p(V - b) = RT. Therefore, Z = pV/RT = 1 + pb/RT, so for a real gas at high pressure, Z > 1.
  7. Compressibility factor and van der Waals constant a: A lower Z value indicates stronger intermolecular attractions. NH3 and CO2 have stronger attractions than N2, so their van der Waals constant a is greater. Hence NH3 and CO2 show lower compressibility factor than N2.
  8. Gaseous hydrocarbon combustion by volume ratio: At the same temperature and pressure, gaseous volumes are proportional to mole ratios. For CxHy + (x + y/4)O2 → xCO2 + (y/2)H2O, the given volume ratio is hydrocarbon : CO2 : H2O = 10 : 40 : 50 = 1 : 4 : 5. Hence x = 4 and y/2 = 5, so y = 10. Therefore, the molecular formula is C4H10.
  9. Diffusion under different pressures: Rate of diffusion is directly proportional to pressure and inversely proportional to square root of molar mass, so r ∝ p/√M. Therefore, rCH4 / rX = (pCH4 / pX) √(MX / MCH4). Here, 2 = (1.0/1.45)√(MX/16), which gives √(MX/16) = 2.9 and hence MX ≈ 16(2.9)2 ≈ 134.6 g mol-1. The textbook solution ignores the pressure term and uses only Graham’s law, giving MX = 64 g mol-1.
  10. van der Waals constant a measures intermolecular attraction: Greater attraction means larger a. Among H2, He, CO2 and NH3, NH3 has the maximum a because it shows the strongest intermolecular attraction due to hydrogen bonding.
  11. Real gas at high pressure using van der Waals correction: At very high pressure, the volume correction b becomes important and the attraction term a can be neglected. Then p(V - nb) = nRT. For 1 mole, p(V - b) = RT, so V = (RT/p) + b. Here, V = (0.0821 × 300 / 100) + 0.005 = 0.2513 L. Therefore, Z = pV/RT = (100 × 0.2513)/(0.0821 × 300) ≈ 1.02. The ideal volume is RT/p = 0.2463 L, so the percentage deviation in volume is [(0.2513 - 0.2463)/0.2513] × 100 ≈ 2%.
  12. Pressure of a gaseous mixture: First convert each gas mass into moles and add. Here, n(CH4) = 4/16 = 0.25 mol and n(CO2) = 4.4/44 = 0.10 mol, so total moles = 0.35 mol. Then use the ideal gas equation for the mixture: pV = nRT. For V = 1 L and T = 27°C = 300 K, p = nRT/V = 0.35 × 0.0821 × 300 / 1 = 8.62 atm.

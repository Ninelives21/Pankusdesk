    Exam map / pattern map
  • From this paper, Atomic Structure is testing two high-yield clusters: Bohr model for hydrogen-like species and photoelectric effect.
  • Bohr-model numericals usually reduce to direct proportionality: \(r_n \propto \dfrac{n^2}{Z}\), \(E_n \propto -\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\).
  • Photoelectric-effect numericals usually reduce to one line: \(K_{\max}=h\nu-\phi=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}-\phi\).
  • Expect quick one-step comparisons, ratio questions, threshold ideas, or velocity/kinetic-energy calculation.

    Core formula and rule map
  • Hydrogen-like species means only one electron: \(\mathrm{H},\ \mathrm{He}^+,\ \mathrm{Li}^{2+},\ \mathrm{Be}^{3+}\).
  • Bohr orbit radius: \(r_n=\dfrac{n^2a_0}{Z}\).
  • Fast ratio method: \(\dfrac{r_A}{r_B}=\dfrac{n_A^2/Z_A}{n_B^2/Z_B}\).
  • Photon energy: \(E=h\nu=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).
  • Fast conversion: \(E(\mathrm{eV})=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\mathrm{nm})}\).
  • Photoelectron velocity: \(K_{\max}=\dfrac{1}{2}mv^2\Rightarrow v=\sqrt{\dfrac{2K_{\max}}{m}}\).

    Fast comparison rules
  • At fixed \(Z\): larger \(n\) means larger orbit, since radius grows as \(n^2\).
  • At fixed \(n\): larger \(Z\) means smaller orbit, due to stronger nuclear pull.
  • For photoelectric effect, higher frequency means larger \(K_{\max}\); higher intensity mainly increases number of electrons, not their maximum kinetic energy.
  • If photon energy is just slightly greater than work function, emitted electron speed will be moderate, not huge.

    Common traps / mistakes
  • Do not treat neutral He as hydrogen-like; only \(\mathrm{He}^+\) is one-electron.
  • Do not use \(r_n\propto \dfrac{n}{Z}\); it is \(r_n\propto \dfrac{n^2}{Z}\).
  • In photoelectric effect, always subtract work function before using kinetic-energy formula.
  • Do not mix \(\mathrm{eV}\) and joule carelessly; convert before using \(K=\dfrac{1}{2}mv^2\).
  • Do not forget nm-to-m conversion when using SI form of \(\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).

    Question-to-rule mapping
  • If the question compares radii of \(\mathrm{H}\), \(\mathrm{He}^+\), \(\mathrm{Li}^{2+}\), first write \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z}\); this is usually enough.
  • If wavelength and work function are given, first find photon energy in eV using \(\dfrac{1240}{\lambda}\), then subtract \(\phi\).
  • If kinetic energy is small, estimate speed scale before full calculation to avoid option traps.

    Atomic models and scope
  • Atomic Structure in TG EAPCET spans subatomic particles, atomic models, electromagnetic radiation, quantum ideas, orbitals and electronic configuration.
  • Thomson model proposed positive charge spread with electrons embedded in it.
  • Rutherford nuclear model established a tiny dense positively charged nucleus but could not explain atomic stability and line spectra.
  • Bohr model successfully explained hydrogen and hydrogen-like species using quantized stationary orbits.

    Bohr model for hydrogen-like species
  • Applicable only to one-electron species such as \(\mathrm{H},\ \mathrm{He}^+,\ \mathrm{Li}^{2+}\).
  • Radius of \(n^{\text{th}}\) orbit: \(r_n=\dfrac{n^2a_0}{Z}\).
  • Hence radius increases with \(n^2\) and decreases with nuclear charge \(Z\).
  • Energy of \(n^{\text{th}}\) orbit varies as \(E_n\propto -\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\); more negative means more tightly bound electron.
  • Line spectrum arises due to transitions between quantized energy levels.
  • For exam numericals, comparison and ratio form is usually faster than absolute calculation.

    Electromagnetic radiation and quantum idea
  • Electromagnetic radiation has wavelength \(\lambda\), frequency \(\nu\), and speed \(c\), related by \(c=\lambda \nu\).
  • Planck’s quantum theory states radiant energy is emitted or absorbed in discrete packets called quanta.
  • Energy of one photon is \(E=h\nu=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).
  • Shorter wavelength means higher frequency and therefore higher photon energy.

    Photoelectric effect
  • When light falls on a metal, electrons are emitted only if incident frequency exceeds threshold frequency.
  • Einstein equation: \(K_{\max}=h\nu-\phi=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}-\phi\).
  • \(\phi\) is the work function: minimum energy needed to eject an electron from the metal surface.
  • Threshold frequency: \(\nu_0=\dfrac{\phi}{h}\); threshold wavelength: \(\lambda_0=\dfrac{hc}{\phi}\).
  • If \(h\nu<\phi\), no emission occurs.
  • If \(h\nu>\phi\), excess energy appears as maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons.
  • Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency, while intensity mainly affects number of emitted electrons.

    Dual behaviour and quantum mechanics
  • de Broglie proposed matter-wave nature: \(\lambda=\dfrac{h}{p}\).
  • Heisenberg uncertainty principle states exact position and momentum cannot both be known simultaneously.
  • These ideas led to the quantum mechanical model of atom, replacing fixed Bohr orbits with probability-based orbitals.

    Orbitals and quantum numbers
  • An orbital is a region of high probability of finding an electron.
  • Main quantum numbers: \(n\) (shell), \(l\) (subshell), \(m_l\) (orientation), \(m_s\) (spin).
  • Shapes: \(s\) orbitals are spherical; \(p\) orbitals are dumbbell-shaped.
  • Energy ordering for many-electron atoms is guided by Aufbau principle and \((n+l)\) rule.

    Electronic configuration rules
  • Aufbau principle: electrons fill lower-energy orbitals first.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers.
  • Hund’s rule: in degenerate orbitals, electrons occupy singly first with parallel spins.
  • Half-filled and fully filled subshells show extra stability, which helps explain common configuration exceptions.

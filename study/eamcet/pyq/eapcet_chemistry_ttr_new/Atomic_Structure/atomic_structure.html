Atomic Structure

    Exam pattern map
  • Most repeated cluster: Bohr-model ratios for \(r_n\), \(E_n\), \(v_n\), angular momentum, and hydrogen-like ions.
  • Second major cluster: photoelectric effect — threshold, work function, emitted metals, \(K E_{\max}\), velocity.
  • Third major cluster: hydrogen spectrum — series identification, line counting, wave number / wavelength relations.
  • Then: quantum-number validity, nodes, orbital counting, degeneracy, de Broglie, uncertainty principle.

    Core Bohr formulas
  • Hydrogen-like species only: one-electron systems such as \(\mathrm{H},\ \mathrm{He^+},\ \mathrm{Li^{2+}},\ \mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
  • \(r_n = a_0\dfrac{n^2}{Z}\)
  • \(E_n = -13.6\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \mathrm{eV} = -2.18\times10^{-18}\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \mathrm{J}\)
  • \(v_n = 2.18\times10^6\dfrac{Z}{n}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\)
  • \(mvr = \dfrac{nh}{2\pi}\)
  • If same radius is asked, immediately compare \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z}\).
  • If same energy is asked, immediately compare \(\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\).

    Fast ratio rules
  • For same species: \(r_n \propto n^2,\ E_n \propto -\dfrac{1}{n^2},\ v_n \propto \dfrac{1}{n}\).
  • From \(n=2\) to \(n=3\): radius multiplies by \(\dfrac{9}{4}\), energy magnitude multiplies by \(\dfrac{4}{9}\).
  • Energy ratios for H atom at \(n=1,2,3\): \(1:\dfrac14:\dfrac19\Rightarrow 36:9:4\).
  • Ground-state H angular momentum: \(\dfrac{h}{2\pi}\approx 1.055\times10^{-34}\ \mathrm{J\,s}\).
  • \(\dfrac{c}{v_1}\) for H atom \(\approx 137:1\).

    Hydrogen spectrum solve-fast sheet
  • \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_f^2}-\dfrac{1}{n_i^2}\right)\), with \(n_i>n_f\).
  • Lyman: \(n_f=1\), Balmer: \(n_f=2\), Paschen: \(n_f=3\), Brackett: \(n_f=4\), Pfund: \(n_f=5\).
  • If final orbit / final radius is given, identify series from final \(n\), not initial \(n\).
  • First line means \(n_i=n_f+1\); second line means \(n_i=n_f+2\).
  • Total lines from highest \(n\) to lowest \(m\): \(\dfrac{(n-m)(n-m+1)}{2}\).
  • Common trap: do not subtract \(\dfrac1\lambda\) when question asks \(\lambda_1-\lambda_2\).

    Photoelectric effect shortcuts
  • \(K E_{\max}=h\nu-\phi = h(\nu-\nu_0)\)
  • \(\nu_0=\dfrac{\phi}{h}\), \(\lambda_0=\dfrac{hc}{\phi}\)
  • Quick photon energy in eV: \(E=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\mathrm{nm})}\).
  • If asked whether emission happens: compare \(E_{\text{photon}}\) with \(\phi\).
  • If asked ratio of maximum kinetic energies, use \((\nu-\nu_0)\) ratio; \(h\) cancels.
  • Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency; number of electrons depends on intensity.
  • No appreciable time lag for suitable radiation.
  • Alkali metals like K, Rb, Cs are easy emitters because of low work function.

    Quantum mechanical mini-rules
  • Allowed values: \(n=1,2,3,\dots\); \(l=0\) to \(n-1\); \(m=-l\) to \(+l\); \(s=\pm\dfrac12\).
  • Impossible set check starts with \(l\le n-1\).
  • Probability density is \(|\psi|^2\), not \(|\psi|^3\).
  • \(m_l\) gives orientation of orbital in space.
  • Orbitals with same \(n,l\) but different \(m\) are degenerate in absence of field.
  • For H atom, energy depends only on \(n\), so \(2s\) and \(2p\) are same.
  • For multi-electron atoms like He, \(2s\) and \(2p\) are not same.

    Nodes and orbital counting
  • Total nodes \(= n-1\)
  • Angular nodes \(= l\)
  • Radial nodes \(= n-l-1\)
  • Orbitals in a subshell \(=2l+1\)
  • Orbitals in a shell \(=n^2\)
  • Maximum electrons in a shell \(=2n^2\)
  • Maximum \(+\dfrac12\) spin electrons in a shell \(=n^2\).

    de Broglie and uncertainty
  • \(\lambda=\dfrac{h}{p}=\dfrac{h}{mv}\)
  • If \(\lambda\) is given, directly use \(p=\dfrac{h}{\lambda}\).
  • Using \(\lambda=\dfrac{c}{\nu}\), de Broglie-wave velocity form becomes \(v=\dfrac{h\nu}{m c}\).
  • \(\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\); in MCQs, equality is usually used.
  • When \(\Delta v\) is given, first convert: \(\Delta p = m\Delta v\).
  • Percentage uncertainty in speed \(= \dfrac{\Delta v}{v}\times100\).

    Electronic-configuration shortcuts
  • Map azimuthal quantum number: \(l=0\to s,\ 1\to p,\ 2\to d,\ 3\to f\).
  • Cr \(=3d^5 4s^1\), Cu \(=3d^{10}4s^1\) — memorize exactly.
  • For cations of transition metals, remove electrons from \(ns\) before \((n-1)d\).
  • In \((n+l)\) counting questions, first write the correct exceptional configuration.
  • For isoelectronic species, size decreases as nuclear charge increases.
  • Common asked set: \(\mathrm{O^{2-}},\ \mathrm{F^-},\ \mathrm{Na^+},\ \mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) all have 10 electrons.

    Common traps
  • Using \(r_n\propto n^2\) but forgetting division by \(Z\).
  • Using incident frequencies directly in photoelectric ratio instead of excess frequencies.
  • Confusing first line / second line with first orbit / second orbit.
  • Missing that He\(^+\), Li\(^{2+}\), Be\(^{3+}\) are hydrogen-like, but neutral He is not.
  • Writing Cr as \(3d^4 4s^2\) or Cu as \(3d^9 4s^2\).
  • Writing probability density as \(|\psi|^3\) or treating spin quantum number as nuclear spin.

    Introduction and sub-atomic particles
  • Atom contains electrons, protons, and neutrons; chemistry of atom mainly tracks electron arrangement and energy.
  • Electron: charge \(=-1\), proton: charge \(=+1\), neutron: neutral.
  • Atomic number \(Z\) gives proton count; for neutral atom, electron count \(=Z\).
  • Mass number \(A = p + n\).

    Atomic models
  • Thomson model: positive sphere with embedded electrons; could not explain scattering results.
  • Rutherford nuclear model: atom mostly empty space, tiny dense positively charged nucleus, electrons around nucleus.
  • Drawbacks of Rutherford model: could not explain atomic stability and line spectra.
  • Bohr model development: electrons move in certain allowed circular orbits without radiating continuously.

    Electromagnetic radiation and Planck theory
  • Electromagnetic radiation shows wave properties: wavelength \(\lambda\), frequency \(\nu\), wave number \(\bar{\nu}=\dfrac1\lambda\).
  • \(c=\nu\lambda\)
  • Energy of one photon: \(E=h\nu=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).
  • Shorter wavelength means higher frequency and higher photon energy.
  • Planck’s quantum theory: radiation is emitted/absorbed in discrete quanta.
  • UV, visible, IR ordering matters in spectrum questions: Lyman lies in UV, Balmer in visible, Paschen in IR.

    Bohr model for hydrogen atom
  • Allowed angular momentum: \(mvr=\dfrac{nh}{2\pi}\).
  • Radius of \(n^{\text{th}}\) orbit for hydrogen-like species: \(r_n=a_0\dfrac{n^2}{Z}\).
  • For H atom specifically: \(r_n=a_0n^2\), where \(a_0=0.529\ \text{\AA}=52.9\ \mathrm{pm}\).
  • Energy of orbit: \(E_n=-13.6\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \mathrm{eV}\).
  • Velocity in Bohr orbit: \(v_n=2.18\times10^6\dfrac{Z}{n}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\).
  • Higher \(n\): larger radius, higher energy, lower velocity, closer energy spacing.

    Hydrogen line spectrum
  • Line spectrum arises because electron transitions occur between discrete energy levels.
  • Rydberg equation: \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_f^2}-\dfrac{1}{n_i^2}\right)\).
  • Lyman: \(n_f=1\), Balmer: \(n_f=2\), Paschen: \(n_f=3\), Brackett: \(n_f=4\), Pfund: \(n_f=5\).
  • First line of a series: \(n_i=n_f+1\); second line: \(n_i=n_f+2\).
  • Total spectral lines among \(m\) energy levels: \(\dfrac{m(m-1)}{2}\).
  • For transition from \(n\) to \(m\), one shortcut is \(\dfrac{(n-m)(n-m+1)}{2}\).

    Limitations of Bohr model
  • Works well only for hydrogen and one-electron species.
  • Cannot explain spectra of multi-electron atoms properly.
  • Cannot explain fine structure, Zeeman effect, and Stark effect fully.
  • Cannot justify why only certain orbits are allowed beyond postulate form.

    Dual behaviour of matter and de Broglie
  • Radiation shows wave and particle nature.
  • Moving particles also show wave nature.
  • de Broglie relation: \(\lambda=\dfrac{h}{p}=\dfrac{h}{mv}\).
  • Thus momentum and wavelength are inversely related.
  • If wavelength is given, momentum is found fastest by \(p=\dfrac{h}{\lambda}\).

    Heisenberg uncertainty principle
  • \(\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\)
  • With \(p=mv\), \(\Delta p = m\Delta v\).
  • Microscopic particles cannot have exact position and momentum simultaneously.
  • This is one key reason the exact Bohr-path picture fails in quantum mechanics.

    Quantum mechanical model of atom
  • Electron is described by a wavefunction \(\psi\), not a fixed circular path.
  • \(|\psi|^2\) gives probability density of finding electron in space.
  • Orbitals are regions of high probability, not orbits.
  • In H atom, energy depends only on \(n\); in multi-electron atoms, energy depends on both \(n\) and \(l\).

    Quantum numbers
  • Principal quantum number \(n\): shell, size, and energy level.
  • Azimuthal quantum number \(l\): subshell and shape; \(l=0,1,2,3\Rightarrow s,p,d,f\).
  • Magnetic quantum number \(m_l\): orientation; values from \(-l\) to \(+l\).
  • Spin quantum number \(m_s\): \(+\dfrac12\) or \(-\dfrac12\).
  • Allowed ranges: \(l=0\) to \(n-1\), \(m_l=-l\) to \(+l\).

    Orbitals, shapes, energies, nodes
  • \(s\)-orbitals are spherical; \(p\)-orbitals are dumb-bell shaped.
  • \(d\)- and \(f\)-orbitals have more complex shapes.
  • Orbitals in a subshell \(=2l+1\).
  • Orbitals in a shell \(=n^2\).
  • Total electrons in a shell \(=2n^2\).
  • Total nodes \(=n-1\), angular nodes \(=l\), radial nodes \(=n-l-1\).

    Filling of orbitals and configuration
  • Aufbau principle: lower energy orbitals fill first; practical guide uses \((n+l)\) rule.
  • If \((n+l)\) is same, lower \(n\) fills first.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom have the same four quantum numbers.
  • Hund’s rule: degenerate orbitals fill singly with parallel spins before pairing.
  • Exceptional stability: half-filled and fully-filled subshells are extra stable.
  • Important exceptions: \(\mathrm{Cr}=3d^5 4s^1\), \(\mathrm{Cu}=3d^{10}4s^1\).

    Counting patterns from PYQs
  • Count \(s/p/d\) electrons by writing full configuration, then summing only required subshells.
  • In ionic species, electron count must reflect gain/loss before comparing ratios.
  • For transition-metal cations, remove \(ns\) electrons before \((n-1)d\).
  • Isoelectronic species have same electron count; within isoelectronic series, size decreases with increasing nuclear charge.
  • \((n+l)\)-value questions require grouping electrons by occupied subshells that share the same \((n+l)\).

    Photoelectric effect
  • Einstein equation: \(K E_{\max}=h\nu-\phi\).
  • Threshold frequency: \(\nu_0=\dfrac{\phi}{h}\).
  • Threshold wavelength: \(\lambda_0=\dfrac{hc}{\phi}\).
  • Emission occurs only when \(h\nu\ge \phi\).
  • Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency; number of emitted electrons depends on intensity.
  • Useful shortcut: \(E(\mathrm{eV})=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\mathrm{nm})}\).

    Final exam-use recap
  • If same radius → compare \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z}\).
  • If same energy → compare \(\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\).
  • If spectral series asked → identify final orbit first.
  • If line count asked → use \(\dfrac{m(m-1)}{2}\).
  • If impossible quantum numbers asked → check \(l\le n-1\) first.
  • If photoelectric ratio asked → use excess frequency \((\nu-\nu_0)\), not raw \(\nu\).
  • If Cr/Cu configuration appears → use exceptional configurations directly.

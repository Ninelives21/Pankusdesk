Atomic Structure

    Bohr Radius: Hydrogen and Hydrogen-like Species
  • Core formula and trend
    • For hydrogen-like species, use \(r_n=\dfrac{n^2a_0}{Z}\).
    • Radius increases as \(n^2\) and decreases as nuclear charge \(Z\) increases.
    • For radius comparison questions, use the ratio form directly: \(\dfrac{r_A}{r_B}=\dfrac{n_A^2/Z_A}{n_B^2/Z_B}\).
    • The ratio form avoids unnecessary calculation of \(a_0\).
  • Hydrogen-like species and \(Z\) values
    • \(\mathrm{He^+}\), \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\) are hydrogen-like one-electron species.
    • Use \(Z=2\) for \(\mathrm{He^+}\), \(Z=3\) for \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), and \(Z=4\) for \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
  • Radius comparison examples
    • If \(H(n=2)\) has radius \(x\), then \(\mathrm{He^+}(n=3)\) has radius \(\dfrac{9x}{8}\), because \(\dfrac{9/2}{4}=\dfrac{9}{8}\).
    • If \(H(n=3)\) has radius \(R\), then \(\mathrm{He^+}(n=2)\) has radius \(\dfrac{2}{9}R\), because \(H(n=3)=9a_0\) and \(\mathrm{He^+}(n=2)=2a_0\).
  • Equal-radius questions
    • For equal-radius questions, compare \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z}\).
    • First orbit of hydrogen has \(\dfrac{n^2}{Z}=1\).
    • \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}(n=2)\) has the same radius as \(H(n=1)\) because \(\dfrac{2^2}{4}=1\).
  • Useful radius values for hydrogen
    • \(r_1=0.529\ \text{Å}\)
    • \(r_2=2.116\ \text{Å}\)
    • \(r_3=4.761\ \text{Å}\)
    • \(r_5=1.3225\ \text{nm}\)
    • These values help identify \(n\) quickly from a given radius.
  • Radius linked to spectral series
    • When a radius is given and spectral series is asked, first find the final orbit \(n\) using \(r_n=0.529n^2\ \text{Å}\).
    • Then identify the series from that final orbit.

    Bohr Energy and Excitation
  • Bohr energy formulae
    • For hydrogen atom, \(E_n=-\dfrac{13.6}{n^2}\ \text{eV}=-\dfrac{2.18\times10^{-18}}{n^2}\ \text{J}\).
    • For hydrogen-like species, \(E_n=-13.6\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV}=-2.18\times10^{-18}\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{J}\).
    • Energy becomes less negative as \(n\) increases.
    • The \(Z^2\) factor is essential for hydrogen-like species.
  • Using \(Z\) in hydrogen-like ions
    • For \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), \(Z=3\).
    • Therefore, \(E_1=-2.18\times10^{-18}\times9=-1.962\times10^{-17}\ \text{J}\).
    • If first-orbit radius of a hydrogen-like ion is given, use \(r_1=\dfrac{a_0}{Z}\) to identify \(Z\).
    • Then use \(E_1=-2.18\times10^{-18}Z^2\ \text{J}\).
  • Equal-energy and energy-ratio questions
    • For equal-energy questions among hydrogen-like species, compare \(\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\).
    • Equal energy requires equal \(\dfrac{Z}{n}\).
    • \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}(n=3)\) and \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}(n=4)\) have equal energy because \(\dfrac{Z}{n}=1\) for both.
    • Energy ratio for hydrogen levels \(n=1,2,3\) follows \(1:\dfrac14:\dfrac19=36:9:4\).
    • The negative sign indicates binding and does not change the magnitude ratio.
  • Excitation energy
    • For excitation, energy absorbed is positive: \(\Delta E=E_f-E_i\).
    • From \(n=1\) to \(n=5\), \(\Delta E=2.18\times10^{-18}\left(1-\dfrac{1}{25}\right)\ \text{J}\).
    • Then frequency is calculated using \(\nu=\dfrac{\Delta E}{h}\).
    • For excitation from \(n=2\) to \(n=3\) in hydrogen, \(\Delta E=13.6\left(\dfrac14-\dfrac19\right)\approx1.9\ \text{eV}\).
  • Energy-gap comparisons
    • When comparing energy gaps, compare differences, not level values.
    • For \((E_2-E_1):(E_3-E_2)\), the ratio is \(\dfrac{27}{5}\).
  • Radius-energy scaling
    • Radius and energy scale oppositely with \(n^2\).
    • For hydrogen, from \(n=2\) to \(n=3\), \(r_3=\dfrac94r_2\) and \(E_3=\dfrac49E_2\).

    Bohr Angular Momentum and Electron Speed
  • Bohr angular momentum condition
    • Bohr’s angular momentum condition is \(mvr=\dfrac{nh}{2\pi}\).
    • For ground-state hydrogen, \(n=1\), so \(mvr=\dfrac{h}{2\pi}\).
    • Numerically, \(\dfrac{h}{2\pi}\approx1.055\times10^{-34}\ \text{J s}=1055\times10^{-37}\ \text{J s}\).
  • Electron speed in Bohr orbit
    • Electron speed in the first Bohr orbit of hydrogen is approximately \(2.18\times10^6\ \text{m s}^{-1}\).
    • Hence \(\dfrac{c}{v}\approx137\), giving the ratio \(137:1\).
  • Quantum-mechanical orbital angular momentum
    • Quantum-mechanical orbital angular momentum is \(L=\sqrt{l(l+1)}\hbar\).
    • For a \(d\)-orbital, \(l=2\), so \(L=\sqrt6\,\hbar\).

    Hydrogen Spectrum and Spectral Series
  • Rydberg formula and level notation
    • Rydberg formula: \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2}-\dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\).
    • Here \(n_1\) is the final lower level and \(n_2>n_1\).
    • While comparing H and hydrogen-like ions, include the \(Z^2\) factor in the Rydberg expression.
  • Spectral series by final orbit
    • \(n_1=1\): Lyman series
    • \(n_1=2\): Balmer series
    • \(n_1=3\): Paschen series
    • \(n_1=4\): Brackett series
    • \(n_1=5\): Pfund series
    • Lyman series lies in the ultraviolet region, Balmer series in the visible region, and Paschen/Brackett/Pfund series in the infrared region.
  • Common line identification traps
    • If an electron falls to an orbit of radius \(476.1\ \text{pm}=4.761\ \text{Å}\), then \(n_f=3\), so the line belongs to Paschen series.
    • For Balmer series, always set \(n_1=2\).
    • The second Balmer line is \(4\to2\), not \(3\to2\).
    • The first Lyman line is \(2\to1\).
  • Wavelength and wavenumber comparisons
    • If the second Balmer line of H has wavelength \(\lambda\), then the first Lyman line of \(\mathrm{He^+}\) has wavelength \(\dfrac{\lambda}{16}\).
    • For wavelength-difference questions, first calculate each wavelength separately.
    • For \(4\to2\), \(\lambda_1=\dfrac{16}{3R_H}\).
    • For \(3\to1\), \(\lambda_2=\dfrac{9}{8R_H}\).
    • Hence \(\lambda_1-\lambda_2=\dfrac{101}{24R_H}\).
    • For wavenumber comparisons, use \(\overline{\nu}=RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_f^2}-\dfrac{1}{n_i^2}\right)\).
    • Do not compare wavelengths and wavenumbers as though they vary in the same direction.
  • Number of spectral lines
    • Total number of spectral lines among \(N\) involved energy levels is \(\dfrac{N(N-1)}{2}\).
    • From \(n=6\) down to \(n=2\), the involved levels are \(6,5,4,3,2\).
    • Therefore, lines \(=\dfrac{5(4)}{2}=10\).

    Electromagnetic Radiation and Photon Energy
  • Core relations
    • \(c=\nu\lambda\)
    • \(E=h\nu=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)
    • \(\overline{\nu}=\dfrac{1}{\lambda}\)
  • Energy trends
    • Photon energy is directly proportional to frequency and wavenumber: \(E\propto\nu\), \(E\propto\overline{\nu}\).
    • Photon energy is inversely proportional to wavelength: \(E\propto\dfrac{1}{\lambda}\).
    • Shorter wavelength means higher frequency and higher energy.
    • X-rays have much shorter wavelength than microwaves.
  • Photon energy shortcut
    • Useful shortcut: \(E(\text{eV})=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})}\).
    • For 310 nm radiation, \(E=4\ \text{eV}\).
    • For 300 nm radiation, \(E\approx4.13\ \text{eV}\).
  • Photon-emission power questions
    • For photon-emission power questions, use \(P=N\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\), where \(N\) is the number of photons emitted per second.
    • Therefore, \(\lambda=\dfrac{Nhc}{P}\).
  • Unit conversion
    • When wavelength is required in Å, use \(1\ \text{Å}=10^{-10}\ \text{m}\).

    Photoelectric Effect
  • Einstein’s photoelectric equation
    • Einstein’s photoelectric equation is \(K_{\max}=h\nu-\phi=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}-\phi\), where \(\phi\) is the work function.
    • For threshold-frequency problems, use \(K_{\max}=h(\nu-\nu_0)\).
    • Threshold frequency can be found from \(\nu_0=\nu-\dfrac{K}{h}\) when incident frequency and kinetic energy are given.
  • Emission condition and threshold idea
    • Emission occurs only when photon energy is at least equal to the work function: \(h\nu\geq\phi\).
    • If \(h\nu<\phi\), no photoelectrons are emitted.
    • There is no observable time lag in photoelectric emission when the incident frequency is sufficient.
  • Kinetic energy and velocity questions
    • For velocity questions, first find \(K_{\max}\), then use \(K_{\max}=\dfrac12mv^2\).
    • Do not use total photon energy as kinetic energy.
    • For 310 nm radiation, photon energy is \(4\ \text{eV}\).
    • If work function is \(3.55\ \text{eV}\), leftover kinetic energy is \(0.45\ \text{eV}\), giving \(v\approx4\times10^5\ \text{m s}^{-1}\).
  • Ratio questions
    • In ratio questions, \(h\) cancels and the ratio depends on \((\nu-\nu_0)\), not on \(\nu\) directly.
    • If \(\nu_0=10^{15}\ \text{s}^{-1}\), then for \(1.5\times10^{15}\) and \(2.0\times10^{15}\ \text{s}^{-1}\), kinetic energies are in the ratio \(0.5:1.0=1:2\).
  • Unit consistency in work-function questions
    • For mixed-unit work-function questions, keep energies consistently either per photon or per mole.
    • If kinetic energy is in \(\text{J mol}^{-1}\), convert photon energy to molar energy using \(N_A\) before subtracting.
  • Effect of frequency, intensity and metal choice
    • Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency, not intensity.
    • Intensity affects the number of emitted electrons when frequency is above threshold.
    • Alkali metals such as K, Rb and Cs show photoelectric effect easily because of low work function.

    de Broglie relation and Heisenberg uncertainty principle
  • de Broglie relation and momentum
    • de Broglie relation: \(\lambda=\dfrac{h}{mv}=\dfrac{h}{p}\).
    • Hence momentum \(p=\dfrac{h}{\lambda}\).
    • Convert wavelength carefully before substitution.
    • For \(10^3\ \text{nm}=10^{-6}\ \text{m}\), \(p=\dfrac{6.625\times10^{-34}}{10^{-6}}=6.625\times10^{-28}\ \text{kg m s}^{-1}\).
  • Combining matter waves with radiation wavelength
    • Combining de Broglie wavelength with radiation wavelength gives \(\dfrac{h}{mv}=\dfrac{C}{\nu}\).
    • Therefore, \(v=\dfrac{h\nu}{mC}\).
  • Heisenberg uncertainty principle
    • Heisenberg uncertainty principle: \(\Delta x\Delta p\geq\dfrac{h}{4\pi}\).
    • Use \(\Delta p=m\Delta v\).
  • Uncertainty calculation traps
    • For percentage-accuracy questions, calculate \(\Delta v\) first and then use \(\dfrac{\Delta v}{v}\times100\).
    • Convert mass to kg before using SI units.
    • If a question gives \(\Delta v=\dfrac{1}{2m}\sqrt{\dfrac{h}{\pi}}\), then \(\Delta p=m\Delta v=\dfrac12\sqrt{\dfrac{h}{\pi}}\).
    • The corresponding \(\Delta x\) gives \(\Delta x:\Delta p=1:1\).

    Quantum Mechanical Model and Orbitals
  • Wave function and probability density
    • In the quantum mechanical model, \(\psi\) is the wave function.
    • \(|\psi|^2\) gives probability density.
    • Probability density is not \(|\psi|^3\).
  • Meaning of orbital
    • An orbital is the three-dimensional region of maximum probability of finding the electron.
    • It is not a fixed circular path.
  • Orbital energy and degeneracy
    • For hydrogen atom, orbital energy depends only on \(n\), so \(2s\) and \(2p\) are degenerate.
    • In multi-electron atoms, energy depends on both \(n\) and \(l\), so \(2s\) and \(2p\) are not necessarily equal in energy.
    • For orbitals with the same \(n\) and \(l\) but different \(m_l\), energies are the same in the absence of an external field.
    • Example: \(n=3,l=2,m_l=+2\) and \(n=3,l=2,m_l=-2\) are both \(3d\)-orbitals of the same energy.
  • Nodes in orbitals
    • Radial nodes \(=n-l-1\)
    • Angular nodes \(=l\)
    • Total nodes \(=n-1\)

    Quantum Numbers
  • Four quantum numbers and their meaning
    • The four quantum numbers are \(n,l,m_l,m_s\).
    • \(n\) gives shell, size and energy.
    • \(l\) gives subshell and shape.
    • \(m_l\) gives orientation.
    • \(m_s\) gives electron spin.
  • Subshell mapping from \(l\)
    • \(l=0\rightarrow s\)
    • \(l=1\rightarrow p\)
    • \(l=2\rightarrow d\)
    • \(l=3\rightarrow f\)
  • Allowed values of quantum numbers
    • \(n=1,2,3,\dots\)
    • \(l=0\) to \(n-1\)
    • \(m_l=-l\) to \(+l\)
    • \(m_s=+\dfrac12\) or \(-\dfrac12\)
    • For \(n=3\), possible \(l\) values are only \(0,1,2\). Therefore a set containing \(n=3,l=3\) is impossible.
  • Orbitals and electron capacity
    • Number of orbitals in a subshell \(=2l+1\).
    • Thus \(s,p,d,f\) have \(1,3,5,7\) orbitals respectively.
    • Total number of orbitals in the \(n^{\text{th}}\) shell \(=n^2\).
    • For \(n=4\), total orbitals \(=16\).
    • Maximum electrons in a shell \(=2n^2\).
    • Maximum electrons with spin \(+\dfrac12\) in a shell is only \(n^2\), because only one such electron can occupy each orbital.
  • Spin quantum number
    • Spin quantum number describes the orientation of electron spin, not nuclear spin.

    Electronic Configuration and Electron Counting
  • Rules governing electronic configuration
    • Electronic configuration is governed by Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle and Hund’s rule.
    • Aufbau principle uses increasing orbital energy.
    • The \((n+l)\) rule helps compare subshell energies: lower \((n+l)\) fills first; if equal, lower \(n\) fills first.
    • Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers.
    • An orbital can hold maximum two electrons with opposite spins.
    • Hund’s rule: in degenerate orbitals, electrons enter singly with parallel spins before pairing.
  • Subshell electron capacity
    • \(s=2\)
    • \(p=6\)
    • \(d=10\)
    • \(f=14\)
  • Electron counting by subshell type
    • For electron counting by \(l\), count all electrons in that subshell type across the configuration.
    • For Sr \((Z=38)\), total \(s\)-electrons \(=10\) and total \(d\)-electrons \(=10\).
  • Ion configuration before counting
    • For ions, write the configuration after ion formation before counting.
    • In transition-metal ions, remove \(4s\) electrons before \(3d\) electrons.
    • \(K^+\) and \(\mathrm{Cr^{3+}}\) both give total \(s:p=6:12=1:2\) after proper ion configuration is considered.
  • Isoelectronic species
    • Isoelectronic species have the same number of electrons.
    • \(O^{2-}\), \(F^-\), \(Na^+\), and \(Mg^{2+}\) each have 10 electrons.

    Exceptions and Stability of Half-filled/Fully-filled Orbitals
  • Exceptional configurations
    • Chromium and copper show exceptional electronic configurations due to the extra stability of half-filled and fully-filled \(d\)-subshells.
    • \(\mathrm{Cr}=[Ar]\,3d^5\,4s^1\), not \([Ar]\,3d^4\,4s^2\).
    • \(\mathrm{Cu}=[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^1\), not \([Ar]\,3d^9\,4s^2\).
  • Counting using actual configuration
    • For \(Z=24\), use actual chromium configuration while counting \((n+l)\) values.
    • \((n+l)=3\Rightarrow2p^6+3s^2=8\)
    • \((n+l)=4\Rightarrow3p^6+4s^1=7\)
    • \((n+l)=5\Rightarrow3d^5=5\)
  • Stability of half-filled and fully-filled orbitals
    • Half-filled and completely filled orbitals are more stable, not less stable.
    • This explains why nitrogen \((2p^3)\) has higher first ionisation enthalpy than oxygen \((2p^4)\).
    • Oxygen has lower first ionisation enthalpy than nitrogen because oxygen has paired-electron repulsion in \(2p^4\), while nitrogen has stable half-filled \(2p^3\).

    Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Hydrogen-like species and Bohr model mistakes
    • Do not forget \(Z\) in hydrogen-like radius and energy formulas.
    • Radius depends on \(\dfrac{1}{Z}\), while energy depends on \(Z^2\).
  • Spectral series and wavelength mistakes
    • Do not identify spectral series from the initial orbit. Series is always decided by the final orbit.
    • Do not subtract wavenumbers when the question asks for difference in wavelengths; calculate wavelengths first.
  • Photoelectric effect mistakes
    • Do not use incident frequency ratio directly in photoelectric kinetic-energy ratio questions; subtract threshold frequency first.
    • Do not use \(K=mv^2\); kinetic energy is \(K=\dfrac12mv^2\).
  • Quantum model and orbital mistakes
    • Do not write probability density as \(|\psi|^3\); it is \(|\psi|^2\).
    • Do not apply hydrogen-orbital degeneracy blindly to multi-electron atoms.
  • Electronic configuration mistakes
    • Do not ignore exceptional configurations of chromium and copper.
    • Do not confuse total electron capacity \(2n^2\) with number of orbitals \(n^2\) or maximum same-spin electrons \(n^2\).
  • Unit conversion mistakes
    • Always convert units carefully: nm to m, Å to m, pm to Å, eV to J, and grams to kg.

    Variables Used
  • Quantum numbers
    • \(n\) = principal quantum number
    • \(l\) = azimuthal quantum number
    • \(m_l\) = magnetic quantum number
    • \(m_s\) = spin quantum number
  • Bohr model variables
    • \(Z\) = atomic number / nuclear charge of hydrogen-like species
    • \(a_0\) = Bohr radius of first orbit of hydrogen
    • \(r_n\) = radius of \(n^{\text{th}}\) Bohr orbit
    • \(E_n\) = energy of electron in \(n^{\text{th}}\) orbit
    • \(\Delta E\) = energy difference between two levels
  • Radiation and photon variables
    • \(\lambda\) = wavelength
    • \(\nu\) = frequency
    • \(\overline{\nu}\) = wavenumber
    • \(\nu_0\) = threshold frequency
    • \(c\) or \(C\) = speed of light
    • \(h\) = Planck’s constant
    • \(\hbar\) = \(\dfrac{h}{2\pi}\)
    • \(N\) = number of photons emitted per second
    • \(P\) = power
  • Photoelectric-effect variables
    • \(\phi\) = work function
    • \(K_{\max}\) = maximum kinetic energy of photoelectron
  • de Broglie and uncertainty variables
    • \(p\) = momentum
    • \(\Delta x\) = uncertainty in position
    • \(\Delta p\) = uncertainty in momentum
    • \(\Delta v\) = uncertainty in velocity

    Fundamental Particles of Atom
  • Atoms consist of three subatomic particles: electron (charge −1, very small mass), proton (charge +1, ~1840 times mass of electron), and neutron (neutral, mass comparable to proton).
  • Atomic number \(Z\) = number of protons; mass number \(A\) = protons + neutrons.
  • Most of the mass of atom is concentrated in nucleus; electrons occupy the extra-nuclear region.

    Atomic Models: Thomson and Rutherford
  • Thomson’s model: atom as a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded (plum pudding model); fails to explain scattering results.
  • Rutherford’s model: atom has a small, dense, positively charged nucleus; most of atom is empty space; electrons revolve around nucleus.
  • Limitation: according to classical physics, accelerating electrons should lose energy and collapse into nucleus (atom instability problem).
  • Key inference: nucleus contains almost all mass; size of nucleus ≪ size of atom.

    Electromagnetic Radiation and Spectrum
  • Electromagnetic radiation consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and direction of propagation.
  • Velocity relation: \(c = \nu\lambda\), where \(c\) = speed of light \((3 \times 10^8\ \text{m s}^{-1})\).
  • Photon energy: \(E = h\nu = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\); energy \(\propto\) frequency \(\propto\) wavenumber and inversely \(\propto\) wavelength.
  • Wavenumber: \(\bar{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda}\) (in cm\(^{-1}\) or m\(^{-1}\)).
  • Shorter wavelength → higher energy (X-rays > UV > visible > IR > microwaves).
  • Useful relation: \(E(\text{eV}) \approx \dfrac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})}\).

    Planck’s Quantum Theory
  • Energy is emitted/absorbed in discrete packets called quanta (photons).
  • Energy quantization: \(E = nh\nu\), where \(n\) = integer.
  • Explains black body radiation and failure of classical theory at high frequencies.
  • Key idea: energy exchange is not continuous but quantized.

    Photoelectric Effect
  • Emission of electrons from metal surface occurs only if incident frequency ≥ threshold frequency \((\nu_0)\).
  • Einstein equation: \(h\nu = \phi + K.E\) or \(K_{\max} = h\nu - \phi\), where \(\phi\) = work function.
  • Key observations: no time lag; K.E depends on frequency (not intensity); intensity controls number of electrons.
  • Important: \(\nu - \nu_0\) determines kinetic energy in comparison problems.

    Line Spectrum of Hydrogen
  • Continuous spectrum: all wavelengths; line spectrum: discrete wavelengths (characteristic of atoms).
  • Hydrogen shows series of lines grouped as Lyman (UV), Balmer (visible), Paschen, Brackett, Pfund (IR).
  • Rydberg equation:
    \(\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\), where \(n_2 > n_1\)
  • Key insight: spectral series depends on final orbit \((n_1)\), not initial.
  • Total spectral lines from \(n\) levels = \(\dfrac{n(n - 1)}{2}\).

    Bohr’s Model of Hydrogen Atom
  • Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits (stationary states) without energy loss.
  • Angular momentum quantization:
    \(mvr = \dfrac{nh}{2\pi}\)
  • Energy of electron:
    \(E_n = \dfrac{-13.6Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV}\)
  • Radius of orbit:
    \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2a_0}{Z}\), where \(a_0\) = 0.529 Å
  • Energy transition:
    \(\Delta E = h\nu = E_f - E_i\)
  • Key patterns:
    • Energy \(\propto -\dfrac{1}{n^2}\); radius \(\propto n^2\)
    • Higher \(n\) → larger radius, less negative energy
    • \(Z^2\) scaling for hydrogen-like species
  • Limitations: fails for multi-electron atoms; contradicts uncertainty principle.

    Dual Nature of Matter
  • de Broglie wavelength:
    \(\lambda = \dfrac{h}{mv} = \dfrac{h}{p}\)
  • Shows wave nature of particles; significant only for microscopic particles.
  • Electron diffraction experimentally confirms wave nature of electrons.

    Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle
  • It is impossible to simultaneously determine exact position and momentum of an electron.
  • Mathematically:
    \(\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\)
  • Explains why fixed orbits (Bohr model) are not physically realistic.

    Quantum Mechanical Model of Atom
  • Electron behavior described by wave function \(\psi\) (solution of Schrödinger equation).
  • Probability density = \(|\psi|^2\) gives likelihood of finding electron in space.
  • Orbitals are probability regions, not fixed paths.

    Quantum Numbers
  • Principal \((n)\): energy level, size; max electrons = \(2n^2\).
  • Azimuthal \((l)\): subshell/shape; values 0 to \(n - 1\).
  • Magnetic \((m_l)\): orientation; values \(-l\) to \(+l\).
  • Spin \((m_s)\): \(+\dfrac{1}{2}\) or \(-\dfrac{1}{2}\).
  • Number of orbitals in subshell = \(2l + 1\); total orbitals in shell = \(n^2\).

    Shapes and Energies of Orbitals
  • s: spherical; p: dumbbell; d: cloverleaf.
  • Nodes: total nodes = \(n - 1\); radial = \(n - l - 1\); angular = \(l\).
  • For hydrogen: energy depends only on \(n\); for multi-electron atoms: depends on \(n\) and \(l\).

    Electronic Configuration and Filling Rules
  • Aufbau principle: orbitals filled in increasing energy order \((n + l)\) rule.
  • Pauli principle: max 2 electrons per orbital with opposite spins.
  • Hund’s rule: maximum multiplicity; electrons fill degenerate orbitals singly first.
  • Maximum electrons: s = 2, p = 6, d = 10, f = 14.

    Stability of Half-filled and Fully-filled Orbitals
  • Half-filled and fully-filled subshells have extra stability due to symmetry and exchange energy.
  • Important exceptions:
    • Cr: [Ar] 3d5 4s1
    • Cu: [Ar] 3d10 4s1
  • Explains anomalies like N having higher ionization enthalpy than O.

    \(r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)

      \(x = \frac{(2)^2 a_0}{1} = 4a_0\)

      Therefore,

      \(a_0 = \frac{x}{4}\)

    2. Now find radius for \(\mathrm{He}^+\)

      For \(\mathrm{He}^+\):

      \(n = 3,\ Z = 2\)

      Hence,

      \(r_n = \frac{(3)^2 a_0}{2} = \frac{9a_0}{2}\)

      Substitute \(a_0 = \frac{x}{4}\):

      \(r_n = \frac{9}{2}\cdot\frac{x}{4} = \frac{9x}{8}\)

    \(\boxed{\frac{9x}{8}}\)

    Why this works:

    • In Bohr model, orbital radius depends on both principal quantum number and nuclear charge:

      \(r_n \propto \frac{n^2}{Z}\)

    • As \(n\) increases, radius increases as \(n^2\).
    • As nuclear charge \(Z\) increases, electron is pulled more strongly, so radius decreases.
    • \(\mathrm{He}^+\) is a hydrogen-like species because it has only one electron, so the same Bohr formula applies.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • For hydrogen-like atoms:

      \(r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)

    • For hydrogen atom, \(Z=1\), so:

      \(r_n = n^2 a_0\)

    • For \(\mathrm{He}^+\), \(\mathrm{Li}^{2+}\), \(\mathrm{Be}^{3+}\), etc., use the same formula with the proper value of \(Z\).
    • Radius varies directly as \(n^2\) and inversely as \(Z\).

    • Do not calculate actual \(a_0\) in pm.
    • Use ratio method directly:

      \(\frac{r_2}{r_1} = \frac{n_2^2/Z_2}{n_1^2/Z_1}\)

      \(\frac{r(\mathrm{He}^+, n=3)}{r(\mathrm{H}, n=2)} = \frac{9/2}{4/1} = \frac{9}{8}\)

      \(r(\mathrm{He}^+, n=3)=\frac{9x}{8}\)

    • This is the fastest exam method.

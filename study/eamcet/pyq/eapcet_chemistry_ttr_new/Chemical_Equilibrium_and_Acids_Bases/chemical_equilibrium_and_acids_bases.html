Chemical Equilibrium and Acids Bases

    Variables used
  • Equilibrium constants
    • Kc: equilibrium constant in terms of molar concentration
    • Kp: equilibrium constant in terms of partial pressure
    • Q: reaction quotient
  • Ionic equilibrium constants
    • Ka: acid ionisation constant
    • Kb: base ionisation constant
    • Kw: ionic product of water
    • Ksp: solubility product
    • pKa: \(-\log K_a\)
  • Thermodynamic variables
    • Δng: moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants
    • ΔG°: standard Gibbs energy change
    • R: gas constant
    • T: absolute temperature in kelvin
  • Solubility and dissociation variables
    • S: molar solubility
    • \(\alpha\): degree of dissociation

    1. Equilibrium expression: write it cleanly before doing anything else
  • Write the equilibrium expression first
    • For \(aA+bB \rightleftharpoons cC+dD\), write \(K_c=\dfrac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\) first.
    • For gas-phase questions, write the matching \(K_p\) form.
    • Many PYQs become one-line substitutions once the balanced equation is written correctly.
  • Use equilibrium values, not initial values
    • In 1:1:1:1 or similar problems, assign the shift as \(x\).
    • Change each species according to stoichiometry.
    • Substitute only the equilibrium values into \(K_c\).
  • Let \(K\) decide the direction of shift
    • If equal initial amounts of reactants and products are given, do not guess the direction blindly.
    • Use \((\text{initial} \pm x)\) carefully.
    • Then let the value of \(K\) decide the shift.
  • Use stoichiometry immediately when one equilibrium amount is given
    • For reactions such as \(\mathrm{PCl_5 \rightleftharpoons PCl_3 + Cl_2}\), if one product amount at equilibrium is given, immediately use stoichiometry.
    • This gives the other product amount and the remaining reactant.

    2. Gaseous equilibrium: the few formulas that keep repeating
  • \(K_p\)-\(K_c\) relation
    • Use \(K_p=K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g}\), where \(\Delta n_g\) is counted only for gaseous species.
    • If \(\Delta n_g=0\), then \(K_p=K_c\).
    • If \(\Delta n_g<0\), then \(K_p/K_c=(RT)^{\Delta n_g}\) gives an inverse power.
  • Equal partial-pressure shortcut
    • When all equilibrium partial pressures are equal, substitute the same \(p\) everywhere.
    • The final result depends only on the net exponent difference, so \(K_p=(p)^{\Delta n_g}\).
  • Dissociation of \(A \rightleftharpoons 2B\)
    • For \(A \rightleftharpoons 2B\) starting with pure \(A\), mole-fraction partial pressures are the cleanest route.
    • Useful special result for \(N_2O_4 \rightleftharpoons 2NO_2\): \(K_p=\dfrac{4\alpha^2 p}{1-\alpha^2}\).
    • Therefore, \(\alpha=\sqrt{\dfrac{K_p/p}{4+K_p/p}}\).
  • Using given equal-pressure conditions
    • For \(2SO_2+O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3\), if \(p_{SO_2}=p_{SO_3}\), then the expression collapses to \(K_p=\dfrac{1}{p_{O_2}}\).
    • Such equal-pressure conditions are meant to simplify the algebra, so use them immediately.

    3. How equilibrium constants change when the equation is changed
  • If the reaction is reversed, the new equilibrium constant becomes \(1/K\). If every coefficient is multiplied by \(n\), the new constant becomes \(K^n\); if divided by \(n\), it becomes \(K^{1/n}\).
  • When the target reaction is built by adding or scaling given equations, do the same operations on their equilibrium constants: multiply the constants and raise each one to the same power as the corresponding scaled equation.
  • This is one of the easiest places to lose marks by doing chemistry correctly but algebra carelessly, so convert the target reaction first and only then touch the constants.

    4. Fast meaning of \(K\), \(Q\), and \(\Delta G^\circ\)
  • What changes \(K\)
    • At constant temperature, \(K\) changes only with temperature.
    • \(K\) does not change with pressure, concentration, or catalyst.
    • A catalyst only helps the system reach equilibrium faster.
  • \(\Delta G^\circ\) and \(K\)
    • The bridge formula to remember is \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303RT\log K\).
    • If \(\Delta G^\circ\) is negative, then \(K>1\).
    • If \(\Delta G^\circ\) is positive, then \(K<1\).
    • In numericals based on \(\Delta G^\circ\), convert kJ to J before substitution.
  • Using \(Q\) to predict direction
    • If \(Q<K\), the reaction shifts forward.
    • If \(Q>K\), the reaction shifts backward.
    • If \(Q=K\), the system is at equilibrium.

    5. Le Chatelier shortcuts that are actually useful in MCQs
  • Pressure change in gaseous equilibrium
    • Pressure increase shifts a gaseous equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas.
    • Products are favoured by pressure increase only when \(\Delta n_g<0\).
    • This is the quickest way to handle pressure-based equilibrium questions.
  • Effect on equilibrium constant
    • Temperature is the only ordinary factor here that changes \(K\).
    • Concentration and pressure can change the position of equilibrium.
    • They do not change the value of the equilibrium constant if temperature stays fixed.
  • Industrial-equilibrium shortcuts
    • For industrial equilibria such as \(N_2+3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3\) and \(2SO_2+O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3\), link the shift directly to gaseous mole count and reaction heat.
    • Do not memorise isolated statements without checking the mole-count and heat-effect logic.

    6. Heterogeneous equilibrium: the standard omission rule
  • What to omit
    • In heterogeneous equilibrium, pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the equilibrium expression.
    • They are omitted because their active mass is taken constant.
    • This omission is based on phase, not on “small amount”.
  • What to include
    • Include gases.
    • Include dissolved species.
    • Omit pure solids and pure liquids.
  • Standard example
    • For \(A(s)\rightleftharpoons B(s)+C(g)\), write \(K_c=[C]\) and \(K_p=P_C\).

    7. Acids, bases, and oxyacid basics that repeatedly get asked
  • Lewis acid-base idea
    • Lewis base means electron-pair donor.
    • \(NH_3\) is a Lewis base because nitrogen has a lone pair available for donation.
  • Basicity of oxyacids
    • For oxyacids, basicity means the number of ionisable H atoms attached to O as \(-OH\).
    • Do not count the total number of H atoms in the formula blindly.
    • Examples:
      • \(H_3PO_3\) is dibasic.
      • \(H_3PO_2\) is monobasic.
      • \(H_2SO_4\) is dibasic.
    • If the question is about di-, tri-, or polybasic acids, count replaceable protons only.
    • This is a concept question disguised as a memory question.
  • Acidity order of same-type oxoacids
    • For oxoacids of the same type \(HOX\), acidity increases with electronegativity of the central atom.
    • The stronger \(-I\) effect weakens the O–H bond.
    • Therefore, \(HClO > HBrO > HIO\).

    8. Water, pH, and acid-base mixing numericals
  • Neutral water and temperature
    • Neutral water does not always have pH \(7\).
    • As temperature rises, \(K_w\) increases, so the pH of pure water can fall below \(7\).
    • It still remains neutral because \([H^+]=[OH^-]\).
  • Strong acid-strong base mixing method
    • Convert pH or pOH to molarity if needed.
    • Convert concentration to moles using volume.
    • Subtract excess \(H^+\) or \(OH^-\).
    • Divide by final total volume.
    • Only then calculate pH or pOH.
  • Main trap in neutralisation numericals
    • Never decide the final pH from concentration values before doing mole subtraction.
    • Mixing is a mole problem first and a pH problem only after that.

    9. Buffers: the one formula and the one condition to remember
  • Acidic buffer formula
    • For an acidic buffer, use \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a+\log\dfrac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]}\).
    • If the salt and acid concentrations are equal, then \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a\).
  • Effect of dilution
    • Dilution does not change the pH of a buffer if both components are diluted equally.
    • This is because the ratio \([\text{salt}]/[\text{acid}]\) stays unchanged.
  • Condition for buffer formation
    • A buffer forms only when a weak acid or weak base and its conjugate partner both remain in solution after partial neutralisation.
    • Complete neutralisation in equivalent amounts does not give a buffer.

    10. Salt hydrolysis: quick classification before calculation
  • Classify the salt first
    • Strong acid + weak base salt gives an acidic solution.
    • Weak acid + strong base salt gives a basic solution.
    • Strong acid + strong base salt gives a neutral solution.
    • This classification solves many direct PYQs without any formula work.
  • Useful salt-hydrolysis anchors
    • \(AlCl_3\) behaves acidic in water.
    • \(CH_3COONa\) behaves basic in water.
    • \(KCl\) is neutral in water.
  • Do not mix salt hydrolysis with oxide nature
    • \(Al_2O_3\) is amphoteric.
    • This is a separate property from salt hydrolysis and is easy to mix up if you rush.

    11. Solubility product: the standard forms worth memorising
  • Standard \(K_{sp}\)-solubility forms
    • For a \(1:1\) salt \(AB\), if molar solubility is \(S\), then \(K_{sp}=S^2\).
    • For \(AB_2\) or \(A_2B\), if molar solubility is \(S\), then \(K_{sp}=4S^3\).
    • These forms are worth remembering because they recur directly in PYQs.
  • Write ionic breakdown first
    • For \(PbCl_2\), write \(PbCl_2 \rightleftharpoons Pb^{2+}+2Cl^-\).
    • If solubility is \(S\), then \(K_{sp}=S(2S)^2=4S^3\).
    • Writing the ionic breakdown first makes the formula obvious and avoids coefficient mistakes.
  • Mass or volume dissolved questions
    • When the question asks volume or mass dissolved, first find \(S\) in mol L\(^{-1}\).
    • Then convert \(S\) to g L\(^{-1}\) using molar mass.
    • Only after that should you compute the required volume.
  • Solubility order from \(K_{sp}\)
    • For \(1:1\) salts such as metal sulphides, smaller \(K_{sp}\) means lower solubility.
    • Order questions can often be solved by comparing the magnitudes of \(K_{sp}\) directly.

    12. Common ion effect in solubility: the exam pattern
  • Common-ion approximation
    • When a sparingly soluble salt is placed in a solution already containing a common ion, treat that pre-existing ion concentration as fixed if the additional solubility is tiny.
    • Then substitute that common-ion concentration directly into the \(K_{sp}\) expression and solve for the small solubility.
  • Example: \(\mathrm{Cd(OH)_2}\) in KOH
    • For \(\mathrm{Cd(OH)_2 \rightleftharpoons Cd^{2+}+2OH^-}\) in \(0.1\,\mathrm{M}\) KOH, use \([OH^-]\approx 0.1\).
    • Therefore, \(K_{sp}=s(0.1)^2\).
    • This approximation is exactly what the question expects.
  • Mixed saturated-salt problems
    • In mixed saturated-salt problems, first use the known salt to find the common-ion concentration.
    • Then plug that value into the second salt’s \(K_{sp}\).
    • Example: find \([CO_3^{2-}]\) from \(MgCO_3\) first, then use it for \(Ag_2CO_3\).

    13. Must-know equations to keep on the mental front page
  • Equilibrium constant equations
    • \(K_c=\dfrac{[products]^{\text{coefficients}}}{[reactants]^{\text{coefficients}}}\)
    • \(K_p=K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g}\)
  • Thermodynamic link
    • \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303RT\log K\)
  • Acid-base and buffer equations
    • \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a+\log\dfrac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]}\)
    • \(K_w=[H^+][OH^-]\)
  • Important gaseous equilibria
    • \(N_2+3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3\)
    • \(2SO_2+O_2 \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3\)
  • Important solubility equilibria
    • \(PbCl_2 \rightleftharpoons Pb^{2+}+2Cl^-\)
    • \(Cd(OH)_2 \rightleftharpoons Cd^{2+}+2OH^-\)

    14. Quick pitfalls that PYQs keep targeting
  • Equilibrium-expression mistakes
    • Do not include pure solids or pure liquids in equilibrium expressions.
  • Equilibrium-constant mistakes
    • Do not change \(K\) because of pressure, concentration, or catalyst when temperature is constant.
  • Oxyacid basicity mistakes
    • Do not count all hydrogen atoms as ionisable in oxyacids.
    • Count only those attached through oxygen.
  • Acid-base mixing mistakes
    • Do not calculate final pH after mixing acid and base without doing mole subtraction.
    • Use the final total volume before calculating pH.
  • \(K_{sp}\) mistakes
    • Do not apply a memorised \(K_{sp}\) formula without first writing the ionic dissociation.
    • Most mistakes in this chapter start there.

    1. Nature of equilibrium: the big picture
  • Chemical equilibrium is dynamic, not static: forward and reverse reactions continue, but at equilibrium their rates become equal, so concentrations stay constant.
  • Equilibrium is established only in a closed system; if reactants or products escape, true equilibrium is not maintained.
  • Physical equilibria and chemical equilibria both matter for EAPCET. Physical examples include liquid \(\rightleftharpoons\) vapour, solid \(\rightleftharpoons\) vapour, and solute \(\rightleftharpoons\) saturated solution.
  • A catalyst helps equilibrium be reached faster, but does not change the equilibrium composition or value of \(K\).
  • Quick exam idea: “constant concentration” does not mean reaction has stopped; it means opposite reactions are occurring at equal rates.

    2. Homogeneous and heterogeneous equilibrium
  • Homogeneous equilibrium: all reactants and products are in the same phase, usually all gases or all in one liquid phase. Example: \(N_2(g)+3H_2(g)\rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)\).
  • Heterogeneous equilibrium: reactants and products are in different phases. Example: \(CaCO_3(s)\rightleftharpoons CaO(s)+CO_2(g)\).
  • PYQ favourite: in heterogeneous equilibrium, pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the equilibrium expression because their concentration is effectively constant.
  • So for \(A(s)\rightleftharpoons B(s)+C(g)\), write \(K_c=[C]\) and \(K_p=P_C\), not an expression including the solids.

    3. Law of mass action and equilibrium constant
  • For \(aA+bB\rightleftharpoons cC+dD\), the equilibrium constant in concentration form is \(K_c=\dfrac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\), using only equilibrium concentrations.
  • For gaseous systems, partial pressures can be used: \(K_p=\dfrac{(P_C)^c(P_D)^d}{(P_A)^a(P_B)^b}\).
  • Must-know relation: \(K_p=K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g}\), where \(\Delta n_g=\) gaseous product moles \(-\) gaseous reactant moles.
  • For \(N_2+3H_2\rightleftharpoons 2NH_3\), \(\Delta n_g=2-4=-2\), so \(\dfrac{K_p}{K_c}=(RT)^{-2}=\dfrac{1}{(RT)^2}\).
  • If \(\Delta n_g=0\), then \(K_p=K_c\). This is a common shortcut in numericals.
  • The magnitude of \(K\) shows position of equilibrium: \(K\gg1\) means products are favoured, \(K\ll1\) means reactants are favoured.
  • Very common PYQ rule: if an equation is reversed, new constant becomes \(1/K\); if all coefficients are multiplied by \(n\), new constant becomes \(K^n\); if divided by \(n\), new constant becomes \(K^{1/n}\).

    4. Reaction quotient, direction prediction, and \(\Delta G^\circ\)
  • \(Q\) has the same form as the equilibrium constant expression, but it is calculated using the concentrations or partial pressures at any instant.
  • If \(Q<K\), the reaction proceeds forward; if \(Q>K\), it shifts backward; if \(Q=K\), the system is at equilibrium.
  • Must-remember bridge formula from PYQs: \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303RT\log K\).
  • Direct exam meaning: negative \(\Delta G^\circ\) implies \(K>1\), so products are favoured under standard conditions; positive \(\Delta G^\circ\) implies \(K<1\).
  • In numericals, convert kJ to J before substituting in the \(\Delta G^\circ\) relation.

    5. Factors affecting equilibrium: Le Chatelier principle
  • Le Chatelier principle: when an equilibrium is disturbed by change in concentration, pressure, or temperature, the system shifts in the direction that opposes that disturbance.
  • Concentration change: adding a reactant drives the system toward products; adding a product drives it toward reactants.
  • Pressure change matters only when gases are involved. Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas.
  • So products are favoured on pressure increase only when \(\Delta n_g<0\).
  • Temperature change is special because it changes the value of \(K\). For exothermic reactions, heat behaves like a product; for endothermic reactions, heat behaves like a reactant.
  • Catalyst: changes rate, not equilibrium position and not \(K\).
  • Very common trap: changing concentration or pressure may shift equilibrium position, but does not change \(K\) if temperature is unchanged.

    6. Industrial applications: Haber and Contact process
  • Haber process: \(N_2(g)+3H_2(g)\rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g)\), \(\Delta H<0\). Low temperature and high pressure favour ammonia formation; catalyst speeds attainment of equilibrium.
  • Industrial idea: ammonia is continuously removed, and \(N_2\) and \(H_2\) are continuously supplied, so the forward reaction is promoted.
  • Contact process: \(2SO_2(g)+O_2(g)\rightleftharpoons 2SO_3(g)\), \(\Delta H<0\). Lower temperature and higher pressure favour \(SO_3\), though practical conditions balance yield with rate.
  • PYQ-flavoured point: for this reaction, if \(p_{SO_2}=p_{SO_3}\), then \(K_p=\dfrac{1}{p_{O_2}}\).

    7. Acids and bases: Arrhenius, Bronsted-Lowry, and Lewis
  • Arrhenius acid: gives \(H^+\) / \(H_3O^+\) in water. Example: \(HCl+H_2O\rightarrow H_3O^++Cl^-\).
  • Arrhenius base: gives \(OH^-\) in water. Example: \(NaOH\rightarrow Na^++OH^-\).
  • Limitation of Arrhenius concept: it is restricted to aqueous solutions and cannot explain species like \(NH_3\) and \(AlCl_3\) properly.
  • Bronsted-Lowry acid: proton donor; Bronsted-Lowry base: proton acceptor. Example: \(NH_3+H_2O\rightleftharpoons NH_4^++OH^-\).
  • This concept introduces conjugate acid-base pairs: \(NH_4^+/NH_3\) and \(H_2O/OH^-\). Stronger acid \(\Rightarrow\) weaker conjugate base.
  • Lewis acid: electron-pair acceptor; Lewis base: electron-pair donor. Example: \(AlCl_3+NH_3\rightarrow Cl_3Al\leftarrow NH_3\).
  • Water is amphoteric / amphiprotic: it can behave as both acid and base depending on the reaction partner.

    8. Strength of acids and bases; basicity and acidity trends
  • Strong acids and bases ionise almost completely; weak ones ionise only partially and establish equilibrium.
  • For a weak acid \(HA\): \(HA+H_2O\rightleftharpoons H_3O^++A^-\), and \(K_a=\dfrac{[H_3O^+][A^-]}{[HA]}\).
  • For a weak base \(B\): \(B+H_2O\rightleftharpoons BH^++OH^-\), and \(K_b=\dfrac{[BH^+][OH^-]}{[B]}\).
  • Larger \(K_a\) means stronger acid; larger \(K_b\) means stronger base.
  • For weak electrolytes, \(\alpha\) is the degree of ionisation; in dilute solutions, \(\alpha\approx\sqrt{K_a/c}\) for a weak acid and \(\alpha\approx\sqrt{K_b/c}\) for a weak base.
  • For oxyacids, basicity = number of ionisable hydrogens attached to oxygen, not total hydrogens present. So \(H_3PO_3\) is dibasic, \(H_3PO_2\) is monobasic, and \(H_2SO_4\) is dibasic.
  • For oxoacids of similar type \(HOX\), acidity increases with electronegativity of the central atom because the \(-I\) effect weakens the O–H bond; hence \(HClO>HBrO>HIO\).
  • Polyprotic acids ionise stepwise, each step having its own ionisation constant; usually \(K_{a1}>K_{a2}>K_{a3}\).

    9. Ionic product of water and pH scale
  • Water auto-ionises: \(2H_2O\rightleftharpoons H_3O^++OH^-\).
  • Ionic product of water: \(K_w=[H_3O^+][OH^-]\). At 298 K, \(K_w=1.0\times10^{-14}\).
  • In pure water at 298 K, \([H_3O^+]=[OH^-]=1.0\times10^{-7}\), so pH = 7.
  • \(pH=-\log[H_3O^+]\), \(pOH=-\log[OH^-]\), and at 298 K, \(pH+pOH=14\).
  • Acidic solution: \([H_3O^+]>[OH^-]\). Basic solution: \([OH^-]>[H_3O^+]\). Neutral solution: \([H_3O^+]=[OH^-]\).
  • Important PYQ caution: neutral water does not always have pH 7. At higher temperature, \(K_w\) increases, so neutral water can have pH below 7 while still remaining neutral because \([H^+]=[OH^-]\).
  • For strong acid–strong base mixtures, the safe order is: convert pH/pOH to molarity if needed, convert to moles, subtract excess, divide by total final volume, then find final pH or pOH.

    10. Common ion effect and buffer solutions
  • Common ion effect: ionisation of a weak acid or weak base is suppressed by adding a strong electrolyte containing a common ion.
  • Example: ionisation of acetic acid is suppressed by adding sodium acetate; ionisation of ammonium hydroxide is suppressed by adding ammonium chloride.
  • Buffer solution: resists change in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added.
  • Acidic buffer: weak acid + its salt with a strong base, for example \(CH_3COOH/CH_3COONa\).
  • Basic buffer: weak base + its salt with a strong acid, for example \(NH_4OH/NH_4Cl\).
  • Must-know acidic buffer formula: \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a+\log\dfrac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]}\).
  • PYQ shortcut: if \([\text{salt}]=[\text{acid}]\), then \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a\). Equal dilution of both does not change the ratio, so pH remains the same.
  • Buffer forms only when weak acid/base and its conjugate partner both remain after partial neutralisation; complete neutralisation in equivalent amount does not produce a buffer.

    11. Hydrolysis of salts and pH of salt solutions
  • Salt hydrolysis happens when ions of a salt react with water to produce \(H_3O^+\) or \(OH^-\), making the solution acidic or basic.
  • Strong acid + strong base salt: neutral solution. Example: \(NaCl\), \(KCl\).
  • Strong acid + weak base salt: acidic solution. Example: \(NH_4Cl\), \(AlCl_3\).
  • Weak acid + strong base salt: basic solution. Example: \(CH_3COONa\), \(Na_2CO_3\).
  • Weak acid + weak base salt: pH depends on relative strengths of the acid and base.
  • For fast MCQs, first identify the parent acid and base, then decide whether hydrolysis increases \(H_3O^+\) or \(OH^-\).

    12. Solubility equilibrium and solubility product
  • For a sparingly soluble salt, undissolved solid and dissolved ions are in equilibrium. Example: \(AgCl(s)\rightleftharpoons Ag^++Cl^-\).
  • Its solubility product is \(K_{sp}=[Ag^+][Cl^-]\).
  • If molar solubility is \(S\), then:
    • For \(AB\): \(K_{sp}=S^2\)
    • For \(AB_2\): \(K_{sp}=S(2S)^2=4S^3\)
    • For \(A_2B\): \(K_{sp}=(2S)^2(S)=4S^3\)
    • For \(A_2B_3\): \(K_{sp}=(2S)^2(3S)^3=108S^5\)
  • PYQ must-know: for \(PbCl_2\), if molar solubility is \(S\), use \(K_{sp}=4S^3\).
  • After finding \(S\) in mol L\(^{-1}\), convert to g L\(^{-1}\) using molar mass if the question asks mass solubility or required volume.
  • Smaller \(K_{sp}\) generally means lower solubility for salts of the same stoichiometric type.

    13. Common ion effect on solubility
  • Adding a common ion decreases the solubility of a sparingly soluble salt by shifting dissolution equilibrium backward.
  • Example: \(AgCl\) becomes less soluble in \(NaCl\) solution because extra \(Cl^-\) is already present.
  • Typical numerical approach: first find the pre-existing common-ion concentration, then substitute it into the \(K_{sp}\) expression and solve for the small remaining solubility.
  • For \(Cd(OH)_2\rightleftharpoons Cd^{2+}+2OH^-\) in \(0.1\,M\) KOH, take \([OH^-]\approx 0.1\) if the added solubility is very small, then \(K_{sp}=s(0.1)^2\).
  • In mixed saturated-salt problems, use the more directly known salt first to obtain common-ion concentration, then use it in the second salt’s \(K_{sp}\).

    14. Must-know equations and chemical equations for exam recall
  • \(K_c=\dfrac{[products]^{coefficients}}{[reactants]^{coefficients}}\)
  • \(K_p=K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g}\)
  • \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303RT\log K\)
  • \(Q\) compared with \(K\): \(Q<K\) forward, \(Q>K\) backward, \(Q=K\) equilibrium
  • \(K_a=\dfrac{[H_3O^+][A^-]}{[HA]}\), \(\;K_b=\dfrac{[BH^+][OH^-]}{[B]}\)
  • \(K_w=[H_3O^+][OH^-]=1.0\times10^{-14}\) at 298 K

  • \(\mathrm{pH}=pK_a+\log\dfrac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]}\) for acidic buffer

    15. Quick pitfalls to avoid
  • Do not include pure solids or pure liquids in equilibrium expressions.
  • Do not say catalyst changes equilibrium constant or equilibrium composition.
  • Do not decide pH after mixing acid and base before doing mole subtraction and dividing by total final volume.
  • Do not count all hydrogens in oxyacids as ionisable; count only those attached through \(-OH\).
  • Do not assume pH 7 means neutral at every temperature; neutral means \([H^+]=[OH^-]\).
  • In \(K_{sp}\) numericals, write ionic stoichiometry first; many mistakes come from wrong ion ratios.

    2025
  1. For \(A \rightleftharpoons 2B\) starting with pure \(A\): use mole-fraction partial pressures and remember \(K_p=\dfrac{p_B^2}{p_A}\); for \(N_2O_4 \rightleftharpoons 2NO_2\), \(K_p=\dfrac{4\alpha^2 p}{1-\alpha^2}\), so \(\alpha=\sqrt{\dfrac{K_p/p}{4+K_p/p}}\).
  2. For oxyacids, basicity = number of ionisable H attached to O (–OH), not total H atoms; hence H3PO3 is dibasic (2 –OH, one P–H non-ionisable), H3PO2 is monobasic, and H2SO4 is dibasic.
  3. For simple equilibrium numericals, write \(K_c\) directly from the balanced equation and substitute equilibrium concentrations; here \(K_c=\dfrac{[CO][H_2O]}{[CO_2][H_2]}\), so solve the unknown concentration in one step.
  4. Must-remember link: \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303RT\log K\); so negative \(\Delta G^\circ\) means \(K>1\) and \(\log K\) is positive (convert kJ to J before substituting).
  5. For strong acid–strong base mixing, convert pH/pOH to molarity, then to moles, subtract excess \(H^+\) or \(OH^-\), divide by final total volume, and only then find pH/pOH.
  6. In mixed saturated salts with a common ion, use the known salt to first find the common-ion concentration, then substitute that into the other salt’s \(K_{sp}\); here find \([CO_3^{2-}]\) from \(K_{sp}(MgCO_3)=[Mg^{2+}][CO_3^{2-}]\), then use \(K_{sp}(Ag_2CO_3)=[Ag^+]^2[CO_3^{2-}]\).
  7. For \(A B_2\)-type sparingly soluble salts like \(PbCl_2\): if molar solubility is \(S\), then \(K_{sp}=[A^{2+}][B^-]^2=S(2S)^2=4S^3\); after finding \(S\) in mol L\(^{-1}\), convert to g L\(^{-1}\) using molar mass, then volume \(=\dfrac{\text{required mass}}{\text{solubility in g L}^{-1}}\).
  8. When a balanced equation is multiplied by \(n\), the equilibrium constant becomes \(K^n\); when divided by \(n\), it becomes \(K^{1/n}\) (reverse reaction \(\Rightarrow 1/K\)).

    2024
  1. For \(aA+bB \rightleftharpoons cC+dD\), if equal initial amounts of reactants and products are given, assume a shift \(x\) and use equilibrium concentrations in \(K_c\); here \(K_c=\frac{(1+x)^2}{(1-x)^2}=16\), so \(\frac{1+x}{1-x}=4\) and \([BO]_{eq}=1+x=1.6\ \text{mol L}^{-1}\).
  2. For 1:1 dissociation like \( \mathrm{PCl_5 \rightleftharpoons PCl_3 + Cl_2} \), if product formed at equilibrium is given, directly substitute equilibrium values in \(K_c=\frac{[\mathrm{PCl_3}][\mathrm{Cl_2}]}{[\mathrm{PCl_5}]}\); here \([\mathrm{Cl_2}]=[\mathrm{PCl_3}]=0.2\) and \([\mathrm{PCl_5}]_{eq}=x-0.2\), so \(2\times10^{-2}=\frac{0.2\times0.2}{x-0.2}\Rightarrow x=2.2\) mol.
  3. In strong acid–strong base mixtures, first do mole subtraction, then divide leftover excess moles by total final volume after dilution; here excess HCl \(=0.05-0.04=0.01\) mol in \(1.0\) L, so \([H^+]=0.01\) M and \( \mathrm{pH}=2\).
  4. When target reaction is obtained by adding/scaling given equilibria, multiply equilibrium constants and raise them to the same powers as the equations are scaled; here required reaction is half of (i) plus half of (ii), so \(K=(K_1)^{1/2}(K_2)^{1/2}=\sqrt{16\times25}=20\).

    2023
  1. For an acidic buffer, use Henderson equation \( \mathrm{pH}=pK_a+\log\frac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]} \); dilution does not change the ratio if both are diluted equally, so when \([\text{salt}]=[\text{acid}]\), \( \mathrm{pH}=pK_a \).
  2. Equilibrium constant depends only on temperature; at the same temperature it is unchanged by pressure, concentration or catalyst, so if \(T\) is constant, \(K\) remains the same.
  3. In 1:1:1:1 equilibrium problems, if \(x\) mol of one product forms, the other product also forms \(x\) and each reactant decreases by \(x\); then substitute directly into \(K_c=\frac{[\text{products}]}{[\text{reactants}]}\).
  4. If a reaction is reversed, take reciprocal of \(K\); if all coefficients are multiplied by \(n\), the new constant becomes \(K^n\). Here \(2\mathrm{NH_3}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{N_2}+3\mathrm{H_2}\) is the reverse of 3 times the given reaction, so \(K=(1/50)^3=8\times10^{-6}\).
  5. A buffer forms only when a weak acid/base and its conjugate salt remain together after partial neutralization; complete neutralization in equivalent amounts (for example weak acid + strong base in 1:1 ratio) does not give a buffer.
  6. For \(K_{sp}\) problems in a solution already containing a common ion, take the pre-existing ion concentration as fixed if solubility is very small; for \( \mathrm{Cd(OH)_2 \rightleftharpoons Cd^{2+}+2OH^-} \), in \(0.1\,\mathrm{M}\) KOH use \(K_{sp}=[\mathrm{Cd^{2+}}][\mathrm{OH^-}]^2=s(0.1)^2\), so \(s=2.5\times10^{-12}=25\times10^{-13}\).

    2022
  1. In a heterogeneous equilibrium, pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the equilibrium expression; for \( \mathrm{A(s)\rightleftharpoons B(s)+C(g)} \), \(K_c=[C]\) and \(K_p=P_C\). Also, \(K\) depends only on temperature, while a catalyst does not change \( \Delta H \).
  2. Neutral water does not always have pH 7: as temperature increases, \(K_w\) increases, so the pH of pure water decreases below 7 even though it remains neutral because \([H^+]=[OH^-]\).
  3. Use \(K_p=K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g}\) for gaseous equilibria, where \(\Delta n_g=\) moles of gaseous products \(-\) moles of gaseous reactants; for \(\mathrm{N_2+3H_2\rightleftharpoons 2NH_3}\), \(\Delta n_g=2-4=-2\), so \(\frac{K_p}{K_c}=(RT)^{-2}=\frac{1}{(RT)^2}\).
  4. For salt hydrolysis, remember the quick classifications: strong acid + weak base salt gives acidic solution (for example AlCl3), weak acid + strong base salt gives basic solution (for example CH3COONa), strong acid + strong base salt gives neutral solution/high conductivity (for example KCl), and Al2O3 is amphoteric.
  5. For oxoacids of the same type \( \mathrm{HOX} \), acidity increases with electronegativity of the central atom because the \(-I\) effect weakens the O–H bond; hence \( \mathrm{HClO > HBrO > HIO} \).
  6. Pressure increase shifts gaseous equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas; so products are favoured only when \( \Delta n_g = \text{gaseous products} - \text{gaseous reactants} < 0 \).
  7. For gaseous equilibria, write \(K_p\) in terms of partial pressures and use any given equality directly; for \(2\mathrm{SO_2}+ \mathrm{O_2}\rightleftharpoons 2\mathrm{SO_3}\), if \(p_{\mathrm{SO_2}}=p_{\mathrm{SO_3}}\), then \(K_p=\frac{(p_{\mathrm{SO_3}})^2}{(p_{\mathrm{SO_2}})^2 p_{\mathrm{O_2}}}=\frac{1}{p_{\mathrm{O_2}}}\), so \(p_{\mathrm{O_2}}=\frac{1}{K_p}\).
  8. For 1:1 sparingly soluble salts like metal sulphides, \(K_{sp}=s^2\); smaller \(K_{sp}\) means lower solubility, so in match/order questions compare salts by magnitude of \(K_{sp}\) rather than memorizing individual values.
  9. In \(K_c\) problems, write equilibrium moles from stoichiometry first and convert to concentration only at the end; for \( \mathrm{N_2+3H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3} \) starting with \(1,3,0\) mol, use \(1-x,\ 3-3x,\ 2x\), so \(K_c=\frac{[NH_3]^2}{[N_2][H_2]^3}\) and remember \(3-3x=3(1-x)\) for simplification.
  10. Count ionisable \(H^+\), not just total H atoms: diprotic acids have two replaceable protons; among the given acids, all except citric acid are diprotic, because citric acid is triprotic.
  11. When all equilibrium partial pressures are equal in a gaseous reaction, substitute the same value into \(K_p\); the result depends only on the net exponent difference \( \Delta n_g \), so \(K_p=(p)^{\Delta n_g}\).
  12. Lewis base = electron-pair donor; NH3 acts as a Lewis base because nitrogen has a lone pair available for donation.

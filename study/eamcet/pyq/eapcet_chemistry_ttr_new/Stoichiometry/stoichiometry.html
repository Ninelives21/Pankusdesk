Stoichiometry

    Measurement, significant figures and basic numerical rules
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant; leading zeros are not; trailing zeros are significant only if they are to the right of a decimal point.
  • In addition/subtraction, round the final answer to the least number of decimal places among the given values.
  • To round to n significant figures, keep the first n digits, look at the next digit, and increase the last retained digit by 1 if that next digit is 5 or more.
  • Pitfall: do not count significant figures before doing the required rounding step.

    Mole concept, stoichiometry and gaseous relations
  • Core method for stoichiometry: convert given quantity to moles, use the balanced-equation mole ratio, then convert the required moles back to mass/volume/particles.
  • For amount of solute from molarity: moles = M × V and mass = M × V × molar mass.
  • To compare number of atoms, use: total atoms = moles × (atoms per molecule) × NA.
  • At the same temperature and pressure, gas volumes are proportional to mole ratios; for hydrocarbon combustion, volume ratio directly follows the balanced gaseous equation.
  • Limiting reagent questions: write the balanced equation first, compare available mole ratio with required stoichiometric ratio, then calculate products from the limiting reagent only.
  • Yield/efficiency questions: first find theoretical amount from stoichiometry, then apply efficiency using actual = theoretical / fractional yield.
  • Pitfall: never mix mass ratio with mole ratio without first converting to moles.

    Percentage composition, solution concentration and mixed-sample shortcuts
  • For % (w/w) solutions: % (w/w) = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100; first get pure solute needed from stoichiometry, then calculate total solution mass.
  • Mixed carbonate-bicarbonate with acid: remember acid demand ratio Na2CO3 : HCl = 1 : 2 and NaHCO3 : HCl = 1 : 1.
  • Thermal decomposition shortcut for sodium bicarbonate: 2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O; use mass loss to back-calculate NaHCO3 present.
  • Oleum shortcut: x% oleum means 100 g oleum contains (x − 100) g free SO3; treat it as H2SO4 + free SO3 and neutralize both separately.
  • Must-remember equation type: decomposition/acid-reaction stoichiometry questions become easy once the balanced equation is written first.

    Equivalent weight, normality and gram-equivalent method
  • Equivalent weight = molar mass / n-factor.
  • Medium matters: the same substance can have different n-factor and hence different equivalent weight in acidic, basic or neutral medium.
  • Must-know cases: Na2CO3 = M/2; KMnO4 in acidic medium = M/5; KMnO4 in neutral/basic medium = M/3; K2Cr2O7 in acidic medium = M/6.
  • At equivalence in redox or acid-base titrimetry, gram-equivalents are equal.
  • For H2O2, volume strength and normality are linked by: N = volume strength / 5.6.
  • Pitfall: do not memorize equivalent weight as a fixed value without checking reaction medium and actual change in oxidation number/H+/OH−.

    Oxidation number essentials
  • Assign oxidation number before judging oxidation/reduction; increase in oxidation number = oxidation, decrease = reduction.
  • Hydrogen is usually +1 with non-metals but −1 in metal hydrides.
  • For oxidation state of individual atoms in covalent molecules, use the correct structure first; structure can change the oxidation state assigned to different atoms of the same element.
  • In KClO3 decomposition, Cl changes from +5 to −1 and O changes from −2 to 0.
  • Standard oxidation-state comparisons are often faster than full reaction writing in objective questions.
  • Pitfall: do not assign oxidation state atom-by-atom in a covalent molecule unless the bonding structure is clear.

    Redox reaction types and disproportionation
  • Redox only means oxidation and reduction occur together; disproportionation is a special case where the same element in the same reactant is both oxidised and reduced.
  • To identify disproportionation, check whether one element starts with one oxidation state and ends up in two products having one higher and one lower oxidation state.
  • Classic must-know disproportionation examples: 2H2O2 → 2H2O + O2 and Cl2 + 2NaOH → NaCl + NaOCl + H2O.
  • MnO42− can disproportionate, but KMnO4 decomposition is not disproportionation because Mn(+7) goes only to lower oxidation states.
  • For product-prediction PYQs in disproportionation, track only the oxidation state split of the common element first.
  • Pitfall: if oxidation and reduction happen on different elements/species, it is redox but not disproportionation.

    Standard medium-based redox results to remember
  • In aqueous acidic KMnO4, Mn(+7) is reduced to Mn2+.
  • In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4− oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+.
  • In acidic medium, 1 mol Cr2O72− oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+.
  • In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4− oxidizes 2.5 mol C2O42−.
  • Zinc with concentrated HNO3 gives NO2, while with dilute HNO3 it gives N2O.
  • Must-remember reaction set for titrimetry: permanganate/Fe2+, dichromate/Fe2+, permanganate/oxalate.

    Balancing and titrimetry strategy
  • For reaction-coefficient questions, conserve one element at a time and convert atom-balance conditions into simple equations; start with the easiest unique-count element.
  • For redox balancing, oxidation-number method is often quickest in objective problems; half-reaction method is safer in ionic medium-based reactions.
  • Back-titration shortcut: calculate initial moles added, subtract unreacted moles from the back-titration data, then use stoichiometry to get the required unknown.
  • In redox titrimetry, gram-equivalent equality is often the fastest route and avoids memorizing the full balanced equation each time.
  • Pitfall: never use titration mole ratios or equivalent-weight shortcuts without confirming the reaction medium and final oxidation state.

  1. Amount of solute from molarity: Number of moles = M × V, so mass of solute = M × V × molar mass. To compare “highest amount of solute” in grams, calculate M × V × molar mass for each solution.
  2. Oxidation state of hydrogen: H is -1 in metal hydrides where hydrogen is bonded to a less electronegative element, and +1 in compounds with non-metals such as H2O. Thus, in LiAlH4, hydrogen is -1, while in H2O, hydrogen is +1.
  3. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+ and 1 mol Cr2O72- oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+. So for this standard pair, x = 5 and y = 6, hence x + y = 11.
  4. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+, while 1 mol Cr2O72- oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+. So if 1 mol acidified MnO4- oxidizes x mol Fe2+, then 1 mol acidified Cr2O72- oxidizes 6x/5 mol Fe2+.
  5. Zinc with concentrated HNO3 gives NO2, in which nitrogen has oxidation state +4. Zinc with dilute HNO3 gives N2O, in which the average oxidation state of nitrogen is +1.
  6. Thermal decomposition of sodium bicarbonate: On heating, 2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O. So 168 g NaHCO3 causes a mass loss of 62 g. Hence, if mass loss is given, mass of NaHCO3 = (168/62) × mass loss. The remaining mass belongs to original Na2CO3. Here, mass of NaHCO3 = (168/62) × 0.62 = 1.68 g, so mass of Na2CO3 = 4.00 - 1.68 = 2.32 g, and percentage of Na2CO3 = (2.32/4.00) × 100 = 58%.
  7. Oxidation state comparison of nitrogen: Use the sum of oxidation states = overall charge. For these standard compounds, N in NH2OH = -1, in NH4Cl = -3, in N2H4 = -2, and in HNO2 = +3. So the lowest oxidation state is in NH4Cl.
  8. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 2.5 mol C2O42-. Standard stoichiometric relation: MnO4- + 8H+ + (5/2)C2O42- → Mn2+ + 4H2O + 5CO2.
  9. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+, while 1 mol Cr2O72- oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+. So if 1 mol acidified MnO4- oxidizes x mol Fe2+, then 1 mol acidified Cr2O72- oxidizes 6x/5 mol Fe2+.

  11. Balancing redox reactions in basic medium: A standard balanced form to remember is 2Cr(OH)3 + IO3- + 4OH- → 2CrO42- + I- + 5H2O. So the coefficients are x = 2, y = 1, z = 4, a = 2, b = 1, c = 5.
  12. Limiting reagent and stoichiometric yield in redox precipitation: First write the balanced reaction, 2Cu2+ + 4I- → 2CuI + I2, and note that CuI combines to give Cu2I2. Compare available mole ratio with required stoichiometric ratio to find the limiting reagent. Here, n(Cu2+) = 0.100 × 0.05 = 5 × 10-3 mol and n(I-) = 1.0 × 0.1 = 0.1 mol, so Cu2+ is limiting. From the balanced equation, 2 mol Cu2+ produce 1 mol I2 and 1 mol Cu2I2. Therefore, moles of I2 = moles of Cu2I2 = (5 × 10-3)/2 = 2.5 × 10-3 mol.

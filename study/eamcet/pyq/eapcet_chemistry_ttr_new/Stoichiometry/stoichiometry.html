    2025
  1. Amount of solute from molarity: Number of moles = M × V, so mass of solute = M × V × molar mass. To compare “highest amount of solute” in grams, calculate M × V × molar mass for each solution.
  2. Oxidation state of hydrogen: H is -1 in metal hydrides where hydrogen is bonded to a less electronegative element, and +1 in compounds with non-metals such as H2O. Thus, in LiAlH4, hydrogen is -1, while in H2O, hydrogen is +1.
  3. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+ and 1 mol Cr2O72- oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+. So for this standard pair, x = 5 and y = 6, hence x + y = 11.
  4. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+, while 1 mol Cr2O72- oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+. So if 1 mol acidified MnO4- oxidizes x mol Fe2+, then 1 mol acidified Cr2O72- oxidizes 6x/5 mol Fe2+.
  5. Zinc with concentrated HNO3 gives NO2, in which nitrogen has oxidation state +4. Zinc with dilute HNO3 gives N2O, in which the average oxidation state of nitrogen is +1.
  6. Thermal decomposition of sodium bicarbonate: On heating, 2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O. So 168 g NaHCO3 causes a mass loss of 62 g. Hence, if mass loss is given, mass of NaHCO3 = (168/62) × mass loss. The remaining mass belongs to original Na2CO3. Here, mass of NaHCO3 = (168/62) × 0.62 = 1.68 g, so mass of Na2CO3 = 4.00 - 1.68 = 2.32 g, and percentage of Na2CO3 = (2.32/4.00) × 100 = 58%.
  7. Oxidation state comparison of nitrogen: Use the sum of oxidation states = overall charge. For these standard compounds, N in NH2OH = -1, in NH4Cl = -3, in N2H4 = -2, and in HNO2 = +3. So the lowest oxidation state is in NH4Cl.
  8. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 2.5 mol C2O42-. Standard stoichiometric relation: MnO4- + 8H+ + (5/2)C2O42- → Mn2+ + 4H2O + 5CO2.
  9. In acidic medium, 1 mol MnO4- oxidizes 5 mol Fe2+, while 1 mol Cr2O72- oxidizes 6 mol Fe2+. So if 1 mol acidified MnO4- oxidizes x mol Fe2+, then 1 mol acidified Cr2O72- oxidizes 6x/5 mol Fe2+.
  10. Zinc with concentrated HNO3 gives NO2, in which nitrogen has oxidation state +4. Zinc with dilute HNO3 gives N2O, in which the average oxidation state of nitrogen is +1.
  11. Balancing redox reactions in basic medium: A standard balanced form to remember is 2Cr(OH)3 + IO3- + 4OH- → 2CrO42- + I- + 5H2O. So the coefficients are x = 2, y = 1, z = 4, a = 2, b = 1, c = 5.
  12. Limiting reagent and stoichiometric yield in redox precipitation: First write the balanced reaction, 2Cu2+ + 4I- → 2CuI + I2, and note that CuI combines to give Cu2I2. Compare available mole ratio with required stoichiometric ratio to find the limiting reagent. Here, n(Cu2+) = 0.100 × 0.05 = 5 × 10-3 mol and n(I-) = 1.0 × 0.1 = 0.1 mol, so Cu2+ is limiting. From the balanced equation, 2 mol Cu2+ produce 1 mol I2 and 1 mol Cu2I2. Therefore, moles of I2 = moles of Cu2I2 = (5 × 10-3)/2 = 2.5 × 10-3 mol.
  13. Mixed carbonate–bicarbonate with acid: Remember the stoichiometric acid demand Na2CO3 : HCl = 1 : 2 and NaHCO3 : HCl = 1 : 1. If equal moles of both are present, first find the common moles from total mass, then total HCl needed = 3n. Finally use V = n/M for the acid volume.

    2024
  1. Equivalent weight of an element = mass of element that combines with 8 g of oxygen; so if 12 g combines with 32 g O, equivalent weight = 12 × 8 / 32 = 3.
  2. Significant figures rule to remember: zeros between two non-zero digits are significant; leading zeros are not; trailing zeros are significant only when they are to the right of a decimal point.
  3. Equivalent weight shortcut: \( \text{Eq. wt.} = \dfrac{M}{n\text{-factor}} \); must-know common cases: Na\(_2\)CO\(_3\) → \(M/2\), KMnO\(_4\) in acidic medium → \(M/5\), KMnO\(_4\) in neutral/basic medium → \(M/3\), K\(_2\)Cr\(_2\)O\(_7\) in acidic medium → \(M/6\).
  4. For H₂O₂, volume strength and normality are directly linked: \(N = \dfrac{\text{volume strength}}{5.6}\); so 20-volume H₂O₂ has \(N = 20/5.6 \approx 3.57\).
  5. For redox PYQs, first assign oxidation numbers and compare reactant → product: increase = oxidation, decrease = reduction; here O goes from −2 to 0 (oxidised) and Cl goes from +5 to −1 (reduced).
  6. Titration stoichiometry shortcut: calculate initial moles, subtract unreacted moles found from back-titration, then use the balanced equation to get moles of substance; finally \( \text{molar mass} = \dfrac{\text{mass}}{\text{moles}} \).

    2023
  1. To compare equivalent weights, use \( \text{Eq. wt.}=\dfrac{\text{molar mass}}{n\text{-factor}} \); for the same species in different media, medium changes \(n\)-factor and hence equivalent weight (e.g. KMnO\(_4\): acidic \(=M/5\), neutral/basic \(=M/3\)).
  2. Mass-based stoichiometry shortcut: convert given mass to moles, use mole ratio from the balanced equation, then convert required product moles back to mass.
  3. In addition/subtraction, round the final answer to the least number of decimal places among the given values; then count significant figures from that rounded result.
  4. To compare number of atoms, use: total atoms \(=\text{moles}\times\)(atoms per molecule)\(\times N_A\); substances have the same number of atoms when this value is equal.

  1. For % (w/w) solution problems: first find pure solute needed from stoichiometry, then use \( \%\text{(w/w)}=\dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}}\times100 \) to get mass of solution.

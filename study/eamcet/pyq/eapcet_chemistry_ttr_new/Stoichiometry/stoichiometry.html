Stoichiometry

    Significant Figures and Measurement
  • Counting significant figures
    • All non-zero digits are significant.
    • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
    • Trailing zeros to the right of a decimal point are significant.
    • Leading zeros are never significant.
  • Rules for operations
    • In addition and subtraction, round the final answer to the least number of decimal places among the given values.
      Example: \(12.35+1.2=13.55\), reported as \(13.6\).
    • In multiplication and division, round to the least number of significant figures.
      Example: \(2.5\times3.42=8.55\), reported as \(8.6\).
  • Rounding to \(n\) significant figures
    • Keep the first \(n\) significant digits and inspect the next digit.
    • If the next digit is \(5\) or more, increase the last retained digit by \(1\).
    • Example: \(4.567\) to \(3\) significant figures is \(4.57\), while \(4.562\) to \(3\) significant figures is \(4.56\).

    Mole Concept and Amount Calculations
  • Moles and mass from molarity
    • Amount of solute from molarity follows: \(\mathrm{moles=M\times V}\), where \(V\) must be in litres.
    • Mass of solute \(=\mathrm{M\times V\times molar\ mass}\).
    • To compare “highest amount of solute” in grams across different solutions, calculate \(\mathrm{M\times V\times molar\ mass}\) for each option.
    • Do not compare molarity alone.
  • Number of atoms
    • To compare number of atoms, use: \(\mathrm{total\ atoms=moles\times atoms\ per\ molecule\times N_A}\).
    • Two samples have the same number of atoms when this total is equal, not merely when their mole numbers are equal.
  • Mass-based stoichiometry
    • Convert given mass to moles.
    • Apply the mole ratio from the balanced equation.
    • Convert required product moles back to mass.

    Concentration, Percentage and Solution Stoichiometry
  • Percentage by mass
    • For percentage by mass: \(\mathrm{\%(w/w)=\dfrac{mass\ of\ solute}{mass\ of\ solution}\times100}\).
    • In solution-preparation problems, first find pure solute required from stoichiometry, then calculate mass of solution using the percentage.
  • Acid/base volume from stoichiometry
    • For volume of acid/base solution, use \(\mathrm{V=\dfrac{n}{M}}\), where \(n\) is moles required by stoichiometry and \(M\) is molarity.
    • Always check whether the salt consumes \(1\), \(2\), or more moles of acid/base per mole.
  • Carbonate-bicarbonate acid consumption
    • For mixed carbonate-bicarbonate acid consumption, remember: \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3:HCl=1:2}\) and \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3:HCl=1:1}\).
    • If equal moles of both salts are present, total acid needed \(=3n\).
  • Oleum shortcut
    • \(x\%\) oleum means \(100\,\mathrm{g}\) oleum contains \((x-100)\,\mathrm{g}\) free \(\mathrm{SO_3}\).
    • Split oleum into \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) and free \(\mathrm{SO_3}\).
    • Then calculate \(\mathrm{NaOH}\) needed for both separately.
  • \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) volume strength and normality
    • For \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), volume strength and normality are linked by: \(\mathrm{N=\dfrac{volume\ strength}{5.6}}\).
    • Thus \(20\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) has normality \(\mathrm{\dfrac{20}{5.6}\approx3.57\,N}\).

    Equivalent Weight and Normality
  • Equivalent weight basics
    • Equivalent weight of an element is the mass of the element that combines with \(8\,\mathrm{g}\) of oxygen.
    • If \(12\,\mathrm{g}\) of an element combines with \(32\,\mathrm{g}\) oxygen, equivalent weight \(\mathrm{=\dfrac{12\times8}{32}=3}\).
    • General shortcut: \(\mathrm{Equivalent\ weight=\dfrac{molar\ mass}{n\text{-}factor}}\).
    • Do not use molar mass directly when equivalents are involved.
  • Common \(n\)-factor cases
    • \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3\rightarrow M/2}\)
    • Acidic \(\mathrm{KMnO_4\rightarrow M/5}\)
    • Neutral/basic \(\mathrm{KMnO_4\rightarrow M/3}\)
    • Acidic \(\mathrm{K_2Cr_2O_7\rightarrow M/6}\)
  • Medium effect on equivalent weight
    • For the same species in different media, the \(n\)-factor may change; therefore equivalent weight also changes.
    • This is especially important for \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\), whose equivalent weight is different in acidic and neutral/basic media.
  • Titrimetry shortcuts
    • In redox titrimetry, use gram-equivalent equality at equivalence: \(\mathrm{moles_1\times n_1=moles_2\times n_2}\).
    • This often avoids writing the full balanced redox equation.
    • In back-titration problems, calculate initial moles, subtract unreacted moles found from the back-titration, then use the balanced equation to find the moles of the unknown.
    • Finally use \(\mathrm{molar\ mass=\dfrac{mass}{moles}}\).

    Stoichiometry, Limiting Reagent and Yield
  • Limiting reagent method
    • First calculate moles of each reactant.
    • Then compare the available mole ratio with the stoichiometric ratio in the balanced equation.
    • The reactant that runs out first controls the amount of product.
  • Iodide-cupric ion precipitation redox pattern
    • \(\mathrm{2Cu^{2+}+4I^-\rightarrow2CuI+I_2}\), and \(\mathrm{CuI}\) combines as \(\mathrm{Cu_2I_2}\).
    • Thus \(2\) moles \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) produce \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{I_2}\) and \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{Cu_2I_2}\).
  • Yield and efficiency
    • For yield/efficiency problems, first calculate the theoretical amount from the balanced equation.
    • Then adjust using: \(\mathrm{actual\ input\ required=\dfrac{theoretical\ input}{fractional\ yield}}\).
  • Gaseous hydrocarbon combustion
    • For gaseous hydrocarbon combustion at the same \(T\) and \(P\), gas volumes are proportional to mole ratios.
    • CxHy → xCO2 + (y/2)H2O.
    • Hence volume ratio hydrocarbon : \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) : \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) directly gives \(\mathrm{1:x:\dfrac{y}{2}}\).

    Carbonate, Bicarbonate and Thermal Decomposition
  • Thermal decomposition of sodium bicarbonate
    • \(\mathrm{2NaHCO_3\rightarrow Na_2CO_3+CO_2+H_2O}\).
    • Here \(168\,\mathrm{g}\) \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\) gives a mass loss of \(62\,\mathrm{g}\) due to \(\mathrm{CO_2+H_2O}\).
  • Carbonate-bicarbonate mixture heated to constant mass
    • \(\mathrm{mass\ of\ NaHCO_3=\dfrac{168}{62}\times mass\ loss}\).
    • The remaining original mass is due to \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\).
  • Standard example
    • In the standard example with \(4.00\,\mathrm{g}\) mixture and \(0.62\,\mathrm{g}\) mass loss, \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3=\dfrac{168}{62}\times0.62=1.68\,g}\).
    • \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3=4.00-1.68=2.32\,g}\), so percentage \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3=58\%}\).

    Oxidation Number: Core Rules and Special Cases
  • Core oxidation-state rule
    • For oxidation state calculations, use the rule: sum of oxidation states equals the overall charge of the species.
    • For neutral molecules, the sum is zero.
  • Hydrogen oxidation state
    • Hydrogen is usually \(+1\).
    • It is \(-1\) in metal hydrides where hydrogen is bonded to a less electronegative element.
    • Thus hydrogen is \(-1\) in \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), but \(+1\) in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).
  • Nitrogen examples
    • \(N\) in \(\mathrm{NH_2OH}\) is \(-1\).
    • \(N\) in \(\mathrm{NH_4Cl}\) is \(-3\).
    • \(N\) in \(\mathrm{N_2H_4}\) is \(-2\).
    • \(N\) in \(\mathrm{HNO_2}\) is \(+3\).
    • Therefore the lowest oxidation state among these is in \(\mathrm{NH_4Cl}\).
  • Structure-based oxidation states
    • For individual atoms in covalent molecules, use the correct structure, not only the molecular formula.
    • In \(\mathrm{O=C=C=C=O}\) \((\mathrm{C_3O_2})\), terminal carbon atoms are \(+2\) each and the middle carbon is \(0\).
  • Redox role identification from oxidation states
    • In \(\mathrm{KClO_3}\) decomposition, assign oxidation states before identifying redox roles.
    • Chlorine changes from \(+5\) to \(-1\), so it undergoes reduction.
    • Oxygen changes from \(-2\) to \(0\), so it undergoes oxidation.
  • Zinc with concentrated and dilute \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\)
    • Zinc with concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) gives \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), in which nitrogen is \(+4\).
    • Zinc with dilute \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) gives \(\mathrm{N_2O}\), where the average oxidation state of nitrogen is \(+1\).

    Redox Types and Disproportionation
  • Redox vs disproportionation
    • A reaction may be redox without being disproportionation.
    • In ordinary redox reactions, oxidation and reduction occur on different elements/species.
    • In disproportionation, the same element is both oxidised and reduced.
  • How to identify disproportionation
    • The same element must form two products with one higher and one lower oxidation state than before.
    • In disproportionation product questions, track the same element’s oxidation state.
  • \(\mathrm{Se_2Cl_2}\) disproportionation example
    • For \(\mathrm{Se_2Cl_2}\), selenium is \(+1\).
    • Disproportionation products are \(\mathrm{SeCl_4}\) \((Se=+4)\) and \(\mathrm{Se}\) \((0)\).
  • Standard disproportionation patterns and trap
    • \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), and \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\) can show disproportionation in standard patterns.
    • \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) decomposition is not disproportionation because manganese \(+7\) only decreases to lower states such as \(+6\) and \(+4\).

    Standard Redox Products and Medium Effects
  • Acidic \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) product
    • In aqueous acidic \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\), manganese \((+7)\) is reduced to \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\).
    • Always check the medium before deciding the product of permanganate reduction.
  • \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) oxidation by permanganate and dichromate
    • In acidic medium, \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) oxidises \(5\) moles \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\).
    • In acidic medium, \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\) oxidises \(6\) moles \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\).
    • Thus the standard pair gives \(x=5,\ y=6\), and \(x+y=11\).
    • If \(1\) mole acidified \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) oxidises \(x\) moles \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\), then \(1\) mole acidified \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\) oxidises \(\mathrm{\dfrac{6x}{5}}\) moles \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\).
  • Oxalate oxidation by acidic permanganate
    • In acidic medium, \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) oxidises \(2.5\) moles \(\mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}}\).
    • Standard relation: \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-+8H^++\dfrac{5}{2}C_2O_4^{2-}\rightarrow Mn^{2+}+4H_2O+5CO_2}\).

    Redox Balancing and Coefficient Questions
  • Coefficient-balancing strategy
    • For balancing coefficient PYQs, conserve one element at a time and convert atom-balance conditions into simple equations.
    • Start with the element that appears in the fewest places, then back-substitute.
  • Basic-medium standard balanced form
    • In basic medium, a standard balanced form to remember is: \(\mathrm{2Cr(OH)_3+IO_3^-+4OH^-\rightarrow2CrO_4^{2-}+I^-+5H_2O}\).
    • Thus the coefficients are \(2,1,4,2,1,5\).
  • Oxidation-number or half-reaction method
    • First identify atoms undergoing oxidation and reduction.
    • Then balance atoms other than H and O.
    • Then balance O/H according to the medium.
    • Finally balance charge.

    Common Pitfalls
  • Amount and stoichiometry traps
    • Do not compare molarity alone when the question asks for mass of solute; volume and molar mass also matter.
    • Do not forget stoichiometric coefficients in formation-enthalpy, redox, titration or precipitation calculations; many wrong answers come from using the correct formula with missing coefficients.
  • Equivalent weight and \(n\)-factor traps
    • Do not use one \(n\)-factor for a redox species in all media.
    • \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) is the standard example where medium changes the \(n\)-factor.
  • Redox and oxidation-state traps
    • Do not confuse redox with disproportionation; disproportionation requires the same element to undergo both oxidation and reduction.
    • Do not assign oxidation states of individual atoms in unusual covalent molecules without checking the structure.
  • Concentration-expression traps
    • Do not treat \(20\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) as \(20\%\); volume strength, normality and percentage are different concentration expressions.
    • In mixed carbonate-bicarbonate calculations, remember that \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\) consumes twice as much acid per mole as \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\).

    Variables Used
  • Solution concentration symbols
    • \(M\): molarity or molar mass, depending on context
    • \(V\): volume of solution, usually in litres for molarity
    • \(N\): normality
  • Counting and amount symbols
    • \(N_A\): Avogadro constant
    • \(n\)-factor: number of electrons exchanged or replaceable units per formula unit
    • \(x\): percentage strength or variable amount, depending on question

    Basic Concepts, Measurement and Significant Figures
  • Matter is studied quantitatively using measurable properties such as mass, volume, amount of substance, temperature and concentration. In chemistry, calculations are meaningful only when units are handled consistently.
  • The SI unit of amount of substance is mole \((\mathrm{mol})\), and the SI unit of mass is kilogram \((\mathrm{kg})\). In chemistry numericals, mass is often used in grams, so conversions such as \(1\,\mathrm{kg}=1000\,\mathrm{g}\) must be automatic.
  • Common prefixes include kilo \((10^3)\), centi \((10^{-2})\), milli \((10^{-3})\), micro \((10^{-6})\), nano \((10^{-9})\) and pico \((10^{-12})\). Prefix mistakes usually lead to errors by powers of \(10\).
  • Significant figures express the reliability of a measured value. All non-zero digits are significant; zeros between non-zero digits are significant; leading zeros are not significant; trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant.
    Example: \(305\) has \(3\) significant figures, \(0.0045\) has \(2\), and \(2.300\) has \(4\).
  • In addition and subtraction, round the final answer to the least number of decimal places among the given values.
    Example: \(12.35+1.2=13.55\), reported as \(13.6\). In multiplication and division, round to the least number of significant figures.
    Example: \(2.5\times3.42=8.55\), reported as \(8.6\).
  • To round to \(n\) significant figures, keep the first \(n\) significant digits and inspect the next digit. If the next digit is \(5\) or more, increase the last retained digit by \(1\).
    Example: \(4.567\) to \(3\) significant figures is \(4.57\), while \(4.562\) to \(3\) significant figures is \(4.56\).

    Laws of Chemical Combination
  • Law of conservation of mass: mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. Therefore, a balanced chemical equation must have the same number of atoms of each element on both sides.
  • Example: if \(1.5\,\mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{C_2H_6}\) burns to give \(4.4\,\mathrm{g}\) \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) and \(2.7\,\mathrm{g}\) \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), then the total product mass is \(7.1\,\mathrm{g}\). The missing reactant mass is oxygen, so the total reactant mass must also become \(7.1\,\mathrm{g}\).
  • Law of definite proportions: a pure compound always contains the same elements in the same fixed mass ratio, regardless of its source. Water, for example, always has hydrogen and oxygen in a fixed mass ratio.
  • Example: if magnesium oxide is prepared in two experiments, calculate the mass of oxygen each time using \(\mathrm{mass\ of\ MgO-mass\ of\ Mg}\). If the ratio \(\mathrm{Mg:O}\) is the same in both experiments, the data supports the law of definite proportions.
  • Law of multiple proportions: when two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in a simple whole-number ratio.
  • Example: nitrogen and oxygen form several oxides such as \(\mathrm{N_2O}\), \(\mathrm{NO}\), \(\mathrm{N_2O_3}\), \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{N_2O_5}\). On fixing nitrogen mass as \(14\,\mathrm{g}\), the oxygen masses become \(8,16,24,32,40\), which are in the simple ratio \(1:2:3:4:5\).
  • Gay-Lussac’s law of gaseous volumes: when gases react at the same temperature and pressure, their volumes are in simple whole-number ratios. This is the basis for using gaseous volume ratios directly as mole ratios.
  • Example: in \(\mathrm{CH_4+2O_2\rightarrow CO_2+2H_2O}\), gaseous volumes follow the coefficients for the gases. Thus \(100\,\mathrm{mL}\) \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) needs \(200\,\mathrm{mL}\) \(\mathrm{O_2}\) and forms \(100\,\mathrm{mL}\) \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), when compared at the same \(T\) and \(P\).
  • Avogadro’s law: equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules. Hence, for gaseous reactions at the same \(T\) and \(P\), volume ratio \(=\) mole ratio.
  • Example: under the same temperature and pressure, \(60\,\mathrm{mL}\) \(\mathrm{H_2}\), \(60\,\mathrm{mL}\) \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) and \(60\,\mathrm{mL}\) \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) contain the same number of molecules, although their molecular masses are different.
  • Dalton’s atomic theory: these laws are supported by the idea that chemical reactions are rearrangements of atoms in fixed whole-number ratios.

    Mole Concept and Avogadro Constant
  • A mole is the amount of substance containing as many elementary entities as there are atoms in exactly \(12\,\mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{^{12}C}\). Numerically, \(1\,\mathrm{mol}=6.022\times10^{23}\) entities.
  • Avogadro constant is \(N_A=6.022\times10^{23}\,\mathrm{mol^{-1}}\). The number of particles is: \[ \mathrm{number\ of\ entities=n\times N_A} \] where \(n\) is amount in moles.
  • The elementary entity depends on the substance: atoms for monoatomic elements, molecules for molecular substances, ions for ionic species, and formula units for ionic compounds.
  • For atom-count comparison, use: \[ \mathrm{total\ atoms=moles\times atoms\ per\ molecule\times N_A} \] Equal moles of different substances do not necessarily contain equal total atoms.
  • Useful relations: \[ \mathrm{n=\dfrac{given\ mass}{molar\ mass}},\qquad \mathrm{mass=n\times molar\ mass} \] These form the core of most mass-based stoichiometry questions.

    Atomic Mass, Molecular Mass and Molar Mass
  • Atomic mass unit \((\mathrm{amu})\) is defined using carbon-12: \(1\,\mathrm{amu}=\dfrac{1}{12}\) of the mass of one \(\mathrm{^{12}C}\) atom.
  • Relative atomic mass and relative molecular mass are pure numbers. Atomic, molecular and formula masses have units such as amu, while molar mass is expressed in \(\mathrm{g\,mol^{-1}}\).
  • Molecular mass is found by adding atomic masses of all atoms in the molecule. Formula mass is similarly calculated for ionic compounds by adding the masses of ions present in one formula unit.
  • For ions, the mass of gained or lost electrons is neglected in ordinary stoichiometry. Therefore, ionic mass is taken approximately equal to the corresponding atomic or group mass.
  • Molar mass is the mass of one mole of particles. Thus, \(18\,\mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) contains \(1\) mole of water molecules, while \(58.5\,\mathrm{g}\) of \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) contains \(1\) mole of formula units.

    Empirical Formula, Molecular Formula and Percentage Composition
  • Molecular formula gives the actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule. Empirical formula gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms.
  • For ionic compounds such as \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), the formula usually represents the simplest ratio of ions, so it is treated as a formula unit rather than a molecule.
  • Percentage composition from a known formula is calculated by: \[ \mathrm{\%\ element=\dfrac{mass\ of\ that\ element\ in\ one\ mole\ of\ compound}{molar\ mass\ of\ compound}\times100} \]
  • To find empirical formula from percentage composition, assume \(100\,\mathrm{g}\) sample, convert each percentage to mass, convert mass to moles, divide all mole values by the smallest, and convert to simple whole numbers.
  • Molecular formula is obtained from empirical formula using: \[ \mathrm{molecular\ formula=(empirical\ formula)_n} \] where \(n=\dfrac{\mathrm{molecular\ mass}}{\mathrm{empirical\ formula\ mass}}\).
  • In carbonate-bicarbonate mixture problems, thermal decomposition gives a useful mass-loss shortcut: \[ \mathrm{2NaHCO_3\rightarrow Na_2CO_3+CO_2+H_2O} \] Here \(168\,\mathrm{g}\) \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\) gives \(62\,\mathrm{g}\) mass loss, so \[ \mathrm{mass\ of\ NaHCO_3=\dfrac{168}{62}\times mass\ loss} \]

    Concentration Terms and Solution Calculations
  • Molarity \((M)\) is moles of solute per litre of solution: \[ \mathrm{M=\dfrac{n}{V}} \] where \(V\) is volume in litres. Hence, \(\mathrm{n=M\times V}\).
  • From molarity, mass of solute is calculated as: \[ \mathrm{mass=M\times V\times molar\ mass} \] To compare amount of solute in different solutions, compare this full product, not molarity alone.
  • Mass percent \((w/w)\) is mass of solute per mass of solution: \[ \mathrm{\%(w/w)=\dfrac{mass\ of\ solute}{mass\ of\ solution}\times100} \] In solution-preparation questions, first find required pure solute, then calculate the mass of solution needed.
  • Example for mass percent \((w/w)\): if \(10\,\mathrm{g}\) sucrose is dissolved in \(100\,\mathrm{g}\) water, the mass of solution is \(110\,\mathrm{g}\). Therefore, \[ \mathrm{\%(w/w)=\dfrac{10}{110}\times100=9.09\%} \] Do not use only the mass of water in the denominator.
  • Volume percent \((v/v)\), mass-volume percent \((w/v)\) and mole fraction are different ways of expressing composition. Always identify whether the question uses mass of solution, volume of solution or total moles.
  • Example for \((w/v)\) and \((w/w)\): \(10\%\,(w/v)\) means \(10\,\mathrm{g}\) solute in \(100\,\mathrm{mL}\) solution, while \(10\%\,(w/w)\) means \(10\,\mathrm{g}\) solute in \(100\,\mathrm{g}\) solution. If density is given, use \(\mathrm{mass=density\times volume}\) to convert between mass-based and volume-based concentration data.
  • Molality \((m)\) is moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. It differs from molarity because molarity uses volume of solution, while molality uses mass of solvent.
  • Normality \((N)\) is gram-equivalents per litre of solution: \[ \mathrm{N=M\times n\text{-}factor} \] It is especially useful in acid-base and redox titration questions.
  • For \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) volume strength, volume strength and normality are linked by: \[ \mathrm{N=\dfrac{volume\ strength}{5.6}} \] Thus \(20\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) has \(\mathrm{N=\dfrac{20}{5.6}\approx3.57\,N}\).

    Chemical Equations and Stoichiometric Calculations
  • A balanced chemical equation gives both qualitative and quantitative information. Quantitatively, coefficients represent ratios of particles, moles, masses and, for gases at the same \(T,P\), volumes.
  • For any stoichiometric calculation, the standard sequence is: convert given quantity to moles, use coefficient ratio from the balanced equation, then convert required moles to the asked unit.
  • In mass-based questions: \[ \mathrm{given\ mass\rightarrow moles\rightarrow required\ moles\rightarrow required\ mass} \] Skipping the mole-ratio step is the most common error.
  • For gaseous reactions at the same temperature and pressure, volume ratios follow mole ratios. For hydrocarbon combustion: \[ \mathrm{C_xH_y \rightarrow xCO_2 + \dfrac{y}{2}H_2O} \]Thus the volume ratio hydrocarbon : \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) : \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) gives \(1:x:\dfrac{y}{2}\).
  • In mixed acid-reaction questions, remember: \[ \mathrm{Na_2CO_3:HCl=1:2},\qquad \mathrm{NaHCO_3:HCl=1:1} \] If equal moles of both are present, total acid required is \(3n\).
  • For volume of acid or base solution: \[ \mathrm{V=\dfrac{n}{M}} \] where \(n\) is moles required and \(M\) is molarity.

    Limiting Reagent and Yield
  • The limiting reagent is the reactant that is completely consumed first and therefore limits the amount of product formed.
  • To identify the limiting reagent, calculate moles of each reactant and compare the available mole ratio with the required stoichiometric ratio.
  • The maximum amount of product is always calculated from the limiting reagent, not from the reactant present in excess.
  • In the iodide-cupric ion redox precipitation pattern: \[ \mathrm{2Cu^{2+}+4I^-\rightarrow2CuI+I_2} \] and \(\mathrm{CuI}\) combines as \(\mathrm{Cu_2I_2}\). Thus \(2\) moles of \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) produce \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{I_2}\) and \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{Cu_2I_2}\).
  • For percentage yield or efficiency: \[ \mathrm{actual\ input\ required=\dfrac{theoretical\ input}{fractional\ yield}} \] or \(\mathrm{actual\ product=theoretical\ product\times fractional\ yield}\), depending on what is asked.

    Equivalent Weight and n-Factor
  • Equivalent weight is the mass that combines with or replaces a fixed amount of another substance. For oxygen-based definition, it is the mass of element that combines with \(8\,\mathrm{g}\) of oxygen.
  • General relation: \[ \mathrm{Equivalent\ weight=\dfrac{molar\ mass}{n\text{-}factor}} \] The \(n\)-factor depends on the reaction, not only on the formula.
  • Common cases: \[ \mathrm{Na_2CO_3\rightarrow \dfrac{M}{2}},\quad \mathrm{KMnO_4(acidic)\rightarrow \dfrac{M}{5}},\quad \mathrm{KMnO_4(neutral/basic)\rightarrow \dfrac{M}{3}},\quad \mathrm{K_2Cr_2O_7(acidic)\rightarrow \dfrac{M}{6}} \]
  • The same oxidising agent can have different equivalent weights in different media because the number of electrons exchanged changes. This is repeatedly important for \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\).
  • At equivalence in redox titrimetry: \[ \mathrm{moles_1\times n_1=moles_2\times n_2} \] This is often faster than writing the full ionic equation.
  • In back-titration, calculate initial moles, subtract the unreacted amount found by titration, then use the balanced equation to find the unknown. If molar mass is asked: \[ \mathrm{molar\ mass=\dfrac{mass}{moles}} \]

    Redox Reactions and Oxidation Number
  • Classically, oxidation means addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen; reduction means removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen. In electron transfer terms, oxidation is loss of electrons and reduction is gain of electrons.
  • Oxidation number increases during oxidation and decreases during reduction. This rule is the fastest way to classify redox changes in EAPCET questions.
  • The sum of oxidation numbers equals the total charge of the species. For a neutral molecule, the sum is zero; for an ion, the sum equals the ionic charge.
  • Hydrogen is usually \(+1\), but it is \(-1\) in metal hydrides where hydrogen is bonded to less electronegative elements. Thus \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) has hydride hydrogen \((-1)\), while \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) has hydrogen \(+1\).
  • For nitrogen compounds, apply the sum rule carefully: \(\mathrm{NH_2OH}\) has \(N=-1\), \(\mathrm{NH_4Cl}\) has \(N=-3\), \(\mathrm{N_2H_4}\) has average \(N=-2\), and \(\mathrm{HNO_2}\) has \(N=+3\).
  • For individual atoms in covalent molecules, the correct structure may be essential. In \(\mathrm{O=C=C=C=O}\) \((\mathrm{C_3O_2})\), the terminal carbon atoms are \(+2\) each, while the middle carbon is \(0\).
  • In \(\mathrm{KClO_3}\) decomposition, chlorine changes from \(+5\) to \(-1\), so chlorine is reduced; oxygen changes from \(-2\) to \(0\), so oxygen is oxidised.
  • Zinc with concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) gives \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), where nitrogen is \(+4\). Zinc with dilute \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) gives \(\mathrm{N_2O}\), where average nitrogen oxidation state is \(+1\).

    Types of Redox Reactions and Disproportionation
  • Combination, decomposition, displacement and disproportionation reactions can all involve redox changes, but not every redox reaction is disproportionation.
  • In ordinary redox reactions, oxidation and reduction occur on different species or elements. In disproportionation, the same element is both oxidised and reduced.
  • To identify disproportionation, check whether the same element forms two products: one with a higher oxidation state and one with a lower oxidation state.
  • For \(\mathrm{Se_2Cl_2}\), selenium has oxidation state \(+1\). Disproportionation gives \(\mathrm{SeCl_4}\), where selenium is \(+4\), and elemental \(\mathrm{Se}\), where selenium is \(0\).
  • \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) and \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\) are common species showing disproportionation in standard patterns. \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) decomposition is not treated as disproportionation if manganese only goes to lower oxidation states.

    Standard Redox Products and Medium Effects
  • The medium changes the final product and \(n\)-factor of many redox species. Therefore, acidic, neutral and basic media must not be treated as interchangeable.
  • In acidic medium, \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) is reduced to \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\), involving a \(5\)-electron change: \[ \mathrm{MnO_4^-+8H^++5e^-\rightarrow Mn^{2+}+4H_2O} \]
  • In acidic medium, \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) oxidises \(5\) moles \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\), while \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\) oxidises \(6\) moles \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\). Hence the standard comparison is \(5:6\).
  • If \(1\) mole acidified \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) oxidises \(x\) moles of \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\), then \(1\) mole acidified \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\) oxidises \(\dfrac{6x}{5}\) moles of \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\).
  • In acidic medium, \(1\) mole \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) oxidises \(2.5\) moles \(\mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}}\): \[ \mathrm{MnO_4^-+8H^++\dfrac{5}{2}C_2O_4^{2-}\rightarrow Mn^{2+}+4H_2O+5CO_2} \]

    Balancing Redox Reactions
  • In oxidation-number method, identify the atoms whose oxidation numbers change, equalise total increase and decrease in oxidation number, then balance remaining atoms.
  • In half-reaction method, split the reaction into oxidation and reduction halves, balance atoms other than H and O, balance O using \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), balance H using \(\mathrm{H^+}\) in acidic medium, and balance charge using electrons.
  • In basic medium, after balancing as if acidic, add \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) to both sides to neutralise \(\mathrm{H^+}\), then simplify \(\mathrm{H^+ + OH^-}\) as \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).
  • For coefficient-based questions, conserve one element at a time and start with an element appearing in the fewest places. Then use charge and oxygen/hydrogen balance to finish.
  • A standard balanced equation in basic medium is: \[ \mathrm{2Cr(OH)_3+IO_3^-+4OH^-\rightarrow2CrO_4^{2-}+I^-+5H_2O} \] The coefficients are \(2,1,4,2,1,5\).

    Redox Titrimetry
  • Redox titrimetry uses stoichiometric electron transfer between an oxidising agent and a reducing agent. At equivalence, equivalents of oxidant and reductant are equal.
  • Use: \[ \mathrm{N_1V_1=N_2V_2} \] only when normalities are known. When molarity is given, convert using \(\mathrm{N=M\times n\text{-}factor}\).
  • In permanganate and dichromate titrations, the medium decides \(n\)-factor. Acidified \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) has \(n=5\), and acidified \(\mathrm{K_2Cr_2O_7}\) has \(n=6\).
  • In back-titration, the amount reacted with the unknown is: \[ \mathrm{initial\ amount - unreacted\ amount} \] Only this reacted amount should be used to calculate the unknown.

    Common Calculation Pitfalls
  • Do not compare molarity alone when the question asks for mass of solute; volume and molar mass must also be included.
  • Do not forget stoichiometric coefficients. Many errors come from using the correct formula but ignoring the mole ratio in the balanced equation.
  • Do not use one \(n\)-factor for a redox species in all media. \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) is the standard warning example.
  • Do not confuse redox with disproportionation. Disproportionation requires the same element to undergo both oxidation and reduction.
  • Do not assign oxidation states of individual atoms in unusual covalent molecules without using the structure.
  • Do not treat \(20\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) as \(20\%\). Volume strength, normality and percentage are different concentration expressions.
  • In mixed carbonate-bicarbonate calculations, remember that \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\) consumes twice as much acid per mole as \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\).

    Variables Used
  • \(n\): number of moles
  • \(N_A\): Avogadro constant
  • \(M\): molarity or molar mass, depending on context
  • \(m\): molality, when used as concentration unit
  • \(V\): volume, usually in litres for molarity calculations
  • \(N\): normality
  • \(n\)-factor: electrons exchanged or replaceable reacting units per formula unit
  • \(T\): temperature
  • \(P\): pressure
  • \(x,y\): variable composition, oxidation-state or formula subscripts depending on question

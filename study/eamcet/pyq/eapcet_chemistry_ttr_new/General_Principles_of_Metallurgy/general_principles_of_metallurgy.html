General Principles of Metallurgy

    Variables used
  • \(\Delta G^\circ\) or \(\Delta G^\Theta\): standard Gibbs energy change used in Ellingham diagrams to compare oxide stability.
  • \(T\): temperature; Ellingham diagram is a plot of \(\Delta G^\circ\) versus \(T\).

    Occurrence of metals and ore identification
  • Direct ore-to-metal mappings
    • Ore-name questions are usually direct memory checks. Sphalerite is \(\mathrm{ZnS}\), so it gives zinc; siderite is \(\mathrm{FeCO_3}\), so it is an iron carbonate ore.
    • Common ore mappings from this PYQ set: sphalerite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Zn}\), siderite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Fe}\), malachite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Cu}\), calamine \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Zn}\), and kaolinite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Al}\).
    • Kaolinite is an aluminium silicate mineral, while calamine is zinc carbonate. The correct metal pair is therefore \(\mathrm{Al,Zn}\), not the reverse.
  • Formula and atomic-number checks
    • For atomic-number matching, remember: \(\mathrm{Zn}=30\), \(\mathrm{Fe}=26\), and \(\mathrm{Cu}=29\). Thus sphalerite, siderite and malachite correspond to \(30,26,29\).
    • Siderite \(=\mathrm{FeCO_3}\); \(\mathrm{ZnCO_3}\) is calamine, \(\mathrm{CuCO_3\cdot Cu(OH)_2}\) is malachite, and \(\mathrm{CuFeS_2}\) is copper pyrites or chalcopyrite.

    Concentration of ores and leaching
  • Basic idea of leaching
    • Leaching is used when the ore or desired component dissolves selectively in a suitable reagent while impurities remain undissolved, or vice versa.
  • Bauxite and aluminium
    • Bauxite, the ore of aluminium, is the most repeated leaching example. In Bayer’s process, bauxite is treated with concentrated \(\mathrm{NaOH}\), and alumina dissolves while many impurities remain undissolved.
    • When the question asks “ore concentrated by leaching,” the safest direct association in this syllabus is bauxite \(\rightarrow\) aluminium.
  • Cyanide leaching of silver
    • Cyanide leaching of silver works by complex formation: \(\mathrm{4Ag+8CN^-+2H_2O+O_2\rightarrow4[Ag(CN)_2]^-+4OH^-}\). This is chemical dissolution, not a physical concentration method like froth flotation or levigation.

    Calcination, roasting and conversion to oxide
  • Calcination
    • Calcination is heating an ore in absence or limited supply of air, usually to remove water or \(\mathrm{CO_2}\). Hydrated and carbonate ores are typical calcination cases.
    • The reaction \(\mathrm{Fe_2O_3\cdot xH_2O\xrightarrow{\Delta}Fe_2O_3+xH_2O}\) is calcination because it shows dehydration of a hydrated ore.
  • Roasting
    • Roasting is heating sulphide ores in excess air or oxygen. If a reaction contains sulphide ore, \(\mathrm{O_2}\), and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), it generally indicates roasting, not calcination.
    • In copper extraction, roasting includes \(\mathrm{2Cu_2S+3O_2\rightarrow2Cu_2O+2SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{2FeS+3O_2\rightarrow2FeO+2SO_2}\). These reactions convert sulphides partly into oxides and remove sulphur as \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).

    Extraction of copper
  • Overall extraction sequence
    • Copper pyrites/copper sulphide extraction repeatedly uses the sequence: roasting of sulphides, removal of iron impurity as slag, and self-reduction to copper.
    • Copper matte is mainly a mixture of sulphides of copper and iron, \(\mathrm{Cu_2S+FeS}\). Do not confuse matte with slag; matte is sulphide-rich, while slag is silicate-rich.
  • Roasting and slag formation
    • Iron impurity in copper extraction is removed using silica flux: \(\mathrm{FeO+SiO_2\rightarrow FeSiO_3}\). The slag is ferrous silicate, \(\mathrm{FeSiO_3}\).
    • \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\rightarrow CaSiO_3}\) is not the standard slag reaction in copper extraction; it belongs more naturally to iron extraction where lime removes silica.
  • Self-reduction and linked shape question
    • Self-reduction in copper extraction uses \(\mathrm{2Cu_2O+Cu_2S\rightarrow6Cu+SO_2}\). The gaseous product is \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).
    • In linked molecular-shape questions, once \(Y=\mathrm{SO_2}\) is identified, its shape is angular or bent because sulfur has one lone pair. It is not linear like \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).

    Extraction of iron and slag formation
  • Flux, gangue and slag
    • In the blast furnace, the common acidic gangue impurity is silica, \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\). Limestone, \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\), is added as flux.
    • Limestone first decomposes: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}CaO+CO_2}\). The basic oxide \(\mathrm{CaO}\) then reacts with acidic silica to form slag: \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\rightarrow CaSiO_3}\).
    • For iron extraction, remember the pair: impurity \(=\mathrm{SiO_2}\), added flux \(=\mathrm{CaCO_3}\), slag \(=\mathrm{CaSiO_3}\). Do not confuse flux with slag.

    Thermodynamic principles and Ellingham diagram
  • What the diagram represents
    • Ellingham diagram is a plot of standard Gibbs energy change \(\Delta G^\circ\) against temperature \(T\). It is used to compare oxide stability and to predict a suitable reducing agent for oxide ores.
    • Do not confuse Ellingham diagram with plots involving \(\dfrac{1}{T}\). Ellingham diagram uses \(T\) on the x-axis, not \(\dfrac{1}{T}\).
  • Formula and interpretation
    • The basic relation behind the diagram is \(\Delta G^\circ=\Delta H^\circ-T\Delta S^\circ\). However, the repeatedly tested exam point is not calculation but interpretation of line position.
    • Lower \(\Delta G^\circ\) means more stable oxide. A statement saying “higher \(\Delta G^\circ\) means more stable oxide” is incorrect.
    • A reducing agent can reduce a metal oxide when the line for oxidation of the reducing agent lies below the line for formation of the metal oxide at that temperature. The lower line indicates stronger tendency to form oxide.

    Refining of crude metals
  • Distillation and liquation
    • Distillation is used when the metal has low boiling point and the impurity has high boiling point. The metal vaporises on heating and is collected after condensation.
    • Zinc from sphalerite is purified by distillation because zinc is relatively volatile. Common distillation examples are \(\mathrm{Zn}\) and \(\mathrm{Hg}\).
    • Liquation is based on low melting point of the metal, not low boiling point. This distinction is important because liquation and distillation are easily confused.
  • Poling and zone refining
    • Poling is used for refining metals such as copper and tin by removing oxide impurities from the molten metal.
    • Zone refining is used when very high purity is required, especially for semiconductor-type metals. In this PYQ set, gallium is matched with zone refining.
  • Vapour phase refining
    • Vapour phase refining requires the metal to form a volatile compound which later decomposes to give pure metal. Do not confuse it with simple distillation, where the metal itself vaporises.
    • Special vapour phase examples: Mond process refines \(\mathrm{Ni}\), while van Arkel process refines \(\mathrm{Ti}\) or \(\mathrm{Zr}\) through volatile iodide formation and decomposition.
  • High-yield refining matches
    • High-yield refining matches from this set: Ga \(\rightarrow\) zone refining, Cu \(\rightarrow\) poling, Sn \(\rightarrow\) liquation, Ti \(\rightarrow\) vapour phase refining, Zn \(\rightarrow\) distillation.

    Uses of metals and alloys
  • Fixed alloy-use pairs
    • Alloy-use matching is memory-based and should be revised as fixed pairs. \(\mathrm{Li-Pb}\) alloy is used for bearings in motor engines.
    • \(\mathrm{Be-Cu}\) alloy is used for high-strength springs, \(\mathrm{Mg-Al}\) alloy is used in aircraft construction, and \(\mathrm{Na-Pb}\) alloy is used to make tetraethyl lead.
    • Do not confuse \(\mathrm{Li-Pb}\) and \(\mathrm{Na-Pb}\). Both contain lead, but their uses are different: \(\mathrm{Li-Pb}\) \(\rightarrow\) bearings; \(\mathrm{Na-Pb}\) \(\rightarrow\) tetraethyl lead.

    Quick decision rules
  • Ore and concentration clues
    • Ore name first, then metal: sphalerite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Zn}\), siderite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Fe}\), malachite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Cu}\), calamine \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Zn}\), kaolinite \(\rightarrow\mathrm{Al}\).
    • Concentration keyword “leaching” usually points to bauxite/aluminium, unless the reaction clearly shows cyanide complex formation for silver.
  • Thermal treatment clues
    • Sulphide ore + \(\mathrm{O_2}\) + \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) generally signals roasting. Hydrated ore losing water on heating signals calcination.
  • Flux-gangue and refining clues
    • Flux-gangue logic: acidic impurity requires basic flux, and basic impurity requires acidic flux. In iron extraction, \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) is removed by \(\mathrm{CaO}\); in copper extraction, \(\mathrm{FeO}\) is removed by \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\).
    • Refining keyword logic: low boiling point \(\rightarrow\) distillation; low melting point \(\rightarrow\) liquation; oxide impurity in molten metal \(\rightarrow\) poling; volatile compound formation \(\rightarrow\) vapour phase refining.

    • \(\Delta H^\circ\): standard enthalpy change; \(\Delta S^\circ\): standard entropy change.

      Occurrence of metals
    • Metals occur either in native state or combined state. Less reactive noble metals such as gold, silver and platinum may occur free, while reactive metals occur mainly as oxides, sulphides, carbonates, halides or silicates.
    • A mineral is a naturally occurring substance containing a metal or its compound. An ore is a mineral from which the metal can be extracted economically and in useful amount.
    • Gangue or matrix refers to unwanted earthy and rocky impurities present along with the ore. Metallurgy involves removal of gangue, conversion of the ore to a reducible form, reduction to metal and final refining.
    • Common ore-name links are important for quick recall: sphalerite/zinc blende \(=\mathrm{ZnS}\), siderite \(=\mathrm{FeCO_3}\), calamine \(=\mathrm{ZnCO_3}\), malachite \(=\mathrm{CuCO_3\cdot Cu(OH)_2}\), copper pyrites/chalcopyrite \(=\mathrm{CuFeS_2}\).
    • Kaolinite is an aluminium silicate mineral, while bauxite is the main ore of aluminium. Useful atomic numbers from the PYQs are \(\mathrm{Zn}=30\), \(\mathrm{Fe}=26\), and \(\mathrm{Cu}=29\).

      General steps in metallurgy
    • The common sequence is crushing and pulverisation, concentration or dressing of ore, calcination or roasting, reduction of oxide to free metal, and purification or refining.
    • Crushing breaks large ore lumps into smaller pieces, while pulverisation converts crushed ore into fine powder. This increases working surface and prepares the ore for concentration.
    • Concentration or ore dressing removes gangue from powdered ore. The method is chosen from the physical or chemical property that best separates ore from impurity.

      Concentration of ores
    • Gravity separation or hydraulic washing is used when ore particles are heavier than gangue. A stream of water washes away lighter earthy impurities while denser ore particles settle.
    • Magnetic separation is used when either the ore or impurity is magnetic. For example, tinstone \(\mathrm{SnO_2}\) is non-magnetic but may contain magnetic tungstate impurities.
    • Froth flotation is mainly used for sulphide ores such as galena \(\mathrm{PbS}\), zinc blende \(\mathrm{ZnS}\), and copper pyrites \(\mathrm{CuFeS_2}\). Sulphide ore is preferentially wetted by oil and rises with froth, while gangue is wetted by water and sinks.
    • Depressants help separate two sulphide ores. In a mixture containing \(\mathrm{ZnS}\) and \(\mathrm{PbS}\), \(\mathrm{NaCN}\) depresses \(\mathrm{ZnS}\) and prevents it from coming into the froth.
    • Leaching is a chemical concentration method in which a suitable reagent dissolves the desired ore component, leaving insoluble impurities behind. It is especially important for bauxite in aluminium extraction.
    • In Bayer’s process, bauxite is treated with concentrated \(\mathrm{NaOH}\); alumina dissolves as sodium aluminate while impurities such as \(\mathrm{Fe_2O_3}\), \(\mathrm{TiO_2}\), and \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) are removed. A useful representation is \(\mathrm{Al_2O_3+6NaOH\rightarrow2Na_3AlO_3+3H_2O}\).
    • Cyanide leaching of silver and gold is also based on selective complex formation. For silver, \(\mathrm{4Ag+8CN^-+2H_2O+O_2\rightarrow4[Ag(CN)_2]^-+4OH^-}\); this is leaching, not froth flotation or levigation.

      Calcination and roasting
    • Calcination is heating concentrated ore in limited supply of air so that moisture, water of hydration or volatile gases are removed without melting the ore. It is common for hydrated and carbonate ores.
    • Typical calcination reactions are \(\mathrm{Al_2O_3\cdot2H_2O\xrightarrow{\Delta}Al_2O_3+2H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{ZnCO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}ZnO+CO_2}\), and \(\mathrm{Fe_2O_3\cdot xH_2O\xrightarrow{\Delta}Fe_2O_3+xH_2O}\).
    • Roasting is heating ore in free supply of air. It removes volatile impurities and converts sulphide ores into oxides with evolution of \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).
    • For sulphide ores, the strong recognition pattern is sulphide \(+\mathrm{O_2}\rightarrow\) oxide \(+\mathrm{SO_2}\). Examples are \(\mathrm{2ZnS+3O_2\rightarrow2ZnO+2SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{2PbS+3O_2\rightarrow2PbO+2SO_2}\).
    • If the reaction shows a hydrated ore losing water, choose calcination; if it shows sulphide ore heated with oxygen and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) formation, choose roasting.

      Reduction of metal oxides and smelting
    • After calcination or roasting, metal oxides are commonly reduced to metals. Smelting is reduction of oxide ore in molten state using carbon, carbon monoxide or another reducing agent.
    • Carbon or carbon monoxide is used for metals such as iron, tin and zinc: \(\mathrm{Fe_2O_3+3C\rightarrow2Fe+3CO}\), \(\mathrm{SnO_2+2C\rightarrow Sn+2CO}\), and \(\mathrm{ZnO+C\rightarrow Zn+CO}\).
    • Some oxides require stronger reducing agents. In Goldschmidt aluminothermic reduction, aluminium reduces oxides such as \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_3}\) and \(\mathrm{Mn_3O_4}\): \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_3+2Al\rightarrow2Cr+Al_2O_3+heat}\).
    • Self-reduction is used for some sulphide ores of copper, mercury and lead. A part of sulphide is oxidised to oxide or sulphate, which then reacts with remaining sulphide to give metal.
    • For copper glance, the key self-reduction sequence is \(\mathrm{2Cu_2S+3O_2\rightarrow2Cu_2O+2SO_2}\), followed by \(\mathrm{2Cu_2O+Cu_2S\rightarrow6Cu+SO_2}\). The \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) formed gives blister copper its blistered appearance.

      Flux and slag
    • Flux is a substance added to combine with gangue at high temperature. Slag is the fusible product formed by reaction of flux with gangue, and it separates from molten metal.
    • The guiding rule is: acidic impurity is removed by basic flux, and basic impurity is removed by acidic flux.
    • In copper extraction, \(\mathrm{FeO}\) is a basic impurity and silica \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) is added as acidic flux. The slag is ferrous silicate: \(\mathrm{FeO+SiO_2\rightarrow FeSiO_3}\).
    • In iron extraction, \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) is the acidic gangue and limestone \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\) is added. Limestone decomposes to basic \(\mathrm{CaO}\), which forms calcium silicate slag: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}CaO+CO_2}\), then \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\rightarrow CaSiO_3}\).
    • The pair \(\mathrm{FeO+SiO_2\rightarrow FeSiO_3}\) is central to copper extraction, while \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\rightarrow CaSiO_3}\) is central to iron extraction. This distinction directly prevents wrong answers.

      Thermodynamic principles and Ellingham diagram
    • Thermodynamic feasibility of metallurgical reactions is judged using Gibbs energy. The basic relation is \(\Delta G^\circ=\Delta H^\circ-T\Delta S^\circ\); a negative \(\Delta G^\circ\) indicates feasibility under the given conditions.
    • Ellingham diagram is a graph of \(\Delta G^\circ\) versus temperature \(T\) for oxide formation reactions. It is used to compare oxide stability and choose reducing agents.
    • A lower line, or lower \(\Delta G^\circ\), represents a more stable oxide. A reducing agent can reduce a metal oxide if the line for oxidation of the reducing agent lies below the metal oxide line at that temperature.
    • Do not confuse Ellingham diagram with plots of \(\dfrac{1}{T}\). Ellingham diagram uses temperature \(T\) on the x-axis.
    • Ellingham diagram predicts feasibility, not reaction rate. It assumes equilibrium and does not give kinetic information.
    • For iron extraction, reduction by coke or carbon monoxide becomes feasible at suitable furnace temperatures because the carbon-to-carbon monoxide line lies below the iron oxide line. For copper oxide, reduction is comparatively easier because the \(\mathrm{Cu_2O}\) line lies high.

      Extraction of iron
    • Iron oxide ores are reduced in the blast furnace by carbon and carbon monoxide. The temperature is highest near the bottom and lower near the top, so different reductions occur in different zones.
    • Important blast-furnace reactions include \(\mathrm{3Fe_2O_3+CO\rightarrow2Fe_3O_4+CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{Fe_3O_4+CO\rightarrow3FeO+CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{FeO+CO\rightarrow Fe+CO_2}\), and \(\mathrm{FeO+C\rightarrow Fe+CO}\).
    • Coke forms carbon monoxide inside the furnace: \(\mathrm{C+O_2\rightarrow CO_2}\), followed by \(\mathrm{C+CO_2\rightarrow2CO}\). Carbon monoxide acts as the main reducing gas in many furnace zones.
    • Limestone removes silica impurity as slag: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}CaO+CO_2}\), then \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\rightarrow CaSiO_3}\). Thus in blast-furnace PYQs, \(X=\mathrm{SiO_2}\) and \(Y=\mathrm{CaCO_3}\).
    • Pig iron contains about \(4\%\) carbon along with impurities. Cast iron has lower carbon than pig iron, and wrought iron is the nearly pure malleable form prepared by oxidising impurities.

      Extraction of copper
    • Copper commonly occurs as sulphide ores such as copper pyrites and copper glance. During extraction, sulphides are roasted to oxides and then reduced, with important slag formation steps.
    • The roasting reactions include \(\mathrm{2Cu_2S+3O_2\rightarrow2Cu_2O+2SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{2FeS+3O_2\rightarrow2FeO+2SO_2}\). Iron oxide impurity is removed by silica: \(\mathrm{FeO+SiO_2\rightarrow FeSiO_3}\).
    • Copper matte is mainly a mixture of \(\mathrm{Cu_2S}\) and \(\mathrm{FeS}\). It is sulphide-rich, while slag is silicate-rich; this distinction is often tested directly.
    • Self-reduction gives copper: \(\mathrm{2Cu_2O+Cu_2S\rightarrow6Cu+SO_2}\). The gas formed is \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), whose molecular shape is angular because sulfur has a lone pair.
    • Blister copper is named so because escaping \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) gas forms blisters on the surface of solidifying copper.

      Extraction of zinc and aluminium
    • Zinc blende or sphalerite is \(\mathrm{ZnS}\). It is roasted to \(\mathrm{ZnO}\), then reduced with coke: \(\mathrm{ZnO+C\rightarrow Zn+CO}\) at high temperature.
    • Crude zinc is purified by distillation because zinc is relatively volatile. Thus the chain \(\mathrm{ZnS\rightarrow Zn\rightarrow distillation}\) is a frequent exam shortcut.
    • Aluminium is extracted from purified alumina by the Hall-Heroult process. Pure \(\mathrm{Al_2O_3}\) is dissolved in molten cryolite \(\mathrm{Na_3AlF_6}\) or \(\mathrm{CaF_2}\) to lower melting point and increase conductivity.
    • The cathodic reaction is \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}+3e^-\rightarrow Al(l)}\). Carbon anodes are consumed because oxygen-containing ions react with carbon to form \(\mathrm{CO}\) and \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • The overall aluminium extraction reaction may be written as \(\mathrm{2Al_2O_3+3C\rightarrow4Al+3CO_2}\).

      Electrochemical principles of metallurgy
    • Electrochemical extraction and displacement reactions follow the relation \(\Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ\). If \(E^\circ\) is positive, \(\Delta G^\circ\) becomes negative and the redox process is feasible.
    • Reactive metals with very negative reduction potentials are not easily reduced from aqueous solution and are often obtained by electrolysis of fused salts or molten oxides.
    • Less reactive metals can be displaced from solution by more reactive metals. For example, \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}(aq)+Fe(s)\rightarrow Cu(s)+Fe^{2+}(aq)}\) is used for recovery of copper from low-grade ores or scraps.
    • Oxidative extraction is used for some non-metals. For example, chlorine can be obtained from brine with external emf because the reaction has positive \(\Delta G^\circ\) and needs electrolysis.

      Refining of metals
    • Refining removes impurities such as other metals, unreduced oxide, non-metals, flux or slag. The method is selected from the physical or chemical property that best separates metal from impurity.
    • Liquation is used for easily fusible metals such as tin and lead. The impure metal is heated slightly above its melting point, and molten pure metal drains away from infusible impurities.
    • Poling is used for copper and tin. The impure molten metal is stirred with green logs or bamboo, whose hydrocarbons reduce oxide impurities.
    • Distillation is used for volatile metals such as zinc and mercury. Low boiling metal vaporises and distils over, leaving high boiling or non-volatile impurities behind.
    • Electrolytic refining uses impure metal as anode, pure metal sheet as cathode and a suitable metal salt solution as electrolyte. Metal dissolves from anode and deposits in pure form on cathode; less electropositive impurities settle as anode mud.
    • In electrolytic refining of copper, \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}+2e^-\rightarrow Cu}\) at cathode and \(\mathrm{Cu\rightarrow Cu^{2+}+2e^-}\) at anode. Silver and gold may collect as anode mud.
    • Zone refining is based on greater solubility of impurities in molten metal than in solid metal. A moving molten zone carries impurities to one end, which is finally cut off.
    • Zone refining gives very high purity and is used for elements such as \(\mathrm{Ge}\), \(\mathrm{Si}\), \(\mathrm{Ga}\), \(\mathrm{B}\), and \(\mathrm{In}\). In the PYQs, gallium is matched with zone refining.
    • Vapour phase refining requires formation of a volatile compound followed by decomposition to pure metal. This is different from distillation, where the metal itself vaporises.
    • In Mond process, \(\mathrm{Ni}\) forms volatile \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\), which decomposes to pure nickel. In van Arkel method, \(\mathrm{Ti}\) or \(\mathrm{Zr}\) forms volatile iodide such as \(\mathrm{ZrI_4}\), which decomposes on a hot filament.

      Uses of aluminium, copper, zinc and iron
    • Copper is a good conductor of heat and electricity, so it is used in electrical and thermal equipment. Important copper alloys include brass, bronze, gun metal and nickel silver.
    • Zinc is used in batteries, galvanisation of iron, alloys such as brass and German silver, and as a reducing agent in synthetic chemistry.
    • Iron is used widely in construction and engineering. Wrought iron is used for wires, bolts, nuts, anchors, chains and agricultural equipment; nickel steel and chrome steel have specialised mechanical uses.
    • Aluminium is used in food foils, paints and lacquers, electrical instruments and light-weight alloys. It is also used as a reducing agent in extraction of chromium and manganese from their oxides.
    • Important alloy-use pairs from PYQs: \(\mathrm{Li-Pb}\) alloy for motor-engine bearings, \(\mathrm{Be-Cu}\) for high-strength springs, \(\mathrm{Mg-Al}\) for aircraft construction, and \(\mathrm{Na-Pb}\) for preparing tetraethyl lead.

      Exam-useful decision links
    • Ore-name route: first identify the ore, then metal, then process. Example: sphalerite \(=\mathrm{ZnS}\rightarrow\mathrm{Zn}\rightarrow\) distillation.
    • Concentration route: sulphide ore \(\rightarrow\) froth flotation; bauxite \(\rightarrow\) leaching with \(\mathrm{NaOH}\); silver/gold cyanide complex formation \(\rightarrow\) leaching.
    • Thermal conversion route: hydrated/carbonate ore losing water or \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) \(\rightarrow\) calcination; sulphide ore with \(\mathrm{O_2}\) giving \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) \(\rightarrow\) roasting.
    • Refining route: low melting point \(\rightarrow\) liquation; low boiling point \(\rightarrow\) distillation; oxide impurity in molten Cu/Sn \(\rightarrow\) poling; volatile compound formation \(\rightarrow\) vapour phase refining; ultra-pure semiconductor-type metal \(\rightarrow\) zone refining.
    • Ellingham route: lower line means more stable oxide; reducing-agent oxidation line must lie below the metal-oxide line; the diagram predicts feasibility, not speed.

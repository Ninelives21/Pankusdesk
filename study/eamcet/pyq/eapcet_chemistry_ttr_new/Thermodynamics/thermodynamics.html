Thermodynamics

    Thermodynamic basics and property types
  • State vs path: Internal energy \(\Delta U\) is a state function, so it depends only on initial and final states; work \(w\) is a path function, so different paths between the same states can give different work.
  • On a \(P\)-\(V\) graph: work is the area under the curve, \(w=-\int P\,dV\). For the same start and end states, the higher curve gives larger magnitude of work.
  • Intensive properties: independent of amount of substance — temperature, density, molar volume.
  • Extensive properties: depend on amount of substance — mass, volume, internal energy, enthalpy.
  • Pitfall: do not confuse molar volume with volume — molar volume is intensive, volume is extensive.

    Work, heat and ideal-gas processes
  • Isothermal reversible expansion/compression: \(w=-nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)=-nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)=-2.303\,nRT\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)\).
  • Fast exam use: if pressure form is easier, use \(w=-nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)\); if \(P_1/P_2=10\), then \(\log_{10}(10)=1\), which makes calculation quick.
  • Reverse-use formula: if work is given and temperature is asked in an isothermal reversible process, use \(T=\dfrac{-w}{nR\ln(V_2/V_1)}\).
  • Unit shortcut: if \(R=0.0821\ \text{L atm mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}\), the work comes directly in L·atm; convert later using \(1\ \text{L atm}=101.3\ \text{J}\).
  • Irreversible process against constant external pressure: \(w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\). This is not the same as the reversible \(\ln\) formula.
  • Gas generated at constant external pressure: for ideal gases, \(P\Delta V=\Delta n_gRT\), so when gas is produced from zero initial gas volume, \(w=-\Delta n_gRT\).
  • At constant pressure: \(q_p=nC_p\Delta T\) and \(\Delta H=q_p=nC_p\Delta T\).
  • Pitfall: use chemistry sign convention carefully — expansion work is negative, compression work is positive.

    \(\Delta U\), \(\Delta H\) and gaseous mole change
  • Core relation: \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\), where \(\Delta n_g\) counts only gaseous moles.
  • Very common use: first convert \(\Delta U\) to \(\Delta H\), then use \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\) if Gibbs energy is asked.
  • Special case: if no gaseous mole change occurs, \(\Delta n_g=0\), so \(\Delta H=\Delta U\).
  • Phase-change pitfall: for transformations involving only solids/liquids, do not insert gas correction unless gaseous moles actually change.

    Reversible, irreversible and adiabatic processes
  • Reversible process criterion: it proceeds through equilibrium states and can be reversed by an infinitesimal change in conditions.
  • Standard reversible examples: melting at melting point and vaporisation at boiling point.
  • Standard irreversible examples: free expansion into vacuum and neutralisation of acid by base.
  • Adiabatic relation: \(PV^\gamma=\text{constant}\).
  • Percentage-change form: \(\dfrac{dP}{P}=-\gamma\dfrac{dV}{V}\), so pressure and volume change in opposite directions; for small changes, \(|\%\Delta P|=\gamma\,|\%\Delta V|\).
  • Pitfall: “adiabatic” does not mean “isothermal”; do not use the isothermal \(\ln\) work formula in adiabatic questions.

    Enthalpy of reaction, formation, combustion and Hess’s law
  • Master formula: \(\Delta_r H^\circ=\sum \nu \Delta_f H^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu \Delta_f H^\circ(\text{reactants})\).
  • Must-remember fact: \(\Delta_f H^\circ=0\) for any element in its standard state.
  • Carbon special fact: standard state of carbon is graphite, so \(\Delta_f H^\circ(\text{graphite})=0\), not diamond or fullerene.
  • Meaning of \(\Delta_f H^\circ\): it is for formation of 1 mole of compound from its constituent elements in standard states.
  • Stoichiometric scaling: if the balanced reaction forms 2 moles of product, multiply the molar \(\Delta_f H^\circ\) by 2.
  • Hess’s law method: reverse and multiply equations as needed, then add them so unwanted species cancel; the target \(\Delta H\) is the algebraic sum of the adjusted enthalpies.
  • Combustion-data shortcut for \(\Delta_f H^\circ\): when formation enthalpy of a compound is asked from combustion values, add combustion enthalpies of the constituent elements to form the same final combustion products, then subtract combustion enthalpy of the compound.
  • Combustion of CO pattern: \(\Delta_c H^\circ(\mathrm{CO})=\Delta_f H^\circ(\mathrm{CO_2})-\Delta_f H^\circ(\mathrm{CO})\).
  • Decomposition questions: use the same formation-enthalpy identity directly and solve the unknown \(\Delta_f H^\circ\).
  • Pitfall: do not forget to multiply enthalpy values when you multiply the chemical equation.

    Bond enthalpy and approximate reaction enthalpy
  • Bond enthalpy formula: \(\Delta H \approx \sum(\text{bond enthalpies of bonds broken})-\sum(\text{bond enthalpies of bonds formed})\).
  • Standard application type: formation of water/heavy water — break H–H or D–D and \(\tfrac12\)O=O, then form two O–H or O–D bonds.
  • Pitfall: bond enthalpy questions are often approximate; count bonds carefully and distinguish bonds broken from bonds formed.

    Gibbs energy, entropy, spontaneity and equilibrium
  • Gibbs relation: \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\).
  • Direction-change temperature: at equilibrium, \(\Delta G=0\), so \(T=\dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}\) provided units are consistent.
  • Standard Gibbs-equilibrium relation: \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303\,RT\log_{10}K\).
  • Quick inference: if \(\Delta G^\circ<0\), then \(K>1\); if \(\Delta G^\circ>0\), then \(K<1\).
  • At 298 K exam form: \(\log_{10}K=\dfrac{-\Delta G^\circ}{2.303RT}\).
  • Pitfall: before using \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\), make sure \(\Delta H\) and \(T\Delta S\) are in the same units.

    Equilibrium constant and catalyst facts
  • In equilibrium expressions, pure solids and pure liquids are omitted because their activity is taken as 1.
  • Typical form: for \(A(s)\rightleftharpoons B(s)+C(g)\), the equilibrium constant depends only on gaseous species, so \(K_p=p_C\).
  • Catalyst fact: a catalyst changes only the rate of reaching equilibrium; it does not change \(\Delta H\), \(\Delta G^\circ\), \(K\), or equilibrium composition.
  • Pitfall: never include pure solids/pure liquids in \(K_c\) or \(K_p\) expressions.

    Must-remember exam patterns and pitfalls
  • Most repeated patterns from PYQs: Hess’s law manipulation, \(\Delta_r H^\circ\) from \(\Delta_f H^\circ\), isothermal reversible work, irreversible work against constant \(P_{\text{ext}}\), \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\), and \(\Delta G^\circ\)-\(K\) relation.
  • Carbon allotrope order: fullerene \(>\) diamond \(>\) graphite in \(\Delta_f H^\circ\), because graphite is the most stable standard state.
  • Do not mix formulas: reversible isothermal \(\ln\) formula and irreversible constant-\(P_{\text{ext}}\) formula are different and not interchangeable.
  • Always check: standard state, stoichiometric coefficient, gaseous mole change, and unit conversion before finalising the answer.

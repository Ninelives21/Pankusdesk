Thermodynamics

    Work, path and process-type PYQs
  • The most repeated gas-work pattern is this: for reversible isothermal expansion or compression of an ideal gas, use \(w=-nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)\), or equivalently \(w=-nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)\).
  • If the question uses common logarithm, write the same result as \(w=-2.303\,nRT\log\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)\) or \(w=-2.303\,nRT\log\!\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)\).
  • The sign convention is a frequent trap: during expansion, work done by the gas is negative in chemistry convention; during compression, work done on the gas is positive.
  • If the process is irreversible and occurs against constant external pressure, do not use the logarithmic formula; use \(w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\).
  • When gas is generated from effectively zero initial gas volume at constant external pressure, the shortcut \(w=-\Delta n_gRT\) is often the fastest route.
  • On a \(P\)-\(V\) graph, work depends on the path and equals the area under the curve, while \(\Delta U\) remains the same for all paths between the same initial and final state.
  • Conceptual PYQs on reversibility usually reduce to one idea: reversible processes pass through equilibrium states, so melting or boiling exactly at the transition temperature is reversible, whereas free expansion into vacuum and ordinary neutralisation are irreversible.

    \(\Delta U\), \(\Delta H\) and direct-use formulas
  • The basic first-law equation is \(\Delta U=q+w\), but in objective questions it usually appears through its special cases rather than in raw form.
  • At constant volume, \(\Delta U=q_v\), so heat measured at constant volume gives internal-energy change directly.
  • At constant pressure, \(\Delta H=q_p\), and this is why many heating questions directly become enthalpy questions.
  • A direct high-yield result from the PYQs is \(q_p=nC_p\Delta T\), so for constant-pressure heating of an ideal gas, \(\Delta H=nC_p\Delta T\).
  • For gas-phase reactions, the bridge formula \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\) must be remembered exactly, because several PYQs first make you convert \(\Delta U\) to \(\Delta H\) and only then continue.
  • In \(\Delta n_g\), count only gaseous moles; solids and liquids do not contribute.
  • If there is no gaseous mole change, then \(\Delta n_g=0\), so \(\Delta H=\Delta U\), which is often the quickest elimination step in MCQs.

    Formation, combustion and Hess-law PYQs
  • The most-used reaction-enthalpy formula is \(\Delta_rH^\circ=\sum \nu\,\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu\,\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants})\), and many PYQs are simply careful substitution into this relation.
  • The definition of \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) matters in exam language: it is the enthalpy change for forming one mole of a compound from its elements in their standard states.
  • A very common trap is forgetting coefficients: if the balanced equation forms 2 moles of a compound, its \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) contribution must also be multiplied by 2.
  • The fact \(\Delta_fH^\circ=0\) for an element in its standard state is repeatedly tested, and for carbon the standard state is graphite, not diamond or fullerene.
  • Combustion-based PYQs are usually Hess’s-law questions in disguise: make both routes end at the same combustion products, then add or subtract equations so the target reaction survives.
  • Hess’s law should be treated algebraically: reverse an equation and the sign of \(\Delta H\) changes; multiply an equation and \(\Delta H\) must be multiplied by the same factor.
  • If the target reaction is not directly measurable, that is usually your signal that Hess’s law is the intended method.
  • A neat recurring pattern is that combustion enthalpy and formation enthalpy can be interconverted by comparing two routes to the same final products.

    Bond enthalpy and special factual points
  • Bond-enthalpy numericals use one standard estimate: \(\Delta H \approx \sum(\text{bond enthalpies of bonds broken})-\sum(\text{bond enthalpies of bonds formed})\).
  • The safest way to avoid mistakes is to list bonds broken and bonds formed separately before inserting numbers.
  • Bond dissociation enthalpy refers to breaking a particular bond in a particular gaseous molecule, whereas bond enthalpy is an average value for that bond type across gaseous compounds.
  • Among allotropes, the more stable form has lower \(\Delta_fH^\circ\), so for carbon the order is fullerene \(>\) diamond \(>\) graphite.
  • The intensive-versus-extensive classification has appeared directly in PYQs, so it is worth keeping a compact memory set: temperature, density and molar volume are intensive, whereas mass, volume, internal energy and enthalpy are extensive.

    Entropy, Gibbs energy and spontaneity PYQs
  • The chapter’s most useful spontaneity formula is \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\), and PYQs mostly use it either to judge spontaneity or to find the temperature where spontaneity changes.
  • If \(\Delta G<0\), the process is spontaneous; if \(\Delta G=0\), the system is at equilibrium; and if \(\Delta G>0\), the process is non-spontaneous.
  • When the question asks for the temperature at which behaviour changes, set \(\Delta G=0\) and use \(T=\dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}\), after first making the units consistent.
  • The sign-combination logic is highly testable: \(\Delta H<0,\Delta S>0\) means spontaneous at all temperatures, while \(\Delta H>0,\Delta S<0\) means non-spontaneous at all temperatures.
  • If both \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta S\) are negative, the reaction is favoured at low temperature; if both are positive, it is favoured at high temperature.
  • Entropy-based conceptual PYQs usually rely on simple trends: disorder increases from solid to liquid to gas, and entropy usually increases on mixing, melting, vaporisation, or increase in gaseous particles.
  • For phase transitions at the transition temperature, the handy relation is \(\Delta S=\dfrac{\Delta H_{\text{transition}}}{T}\).
  • The standard equilibrium link is \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303\,RT\log K\), so \(\Delta G^\circ<0\Rightarrow K>1\) and \(\Delta G^\circ>0\Rightarrow K<1\).
  • In many PYQs, once \(\Delta G^\circ\) is known, finding \(K\) is just a direct substitution step and not a new concept.

    Fast exam traps
  • Do not mix up reversible isothermal work with irreversible work against constant external pressure; that is probably the single most common method error in this chapter.
  • Do not forget physical states in thermochemical equations, because enthalpy depends on whether a substance is in the solid, liquid, gaseous or aqueous state.
  • Do not forget to convert \(\Delta S\) into kJ K\(^{-1}\) mol\(^{-1}\) if \(\Delta H\) is in kJ before using \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\).
  • Do not count solids and liquids in \(\Delta n_g\), and do not forget that pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from equilibrium expressions.
  • Do not forget that a catalyst changes the rate of reaching equilibrium but does not change \(\Delta H\), \(\Delta G^\circ\) or \(K\).
  • In Hess’s-law questions, if your number looks odd, first check whether you reversed a reaction without changing the sign of \(\Delta H\), or changed coefficients without scaling \(\Delta H\).

    Thermodynamic basics
  • Thermodynamics studies energy changes that accompany physical and chemical processes. For this chapter, the exam focus is mainly on heat, work, internal energy, enthalpy, entropy and Gibbs energy.
  • System is the part under study; everything else is the surroundings.
  • Open system: exchanges both matter and energy with surroundings. Closed system: exchanges energy but not matter. Isolated system: exchanges neither matter nor energy.
  • The state of a system is described by measurable quantities such as pressure, volume and temperature. These are called state variables.
  • State functions depend only on initial and final states, not on path. High-yield examples: \(U\), \(H\), \(S\), \(G\), \(P\), \(V\), \(T\).
  • Path functions depend on how the change occurs. The two must-know path functions here are heat \(q\) and work \(w\).
  • Extensive properties depend on amount of substance: mass, volume, internal energy, enthalpy. Intensive properties do not: temperature, pressure, density. This distinction has been directly tested in PYQs.

    Processes and sign ideas
  • Isothermal process: temperature remains constant. This is the setting of many PYQs on ideal-gas work.
  • Adiabatic process: no heat exchange with surroundings, so \(q=0\).
  • Reversible process: proceeds through a continuous series of equilibrium states and can be reversed by an infinitesimal change in conditions.
  • Irreversible process: happens suddenly or through non-equilibrium states; free expansion into vacuum is the standard example.
  • Exam shortcut: phase changes exactly at the transition temperature are treated as reversible; free expansion and ordinary neutralisation are irreversible. This is directly aligned with the PYQs.
  • Exothermic reactions release heat, so \(\Delta H<0\). Endothermic reactions absorb heat, so \(\Delta H>0\).
  • Chemistry sign convention used here: heat absorbed by the system is positive; work done on the system is positive; work done by the system is negative.

    First law, internal energy and work
  • The first law of thermodynamics is the energy-conservation statement: energy is neither created nor destroyed.
  • The central equation is \(\Delta U = q + w\).
  • Internal energy \(U\) is a state function. Absolute \(U\) is not measured directly, but change in internal energy \(\Delta U\) is meaningful and measurable.
  • For expansion or compression against a constant external pressure, the must-know work formula is \(w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\).
  • At constant volume, \(\Delta V=0\), so \(\Delta U=q_v\).
  • On a \(P\)-\(V\) diagram, work equals the area under the curve; for the same initial and final states, work can change with path, but \(\Delta U\) does not. This exact contrast has appeared in PYQs.

    Enthalpy, heat capacity and the \(C_p\)–\(C_v\) link
  • Enthalpy is defined as \(H=U+PV\). It is also a state function.
  • At constant pressure, the most important result is \(\Delta H=q_p\).
  • For heating at constant pressure, use \(q_p=nC_p\Delta T\); therefore for such cases \(\Delta H=nC_p\Delta T\). This is one of the repeated high-yield links in the PYQs.
  • Heat capacity tells how much heat is needed to raise temperature. \(C_p\) is heat capacity at constant pressure and \(C_v\) at constant volume.
  • For ideal gases, the standard relation is \(C_p-C_v=R\).
  • For gas-phase reactions, the must-remember conversion is \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\), where \(\Delta n_g\) counts only gaseous moles of products minus gaseous moles of reactants.
  • Quick trap: if there is no change in gaseous moles, then \(\Delta n_g=0\) and \(\Delta H=\Delta U\).

    Ideal-gas work formulas that must be remembered
  • For reversible isothermal expansion/compression of an ideal gas, use \(w=-nRT\ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)\).
  • The same result may be written as \(w=-nRT\ln\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)=nRT\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)\).
  • In base-10 form, \(w=-2.303\,nRT\log\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)\) or \(w=-2.303\,nRT\log\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)\).
  • Expansion gives negative work in chemistry convention; compression gives positive work.
  • Very common exam distinction: reversible isothermal work uses the \(\ln\) formula, but irreversible expansion against constant external pressure uses \(w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\).
  • When gas is generated from essentially zero initial gas volume at constant pressure, a quick result is \(w=-\Delta n_gRT\).

    Thermochemical equations and standard enthalpies
  • A thermochemical equation must show physical states: \((s)\), \((l)\), \((g)\), \((aq)\), because enthalpy depends on state.
  • If a chemical equation is multiplied by a factor, \(\Delta H\) is multiplied by the same factor. If the equation is reversed, the sign of \(\Delta H\) changes.
  • Standard state means 1 bar pressure at a specified temperature, with the substance in its most stable form under those conditions.
  • Standard enthalpy of reaction, \(\Delta_rH^\circ\), is the enthalpy change when reactants and products are in their standard states.
  • Standard enthalpy of formation, \(\Delta_fH^\circ\), is the enthalpy change for formation of 1 mole of a compound from its elements in their standard states.
  • Absolute must-know fact: \(\Delta_fH^\circ=0\) for any element in its standard state. For carbon, the standard state is graphite, not diamond. This has appeared in PYQs.
  • Standard enthalpy of combustion, \(\Delta_cH^\circ\), is the heat change when 1 mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion in oxygen.
  • Standard enthalpy of neutralisation is the heat change when 1 mole of \(H^+\) is neutralised by 1 mole of \(OH^-\) in dilute solution to form water; for strong acid–strong base, it is nearly constant.

    Reaction enthalpy from formation data
  • The most-used formula in this topic is \(\Delta_rH^\circ=\sum \nu\,\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu\,\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants})\).
  • Use stoichiometric coefficients carefully; if the balanced equation forms 2 moles of a substance, its contribution is doubled. This exact idea appeared in the NH\(_3\) PYQ.
  • For decomposition or unknown-formation-enthalpy questions, substitute all known \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) values and solve algebraically for the unknown.
  • High-yield combustion shortcut: when a compound’s \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) is asked from combustion data, combine combustion equations so both routes reach the same final products, then subtract appropriately.

    Hess’s law and algebra of thermochemical equations
  • Hess’s law: enthalpy change depends only on initial and final states, not on the route taken.
  • So thermochemical equations can be treated like algebra: reverse, multiply, divide, add and subtract until the target equation is obtained.
  • When reversing an equation, change the sign of \(\Delta H\); when multiplying coefficients, multiply \(\Delta H\) by the same number.
  • This is one of the strongest PYQ patterns in this chapter, especially for getting target reactions that are not directly measurable.
  • Best exam habit: write all given equations neatly, mark which ones must be reversed, then cancel common species line by line before calculating the final \(\Delta H\).

    Bond enthalpy and bond dissociation enthalpy
  • Chemical reactions involve bond breaking and bond formation; energy is absorbed in breaking bonds and released in forming bonds.
  • Bond dissociation enthalpy refers to breaking a particular bond in a particular gaseous molecule.
  • Bond enthalpy is the average of such bond dissociation enthalpies for a given bond type across gaseous compounds.
  • That is why for polyatomic molecules, bond dissociation enthalpy and bond enthalpy are not always numerically identical.
  • The estimation formula is \(\Delta H \approx \sum(\text{bond enthalpies of bonds broken})-\sum(\text{bond enthalpies of bonds formed})\).
  • This method is mainly used for gaseous reactions and is approximate, but it is highly useful in objective questions.

    Spontaneity and entropy
  • The first law tells whether energy is conserved; it does not by itself tell whether a process is spontaneous.
  • A spontaneous process occurs on its own once started; a non-spontaneous one needs continuous outside help.
  • Entropy \(S\) is the measure of disorder or randomness of a system.
  • Usual order of entropy: solid < liquid < gas.
  • Entropy generally increases when gases mix, when a solid melts, when a liquid vaporises, when number of gas particles increases, or when temperature increases.
  • For a reversible change, the defining relation is \(\Delta S=\frac{q_{\text{rev}}}{T}\).
  • For phase change at transition temperature, the very useful result is \(\Delta S=\frac{\Delta H_{\text{transition}}}{T}\), such as fusion or vaporisation.

    Second law and third law
  • The correct entropy criterion for spontaneity is for the universe, not just the system: \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}}=\Delta S_{\text{system}}+\Delta S_{\text{surroundings}}>0\) for a spontaneous process.
  • At equilibrium, \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}}=0\).
  • This explains why \(\Delta H\) alone is not enough and \(\Delta S_{\text{system}}\) alone is also not enough to judge spontaneity.
  • Third law of thermodynamics: the entropy of a pure perfectly crystalline substance approaches zero at absolute zero.
  • From standard molar entropy data, reaction entropy is calculated as \(\Delta_rS^\circ=\sum \nu S_m^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu S_m^\circ(\text{reactants})\).

    Gibbs energy and temperature dependence of spontaneity
  • For constant temperature and pressure, the practical spontaneity criterion is \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\).
  • If \(\Delta G<0\), the process is spontaneous. If \(\Delta G=0\), the system is at equilibrium. If \(\Delta G>0\), the process is non-spontaneous.
  • This equation combines the enthalpy factor and the entropy factor, so it is the most exam-useful test for spontaneity.
  • If \(\Delta H<0\) and \(\Delta S>0\), reaction is spontaneous at all temperatures.
  • If \(\Delta H>0\) and \(\Delta S<0\), reaction is non-spontaneous at all temperatures.
  • If \(\Delta H<0\) and \(\Delta S<0\), spontaneity is favoured at low temperature.
  • If \(\Delta H>0\) and \(\Delta S>0\), spontaneity is favoured at high temperature.
  • Very high-yield PYQ shortcut: when spontaneity changes with temperature, set \(\Delta G=0\) and use \(T=\frac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}\) with consistent units.

    Standard Gibbs energy and equilibrium constant
  • Standard Gibbs energy of formation, \(\Delta_fG^\circ\), is the Gibbs energy change when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
  • For elements in standard state, \(\Delta_fG^\circ=0\).
  • The standard Gibbs energy change of reaction is found exactly like formation enthalpy sums: \(\Delta_rG^\circ=\sum \nu\,\Delta_fG^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu\,\Delta_fG^\circ(\text{reactants})\).
  • The most repeated equilibrium link is \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303RT\log K\).
  • Key interpretation: \(\Delta G^\circ<0 \Rightarrow K>1\), \(\Delta G^\circ=0 \Rightarrow K=1\), and \(\Delta G^\circ>0 \Rightarrow K<1\).
  • This is a direct PYQ favourite, so it should be remembered exactly in this form.

  • An isothermal process occurs at constant temperature. An adiabatic process occurs without heat exchange with the surroundings.     First law, internal energy, heat and work
  • The first law of thermodynamics is the law of conservation of energy. Its working equation is \(\Delta U=q+w\), where \(\Delta U\) is the change in internal energy, \(q\) is heat absorbed by the system, and \(w\) is work done on the system. This is the central equation from which the rest of the energy formulas follow. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
  • Internal energy, \(U\), is the total energy of the particles of the system, including translational, rotational, vibrational, electronic and nuclear contributions. Its absolute value is not measured directly, but change in internal energy is measurable and is a state function. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
  • Heat and work are not state functions, because their values depend on the path. This is one of the cleanest conceptual contrasts in the chapter and explains why two different paths can give different work, but the same \(\Delta U\). :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14} :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}
  • For expansion at constant external pressure, the work expression is \(w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\). At constant volume, \(\Delta V=0\), so the direct result is \(\Delta U=q_v\). These are standard formulas and repeatedly useful in numericals. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

    Enthalpy, heat capacity and the \(C_p\)–\(C_v\) link
  • Enthalpy is defined as \(H=U+PV\). At constant pressure, the book derives the important result \(\Delta H=q_p\), which is why enthalpy is the most convenient energy function for ordinary chemical reactions performed at atmospheric pressure. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}
  • The PYQs strongly reinforce the direct constant-pressure heating relation \(q_p=nC_p\Delta T\), so for such cases one may write \(\Delta H=nC_p\Delta T\). This is one of the few formulas in this chapter that must become reflex-like. :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}
  • For ideal gases, the syllabus includes the relation between heat capacities at constant pressure and constant volume. The standard result to remember is \(C_p-C_v=R\), and \(\gamma=\dfrac{C_p}{C_v}\) is used in adiabatic relations. The PYQs also use the adiabatic result \(PV^\gamma=\text{constant}\). :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}

    \(\Delta H\), \(\Delta U\), gas relations and work formulas
  • For gaseous reactions, the relation between enthalpy and internal energy is \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\), where \(\Delta n_g\) is the number of moles of gaseous products minus the number of moles of gaseous reactants. This formula has been directly tested in PYQs and should be remembered with the words “count only gases.” :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20} :contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}
  • If there is no change in gaseous moles, then \(\Delta n_g=0\), so \(\Delta H=\Delta U\). This becomes especially useful in phase-change or condensed-phase questions. :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}
  • For one mole ideal gas in reversible isothermal expansion or compression, the must-know formula is \(w=-nRT\ln\!\left(\dfrac{V_2}{V_1}\right)=-nRT\ln\!\left(\dfrac{P_1}{P_2}\right)\). In common logarithm form, use \(w=-2.303\,nRT\log\!\left(\dfrac{V_2}{V_1}\right)\) or the corresponding pressure form. This is one of the most repeated PYQ formulas in the whole topic. :contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23} :contentReference[oaicite:24]{index=24}
  • Do not mix the reversible logarithmic formula with the irreversible constant-pressure formula \(w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\). That confusion is one of the most common errors in this unit. :contentReference[oaicite:25]{index=25}
  • On a \(P\)-\(V\) graph, work is the area under the curve, so it is path-dependent. If a graph-based question appears, remember that the higher path gives greater magnitude of work for expansion between the same two states. :contentReference[oaicite:26]{index=26}

    Thermochemical equations and essential reaction equations
  • A thermochemical equation includes not only the balanced chemical equation but also the physical states of reactants and products and the enthalpy change \(\Delta H\). The physical states matter because enthalpy depends on state. If a reaction is reversed, the sign of \(\Delta H\) changes. If the equation is multiplied, \(\Delta H\) is multiplied by the same factor. :contentReference[oaicite:27]{index=27} :contentReference[oaicite:28]{index=28}
  • Some equations are worth remembering because they keep reappearing in theory or PYQ-style reasoning: \(\mathrm{C(graphite)+O_2(g)\rightarrow CO_2(g)}\) for standard enthalpy of formation of carbon dioxide, \(\mathrm{CH_4(g)+2O_2(g)\rightarrow CO_2(g)+2H_2O(l)}\) for exothermic combustion, and \(\mathrm{H^+(aq)+OH^-(aq)\rightarrow H_2O(l)}\) for enthalpy of neutralisation. :contentReference[oaicite:29]{index=29}
  • A useful combustion relation that often appears through Hess’s law is \(\mathrm{CO(g)+\tfrac12 O_2(g)\rightarrow CO_2(g)}\). It is a small equation, but it appears often enough in formation–combustion conversions to be worth remembering. :contentReference[oaicite:30]{index=30} :contentReference[oaicite:31]{index=31}

    Standard enthalpies and reaction enthalpy
  • Standard enthalpy of reaction, \(\Delta_rH^\circ\), is the enthalpy change when reactants and products are in their standard states. Standard enthalpy of formation, \(\Delta_fH^\circ\), is the enthalpy change for the formation of one mole of a compound from its constituent elements in their standard states. :contentReference[oaicite:32]{index=32}
  • By convention, the standard enthalpy of formation of any element in its standard state is zero. For carbon, the standard state is graphite, not diamond or fullerene. This exact point has already been tested in PYQs and should be memorised as a fact. :contentReference[oaicite:33]{index=33} :contentReference[oaicite:34]{index=34}
  • Standard enthalpy of combustion, \(\Delta_cH^\circ\), is the heat evolved when one mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion in oxygen. Standard enthalpy of neutralisation is the heat evolved when one mole of \(H^+\) reacts with one mole of \(OH^-\) to form water; for strong acid–strong base neutralisation, the value is approximately \(-57\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\). :contentReference[oaicite:35]{index=35}
  • The main working formula for reaction enthalpy from formation data is \(\Delta_rH^\circ=\sum \nu\,\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu\,\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants})\). This formula is so common in PYQs that it should be treated as a core formula, not a derived one. :contentReference[oaicite:36]{index=36} :contentReference[oaicite:37]{index=37}
  • Coefficient handling matters: if a balanced equation forms two moles of a product, its \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) contribution must also be multiplied by 2. This is exactly the pattern seen in the NH\(_3\) PYQ. :contentReference[oaicite:38]{index=38}
  • The EAPCET syllabus also names enthalpy of atomization, sublimation, ionization, solution and dilution. These are best understood as named enthalpy changes for specific processes. The uploaded IPE chapters do not develop these in separate detailed sections, so for exam completeness they should be remembered as standard process-specific enthalpy terms even though the book’s strongest treatment is of formation, combustion and neutralisation. :contentReference[oaicite:39]{index=39}

    Hess’s law and calorimetric logic
  • Hess’s law of constant heat summation states that the enthalpy change of a reaction depends only on the initial and final states and is independent of the intermediate steps. This allows thermochemical equations to be added, subtracted, reversed or multiplied exactly like algebraic equations. :contentReference[oaicite:40]{index=40}
  • This law is essential whenever the enthalpy of a target reaction cannot be measured directly. The book’s carbon-to-carbon monoxide example is the model pattern: build two alternate routes to the same final state, then subtract to obtain the required \(\Delta H\). :contentReference[oaicite:41]{index=41}
  • The PYQs lean very heavily on this method. They repeatedly test whether the student can decide which equations to reverse, which to multiply, and how to cancel species without losing the sign of \(\Delta H\). :contentReference[oaicite:42]{index=42} :contentReference[oaicite:43]{index=43}
  • The chapter does mention calorimetric measurement of reaction enthalpy in principle, but it does not build a large separate calorimetry section with apparatus-based detail. For EAPCET, the more relevant takeaway from these pages is the constant-volume result \(\Delta U=q_v\), the constant-pressure result \(\Delta H=q_p\), and the idea that some reaction enthalpies are obtained indirectly using Hess’s law when direct calorimetric measurement is difficult. :contentReference[oaicite:44]{index=44} :contentReference[oaicite:45]{index=45}
  • This Hess-law alternate-path diagram is worth keeping because it visually explains why route does not matter for a state function. Please copy this image into the topic’s images folder. :contentReference[oaicite:46]{index=46}

    Bond enthalpy and bond dissociation enthalpy
  • Chemical reactions involve bond breaking and bond formation. Energy is absorbed in breaking bonds and released in forming bonds, so reaction enthalpy can be related to these two contributions. :contentReference[oaicite:47]{index=47}
  • Bond dissociation enthalpy refers to breaking a specific bond in a specific gaseous molecule. Bond enthalpy is the average value of bond dissociation enthalpies for a given type of bond across different gaseous compounds. For polyatomic molecules, the two are therefore not always numerically identical. :contentReference[oaicite:48]{index=48}
  • The estimation formula to remember is \(\Delta_rH=\sum \text{B.E. of reactant bonds}-\sum \text{B.E. of product bonds}\). In practice, the safest method is to first list the bonds broken, then the bonds formed, and only then substitute numerical values. :contentReference[oaicite:49]{index=49}
  • This part is directly reinforced by the PYQ summary, where bond-enthalpy reasoning is used as a standard reaction-enthalpy shortcut. :contentReference[oaicite:50]{index=50}
  • This average bond enthalpy table is useful enough to include, especially because the examples directly use it. Please copy this image into the topic’s images folder. :contentReference[oaicite:51]{index=51}

    Spontaneity, entropy and the second law
  • The first law tells whether energy is conserved, but it does not decide whether a process can occur on its own. For that, the topic introduces spontaneity. A spontaneous process proceeds naturally once started, while a non-spontaneous process requires continuous external agency. :contentReference[oaicite:52]{index=52}
  • Entropy, \(S\), is the thermodynamic quantity associated with disorder or randomness. Solids are the most ordered and therefore have the lowest entropy, gases are the most disordered and therefore have the highest entropy, and liquids lie in between. :contentReference[oaicite:53]{index=53}
  • For a reversible change, the entropy change is given by \(\Delta S=\dfrac{q_{\text{rev}}}{T}\). For phase transitions at the transition temperature, this becomes the useful relation \(\Delta S_{\text{transition}}=\dfrac{\Delta H_{\text{transition}}}{T}\). :contentReference[oaicite:54]{index=54} :contentReference[oaicite:55]{index=55}
  • A decrease in enthalpy alone is not a criterion for spontaneity. The book explicitly points out that some spontaneous processes, such as evaporation of water at room temperature, are endothermic. Likewise, increase in system entropy alone is not enough; the correct criterion is based on the entropy change of the universe. :contentReference[oaicite:56]{index=56}
  • The second law of thermodynamics is expressed here as \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}}=\Delta S_{\text{system}}+\Delta S_{\text{surroundings}}>0\) for a spontaneous process, and \(\Delta S_{\text{universe}}=0\) at equilibrium. This is the exact theoretical answer to the syllabus line on entropy and spontaneity. :contentReference[oaicite:57]{index=57}

    Absolute entropy, third law and Gibbs energy
  • The third law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of a pure perfectly crystalline substance approaches zero as the temperature approaches absolute zero. This law provides the basis for assigning absolute entropy values. :contentReference[oaicite:58]{index=58}
  • Using standard molar entropies, the standard entropy change of reaction is written as \(\Delta_rS^\circ=\sum \nu S_m^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu S_m^\circ(\text{reactants})\). This parallels the formation-enthalpy method and is useful in entropy numericals. :contentReference[oaicite:59]{index=59}
  • To make spontaneity easier to judge in practice, the chapter defines Gibbs energy as \(G=H-TS\). At constant temperature, this leads to the very important formula \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\). :contentReference[oaicite:60]{index=60}
  • The spontaneity criterion in terms of Gibbs energy is direct: if \(\Delta G<0\), the process is spontaneous; if \(\Delta G>0\), it is non-spontaneous; and if \(\Delta G=0\), the system is at equilibrium. The PYQs also use the temperature-switch idea obtained by setting \(\Delta G=0\), giving \(T=\dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}\) when the sign of spontaneity changes. :contentReference[oaicite:61]{index=61} :contentReference[oaicite:62]{index=62}
  • The sign combinations are worth learning as theory, not just as a table: \(\Delta H<0,\Delta S>0\) gives spontaneity at all temperatures; \(\Delta H>0,\Delta S<0\) gives non-spontaneity at all temperatures; \(\Delta H<0,\Delta S<0\) favours low temperature; and \(\Delta H>0,\Delta S>0\) favours high temperature. :contentReference[oaicite:63]{index=63}

    Gibbs energy and equilibrium
  • Standard Gibbs energy change, \(\Delta_rG^\circ\), is the Gibbs energy change when reactants in their standard states are converted into products in their standard states. Standard Gibbs energy of formation is defined in the same one-mole way as standard enthalpy of formation, and elements in standard state are assigned zero value. :contentReference[oaicite:64]{index=64}
  • The key equilibrium relation is \(\Delta_rG^\circ=-RT\ln K=-2.303\,RT\log K\). This is one of the strongest PYQ formulas in the spontaneity section and must be memorised exactly. It immediately gives the interpretation that \(\Delta G^\circ<0\) means \(K>1\), while \(\Delta G^\circ>0\) means \(K<1\). :contentReference[oaicite:65]{index=65} :contentReference[oaicite:66]{index=66}
  • The PYQs also reinforce the strategy that if \(\Delta G^\circ\) is known, finding \(K\) is a direct substitution step, not a new concept. :contentReference[oaicite:67]{index=67}

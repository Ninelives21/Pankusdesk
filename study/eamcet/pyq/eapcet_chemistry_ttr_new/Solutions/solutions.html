Solutions

    Concentration terms and temperature dependence
  • Molarity is temperature-dependent because \(M=\dfrac{n}{V}\), and solution volume changes with temperature. If \(2\,g\) of \(\mathrm{NaOH}\) is dissolved to make \(200\,mL\) solution, \(n=\dfrac{2}{40}=0.05\) mol and \(M=0.25\) at \(25^\circ C\); on heating to \(90^\circ C\), volume increases, so \(M<0.25\).
  • Molality is preferred in colligative-property formulas because it depends on mass of solvent, not solution volume. In mixed conceptual questions, remember: molarity changes with temperature, but molality does not.

    Henry’s law and gas solubility
  • Henry’s law is \(p=K_Hx\), where \(p\) is partial pressure of the gas, \(K_H\) is Henry’s law constant, and \(x\) is mole fraction of gas dissolved in liquid. At fixed pressure, gas solubility is inversely proportional to \(K_H\).
  • Lower \(K_H\) means higher solubility; therefore, when gases are compared by Henry’s law constant, arrange solubility in the reverse order of \(K_H\). For the tested set, the solubility order is \(\mathrm{HCHO} > \mathrm{CH_4} > \mathrm{CO_2} > \mathrm{Ar}\).
  • In gas-mixture questions, first calculate the gas partial pressure using \(p_i=y_iP_{\text{total}}\), then apply \(p=K_Hx\). For air containing \(0.8\) mole fraction of \(\mathrm{N_2}\) at \(5\,atm\), \(p_{N_2}=0.8\times5=4\,atm\), so \(x_{N_2}=\dfrac{4}{10^5}=4\times10^{-5}\).
  • If the dissolved-gas mole fraction is very small, \(x_{\text{gas}}\approx\dfrac{n_{\text{gas}}}{n_{\text{solvent}}}\). Thus for \(10\) moles of water, \(x_{N_2}=4\times10^{-5}\) gives \(n_{N_2}\approx4\times10^{-4}\) mol.
  • For soda-water calculations, keep pressure units consistent with \(K_H\). If \(K_H\) is in pascal, convert \(2\,atm\) to \(2.026\times10^5\,Pa\), then use \(x=\dfrac{p}{K_H}\) and \(1\,L\) water \(\approx\dfrac{1000}{18}\) mol to get dissolved \(\mathrm{CO_2}\approx2.9\,g\).
  • For comparing dissolved \(\mathrm{N_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\) from air, use both partial pressure and Henry’s constant: \(\dfrac{x_{N_2}}{x_{O_2}}=\dfrac{p_{N_2}/K_{H,N_2}}{p_{O_2}/K_{H,O_2}}\). Dry-air percentages alone are not enough.
  • Henry’s law is valid for gases that dissolve physically without chemical reaction or chemical binding. It is valid for \(\mathrm{O_2}\) in water and \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) in water in the intended exam context, but not for ammonia in water or oxygen binding to haemoglobin in blood.

    Ideal and non-ideal solutions
  • An ideal solution obeys Raoult’s law over the entire concentration range and has similar \(A-A\), \(B-B\), and \(A-B\) intermolecular interactions. It is not defined by the ideal gas equation \(PV=nRT\).
  • For ideal solutions, \(\Delta H_{mix}=0\) and \(\Delta V_{mix}=0\). Therefore \(V_{solution}=V_{solvent}+V_{solute}\), and mixing occurs without heat change or volume change.
  • A chemical reaction is not an ideal-solution example. For instance, \(\mathrm{H_2O+CO_2\rightarrow H_2CO_3}\) involves chemical change, so it should not be counted as ideal physical mixing.
  • \(\mathrm{CH_3OH+CH_3COOH}\) is treated as a non-ideal solution because unlike interactions differ from like interactions, so it does not obey Raoult’s law exactly. In “not correct” statement questions, calling this mixture an ideal solution is the unsafe statement.

    Raoult’s law and vapour pressure of liquid solutions
  • For an ideal binary liquid solution, use \(p_A=p_A^0x_A\), \(p_B=p_B^0x_B\), and \(p_{\text{total}}=p_A+p_B=p_A^0x_A+p_B^0x_B\). Always distinguish pure vapour pressure \(p_i^0\), partial pressure \(p_i\), and total pressure.
  • If total vapour pressure is given before and after adding one mole of a component, recalculate the mole fractions after addition. Do not keep the original composition; this was the key step in deriving \(p_A^0:p_B^0=2:3\).
  • In questions connecting vapour-phase and liquid-phase mole fractions, combine Dalton’s law and Raoult’s law. If vapour mole fraction of \(A\) is \(x_1\) and liquid mole fraction of \(A\) is \(x_2\), then \(p_A=x_1p_{\text{total}}=p_A^0x_2\), so \(p_{\text{total}}=\dfrac{p_A^0x_2}{x_1}\).
  • Vapour composition is richer in the more volatile component. For ideal mixtures, calculate \(y_i=\dfrac{p_i}{p_{\text{total}}}\), not by copying the liquid mole fraction.
  • For benzene-toluene type questions, if liquid \(x_{\text{toluene}}=0.4\), \(p_{\text{toluene}}=0.4p_{\text{toluene}}^0\) and \(p_{\text{benzene}}=0.6p_{\text{benzene}}^0\). Since benzene has higher pure vapour pressure, the vapour phase becomes benzene-rich and \(y_{\text{toluene}}\) is only about \(0.20\).
  • To find the unknown pure vapour pressure of a component, substitute the known mole fractions into \(p_{\text{total}}=p_A^0x_A+p_B^0x_B\). Example pattern: if \(p_A^0=70\), \(x_A=0.8\), \(x_B=0.2\), and \(p_{\text{total}}=84\), then \(p_B^0=140\,\mathrm{torr}\).

    Solutions with non-volatile solutes and relative lowering of vapour pressure
  • For a non-volatile solute in a volatile solvent, \(p_{\text{solution}}=x_{\text{solvent}}p^0_{\text{solvent}}\). If \(0.9\) mol solvent and \(0.1\) mol solute are present, \(x_{\text{solvent}}=\dfrac{0.9}{0.9+0.1}=0.9\), so \(p=0.9p^0\).
  • Relative lowering of vapour pressure is \(\dfrac{p^0-p}{p^0}=x_{\text{solute}}\). For dilute solutions, \(x_{\text{solute}}\approx\dfrac{n_2}{n_1}\), which is useful for molar-mass determination.
  • In molar-mass questions, write \(n_2=\dfrac{w_2}{M_2}\) and \(n_1=\dfrac{w_1}{M_1}\). For water as solvent, \(1\,kg\) water gives \(\dfrac{1000}{18}\) mol; do not use \(1000\) directly as moles.
  • If the question gives one mole of benzene as solvent, the solvent molar mass may be unnecessary. Use \(\dfrac{p^0-p}{p^0}=\dfrac{n}{n+1}\) directly to find moles of non-volatile solute per mole of solvent.

    Colligative properties and van’t Hoff factor
  • Colligative properties depend on the number of dissolved particles, not their chemical identity. For electrolytes, include van’t Hoff factor \(i\) in all formulas: \(\Delta T_b=iK_bm\), \(\Delta T_f=iK_fm\), and \(\pi=iCRT\).
  • For dissociation, use \(i=1+\alpha(n-1)\), where \(n\) is the number of ions produced per formula unit and \(\alpha\) is degree of dissociation. For \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), \(i=1+\alpha\).
  • Same molarity does not mean same colligative effect when one solute dissociates. \(0.1\,M\ \mathrm{KCl}\) has about twice as many effective particles as \(0.1\,M\) urea, so it shows greater boiling point elevation.
  • For mixed-solute osmotic pressure, add effective concentrations, not raw molarities: \(\pi=(\sum iC)RT\). For partially dissociated \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) and glucose together, calculate \(iC\) separately for each solute before adding.
  • When concentration is the same, the solution with the largest \(i\) shows the highest colligative property. The tested comparison is urea \(=1\), \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}=2\), \(\mathrm{BaCl_2}=3\), and \((\mathrm{NH_4})_3\mathrm{PO_4}=4\), so \((\mathrm{NH_4})_3\mathrm{PO_4}\) gives the maximum effect.
  • For equimolal aqueous solutions, highest freezing point means least depression in freezing point. Therefore choose the solute with the smallest \(i\), usually the non-electrolyte; glucose \(\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6}\) gives the highest freezing point among the given salts and glucose.

    Boiling point elevation and freezing point depression
  • Elevation of boiling point is \(\Delta T_b=iK_bm\). In numerical questions, first find the actual elevation \(T_b-T_b^0\), not the absolute boiling point.
  • Depression of freezing point is \(\Delta T_f=iK_fm\), and the new freezing point is \(T_f^0-\Delta T_f\). A common error is to report \(+\Delta T_f\) instead of subtracting it from the pure solvent freezing point.
  • For the same solution, \(\dfrac{\Delta T_f}{\Delta T_b}=\dfrac{K_f}{K_b}\). This allows freezing point to be found from boiling point elevation without separately calculating solute moles again.
  • For aqueous urea-type questions, if \(373.202\,K\) is the boiling point and pure water boils at \(373.15\,K\), then \(\Delta T_b=0.052\,K\). With \(K_b=0.52\), this gives \(m=0.1\), and \(\Delta T_f=1.86\times0.1=0.186\,K\).
  • For mass-ratio questions using urea, use \(m=\dfrac{x/60}{y/1000}\). With \(\Delta T_b=0.052\,K\) and \(K_b=0.52\), \(\dfrac{x}{y}=6.0\times10^{-3}\).
  • The molal depression constant is \(K_f=\dfrac{RT_f^2}{1000L_f}\), where \(T_f\) must be in kelvin and \(L_f\) is in \(\mathrm{J\,g^{-1}}\). For \(T_f=290\,K\) and \(L_f=180\,\mathrm{J\,g^{-1}}\), \(K_f\approx3.88\,\mathrm{K\,kg\,mol^{-1}}\).

    Osmosis and osmotic pressure
  • Osmotic pressure is \(\pi=iCRT\); for non-electrolytes such as urea, \(i=1\). It depends mainly on effective solute concentration and absolute temperature.
  • In dilution problems, use \(\pi\propto CT\). Therefore, \(\dfrac{C_1}{C_2}=\dfrac{\pi_1T_2}{\pi_2T_1}\), not simply \(\dfrac{\pi_1}{\pi_2}\), when temperature changes.
  • For osmotic pressure of \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), calculate \(i=\dfrac{\pi}{CRT}\) and then \(\alpha=i-1\). In the tested pattern, \(i\approx1.96\), so degree of dissociation \(\alpha\approx0.96\).
  • External pressure is not a direct variable in the standard expression \(\pi=CRT\). If concentration and temperature are unchanged, osmotic pressure remains nearly the same when external pressure is increased or decreased.

    Acid-base stoichiometry appearing inside solution calculations
  • In carbonate-bicarbonate mixture questions, use the acid requirement carefully: \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3+2HCl\rightarrow2NaCl+H_2O+CO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3+HCl\rightarrow NaCl+H_2O+CO_2}\).
  • Equal moles of \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\) and \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\) do not consume equal moles of acid. Carbonate needs \(2\) moles of \(HCl\) per mole, while bicarbonate needs \(1\), so total acid required for \(n\) moles each is \(3n\).

    Variables used
  • \(p\): partial pressure of a gas or vapour pressure, depending on context.
  • \(p_i^0\): vapour pressure of pure component \(i\).
  • \(p_i\): partial vapour pressure of component \(i\) in solution.
  • \(p_{\text{total}}\): total vapour pressure above a liquid solution.
  • \(x_i\): mole fraction of component \(i\) in the liquid phase, or dissolved gas mole fraction in Henry’s law.
  • \(y_i\): mole fraction of component \(i\) in the vapour phase or gas phase.
  • \(K_H\): Henry’s law constant.
  • \(K_b\): molal elevation constant or ebullioscopic constant.
  • \(K_f\): molal depression constant or cryoscopic constant.
  • \(m\): molality of solution.
  • \(M\): molarity or molar mass depending on context; read the question carefully.
  • \(i\): van’t Hoff factor.
  • \(\alpha\): degree of dissociation.
  • \(\pi\): osmotic pressure.
  • \(C\): molar concentration used in osmotic pressure.
  • \(T\): absolute temperature in kelvin.
  • \(L_f\): latent heat of fusion of solvent.

    Solutions: basic idea and components
  • A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. The component present in larger amount and having the same physical state as the solution is usually called the solvent, while the substance dissolved in it is called the solute.
  • In common aqueous solutions such as sugar in water, salt in water, urea in water or potassium chloride in water, water is the solvent and the dissolved substance is the solute. For numerical problems, first identify solute, solvent and total solution amount before choosing a concentration formula.

    Types of solutions
  • Binary solutions can be classified according to the physical states of solute and solvent: gas in gas, gas in liquid, gas in solid, liquid in gas, liquid in liquid, liquid in solid, solid in gas, solid in liquid and solid in solid.
  • Important examples are: air as gas in gas, soda water as gas in liquid, hydrogen in palladium as gas in solid, humidity in air as liquid in gas, alcohol in water as liquid in liquid, mercury in gold as liquid in solid, camphor in air as solid in gas, sugar in water as solid in liquid, and alloys such as brass or bronze as solid in solid.
  • Liquid-liquid systems may be completely miscible, partially miscible or immiscible. Benzene and toluene are a useful ideal-solution type pair, while water and benzene are immiscible.
  • Gas-liquid solubility depends on pressure, temperature, and the nature of gas and solvent. At constant pressure, solubility of a gas generally decreases when temperature increases because heating tends to expel dissolved gas from solution.
  • Solid-liquid solutions are the usual setting for colligative properties. In these problems, the solute is often non-volatile, so it lowers the vapour pressure of the solvent.

    Expressing concentration of solutions
  • Molarity is the number of moles of solute per litre of solution: \(M=\dfrac{n}{V}\). Since volume changes with temperature, molarity changes with temperature; if a solution is heated, its volume usually increases and molarity decreases.
  • Molality is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent: \(m=\dfrac{n_B}{W_A/1000}\), where \(n_B\) is moles of solute and \(W_A\) is mass of solvent in grams. Molality does not change with temperature because it depends on mass of solvent, not volume of solution.
  • Normality is the number of gram equivalents of solute per litre of solution. It is useful in acid-base and redox contexts, but most Solutions PYQs here mainly use molarity, molality and mole fraction.
  • Mole fraction of component \(A\) is \(x_A=\dfrac{n_A}{n_A+n_B}\) in a binary solution, and the sum of mole fractions of all components is \(1\). Mole fraction is central in Henry’s law and Raoult’s law.
  • Mass percentage means mass of solute present in \(100\,g\) of solution. For example, \(5\%\) by mass means \(5\,g\) solute in \(100\,g\) solution.
  • In exam numericals, read the concentration unit carefully: \(M\) uses litres of solution, \(m\) uses kilograms of solvent, mole fraction uses moles of all components, and percentage uses mass of total solution.

    Solubility of gases and Henry’s law
  • Henry’s law states that, at constant temperature, the mole fraction or amount of a gas dissolved in a liquid is proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the solution.
  • In the exam form commonly used for numericals, Henry’s law is written as \(p=K_Hx\), where \(p\) is the partial pressure of the gas, \(K_H\) is Henry’s law constant, and \(x\) is the mole fraction of the dissolved gas.
  • Since \(x=\dfrac{p}{K_H}\), lower \(K_H\) means greater gas solubility at the same pressure. Therefore, when gases are ranked by \(K_H\), the solubility order is the reverse of the \(K_H\) order.
  • For gas mixtures, always use partial pressure: \(p_i=y_iP_{\text{total}}\), where \(y_i\) is the gas-phase mole fraction. For air containing \(0.8\) mole fraction \(\mathrm{N_2}\) at \(5\,atm\), \(p_{N_2}=0.8\times5=4\,atm\).
  • Henry’s law is valid best when pressure is not too high, temperature is not too low, and the gas does not dissociate, associate or react chemically with the solvent. This is why simple dissolution of \(\mathrm{O_2}\) in water is acceptable, while ammonia reacting with water is not a simple Henry’s law case.
  • For soda-water questions, pressure units must match \(K_H\). If \(K_H\) is given in pascal, convert \(atm\) to \(Pa\), then use \(x=\dfrac{p}{K_H}\); for \(1\,L\) water, use \(n_{\mathrm{H_2O}}\approx\dfrac{1000}{18}\).
  • A high-yield approximation for dilute gas solutions is \(x_{\text{gas}}\approx\dfrac{n_{\text{gas}}}{n_{\text{solvent}}}\). This converts the mole fraction from Henry’s law into moles or grams of dissolved gas.

    Vapour pressure and Raoult’s law for volatile liquid mixtures
  • Vapour pressure is the pressure exerted by vapour in equilibrium with its liquid. At equilibrium, the rate of evaporation equals the rate of condensation.
  • For a solution of two miscible volatile liquids \(A\) and \(B\), Raoult’s law states that the partial vapour pressure of each component is proportional to its mole fraction in the liquid solution: \(p_A=p_A^0x_A\) and \(p_B=p_B^0x_B\).
  • The total vapour pressure is the sum of partial vapour pressures: \(p_{\text{total}}=p_A+p_B=p_A^0x_A+p_B^0x_B\). This single equation solves most ideal binary vapour-pressure numericals.
  • In PYQs, do not confuse pure vapour pressure \(p_A^0\) with partial vapour pressure \(p_A\). Pure vapour pressure belongs to the pure component; partial pressure is the contribution of that component in the solution vapour.
  • Vapour-phase composition is found using Dalton’s law: \(y_A=\dfrac{p_A}{p_{\text{total}}}\). The vapour phase is richer in the more volatile component, so vapour mole fraction is generally not equal to liquid mole fraction.
  • If a question gives liquid-phase mole fraction \(x_2\) and vapour-phase mole fraction \(x_1\) for the same component \(A\), combine Raoult’s and Dalton’s laws: \(p_A=p_A^0x_2=x_1p_{\text{total}}\), so \(p_{\text{total}}=\dfrac{p_A^0x_2}{x_1}\).
  • When composition changes after adding another mole of one component, recalculate mole fractions before applying Raoult’s law. Carrying forward the old mole fraction is a common source of wrong answers.

    Raoult’s law for non-volatile solutes
  • If a non-volatile solute is dissolved in a volatile solvent, only solvent molecules contribute to vapour pressure. Therefore \(p_{\text{solution}}=p_A^0x_A\), where \(x_A\) is the mole fraction of solvent.
  • Since \(x_A<1\), the vapour pressure of the solution is lower than that of the pure solvent. This lowering of vapour pressure is the basis of several colligative-property formulas.
  • For a binary solution, \(x_A+x_B=1\), so \(\dfrac{p_A^0-p_A}{p_A^0}=x_B\). Thus, relative lowering of vapour pressure equals mole fraction of the non-volatile solute.
  • In dilute solutions, \(x_B=\dfrac{n_B}{n_A+n_B}\approx\dfrac{n_B}{n_A}\) because \(n_B\ll n_A\). This approximation is used to determine molar mass from vapour-pressure lowering.
  • For molar-mass numericals, use \(n_B=\dfrac{w_B}{M_B}\) and \(n_A=\dfrac{w_A}{M_A}\). If water is the solvent and \(1\,kg\) is given, use \(\dfrac{1000}{18}\) moles, not \(1000\) as a mole value.

    Ideal and non-ideal solutions
  • An ideal solution obeys Raoult’s law over the entire range of composition and at all temperatures. In such solutions, unlike interactions \(A-B\) are nearly the same as like interactions \(A-A\) and \(B-B\).
  • Formation of an ideal solution has no heat change and no volume change: \(\Delta H_{mix}=0\) and \(\Delta V_{mix}=0\). Hence \(V_{solution}=V_{solvent}+V_{solute}\).
  • Do not confuse ideal solution with ideal gas. The ideal gas equation \(PV=nRT\) is not the definition of an ideal liquid solution.
  • Non-ideal solutions do not obey Raoult’s law exactly, and their formation is accompanied by heat or volume change. Most real solutions are non-ideal.
  • Positive deviation occurs when \(A-B\) interactions are weaker than \(A-A\) or \(B-B\) interactions. Vapour pressure becomes greater than that predicted by Raoult’s law, and the total vapour pressure may show a maximum at intermediate composition.
  • Negative deviation occurs when \(A-B\) interactions are stronger than \(A-A\) or \(B-B\) interactions. Vapour pressure becomes lower than the Raoult’s law value, and the total vapour pressure may show a minimum at intermediate composition.
  • A chemical reaction during mixing is not ideal-solution behaviour. For example, \(\mathrm{H_2O+CO_2\rightarrow H_2CO_3}\) is a chemical change, not simple ideal physical mixing.

    Colligative properties: core meaning
  • Colligative properties of dilute solutions depend only on the number of solute particles present, not on the chemical nature of the solute particles.
  • The four colligative properties are relative lowering of vapour pressure, elevation of boiling point, depression of freezing point and osmotic pressure.
  • For electrolytes, the number of effective particles increases due to dissociation. Therefore colligative properties must be corrected using the van’t Hoff factor \(i\).
  • For the same concentration, the solution with larger \(i\) shows a larger colligative effect. For example, \((\mathrm{NH_4})_3\mathrm{PO_4}\) gives more particles than \(\mathrm{BaCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\), or urea, so it shows the largest colligative property among equimolar options.

    Relative lowering of vapour pressure
  • Relative lowering of vapour pressure is given by \(\dfrac{p_A^0-p_A}{p_A^0}=x_B\), where \(x_B\) is the mole fraction of non-volatile solute.
  • For dilute solutions, \(x_B\approx\dfrac{n_B}{n_A}\), so: \(\dfrac{p_A^0-p_A}{p_A^0}\approx\dfrac{w_B/M_B}{w_A/M_A}\). This relation is used for determining molar mass of a non-volatile solute.
  • When the question asks for moles of solute per mole of solvent, use \(\dfrac{p^0-p}{p^0}=\dfrac{n}{n+1}\) directly. The solvent molar mass may not be needed if solvent amount is already given as one mole.
  • Accurate measurement of vapour-pressure lowering is difficult, so this method is conceptually important but less convenient experimentally compared with osmotic-pressure methods for many large molecules.

    Elevation of boiling point
  • Boiling point is the temperature at which vapour pressure of a liquid becomes equal to atmospheric pressure. A solution containing a non-volatile solute has lower vapour pressure than the pure solvent, so it must be heated to a higher temperature to boil.
  • Elevation in boiling point is \(\Delta T_b=T_b-T_b^0\), where \(T_b\) is boiling point of solution and \(T_b^0\) is boiling point of pure solvent.
  • For dilute solutions, \(\Delta T_b=K_bm\), where \(K_b\) is molal elevation constant and \(m\) is molality. For electrolytes, use \(\Delta T_b=iK_bm\).
  • \(K_b\) is the elevation in boiling point when one mole of solute is dissolved in one kilogram of solvent. It depends on the nature of the solvent.
  • In numericals, use the elevation, not the absolute boiling point. For example, if the solution boils at \(373.202\,K\) and pure water boils at \(373.15\,K\), then \(\Delta T_b=0.052\,K\).
  • For fixed solute and solvent masses, \(\Delta T_b\) is inversely related to solute molar mass because fewer moles are present when molar mass is larger.

    Depression in freezing point
  • Freezing point is the temperature at which solid and liquid forms have the same vapour pressure. A solution freezes at a lower temperature than the pure solvent because its vapour pressure is lower.
  • Depression in freezing point is \(\Delta T_f=T_f^0-T_f\), where \(T_f^0\) is freezing point of pure solvent and \(T_f\) is freezing point of solution.
  • For dilute solutions, \(\Delta T_f=K_fm\), where \(K_f\) is molal depression constant or cryoscopic constant. For electrolytes, use \(\Delta T_f=iK_fm\).
  • The final freezing point is \(T_f=T_f^0-\Delta T_f\). For water, this often means reporting a negative Celsius value, such as \(-0.62^\circ C\), not \(+0.62^\circ C\).
  • For the same solution, molality is common to boiling point elevation and freezing point depression, so \(\dfrac{\Delta T_f}{\Delta T_b}=\dfrac{K_f}{K_b}\). This shortcut avoids repeating the full molality calculation.
  • If equimolal solutions are compared, the highest freezing point belongs to the solution with the smallest \(i\). Non-electrolytes such as glucose have \(i=1\), so they show the least depression.
  • The cryoscopic constant can be calculated from \(K_f=\dfrac{RT_f^2}{1000L_f}\), where \(T_f\) is in kelvin and \(L_f\) is latent heat of fusion in \(\mathrm{J\,g^{-1}}\). The factor \(1000\) is needed because \(L_f\) is per gram.

    Osmosis, osmotic pressure and reverse osmosis
  • Osmosis is the spontaneous flow of solvent from a solution of lower concentration or pure solvent to a solution of higher concentration through a semipermeable membrane.
  • Osmotic pressure is the excess pressure that must be applied on the solution side to just stop the passage of pure solvent into it through a perfect semipermeable membrane.
  • For dilute solutions, osmotic pressure is given by \(\pi=CRT\), where \(C\) is molar concentration, \(R\) is gas constant and \(T\) is absolute temperature. Equivalently, \(\pi=\dfrac{n_B}{V}RT\).
  • For electrolytes or abnormal solutes, use \(\pi=iCRT\). For mixed solutes, add effective concentrations first: \(\pi=\left(\sum iC\right)RT\).
  • In dilution problems involving temperature change, use \(\pi\propto CT\), so \(\dfrac{C_1}{C_2}=\dfrac{\pi_1T_2}{\pi_2T_1}\). Do not use pressure ratio alone when temperature changes.
  • Osmotic pressure is especially useful for molar-mass determination of biomolecules and macromolecules because it can be measured at room temperature and remains appreciable even for dilute solutions.
  • If pressure greater than osmotic pressure is applied to the solution side, solvent flow is reversed. This process is reverse osmosis and is used in desalination and water purification.

    Abnormal molar masses and van’t Hoff factor
  • Abnormal colligative properties arise when the actual number of solute particles in solution differs from the number expected from the formula unit. This mainly happens due to association or dissociation.
  • In association, two or more solute molecules combine to form a larger particle. The number of effective particles decreases, so the observed colligative property becomes smaller and the calculated molar mass becomes greater than the true molar mass.
  • In dissociation, one solute formula unit splits into more particles. The number of effective particles increases, so the observed colligative property becomes larger and the calculated molar mass becomes lower than the true molar mass.
  • Van’t Hoff factor \(i\) corrects for association or dissociation: \(i=\dfrac{\text{observed colligative property}}{\text{calculated normal colligative property}}\). Since colligative property varies inversely with molar mass, \(i=\dfrac{\text{normal molar mass}}{\text{observed molar mass}}\).
  • For association, \(i<1\). For dissociation, \(i>1\). For non-electrolytes without association or dissociation, \(i=1\).
  • Modified formulas are: \(\Delta T_b=iK_bm\), \(\Delta T_f=iK_fm\), and \(\pi=iCRT\).
  • For dissociation into \(n\) particles with degree of dissociation \(\alpha\), use \(i=1+\alpha(n-1)\). For \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), \(n=2\), so \(i=1+\alpha\).
  • For a partially dissociated salt, do not assume complete dissociation. If \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) is \(94\%\) dissociated, then \(i=1+0.94=1.94\), not \(2\).

    High-frequency numerical patterns
  • For Henry’s law: find partial pressure first, apply \(x=\dfrac{p}{K_H}\), then convert mole fraction into moles or mass if needed.
  • For Raoult’s law: calculate liquid mole fractions first, then partial pressures \(p_i=p_i^0x_i\), then total pressure or vapour-phase mole fraction.
  • For non-volatile solute: use \(p_{\text{solution}}=x_{\text{solvent}}p^0\) or \(\dfrac{p^0-p}{p^0}=x_{\text{solute}}\), depending on what is asked.
  • For boiling/freezing links in the same solution: use the common molality relation \(\dfrac{\Delta T_f}{\Delta T_b}=\dfrac{K_f}{K_b}\).
  • For osmotic pressure of mixtures: calculate each solute’s \(iC\), add them, then multiply by \(RT\).
  • For equimolar or equimolal comparisons: identify \(i\). Highest colligative effect means largest \(i\); highest freezing point means smallest \(i\).

        Theory you should know:

        1. Osmotic pressure formula

          For dilute solutions:

          \(\pi = iCRT\)

          where:

          • \(\pi\) = osmotic pressure
          • \(i\) = van’t Hoff factor
          • \(C\) = molarity
          • \(R\) = gas constant
          • \(T\) = temperature in kelvin
        2. Why do we use \(i\)?

          For electrolytes, one formula unit may produce more than one particle in solution.

          Example:

          \(\mathrm{NaCl \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^-}\)

          Since dissociation may not be complete, we use:

          \(i = 1 + \alpha(n-1)\)

          where:

          • \(\alpha\) = degree of dissociation
          • \(n\) = number of particles formed after dissociation

          For \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), \(n=2\). For glucose, there is no dissociation, so \(i=1\).

        3. Understand van’t Hoff factor

          The van’t Hoff factor, \(i\), tells us the effective number of solute particles present in solution after association or dissociation.

          Mathematically:

          \[ i = \frac{\text{actual number of particles in solution}}{\text{number of formula units dissolved}} \]

          For a non-electrolyte such as glucose, no dissociation occurs, so:

          \(i = 1\)

          For an electrolyte undergoing dissociation, the van’t Hoff factor is:

          \[ i = 1 + \alpha(n-1) \]

          where:

          • \(\alpha\) = degree of dissociation
          • \(n\) = total number of particles formed after complete dissociation

          For sodium chloride:

          \[ \text{NaCl} \rightleftharpoons \text{Na}^+ + \text{Cl}^- \]

          Here, \(n = 2\) and \(\alpha = 0.94\). Hence:

          \[ i_{\text{NaCl}} = 1 + 0.94(2-1) = 1.94 \]

          This means NaCl behaves as if each mole produces \(1.94\) moles of solute particles in solution.

        4. Remember common values of \(n\)

          In the formula \(i = 1 + \alpha(n-1)\), \(n\) is the total number of particles formed after complete dissociation.

          • \(\text{NaCl} \rightarrow 2\)
          • \(\text{KNO}_3 \rightarrow 2\)
          • \(\text{CaCl}_2 \rightarrow 3\)
          • \(\text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 \rightarrow 5\)

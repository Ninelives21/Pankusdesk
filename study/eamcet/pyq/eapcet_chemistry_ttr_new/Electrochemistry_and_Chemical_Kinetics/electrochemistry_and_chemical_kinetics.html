Electrochemistry and Chemical Kinetics

    Variables used
  • \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}\): standard cell potential; \(E_{\mathrm{cell}}\): cell potential under non-standard conditions.
  • \(n\): number of electrons transferred in the balanced redox reaction or electrode process.
  • \(F\): Faraday constant, approximately \(96500\ \mathrm{C\ mol^{-1}}\); \(1F\) is the charge on \(1\) mole of electrons.
  • \(Q\): reaction quotient in the Nernst equation, or electric charge passed in electrolysis depending on context. In electrolysis, \(Q=It\).
  • \(\Lambda_m\): molar conductivity; \(\Lambda_m^\circ\): limiting molar conductivity at infinite dilution; \(\lambda_i^\circ\): limiting ionic molar conductivity.
  • \(\kappa\): conductivity or specific conductance; \(R\): resistance; cell constant \(=\dfrac{l}{A}\).
  • \(\alpha\): degree of dissociation of weak electrolyte; \(K_a\): acid dissociation constant.
  • \(k\): rate constant; \(r\): rate of reaction; \(t_{1/2}\): half-life period.
  • \(E_a\): activation energy; \(E_t\): threshold energy; \(E_i\): internal energy of reactants; \(A\): Arrhenius frequency factor.

    Electrochemical cells and electrode potentials
  • For any galvanic cell, \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=E^\circ_{\mathrm{cathode}}-E^\circ_{\mathrm{anode}}\). The electrode with higher reduction potential acts as cathode, and the lower one acts as anode.
  • Cell feasibility is decided by the sign of \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}\). If \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}\) is negative, the proposed redox reaction is non-spontaneous under standard conditions; for example, \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) cannot oxidise \(\mathrm{Ce^{3+}}\) when \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=0.76-1.60=-0.84\ \mathrm{V}\).
  • Electrode potentials are not directly additive. Since \(\Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ\), combine multi-step electrode potentials using electron-weighted terms \(nE^\circ\), not plain voltage addition or subtraction.
  • In Latimer-style questions, count electrons in each oxidation-state change first. For \(\mathrm{Mn^{7+}\rightarrow Mn^{2+}}\), use \(5(1.51)=3x+2(1.23)\), giving \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{Mn^{7+}/Mn^{4+}}}\approx1.7\ \mathrm{V}\), not \(1.51-1.23\).
  • For hydrogen electrode at \(298\ \mathrm{K}\), \(E=-0.0591\times pH\) when \(P_{\mathrm{H_2}}=1\). Thus at \(pH=10\), \(E\approx-0.591\ \mathrm{V}\).
  • For a chlorine electrode, \(\mathrm{Cl_2(g)+2e^-\rightarrow2Cl^-(aq)}\), the Nernst expression is \(E=E^\circ-\dfrac{0.0591}{2}\log\dfrac{[\mathrm{Cl^-}]^2}{P_{\mathrm{Cl_2}}}\). Increasing chloride-ion concentration lowers the reduction potential, so maximum potential occurs at minimum \([\mathrm{Cl^-}]\).
  • For \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}+e^-\rightarrow Fe^{2+}}\), \(E=E^\circ-0.059\log\dfrac{[\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}]}{[\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}]}\). A high product-to-reactant ion ratio lowers \(E\); for ratio \(100\), \(E=0.771-0.118=0.653\ \mathrm{V}\).
  • Aqueous standard reduction potentials of alkali metals are strongly affected by hydration enthalpy. Lithium has an exceptionally negative value, so \(E^\circ(\mathrm{Li^+/Li})=-3.04\ \mathrm{V}\), \(E^\circ(\mathrm{Na^+/Na})=-2.714\ \mathrm{V}\), and the negative-potential order among Li, K, Na is \(\mathrm{Li>K>Na}\).
  • Oxidation-state logic can appear inside electrochemistry-linked redox questions. In \(\mathrm{Se_2Cl_2}\), selenium is \(+1\); disproportionation requires one product with higher oxidation state and another with lower oxidation state, so \(\mathrm{SeCl_4}\) \((+4)\) and elemental \(\mathrm{Se}\) \((0)\) are formed.

    Nernst equation, equilibrium constant and Gibbs energy
  • At \(298\ \mathrm{K}\), use \(E=E^\circ-\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log Q\). Balance the redox reaction first to obtain \(n\), and omit pure solids and pure liquids from \(Q\).
  • For \(\mathrm{Zn(s)+2H^+\rightarrow Zn^{2+}+H_2}\), \(Q=\dfrac{[\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}]P_{\mathrm{H_2}}}{[\mathrm{H^+}]^2}\). The square on \([\mathrm{H^+}]\) and \(n=2\) are essential; solving the Nernst equation can directly give \(pH\).
  • The relation between standard cell potential and equilibrium constant is \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=\dfrac{0.06}{n}\log K\) at \(298\ \mathrm{K}\). For \(K=10^{15}\) and \(n=2\), \(E^\circ=0.45\ \mathrm{V}\).
  • When two half-cell potentials are given, first identify cathode and anode, then use \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=\dfrac{0.06}{n}\log K_c\). For a one-electron cell with \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=1.0\ \mathrm{V}\), \(\log K_c=\dfrac{100}{6}\).
  • Gibbs energy of a cell reaction is calculated from \(\Delta G=-nFE\). The sign is important: a positive cell potential gives negative \(\Delta G\), meaning the cell reaction is spontaneous.
  • For concentration-cell or non-standard cell questions, the numerical answer depends heavily on the correctly balanced \(Q\). Recheck powers of ion concentrations and electron count before substituting in the Nernst equation.

    Conductance and molar conductivity
  • Kohlrausch’s law states that \(\Lambda_m^\circ\) is the sum of limiting ionic conductivities with stoichiometric coefficients. For \(\mathrm{CaCl_2}\), \(\Lambda_m^\circ=\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}}+2\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Cl^-}}=119+2(76)=271\ \mathrm{S\ cm^2\ mol^{-1}}\), not \(195\).
  • For weak acids, \(\Lambda_m^\circ\) is obtained from ionic values: \(\Lambda_m^\circ(\mathrm{HCOOH})=\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{H^+}}+\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{HCOO^-}}\). If \(\alpha=11\%=0.11\), then \(\Lambda_m=\alpha\Lambda_m^\circ=0.11(404.2)=44.46\ \mathrm{S\ cm^2\ mol^{-1}}\).
  • For weak electrolytes, \(\alpha=\dfrac{\Lambda_m}{\Lambda_m^\circ}\). In conductivity-data problems, first calculate \(\Lambda_m=\kappa\times\dfrac{1000}{C}\), then divide by \(\Lambda_m^\circ\).
  • For weak monobasic acids, \(\Lambda_m=\alpha\Lambda_m^\circ\) and \(\kappa=\dfrac{\Lambda_m C}{1000}\). Keep track of whether the question asks for molar conductivity \(\Lambda_m\) or conductivity \(\kappa\).
  • Ostwald’s dilution law for a weak electrolyte is \(K_a=\dfrac{C\alpha^2}{1-\alpha}\); for small \(\alpha\), \(\alpha=\sqrt{\dfrac{K_a}{C}}\). For \(0.01\ \mathrm{M}\) acetic acid with \(K_a=1.8\times10^{-5}\), \(\alpha\approx0.0424\), so \(\Lambda_m\approx16.54\ \mathrm{S\ cm^2\ mol^{-1}}\) when \(\Lambda_m^\circ=390\).
  • Molar conductivity of a weak electrolyte such as acetic acid increases sharply on dilution because the degree of ionisation increases. Do not apply the nearly linear strong-electrolyte pattern of \(\Lambda_m\) versus \(\sqrt{C}\) to weak acids.
  • For resistance questions, use \(\kappa=\dfrac{\text{cell constant}}{R}\) and \(\Lambda_m=\dfrac{\kappa}{C}\) with compatible units. Convert \(0.02\ \mathrm{M}\) to \(20\ \mathrm{mol\ m^{-3}}\) and \(124\ \mathrm{S\ cm^2\ mol^{-1}}\) to \(124\times10^{-4}\ \mathrm{S\ m^2\ mol^{-1}}\) before calculating \(R\).

    Electrolysis and Faraday laws
  • One Faraday is the charge on one mole of electrons: \(1F\approx96500\ \mathrm{C}\). Deposition of one gram equivalent of any substance requires exactly \(1F\), so “one equivalent deposited” immediately means charge \(=1F\).
  • For electrolysis numericals, first calculate charge \(Q=It\), converting time to seconds. Then calculate faradays passed as \(\dfrac{Q}{F}\).
  • Electrochemical equivalent is \(Z=\dfrac{m}{Q}=\dfrac{m}{It}\). In time-based problems, convert minutes to seconds before using \(Q=It\); for \(0.5\ \mathrm{A}\) passed for \(60\) min, \(Q=1800\ \mathrm{C}\).
  • Equivalent weight from deposited mass is obtained using “mass deposited per Faraday = equivalent weight.” For Cu deposition with \(Q=2880\ \mathrm{C}\) and mass \(0.96\ \mathrm{g}\), the equivalent weight is approximately \(31.75\ \mathrm{g}\), while \(63.5\) is the atomic mass.
  • For the same quantity of electricity through different electrolytes, masses deposited are proportional to equivalent masses: \(\dfrac{m_1}{m_2}=\dfrac{E_1}{E_2}\), where \(E=\dfrac{\text{atomic mass}}{\text{valency}}\). Using Ag as monovalent gives the valency of Au as \(3\) from the given Ag/Au deposition ratio.
  • For molten \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\), \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}+3e^-\rightarrow Al}\) and \(2\mathrm{Cl^-}\rightarrow\mathrm{Cl_2}+2e^-\). Use \(3F\) per mole of Al and \(2F\) per mole of \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\); for \(5\times10^{-4}F\), Al deposited is \(4.5\ \mathrm{mg}\) and \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\) evolved is \(5.6\ \mathrm{mL}\) at STP.
  • For chlorine gas liberation, the anode reaction \(2Cl^-\rightarrow Cl_2+2e^-\) requires \(2F\) for \(1\) mole of \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\). After finding \(Q=It\), calculate \(n(\mathrm{Cl_2})=\dfrac{Q}{2F}\) and convert to volume using \(22.4\ \mathrm{L\ mol^{-1}}\) at STP.
  • In aqueous \(\mathrm{Na_2SO_4}\), \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) and \(\mathrm{SO_4^{2-}}\) mainly act as spectator ions and water is electrolysed. Oxygen is liberated at the anode by \(2H_2O\rightarrow O_2+4H^++4e^-\), so \(4F\) gives \(1\) mole of \(\mathrm{O_2}\).
  • In aqueous \(\mathrm{CuSO_4}\) with inert Pt electrodes, \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) is reduced to Cu at the cathode and water is oxidised to \(\mathrm{O_2}\) at the anode. Do not discharge \(\mathrm{SO_4^{2-}}\) at the anode in this standard treatment.
  • Molten and aqueous electrolysis give different products. Molten \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) gives Na at the cathode and \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\) at the anode, while aqueous \(\mathrm{CuSO_4}\) with Pt gives Cu at the cathode and \(\mathrm{O_2}\) at the anode.
  • In the Castner-Kellner process, brine is electrolysed to manufacture \(\mathrm{NaOH}\). Mercury acts as cathode and forms sodium amalgam, while carbon or graphite acts as anode and liberates chlorine by \(2Cl^-\rightarrow Cl_2+2e^-\).

    Rate law, order and molecularity
  • Reaction order is determined experimentally from the rate law, not from stoichiometric coefficients. In initial-rate tables, double one reactant while keeping the other constant to find the order with respect to that reactant.
  • If doubling \([\mathrm{A_2}]\) doubles rate and doubling \([\mathrm{B_2}]\) also doubles rate, then the reaction is first order in each and overall second order: \(\mathrm{Rate}=k[\mathrm{A_2}][\mathrm{B_2}]\).
  • Units of \(k\) reveal the order: zero order has \(\mathrm{mol\ L^{-1}\ s^{-1}}\), first order has \(\mathrm{s^{-1}}\), and second order has \(\mathrm{L\ mol^{-1}\ s^{-1}}\). Thus \(k=1.2\times10^{-2}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}\) immediately indicates first order.
  • For rate \(=k[\mathrm{HI}]^2\), the reaction is second order and \(k\) has units \(\mathrm{L\ mol^{-1}\ s^{-1}}\). Do not confuse this with third-order units \(\mathrm{L^2\ mol^{-2}\ s^{-1}}\).
  • At fixed temperature, the rate constant \(k\) for a given reaction does not change when initial concentration changes. In a first-order reaction, doubling initial concentration doubles initial rate but leaves \(k\) unchanged.
  • When comparing rates from different \(k\) values, apply the actual rate law and concentration. At \([A]=1\ \mathrm{M}\), numerical comparison becomes direct, but the order inferred from the unit of \(k\) must still be respected.
  • In mechanism-based questions, write the rate law from the slow step first, then eliminate intermediates using fast equilibrium. For \(R=k[N_2O_2][H_2]\) and \(2NO\rightleftharpoons N_2O_2\), use \([N_2O_2]\propto[NO]^2\), giving \(R=k[NO]^2[H_2]\).
  • Rate expressions for appearance and disappearance must account for stoichiometric coefficients. For \(\mathrm{N_2O_5\rightarrow2NO_2+\dfrac{1}{2}O_2}\), \(-\dfrac{d[\mathrm{N_2O_5}]}{dt}=\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{NO_2}]}{dt}=2\dfrac{d[\mathrm{O_2}]}{dt}\), so \(2K_1=K_2=4K_3\).

    Integrated rate equations and graphs
  • For zero-order reactions, \([A]=[A]_0-kt\). Therefore, a graph of \([A]\) versus \(t\) is a straight line with slope \(-k\) and intercept \([A]_0\).
  • Zero-order half-life is \(t_{1/2}=\dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}\). Unlike first order, it depends on initial concentration; if \([A]_0=4\) and \(t_{1/2}=0.5\ \mathrm{hr}\), then \(k=4\ \mathrm{mol\ L^{-1}\ hr^{-1}}\).
  • Photochemical \(H_2+Cl_2\xrightarrow{h\nu}2HCl\) is treated as a standard zero-order example in basic kinetics because the rate is controlled mainly by light intensity rather than reactant concentration.
  • For first-order reactions, \(\ln[R]=\ln[R]_0-kt\). Hence \(\ln[R]\) versus \(t\) is linear with slope \(-k\) and y-intercept \(\ln[R]_0\).
  • First-order half-life is \(t_{1/2}=\dfrac{0.693}{k}\) and is independent of initial concentration. If \(t_{1/2}\) does not change when \([R]_0\) changes, the reaction is first order.
  • For first-order reactions, \(\log\dfrac{a}{a-x}=\dfrac{k}{2.303}t\). A graph of \(\log\dfrac{a}{a-x}\) versus \(t\) has slope \(\dfrac{k}{2.303}\), so multiply the slope by \(2.303\) to obtain \(k\).
  • For percentage completion in first-order reactions, use the fraction remaining. At \(90\%\) completion, \(10\%\) remains, so \(\dfrac{a}{a-x}=10\) and \(t=\dfrac{2.303}{k}\).
  • For first-order time-ratio questions, \(t\propto\log\dfrac{\text{initial}}{\text{remaining}}\). Thus time for \(75\%\) completion uses \(\log4=0.6\), while time for \(90\%\) completion uses \(\log10=1\), giving a ratio of \(60\%\).
  • For first-order gas-phase decomposition \(\mathrm{A(g)\rightarrow B(g)+C(g)}\), pressure changes must be converted into remaining reactant pressure. If initial pressure is \(p_0\), total pressure rises to \(p_t=p_0+x\), so \(p_A=p_0-x\), not \(p_t\).
  • For second-order identification from half-life, use \(t_{1/2}\propto\dfrac{1}{a}\). If initial concentration decreases \(10\) times and half-life increases \(10\) times, the reaction is second order.
  • The three most repeated graph forms are: zero order \([A]\) versus \(t\), first order \(\ln[A]\) versus \(t\), and second order \(\dfrac{1}{[A]}\) versus \(t\). Avoid choosing non-standard forms such as \([A]\) versus \(\dfrac{1}{t}\) or \(\dfrac{1}{[A]}\) versus \(t^2\).

    Arrhenius equation, activation energy and catalyst
  • The Arrhenius equation is \(k=Ae^{-E_a/RT}\). In logarithmic form, \(\ln k=\ln A-\dfrac{E_a}{RT}\), so a plot of \(\ln k\) versus \(\dfrac{1}{T}\) has slope \(-\dfrac{E_a}{R}\).
  • For common logarithm, \(\log k=\log A-\dfrac{E_a}{2.303RT}\). If the coefficient of \(\dfrac{1}{T}\) is \(10^4\), then \(E_a=2.303R\times10^4\); the \(2.303\) factor is used only with \(\log\), not \(\ln\).
  • For an Arrhenius plot using \(\ln k\), do not include \(2.303\). If slope \(=-10^3\ \mathrm{K}\), then \(E_a=8.314\times10^3\ \mathrm{J\ mol^{-1}}=8.314\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\).
  • For two-temperature Arrhenius problems, use \(\log\dfrac{k_2}{k_1}=\dfrac{E_a}{2.303R}\left(\dfrac{1}{T_1}-\dfrac{1}{T_2}\right)\). Temperatures must be in Kelvin.
  • The phrase “temperature coefficient holds good” usually means rate or rate constant doubles for a \(10^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) rise. From \(300\ \mathrm{K}\) to \(310\ \mathrm{K}\), \(k_2/k_1=2\) gives \(E_a\approx53.3\text{–}53.5\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\).
  • For first-order reactions, rate is directly proportional to \(k\) at the same concentration pattern. Therefore, “rate doubles from \(300\ \mathrm{K}\) to \(310\ \mathrm{K}\)” can be treated as \(k_2/k_1=2\).
  • A catalyst increases rate by lowering activation energy. At the same temperature, use \(\ln\left(\dfrac{k_1}{k_2}\right)=\dfrac{\Delta E_a}{RT}\); if \(k_2=4k_1\), use \(\ln(1/4)\), giving a negative \(\Delta E_a\).
  • A fourfold increase in rate constant at \(300\ \mathrm{K}\) corresponds to \(\Delta E_a=-1.386\times8.314\times300\approx-3.45\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\). The negative sign is expected because the catalyst lowers the energy barrier.
  • Activation energy is related to threshold energy by \(E_a=E_t-E_i\). Threshold energy itself is not activation energy; if \(E_t=75\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\) and \(E_i=20\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\), then \(E_a=55\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\).

  • \(F\): Faraday constant, approximately \(96500\ \mathrm{C\ mol^{-1}}\); \(1F\) is the charge on \(1\) mole of electrons.
  • \(Q\): reaction quotient in the Nernst equation; in electrolysis, \(Q\) may also mean charge passed, where \(Q=It\).
  • \(\Delta G\): Gibbs energy change of cell reaction.
  • \(\kappa\): conductivity or specific conductance; \(R\): resistance; cell constant \(=\dfrac{l}{A}\).
  • \(\Lambda_m\): molar conductivity; \(\Lambda_m^\circ\): limiting molar conductivity at infinite dilution; \(\lambda_i^\circ\): limiting ionic molar conductivity.
  • \(\alpha\): degree of dissociation of weak electrolyte; \(K_a\): acid dissociation constant.

    Redox basis and oxidation number
  • Electrochemistry is based on electron-transfer reactions. Oxidation is loss of electrons and reduction is gain of electrons; the species losing electrons acts as reducing agent, and the species gaining electrons acts as oxidising agent.
  • Oxidation number is the apparent charge assigned to an atom by assuming that shared electrons belong to the more electronegative atom. It is especially useful for identifying oxidation and reduction in polyatomic species.
  • The oxidation number of an element in its free state is zero; a monatomic ion has oxidation number equal to its charge. Oxygen is usually \(-2\), except in peroxides and superoxides; hydrogen is \(+1\) with non-metals and \(-1\) with metals.
  • In a neutral compound, the sum of oxidation numbers is zero; in a polyatomic ion, the sum equals the charge on the ion. These rules are enough for most EAPCET oxidation-state and redox questions.
  • In disproportionation, the same element in one oxidation state is simultaneously oxidised and reduced. For example, in \(\mathrm{Se_2Cl_2}\), selenium is \(+1\); formation of \(\mathrm{SeCl_4}\) \((+4)\) and elemental \(\mathrm{Se}\) \((0)\) shows both oxidation and reduction.

    Electrolytic conduction, conductivity and molar conductivity
  • Electrolytic conduction occurs due to movement of cations and anions in solution or molten electrolyte. The electrical conductance of an electrolyte depends on nature of solute, valency of ions, concentration and temperature.
  • Conductance \(L\) is the reciprocal of resistance, \(L=\dfrac{1}{R}\), and conductivity \(\kappa\) is the reciprocal of resistivity. For a conductivity cell, \(\kappa=\text{conductance}\times\text{cell constant}\).
  • The cell constant is \(\dfrac{l}{A}\), where \(l\) is the distance between electrodes and \(A\) is their area of cross-section. Therefore, \(\kappa=\dfrac{\text{cell constant}}{R}\), which is the form used in resistance-based numericals.
  • Molar conductivity is the conducting power of all ions produced by one mole of electrolyte at a given concentration. In common \(\mathrm{cm}\)-based units, \(\Lambda_m=\dfrac{1000\kappa}{C}\), where \(C\) is molarity.
  • In SI-based numerical problems, keep units consistent: \(1\ \mathrm{M}=1000\ \mathrm{mol\ m^{-3}}\), and \(\mathrm{S\ cm^2\ mol^{-1}}\) must be converted to \(\mathrm{S\ m^2\ mol^{-1}}\) when \(C\) is in \(\mathrm{mol\ m^{-3}}\).
  • On dilution, conductivity \(\kappa\) decreases because the number of ions per unit volume decreases. Molar conductivity \(\Lambda_m\) increases because the volume containing one mole of electrolyte increases and interionic effects decrease.
  • Strong electrolytes are almost completely ionised at all concentrations, so \(\Lambda_m\) increases gradually on dilution. Weak electrolytes are only partially ionised, so \(\Lambda_m\) increases sharply on dilution due to increase in degree of ionisation.
  • For weak electrolytes, \(\alpha=\dfrac{\Lambda_m}{\Lambda_m^\circ}\). This relation is repeatedly useful for weak acid conductivity questions, where \(\Lambda_m\) may first be calculated from \(\kappa\) and concentration.
  • Ostwald’s dilution law connects conductivity and weak-acid ionisation: \(K_a=\dfrac{C\alpha^2}{1-\alpha}\). For small \(\alpha\), the useful approximation is \(\alpha=\sqrt{\dfrac{K_a}{C}}\).
  • Kohlrausch’s law states that at infinite dilution each ion contributes independently to total molar conductivity. For \(\mathrm{A}_x\mathrm{B}_y\), \(\Lambda_m^\circ=x\lambda_A^\circ+y\lambda_B^\circ\).
  • Stoichiometric coefficients must be included in Kohlrausch-law calculations. For \(\mathrm{CaCl_2}\), \(\Lambda_m^\circ=\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}}+2\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Cl^-}}\), not \(\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}}+\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Cl^-}}\).
  • For weak acids such as \(\mathrm{HCOOH}\), \(\Lambda_m^\circ=\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{H^+}}+\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{HCOO^-}}\). Once \(\Lambda_m^\circ\) is known, \(\Lambda_m=\alpha\Lambda_m^\circ\) gives the molar conductivity at a given dissociation.

    Electrochemical cells and electrode potential
  • An electrochemical cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy or electrical energy into chemical energy. A galvanic cell produces electricity from a spontaneous redox reaction, while an electrolytic cell uses electricity to drive a chemical change.
  • In a galvanic cell, oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction occurs at the cathode. In standard cell notation, the anode is written on the left and the cathode on the right, with a double vertical line representing the salt bridge.
  • For the Daniell cell, zinc undergoes oxidation and copper ion undergoes reduction: \(\mathrm{Zn\rightarrow Zn^{2+}+2e^-}\), \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}+2e^-\rightarrow Cu}\). Electrons flow externally from zinc to copper.
  • The salt bridge completes the internal circuit and maintains electrical neutrality. Anions move towards the oxidation half-cell, while cations move towards the reduction half-cell.
  • Electrode potential is the potential developed when a metal is placed in contact with a solution of its own ions. It cannot be measured independently, so it is measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode.
  • The standard hydrogen electrode has assigned potential \(0.00\ \mathrm{V}\) under standard conditions: \(1\ \mathrm{M}\ \mathrm{H^+}\), \(1\ \mathrm{bar}\ \mathrm{H_2}\), and \(298\ \mathrm{K}\). It may act as anode or cathode depending on the other electrode.
  • The standard electrode potential of an electrode is obtained by coupling it with SHE and observing the cell emf and polarity. Zinc has negative standard reduction potential, while copper has positive standard reduction potential relative to SHE.
  • The electrochemical series arranges electrodes in increasing standard reduction potential. A species with higher reduction potential acts more readily as oxidising agent, while a species with lower reduction potential is more easily oxidised.
  • For a cell, \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=E^\circ_{\mathrm{cathode}}-E^\circ_{\mathrm{anode}}\). A positive \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}\) indicates a spontaneous galvanic reaction under standard conditions.
  • Electrode potentials are reduction potentials by convention. When checking whether one species oxidises another, assign reduction to the oxidising species and oxidation to the other species before applying \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}\).
  • Electrode potentials are not simply additive. Since \(\Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ\), multi-step potential calculations must use electron-weighted terms \(nE^\circ\), not plain voltage addition or subtraction.
  • Aqueous electrode potentials also reflect hydration effects. Lithium has a very negative \(E^\circ(\mathrm{Li^+/Li})\) because its hydration enthalpy is very high; therefore aqueous potential order should not be guessed only from atomic size or group reactivity.

    Nernst equation, equilibrium constant and Gibbs energy
  • The Nernst equation gives cell potential under non-standard conditions. At \(298\ \mathrm{K}\), \(E=E^\circ-\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log Q\), where \(Q\) is the reaction quotient and \(n\) is the number of electrons transferred.
  • Before using the Nernst equation, balance the redox reaction and identify \(n\). Pure solids and pure liquids are not included in \(Q\); only gases and dissolved species appear.
  • For a hydrogen electrode, the potential depends on hydrogen-ion concentration. When \(P_{\mathrm{H_2}}=1\), \(E=-0.0591\times pH\), which makes pH-based electrode-potential questions very direct.
  • For \(\mathrm{Zn(s)+2H^+\rightarrow Zn^{2+}+H_2}\), \(Q=\dfrac{[\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}]P_{\mathrm{H_2}}}{[\mathrm{H^+}]^2}\). The square on \([\mathrm{H^+}]\) follows from the coefficient \(2\) and is a common source of error.
  • For \(\mathrm{Cl_2(g)+2e^-\rightarrow2Cl^-}\), \(E=E^\circ-\dfrac{0.0591}{2}\log\dfrac{[\mathrm{Cl^-}]^2}{P_{\mathrm{Cl_2}}}\). Increasing product-ion concentration lowers the reduction potential.
  • For \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}+e^-\rightarrow Fe^{2+}}\), \(E=E^\circ-0.0591\log\dfrac{[\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}]}{[\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}]}\). A larger \([\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}]/[\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}]\) ratio decreases the electrode potential.
  • At equilibrium, \(E_{\mathrm{cell}}=0\). Therefore, \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log K\) at \(298\ \mathrm{K}\), often rounded to \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=\dfrac{0.06}{n}\log K\).
  • The standard Gibbs energy relation is \(\Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}\), and under non-standard conditions \(\Delta G=-nFE_{\mathrm{cell}}\). Positive cell emf corresponds to negative Gibbs energy change.
  • In cell numericals involving \(\Delta G\), \(K\), or pH, the same sequence is safest: identify anode/cathode, balance electrons, write \(Q\), substitute in Nernst equation, then connect to \(\Delta G\) or \(K\) if required.

    Electrolytic cells and products of electrolysis
  • In an electrolytic cell, electrical energy is converted into chemical energy. Cations move to the cathode and undergo reduction; anions move to the anode and undergo oxidation.
  • For molten salts, the ions of the salt are discharged directly. Molten \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) gives \(\mathrm{Na}\) at the cathode and \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\) at the anode.
  • For aqueous solutions, water competes with dissolved ions. In aqueous \(\mathrm{CuSO_4}\) using inert Pt electrodes, \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) is reduced to Cu at the cathode, while water is oxidised to \(\mathrm{O_2}\) at the anode.
  • In aqueous \(\mathrm{Na_2SO_4}\), \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) and \(\mathrm{SO_4^{2-}}\) mainly remain spectator ions. Water is electrolysed, giving \(\mathrm{H_2}\) at cathode and \(\mathrm{O_2}\) at anode.
  • At the anode in water oxidation, \(2H_2O\rightarrow O_2+4H^++4e^-\). Therefore, \(4F\) of charge produces \(1\) mole of oxygen gas.
  • At a chloride anode, \(2Cl^-\rightarrow Cl_2+2e^-\). Therefore, \(2F\) of charge produces \(1\) mole of chlorine gas.
  • In the Castner-Kellner process for sodium hydroxide manufacture, brine is electrolysed using a mercury cathode and carbon or graphite anode. Sodium forms amalgam at the cathode and chlorine is liberated at the anode.
  • Product-prediction questions are easiest when electrode type and medium are noted first: molten salt, aqueous solution, active electrode, or inert electrode. Confusing aqueous and molten conditions leads to wrong cathode/anode products.

    Faraday laws of electrolysis
  • Faraday’s first law states that the mass liberated or deposited at an electrode is proportional to the quantity of electricity passed: \(m\propto Q\), and \(Q=It\).
  • Electrochemical equivalent \(Z\) is the mass deposited by \(1\ \mathrm{C}\) of charge. Hence \(m=ZIt\) and \(Z=\dfrac{m}{It}\).
  • One Faraday is the charge on one mole of electrons: \(1F=96500\ \mathrm{C}\). One Faraday deposits or liberates one gram equivalent of a substance.
  • The chemical equivalent or equivalent weight is \(E=\dfrac{\text{atomic mass}}{\text{valency}}\). For aluminium, \(E=\dfrac{27}{3}=9\ \mathrm{g}\).
  • The most useful electrolysis formula is \(m=\dfrac{EIt}{96500}\), where \(E\) is equivalent weight. Always convert time into seconds before substituting.
  • Faradays passed \(=\dfrac{It}{96500}\). Once faradays are known, use the electron requirement of the electrode reaction: \(3F\) deposits \(1\) mole of Al, \(2F\) produces \(1\) mole of \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\), and \(4F\) produces \(1\) mole of \(\mathrm{O_2}\).
  • Faraday’s second law states that when the same quantity of electricity passes through different electrolytes, the masses deposited are proportional to equivalent weights: \(\dfrac{m_1}{m_2}=\dfrac{E_1}{E_2}\).
  • For valency determination from deposited masses, write \(E=\dfrac{\text{atomic mass}}{\text{valency}}\). If Ag is involved, use Ag as monovalent unless otherwise stated.
  • For gas-volume electrolysis problems, calculate moles of gas from faradays and then use \(22.4\ \mathrm{L\ mol^{-1}}\) at STP. Do not stop at charge or mass if the question asks for volume.

    Batteries, fuel cells, corrosion and hydrogen economy
  • A battery is a practical electrochemical source of electrical energy. Primary batteries are not designed for recharging, while secondary batteries can be recharged by reversing the cell reaction using external electrical energy.
  • Dry cells are common primary cells. Their usefulness lies in producing portable electrical energy from spontaneous redox reactions.
  • Lead-acid storage batteries are typical secondary batteries used in automobiles. They can be discharged to supply current and recharged by applying external electrical energy.
  • Fuel cells convert the chemical energy of continuously supplied fuel and oxidant directly into electrical energy. A hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell produces water as the main product and is important in clean-energy discussions.
  • Corrosion is an electrochemical process in which a metal is oxidised by environmental substances such as oxygen and water. Rusting of iron involves anodic oxidation of iron and cathodic reduction of oxygen.
  • Protection from corrosion is based on preventing oxidation of the metal or preventing formation of local electrochemical cells. Common methods include coating, galvanisation and cathodic protection.
  • Hydrogen economy refers to using hydrogen as an energy carrier, especially with fuel cells. Its importance is linked with high energy output and cleaner product formation, but storage, transport and production remain practical challenges.

    Rate of reaction and stoichiometric rate expression
  • Chemical kinetics studies the speed of reactions and the factors affecting it. A reaction may be thermodynamically feasible but still slow if the activation barrier is high.
  • Rate of reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time. For reactants, the rate is written with a negative sign because their concentration decreases.
  • For a balanced reaction, rates written using different species must be divided by their stoichiometric coefficients to give the same reaction rate.
  • For \(\mathrm{2N_2O_5\rightarrow4NO_2+O_2}\), the reaction rate is \(-\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{N_2O_5}]}{dt}=\dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{NO_2}]}{dt}=\dfrac{d[\mathrm{O_2}]}{dt}\). Coefficients and reciprocals must be handled carefully.
  • Average rate is calculated over a finite time interval, whereas instantaneous rate is the rate at a particular moment and is written using derivatives such as \(\dfrac{d[A]}{dt}\).
  • The main factors affecting rate are concentration of reactants, temperature and catalyst. Increasing volume of a gaseous system lowers pressure and concentration, so rate usually decreases.

    Rate law, rate constant, order and molecularity
  • The rate law expresses rate as a function of reactant concentrations: \(r=k[A]^x[B]^y\). Here \(x\) and \(y\) are experimentally determined orders with respect to \(A\) and \(B\).
  • The overall order of reaction is \(x+y\). It may be zero, one, two, three or fractional, and it cannot be predicted simply from the balanced equation.
  • The rate constant \(k\) is the proportionality constant in the rate law. It is numerically equal to the rate when all reactant concentrations are unity, and it depends on temperature but not on initial concentration.
  • In initial-rate data, changing one reactant concentration while keeping the other constant reveals the order with respect to that reactant. If doubling a concentration doubles rate, the reaction is first order in that reactant.
  • Units of \(k\) are order-specific. For zero order, \(k\) has units of concentration per time; for first order, \(k\) has units of time\(^{-1}\); for second order, \(k\) has units \(\mathrm{L\ mol^{-1}\ s^{-1}}\).
  • In general, for an \(n\)-th order reaction, units of \(k=(\mathrm{mol\ L^{-1}})^{1-n}\mathrm{s^{-1}}\). Unit-based order identification is a quick method in objective questions.
  • Molecularity is the number of reacting species taking part in an elementary step. Unlike order, molecularity is never zero or fractional and is applied to elementary steps, not necessarily the overall reaction.
  • For a mechanism, the rate law is written from the slow step. If a fast pre-equilibrium forms an intermediate, substitute its concentration in terms of stable reactants before writing the final rate law.

    Zero-order and first-order integrated rate equations
  • For a zero-order reaction, rate is independent of reactant concentration: \(r=k\). The integrated equation is \([A]=[A]_0-kt\).
  • A plot of \([A]\) versus \(t\) for a zero-order reaction is a straight line with slope \(-k\) and intercept \([A]_0\). Its half-life is \(t_{1/2}=\dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}\), so it depends on initial concentration.
  • Zero-order reactions are uncommon but can occur when a surface or external factor controls the reaction, such as decomposition on a saturated catalyst surface or photochemical conditions controlled by light intensity.
  • For a first-order reaction, rate \(=k[A]\). The integrated equation is \(\ln\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]}=kt\), or \(k=\dfrac{2.303}{t}\log\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]}\).
  • The first-order straight-line form is \(\ln[A]=\ln[A]_0-kt\), or \(\log[A]=\log[A]_0-\dfrac{k}{2.303}t\). Therefore, \(\ln[A]\) versus \(t\) has slope \(-k\), while \(\log[A]\) versus \(t\) has slope \(-\dfrac{k}{2.303}\).
  • A graph of \(\log\dfrac{a}{a-x}\) versus \(t\) for a first-order reaction has slope \(\dfrac{k}{2.303}\). Therefore, \(k=2.303\times\text{slope}\).
  • First-order half-life is \(t_{1/2}=\dfrac{0.693}{k}\). It is independent of initial concentration, which is one of the most useful experimental tests for first-order behaviour.
  • For percentage-completion questions, always use the amount remaining. At \(90\%\) completion, \(10\%\) remains, so \(\dfrac{a}{a-x}=10\) and \(t=\dfrac{2.303}{k}\).
  • For first-order gas reactions such as \(\mathrm{A(g)\rightarrow B(g)+C(g)}\), total pressure must be converted into remaining reactant pressure before using the first-order formula. If \(p_t=p_0+x\), then \(p_A=p_0-x\).
  • For second-order recognition by half-life, \(t_{1/2}\propto\dfrac{1}{[A]_0}\). If concentration decreases ten times and half-life increases ten times, the reaction is second order.

    Pseudo-first-order reaction
  • A pseudo-first-order reaction is actually of higher order, but behaves like first order because one reactant is present in large excess and its concentration remains nearly constant.
  • Hydrolysis reactions in water are common examples because water is present in large excess. The rate law then absorbs the nearly constant concentration into a new apparent rate constant.
  • The practical form becomes \(r=k'[A]\), where \(k'=k[B]_{\text{excess}}\). Such reactions are treated using first-order integrated equations and half-life relations.

    Temperature dependence, Arrhenius equation and catalyst
  • Reaction rate generally increases with temperature because a larger fraction of molecules acquire energy equal to or greater than the threshold energy.
  • The rough temperature-coefficient rule states that rates of many reactions approximately double for every \(10^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) rise in temperature. This rule is often connected to Arrhenius-equation numericals.
  • The Arrhenius equation is \(k=Ae^{-E_a/RT}\), where \(A\) is frequency factor, \(E_a\) is activation energy, \(R\) is gas constant, and \(T\) is absolute temperature.
  • For natural logarithm, \(\ln k=\ln A-\dfrac{E_a}{RT}\). A graph of \(\ln k\) versus \(\dfrac{1}{T}\) is a straight line with slope \(-\dfrac{E_a}{R}\).
  • For common logarithm, \(\log k=\log A-\dfrac{E_a}{2.303RT}\). The factor \(2.303\) is used only with \(\log\), not with \(\ln\).
  • For two temperatures, \(\log\dfrac{k_2}{k_1}=\dfrac{E_a}{2.303R}\left(\dfrac{1}{T_1}-\dfrac{1}{T_2}\right)\). Temperatures must always be converted to kelvin.
  • If the rate or rate constant doubles from \(300\ \mathrm{K}\) to \(310\ \mathrm{K}\), using \(k_2/k_1=2\) in the two-temperature Arrhenius equation gives \(E_a\approx53.5\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}\).
  • Activation energy is the energy gap between reactants and the activated complex. If threshold energy \(E_t\) and internal energy of reactants \(E_i\) are given, \(E_a=E_t-E_i\).
  • A catalyst changes the reaction rate without being consumed. It provides an alternative pathway with lower activation energy, thereby increasing \(k\) at the same temperature.
  • For catalyst questions at the same temperature, compare the two Arrhenius expressions. If \(k_2\) is the catalysed rate constant and \(k_2=4k_1\), then \(\ln\dfrac{k_1}{k_2}=\dfrac{\Delta E_a}{RT}\), so \(\Delta E_a\) is negative.

    Collision theory of reaction rates
  • Collision theory explains reaction rate in terms of effective collisions between reacting molecules. A collision is effective only if the molecules collide with sufficient energy and proper orientation.
  • The minimum energy required for effective collision is linked with activation energy. Molecules with energy lower than this barrier collide but do not form products.
  • Increasing temperature increases the fraction of molecules having energy greater than or equal to activation energy. This explains why a small rise in temperature can cause a large increase in rate.
  • A catalyst increases the number of effective collisions by lowering the activation-energy barrier. It does not change the overall thermodynamic feasibility of the reaction.

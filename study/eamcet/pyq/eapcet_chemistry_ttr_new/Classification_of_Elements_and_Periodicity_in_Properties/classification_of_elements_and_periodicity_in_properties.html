Classification of Elements and Periodicity in Properties

    Exam map from the PYQs
  • Most repeated cluster: atomic radius, ionic radius, isoelectronic size order, and lanthanoid-contraction based “same size” pairs.
  • Next biggest cluster: electron gain enthalpy and ionisation enthalpy trends, including standard exceptions like \(Cl>F\), \(S>P\), \(N>O\), \(Be>B\).
  • Frequent direct-application cluster: identify block / group / period / family from atomic number or configuration.
  • Repeated chemical-property cluster: metallic character, acidic/basic/amphoteric oxides, and bonding type from periodic position.
  • One-shot but important factual cluster: nomenclature / symbols of superheavy elements and standard periodic-table facts.

    Atomic-radius and ionic-radius solve-fast rules
  • Across a period: radius generally decreases because effective nuclear charge increases.
  • Down a group: radius generally increases because a new shell is added.
  • Isoelectronic species: more nuclear charge \(\Rightarrow\) smaller radius immediately.
  • For \(10\)-electron species: \(Mg^{2+} < Al^{3+}\) is false; actual order must follow nuclear charge carefully.
  • Typical \(10\)-electron increasing ionic-radius order: \(Mg^{2+} < Na^+ < F^- < O^{2-}\).
  • If species are not isoelectronic, compare shell number first; extra shell usually dominates.
  • Lanthanoid contraction trigger: \(Zr \approx Hf\) and \(Mo \approx W\) type pairs.
  • Classic exception: \(Ga\) is smaller than \(Al\) because of poor \(3d\)-electron shielding.

    Ionisation enthalpy and electron gain enthalpy shortcuts
  • General trend: \(IE\) increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Must-remember exceptions: \(IE_1(Be) > IE_1(B)\) and \(IE_1(N) > IE_1(O)\).
  • Reason: filled \(s^2\) and half-filled \(p^3\) are extra stable.
  • If first, second, third ionisation energies are moderate and the next is huge, valence electrons \(=\) number removed before the jump.
  • So giant jump after 3rd \(IE\) \(\Rightarrow\) group 13 element.
  • Electron gain enthalpy: more negative usually means stronger electron acceptance.
  • Must-remember exceptions: \(Cl\) more negative than \(F\); \(S\) more negative than \(P\).
  • Noble gases usually show positive electron gain enthalpy.

    Question-to-formula / question-to-rule mapping
  • If question asks “same \(s:p\) ratio”, write full electronic configuration and count total occupied \(s\)- and \(p\)-electrons.
  • If question gives atomic number and asks period/group, first write outer configuration, then map block and valence shell.
  • If question asks block, use last electron entry: \(s,p,d,f\).
  • If question gives \(IE_1, IE_2,\Delta_{eg}H\) and asks “most reactive metal”, look for low \(IE_1\), very high \(IE_2\), and metallic character.
  • If question asks bond type from periodic position, alkali metal + nearby nonmetal usually points to ionic bonding.
  • If question asks “difference in radius is maximum”, compare the leftmost-vs-rightmost pair in the same period first.

    Metallic character, oxide nature, and chemical reactivity
  • Metallic character increases down a group and towards the left across a period.
  • So among mixed sets like \(Sn, Cd, Zr, Sr\), the left/down element is most metallic.
  • Across a period, oxides shift: basic \(\rightarrow\) amphoteric \(\rightarrow\) acidic.
  • Typical order from period-2 / period-3 style questions: metal oxide least acidic, nonmetal oxide most acidic.
  • \(BeO\) and \(Al_2O_3\) are amphoteric traps in oxide-acidity questions.
  • Acidic-oxide counting questions are solved fastest by first separating basic / amphoteric / acidic classes.

    Lanthanoid contraction and poor-shielding reminders
  • \(f\)-electrons shield poorly \(\Rightarrow\) gradual size decrease from La to Lu.
  • Consequences repeatedly tested: \(Zr\) and \(Hf\) nearly same size; lanthanoid separation is difficult, not easy.
  • Hydroxide basicity decreases from La\((OH)_3\) to Lu\((OH)_3\).
  • \(d\)-electron poor shielding explains \(Ga < Al\) and several post-transition size anomalies.

    Direct factual reminders that keep appearing
  • Atomic number \(110\) is \(Ds\) (Darmstadtium).
  • Older temporary names like \(Uuq\) point to superheavy \(p\)-block nomenclature questions.
  • \(He\) is second most abundant element in the universe.
  • \(Fr\) is treated as most electropositive in standard exam chemistry.
  • Os is commonly taken as the densest element in exam-oriented chemistry.

    Common traps from these PYQs
  • Do not use “simple down-group increase” blindly; \(Ga\) vs \(Al\) breaks it.
  • Do not say “isoelectronic means same radius”; same-electron species still differ because nuclear charge differs.
  • Do not rank \(F\) above \(Cl\) in electron gain enthalpy without checking the compact-\(2p\) exception.
  • Do not forget \(B\) has lower \(IE_1\) than \(Be\), and \(O\) has lower \(IE_1\) than \(N\).
  • In large-ionisation-jump questions, the jump tells valence count, not the exact subshell directly.

    Need for classification and modern periodic law
  • Classification became necessary because the number of known elements grew rapidly and their properties needed systematic comparison.
  • Modern periodic law: the physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.
  • The long-form periodic table is based on atomic number and electronic configuration.
  • Periods correspond to principal shells being filled; groups collect elements with similar valence-shell patterns.
  • Main practical exam use: predict trends and identify group / period / block from atomic number or configuration.

    Nomenclature above atomic number 100
  • Superheavy-element naming questions may use old temporary names or modern symbols.
  • Examples seen in PYQs: \(Ds\) for atomic number \(110\), and older temporary notation like \(Uuq\).
  • In solve-fast mode, map atomic number \(\rightarrow\) symbol first, then place the element by block/family.

    Electronic configuration and blocks
  • \(s\)-block: valence electron enters an \(s\)-orbital.
  • \(p\)-block: valence electron enters a \(p\)-orbital.
  • \(d\)-block: general configuration \((n-1)d^{1-10}ns^{0-2}\); many school-level questions use \((n-1)d^{1-10}ns^2\) as the standard identifying pattern.
  • \(f\)-block: last electron enters an \(f\)-orbital; includes lanthanoids and actinoids.
  • To find block from atomic number, write configuration and see where the last electron enters.
  • To find period, identify highest \(n\). To find group, inspect valence-shell pattern.

    Atomic radius and ionic radius
  • Atomic radius generally decreases across a period and increases down a group.
  • Cause: across a period, effective nuclear charge increases while shell number remains the same.
  • Ionic radius depends on charge as well as shell count.
  • Cations are smaller than their parent atoms; anions are larger than their parent atoms.
  • Isoelectronic series rule: for same number of electrons, radius decreases as nuclear charge increases.
  • This rule is central for ordering \(O^{2-}, F^-, Na^+, Mg^{2+}, Al^{3+}\) type species.

    Variation in inner-transition size and lanthanoid contraction
  • Lanthanoid contraction is the gradual decrease in atomic / ionic size from La to Lu.
  • Main cause: poor shielding by \(4f\)-electrons.
  • Key consequences:
    • \(Zr\) and \(Hf\) have nearly the same radii.
    • Separation of lanthanoids is difficult because sizes are very similar.
    • Basicity of lanthanoid hydroxides decreases from La to Lu.
    • Covalent character of compounds tends to increase across the series.

    Ionisation enthalpy
  • Ionisation enthalpy is the energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated gaseous atom.
  • General trend: increases across a period, decreases down a group.
  • Important exceptions:
    • \(IE_1(Be) > IE_1(B)\) because \(Be\) has stable filled \(2s^2\).
    • \(IE_1(N) > IE_1(O)\) because \(N\) has stable half-filled \(2p^3\).
  • Successive ionisation energies always increase.
  • A very large jump after removal of \(n\) electrons indicates \(n\) valence electrons.

    Electron gain enthalpy and electronegativity
  • Electron gain enthalpy becomes generally more negative across a period and less negative down a group.
  • Important exceptions:
    • \(Cl\) has more negative electron gain enthalpy than \(F\).
    • \(S\) has more negative electron gain enthalpy than \(P\).
    • Noble gases usually have positive electron gain enthalpy.
  • Electronegativity increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Metallic character shows the reverse trend of electronegativity in broad terms.

    Valence, oxidation states, and periodic reactivity
  • Group number often helps predict common valence / oxidation-state behavior for representative elements.
  • Across a period, metallic character decreases and nonmetallic character increases.
  • Highly metallic elements lose electrons more easily and form cations readily.
  • Highly electronegative nonmetals gain electrons more readily and form anions or covalent bonds.
  • Alkali metals are highly reactive because of low ionisation enthalpy.
  • Noble gases are least reactive because of very stable valence-shell configurations.

    Anomalous second-period behavior and diagonal relationship
  • Second-period elements often differ from their heavier congeners because of small size, high ionisation enthalpy, and no available \(d\)-orbitals.
  • Diagonal relationship arises because diagonally adjacent elements can have similar charge/radius ratios.
  • Common examples:
    • \(Li\) resembles \(Mg\).
    • \(Be\) resembles \(Al\).
    • \(B\) resembles \(Si\).
  • These ideas explain many exceptions in size, oxide nature, and compound behavior.

    Oxide trends and acid-base character
  • Across a period, oxides change from basic to amphoteric to acidic.
  • \(Li_2O\) is basic, \(BeO\) is amphoteric, and nonmetal oxides like \(B_2O_3, CO_2, N_2O_3\) are acidic.
  • \(Al_2O_3\) is a classic amphoteric trap in ordering questions.
  • To solve acidic-strength questions, classify each oxide first, then rank within acidic set.

    Poor shielding and key size anomalies
  • Shielding effectiveness roughly follows \(s > p > d > f\).
  • Poor shielding by \(d\)-electrons can make post-\(d\)-block atoms smaller than expected.
  • Important memorized example: \(Ga\) is slightly smaller than \(Al\).
  • Poor shielding explains several “down the group but not larger as expected” questions.

    Fast memory list for direct-fact PYQs
  • \(He\): second most abundant element in the universe.
  • \(Ds\): atomic number \(110\).
  • \(Fr\): most electropositive in standard exam treatment.
  • \(Os\): usually taken as the densest element in exam-oriented chemistry.
  • These are straight memory marks and should be revised as a compact list.

    Solution:

    We need the increasing order of atomic radius, that is, from smallest to largest.

    1. Use periodic trend across a period

      Atomic radius generally decreases from left to right across a period because effective nuclear charge increases.

      So in Period 2:

      \(\mathrm{N > F}\)

      Hence, in increasing order:

      \(\mathrm{F < N}\)

      Also in Period 3:

      \(\mathrm{Na > Si > P}\)

      Hence, in increasing order:

      \(\mathrm{P < Si < Na}\)

    2. Now combine Period 2 and Period 3 elements

      Atomic radius generally increases down a group because new shells are added.

      So Period 3 atoms are usually larger than comparable Period 2 atoms.

      Thus:

      \(\mathrm{F}\) is smaller than \(\mathrm{N}\), and both are smaller than the larger Period 3 atoms here.

      Among \(\mathrm{P}\), \(\mathrm{Si}\), and \(\mathrm{Na}\):

      \(\mathrm{P < Si < Na}\)

      Therefore the full increasing order is:

      \(\mathrm{F < N < P < Si < Na}\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ \mathrm{F < N < P < Si < Na}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • Atomic radius depends mainly on:

      \( \text{number of shells} \)

      and

      \( \text{effective nuclear charge} \)

    • Across a period, electrons are added to the same shell, but nuclear charge increases, so the outer electrons are pulled closer.
    • Down a group, a new shell is added, so atomic size increases.
    • Therefore:

      Across a period: radius decreases

      Down a group: radius increases

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Across a period:

      Atomic radius decreases from left to right.

    • Down a group:

      Atomic radius increases from top to bottom.

    • Reason across a period:

      Increase in effective nuclear charge with nearly same shielding.

    • Reason down a group:

      Increase in number of shells and shielding effect.

    • Alkali metals like \(\mathrm{Na}\) are among the largest atoms in their period.
    • Halogens like \(\mathrm{F}\) are among the smallest atoms in their period.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Split the elements by period first.
    • For Period 2:

      \(\mathrm{F < N}\)

    • For Period 3:

      \(\mathrm{P < Si < Na}\)

    • Then remember that Period 3 atoms are generally larger than Period 2 atoms.
    • So directly:

      \(\mathrm{F < N < P < Si < Na}\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Confusing increasing order with decreasing order.
    2. Forgetting that radius decreases across a period.
    3. Thinking \(\mathrm{F}\) is larger than \(\mathrm{N}\) because fluorine is “heavier”. Atomic mass is not the deciding factor here.
    4. Mixing Period 2 and Period 3 trends without first arranging them separately.

    • \(B = 11 = \mathrm{Na}\)
    • \(C = 9 = \mathrm{F}\)
    • \(D = 7 = \mathrm{N}\)

    The question says that the ions formed have nearest inert gas configuration. So the common ions will be:

    • \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\)
    • \(\mathrm{Na^+}\)
    • \(\mathrm{F^-}\)
    • \(\mathrm{N^{3-}}\)
    • \(\mathrm{S^{2-}}\)
    1. Write the electron counts of the ions
      • \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\): \(13 - 3 = 10\) electrons
      • \(\mathrm{Na^+}\): \(11 - 1 = 10\) electrons
      • \(\mathrm{F^-}\): \(9 + 1 = 10\) electrons
      • \(\mathrm{N^{3-}}\): \(7 + 3 = 10\) electrons
      • \(\mathrm{S^{2-}}\): \(16 + 2 = 18\) electrons
    2. Compare the sizes of the \(10\)-electron species

      \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}},\ \mathrm{Na^+},\ \mathrm{F^-},\ \mathrm{N^{3-}}\) are all isoelectronic because each has \(10\) electrons.

      For an isoelectronic series, radius decreases as nuclear charge increases.

      So among these:

      \(\mathrm{N^{3-} > F^- > Na^+ > Al^{3+}}\)

      This is because all have the same number of electrons, but the nucleus with more protons pulls those electrons more strongly.

    3. Now compare \(\mathrm{S^{2-}}\)

      \(\mathrm{S^{2-}}\) has \(18\) electrons, so it has one more shell than the \(10\)-electron ions. Therefore it is larger than all of them.

      Hence the largest ion is:

      \(\mathrm{S^{2-}}\)

      So \(X = E\).

    4. Find the smallest ion

      Among the ions listed, the smallest is the one with highest nuclear charge in the \(10\)-electron isoelectronic set:

      \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\)

      So \(Y = A\).

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{X = E,\ Y = A}\)

    \(\boxed{(c)\ E,\ A}\)

    Why this method works:

    • Cations are generally smaller than their parent atoms because loss of electrons reduces electron-electron repulsion and sometimes removes the outer shell completely.
    • Anions are generally larger than their parent atoms because gain of electrons increases repulsion among electrons.
    • In an isoelectronic series, all species have the same number of electrons, so the main factor deciding size is nuclear charge.
    • Greater nuclear charge means stronger attraction on the same electron cloud, so radius becomes smaller.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Cation size: smaller than parent atom
    • Anion size: larger than parent atom
    • Isoelectronic rule: for same number of electrons, size decreases with increase in atomic number
    • Example:

      \(\mathrm{N^{3-} > O^{2-} > F^- > Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}}\)

    • More shells usually means larger ionic size.

      \(\mathrm{Al^{3+},\ Na^+,\ F^-,\ N^{3-}}\)

    • In that set, largest is lowest \(Z\), smallest is highest \(Z\):

      \(\mathrm{N^{3-} > F^- > Na^+ > Al^{3+}}\)

    • Then notice \(\mathrm{S^{2-}}\) has \(18\) electrons and an extra shell, so it is the largest overall.
    • So directly:

      largest \(=\mathrm{S^{2-}}\Rightarrow X=E\)

      smallest \(=\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\Rightarrow Y=A\)

    1. Comparing atomic sizes instead of ionic sizes.

    4. Reversing the isoelectronic trend. In an isoelectronic series, more protons means smaller size.

    Classification of Elements and Periodicity in Properties TTR

    Solution:

    We need the correct order of atomic radii of group 13 elements.

    1. First recall the general trend down a group

      Atomic radius usually increases down the group because a new shell is added at each step.

      So at first glance, we may expect:

      \(\mathrm{Al < Ga < In < Tl}\)

      or in decreasing order:

      \(\mathrm{Tl > In > Ga > Al}\)

      But group 13 shows an important exception between \(\mathrm{Al}\) and \(\mathrm{Ga}\).

    2. Why is \(\mathrm{Ga}\) smaller than expected?

      Gallium comes after the \(3d\)-series.

      The \(d\)-electrons have poor shielding effect.

      Because of this, the outer electrons in \(\mathrm{Ga}\) feel a higher effective nuclear charge than expected, so the size contracts.

      Therefore:

      \(\mathrm{Ga < Al}\)

    3. Now compare the remaining elements

      After that, size increases from \(\mathrm{Ga}\) to \(\mathrm{In}\) due to addition of a new shell.

      Thallium is still larger than indium overall, though the increase is not as large as a simple down-the-group trend might suggest because of poor shielding by inner electrons.

      So the decreasing order becomes:

      \(\mathrm{Tl > In > Al > Ga}\)

    4. Hence

      The correct option is \((d)\).

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{\mathrm{Tl > In > Al > Ga}}\)

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Down a group, atomic radius generally increases because of addition of shells.
    • This simple trend can be disturbed when inner electrons shield poorly.
    • \(d\)-electrons have poor shielding power.
    • Because of poor shielding by \(d\)-electrons, \(\mathrm{Ga}\) has a smaller radius than expected.
    • Therefore in group 13:

      \(\mathrm{Ga < Al}\)

    • This is a standard periodicity exception often asked in one-mark questions.

    Why this exception matters in group 13:

    • \(\mathrm{Al}\) has no filled \(d\)-subshell before it.
    • \(\mathrm{Ga}\) comes after the \(3d\)-series.
    • Those \(3d\)-electrons do not shield effectively.
    • So the valence electrons of \(\mathrm{Ga}\) are pulled inward more strongly.
    • Hence \(\mathrm{Ga}\) becomes slightly smaller than \(\mathrm{Al}\), even though it lies below it.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Memorize just one special fact:

      \(\mathrm{Ga < Al}\)

    • Then apply the normal down-group increase for the rest:

      \(\mathrm{Tl > In}\)

    • Combine both:

      \(\mathrm{Tl > In > Al > Ga}\)

    • That gives option \((d)\) immediately.

    1. What is electron gain enthalpy?

      Electron gain enthalpy is the enthalpy change when an isolated gaseous atom gains an electron:

      \(\mathrm{X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g)}\)

      • If energy is released, the value is negative.
      • The more negative the value, the easier the atom gains an electron.

      “More the value of electron gain enthalpy, greater is the tendency to gain electron.”

      In proper exam language for such values, this usually means:

      • more negative electron gain enthalpy, or
      • greater magnitude of negative value.

    2. NCERT/JEE trend behind this question

      This question belongs to the periodic trend of electron gain enthalpy.

      Across a period

      In general, electron gain enthalpy becomes more negative from left to right.

      Reason:

      • atomic size decreases
      • effective nuclear charge increases
      • the incoming electron is attracted more strongly

      So non-metals usually have more negative values than metals.

      Down a group

      In general, electron gain enthalpy becomes less negative down the group.

      Reason:

      • atomic size increases
      • the added electron goes farther from the nucleus
      • attraction becomes weaker

      So among halogens:

      \(\mathrm{F,\ Cl,\ Br,\ I}\)

      the general expectation is highly negative values, but the detailed order is:

      \(\mathrm{Cl > F > Br > I}\)

      in terms of magnitude of negativity.

      \(\Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{Cl}) < \Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{F}) < \Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{Br}) < \Delta_{\mathrm{eg}}H(\mathrm{I})\)

      if you compare algebraic values, because more negative means smaller numerically.

      \(\mathrm{Cl}\) has the most negative electron gain enthalpy among halogens.

      So although fluorine is smaller, the added electron experiences more crowding. Hence:

      \(\mathrm{Cl}\) has more negative electron gain enthalpy than \(\mathrm{F}\).

      This is one of the most tested exceptions.

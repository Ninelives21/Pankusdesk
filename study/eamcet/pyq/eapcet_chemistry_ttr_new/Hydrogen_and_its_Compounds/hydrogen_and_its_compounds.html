Hydrogen and its Compounds

    Hydrides: classification, oxidation state and structure
  • Oxidation state rule
    • Hydrogen as \(+1\): In most covalent compounds of hydrogen with non-metals, hydrogen has oxidation state \(+1\). Example: in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(2x-2=0\Rightarrow x=+1\).
    • Hydrogen as \(-1\): In metal/electropositive hydrides such as \(\mathrm{NaH}\), \(\mathrm{CaH_2}\), \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), and \(\mathrm{NaBH_4}\), hydrogen is present as hydride ion and has oxidation state \(-1\). Example: for \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), \((+1)+(+3)+4x=0\Rightarrow x=-1\).
    • Trap: Saline or ionic hydrides contain \(\mathrm{H^-}\), not \(\mathrm{H^+}\). Therefore, statements saying that hydrogen has \(+1\) oxidation state in saline hydrides are incorrect.
  • Ionic hydrides
    • Reaction with water: Ionic hydrides react with water to liberate hydrogen gas, not oxygen gas. \(\mathrm{NaH+H_2O\rightarrow NaOH+H_2}\).
    • Electrolysis of molten ionic hydrides: \(\mathrm{H^-}\) moves to the anode and is oxidised: \(\mathrm{2H^-\rightarrow H_2+2e^-}\). Hydrogen gas is therefore liberated at the anode, while the metal cation is reduced at the cathode.
  • Covalent hydrides: three exam categories
    • Electron-deficient: Group 13 hydrides such as \(\mathrm{B_2H_6}\) and \(\mathrm{AlH_3}\).
    • Electron-precise: Group 14 hydrides such as \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{SiH_4}\), \(\mathrm{GeH_4}\), and \(\mathrm{SnH_4}\). \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) and \(\mathrm{SiH_4}\) are standard electron-precise hydrides because the central atom forms four normal covalent bonds without electron deficiency or extra lone-pair character.
    • Electron-rich: Hydrides with lone pairs such as \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{HCl}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2S}\). The quickest test is the presence of lone pairs on the central atom after bond formation.
    • Common traps: Do not classify \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) or \(\mathrm{SiH_4}\) as electron-rich merely because they are covalent hydrides. \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) are electron-rich, not electron-deficient, because nitrogen and oxygen retain lone pairs after bonding with hydrogen.
  • Special structural and stability points
    • \(\mathrm{BeH_2}\) and \(\mathrm{MgH_2}\) are polymeric in the solid state and show significant covalent character due to the small size and high polarising power of \(\mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\). Their structures involve bridging hydrogen atoms rather than simple isolated ionic units.
    • \(\mathrm{LiH}\) is taken as unreactive to oxygen at moderate temperatures in the standard descriptive chemistry of alkali hydrides.
    • The stability of alkali metal hydrides decreases down the group: \(\mathrm{LiH} > \mathrm{NaH} > \mathrm{KH} > \mathrm{RbH} > \mathrm{CsH}\).
    • Interstitial hydrides are mainly associated with transition metals and some \(f\)-block metals, where hydrogen occupies interstitial sites in the metal lattice. \(\mathrm{LaH}\) is treated as an interstitial hydride in the given PYQ key, whereas \(\mathrm{MgH_2}\) should not be marked as an interstitial hydride.

    Hydrogen bonding and hydride-related physical trends
  • Hydrogen bonding condition
    • Molecules such as \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), and \(\mathrm{HF}\) show hydrogen bonding because hydrogen is bonded to a highly electronegative atom and lone pairs are available for intermolecular attraction.
  • Hydrogen halides: boiling point order
    • \(\mathrm{HCl}\), \(\mathrm{HBr}\), and \(\mathrm{HI}\) show increasing boiling point down the group due to stronger van der Waals forces.
    • \(\mathrm{HF}\) has the highest boiling point because of strong intermolecular hydrogen bonding.
    • Final order: \(\mathrm{HCl} < \mathrm{HBr} < \mathrm{HI} < \mathrm{HF}\).

    Water: redox behaviour and physical properties
  • Water as oxidising or reducing agent
    • Water can behave as either an oxidising agent or a reducing agent depending on the reacting species.
    • With sodium: hydrogen from water changes from \(+1\) in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) to \(0\) in \(\mathrm{H_2}\), so water is reduced.
    • With fluorine: oxygen from water changes from \(-2\) in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) to \(0\) in \(\mathrm{O_2}\), so water is oxidised.
    • Quick test: Track the atom from water that appears in the product. Formation of \(\mathrm{H_2}\) from water means reduction of water, while formation of \(\mathrm{O_2}\) from water means oxidation of water.
    • In photosynthesis, water is oxidised to oxygen. This is a useful biological redox fact: oxygen evolved in photosynthesis comes from water, while carbon dioxide is reduced to carbohydrates.
  • Maximum density of water and heavy water
    • Ordinary water has maximum density near \(3.98^\circ\mathrm{C}\), approximately \(4^\circ\mathrm{C}\).
    • Heavy water, \(\mathrm{D_2O}\), has maximum density near \(11.2^\circ\mathrm{C}\).
    • The difference is about \(7.2\ \mathrm{K}\), nearest \(7.0\ \mathrm{K}\).
    • Temperature-difference trap: For temperature differences, the numerical value is the same in \(^\circ\mathrm{C}\) and K. Do not add 273 when subtracting two temperatures.

    Hard and soft water
  • Temporary hardness
    • Temporary hardness is caused by bicarbonates of calcium and magnesium.
    • On boiling, \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2}\) gives insoluble \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\), while \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2}\) gives insoluble \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\).
    • Memory line: calcium gives carbonate, magnesium gives hydroxide.
    • Boiling reactions:
      • \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2\rightarrow CaCO_3\downarrow+CO_2+H_2O}\)
      • \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2\rightarrow Mg(OH)_2\downarrow+2CO_2}\)
  • Clark's method
    • Clark's method uses lime, \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\), to remove bicarbonate hardness by precipitation.
    • Typical clue: \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2+Ca(OH)_2\rightarrow 2CaCO_3+2H_2O}\).
  • Calgon method
    • Calgon is sodium hexametaphosphate.
    • It removes hardness by complexing or sequestering \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\), so these ions no longer cause hardness or scale formation.
    • Permanent hardness is caused mainly by chlorides and sulphates of calcium and magnesium.
    • Sodium hexametaphosphate is used for removal of permanent hardness by binding \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\).
  • Zeolite and ion-exchange methods
    • The zeolite or permutit process works by ion exchange.
    • Equation: \(\mathrm{2NaZ+Ca^{2+}\rightarrow CaZ+2Na^+}\). Here \(Z\) represents the zeolite framework.
    • The ion-exchange process is the most effective water-softening method among the listed options because it removes hardness-causing ions most completely and can produce highly softened or demineralised water.
    • Boiling removes only temporary hardness, while lime-soda and permutit processes are useful but less complete.
  • Method-identification clues
    • Boiling: bicarbonate decomposition on heating.
    • Clark's method: added \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\).
    • Calgon method: phosphate-complex formation.
    • Zeolite/ion-exchange method: \(\mathrm{NaZ}\) or zeolite.

    Hydrogen peroxide
  • Decomposition and volume strength
    • Decomposition equation: \(\mathrm{2H_2O_2\rightarrow 2H_2O+O_2}\). This equation is the basis for volume strength and \((w/v)\%\) conversion questions.
    • \(x\)-volume meaning: “\(x\)-volume” \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) means 1 volume of the solution liberates \(x\) volumes of \(\mathrm{O_2}\) at STP.
    • Example: If \(1\,\mathrm{mL}\) solution gives \(20\,\mathrm{mL}\) oxygen, the solution is \(20\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\).
    • Conversion formula: \((w/v)\%=\dfrac{17x}{56}\).
    • For \(x=20\): \((w/v)\%=\dfrac{17\times20}{56}\approx 6.06\%\).
  • Structure
    • \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) has a non-planar open-book structure.
    • The dihedral angle is about \(111.5^\circ\) in gaseous \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) and \(90.2^\circ\) in solid \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\).
    • Memory order: gas \(111.5^\circ\), solid \(90.2^\circ\).
  • Storage and decomposition
    • Hydrogen peroxide decomposes in light, in alkaline medium, and in contact with metal impurities.
    • It is stored in wax-lined plastic bottles and kept in the dark, using a cool, impurity-free, non-reactive storage condition.
  • Peroxide use clue
    • \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\), sodium peroxide, is a standard oxidising agent. In use-based matching questions, connect peroxide compounds with oxidising behaviour.

    Heavy water
  • Identity and physical constants
    • Heavy water is \(\mathrm{D_2O}\), where ordinary hydrogen is replaced by deuterium.
    • Due to isotopic mass effect, heavy water has higher physical constants than ordinary water.
    • The freezing point of heavy water at 1 atm is about \(3.8^\circ\mathrm{C}\), not \(0^\circ\mathrm{C}\). The \(0^\circ\mathrm{C}\) value belongs to ordinary water.
  • Use
    • \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) is used as a moderator in nuclear reactors.
    • In matching questions, the pair \(\mathrm{D_2O}\rightarrow\) moderator is direct and high-value.

    Standard uses and mixed memory facts
  • Direct use-pairs
    • \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\rightarrow\) oxidising agent.
    • \(\mathrm{D_2O}\rightarrow\) moderator.
    • Cs \(\rightarrow\) photoelectric cells.
    • \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\rightarrow\) antacid.
  • Reason-based use clues
    • Cesium is used in photoelectric cells because of its low ionisation enthalpy and easy electron emission.
    • \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\) is a mild base and is used as an antacid.
  • Calgon name check
    • Calgon must be remembered as sodium hexametaphosphate, not sodium hexametaphosphite.
    • Wrong metal ions such as potassium or calcium in the name are common distractors.

    Related acid basicity point from PYQ
  • Diprotic acid count
    • A diprotic acid donates exactly two ionisable protons per molecule.
    • Among citric acid, chromic acid, oxalic acid, pyrosulphuric acid, and sulphurous acid, the diprotic acids are \(\mathrm{H_2CrO_4}\), \(\mathrm{H_2C_2O_4}\), \(\mathrm{H_2S_2O_7}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2SO_3}\).
    • Citric acid is triprotic, so the count is \(4\).
  • How to count acidic hydrogens
    • In acid-basicity questions, count ionisable acidic hydrogens, not total hydrogen atoms.
    • For oxyacids, acidic hydrogens are usually attached through oxygen.
    • For carboxylic acids, the acidic hydrogen is the hydrogen of the \(\mathrm{-COOH}\) group.

    Symbols and notation used
  • Hydrogen peroxide notation
    • \(x\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\): volume strength of hydrogen peroxide; 1 volume of solution liberates \(x\) volumes of \(\mathrm{O_2}\) at STP.
    • \((w/v)\%\): grams of solute present in \(100\,\mathrm{mL}\) of solution.
    • STP: standard temperature and pressure, used while relating gas volume of \(\mathrm{O_2}\) to volume strength of \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\).
  • Water-softening notation
    • \(Z\): zeolite framework in water-softening equations such as \(\mathrm{2NaZ+Ca^{2+}\rightarrow CaZ+2Na^+}\).
    • \(\mathrm{M^{2+}}\): a general hardness-causing divalent metal ion, mainly \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\).

    Position of hydrogen and isotopes
  • Hydrogen is the first element of the periodic table with electronic configuration \(1s^1\). It resembles alkali metals because it has one valence electron, but it also resembles halogens because it can gain one electron to form the hydride ion \(\mathrm{H^-}\); therefore it is given a unique position in the periodic table.
  • The three isotopes of hydrogen are protium \((^1_1\mathrm{H})\), deuterium \((^2_1\mathrm{H}\ \text{or}\ \mathrm{D})\), and tritium \((^3_1\mathrm{H}\ \text{or}\ \mathrm{T})\). They contain 0, 1, and 2 neutrons respectively, while all have one proton.
  • Protium is the most abundant isotope of hydrogen, deuterium is present in small amount, and tritium is radioactive with half-life about \(12.33\) years. Because the percentage mass difference between hydrogen isotopes is large, isotope effect is also significant.
  • Isotopic substitution affects physical and reaction properties: for example, \(\mathrm{H_2}\) is adsorbed faster than \(\mathrm{D_2}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2}\) reacts faster than \(\mathrm{D_2}\) with chlorine.

    Dihydrogen: preparation, properties and uses
  • In the laboratory, dihydrogen is prepared by reaction of zinc with dilute acids or aqueous alkali: \(\mathrm{Zn+2H^+\rightarrow Zn^{2+}+H_2}\) and \(\mathrm{Zn+2NaOH\rightarrow Na_2ZnO_2+H_2}\).
  • Commercially, hydrogen is obtained by electrolysis of acidified or alkaline water and also as a by-product during electrolysis of brine in the manufacture of sodium hydroxide and chlorine. In brine electrolysis, hydrogen is liberated at the cathode: \(\mathrm{2H_2O+2e^-\rightarrow H_2+2OH^-}\).
  • Hydrogen is also prepared by passing steam over hydrocarbons or coke at high temperature in the presence of a catalyst. The mixture of \(\mathrm{CO+H_2}\) is called syngas, and production of syngas from coal is called coal gasification.
  • Dihydrogen is a colourless, odourless, lightest diatomic gas, poorly soluble in water and most organic solvents. It is adsorbed by metals such as platinum and palladium, a property useful in catalytic hydrogenation.
  • Hydrogen burns in air with a pale blue flame: \(\mathrm{2H_2+O_2\rightarrow 2H_2O}\). It also reduces heated metal oxides to metals, for example: \(\mathrm{CuO+H_2\rightarrow Cu+H_2O}\).
  • Hydrogen combines with suitable non-metals to form compounds such as ammonia, methane, water, and hydrogen chloride: \(\mathrm{N_2+3H_2\rightarrow 2NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{C+2H_2\rightarrow CH_4}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2+Cl_2\rightarrow 2HCl}\).
  • With highly electropositive metals, hydrogen forms hydrides: \(\mathrm{2Na+H_2\rightarrow 2NaH}\) and \(\mathrm{2Li+H_2\rightarrow 2LiH}\). In such hydrides, hydrogen has oxidation state \(-1\), which is a recurring exam distinction.
  • Important uses of hydrogen include manufacture of ammonia, methanol, hydrogenation of vegetable oils to vanaspati/ghee using Ni catalyst at about \(443\,\mathrm{K}\), conversion of coal into synthetic petroleum, rocket fuel, and fuel cells.
  • Hydrogen is considered a clean fuel because combustion gives water and large heat output. Its practical limitations are storage and transport, which require special materials or insulated systems.

    Hydrides
  • Hydrides are binary compounds of hydrogen with other elements. They are classified as ionic or saline hydrides, covalent or molecular hydrides, and metallic or non-stoichiometric hydrides.
  • Ionic or saline hydrides are mainly formed by highly electropositive s-block metals. They are crystalline, non-volatile, non-conducting in the solid state, and react vigorously with water to give hydrogen: \(\mathrm{NaH+H_2O\rightarrow NaOH+H_2}\).
  • In ionic hydrides, hydrogen exists as \(\mathrm{H^-}\), so its oxidation state is \(-1\). This explains why molten ionic hydrides liberate \(\mathrm{H_2}\) at the anode during electrolysis: \(\mathrm{2H^-\rightarrow H_2+2e^-}\).
  • In ordinary covalent compounds of hydrogen with non-metals, hydrogen usually has oxidation state \(+1\); in metal/electropositive hydrides such as \(\mathrm{NaH}\), \(\mathrm{CaH_2}\), \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), and \(\mathrm{NaBH_4}\), hydrogen has oxidation state \(-1\).
  • Covalent hydrides are formed mainly with p-block elements and are classified according to the electron count in their Lewis structures. Electron-deficient hydrides have too few electrons for a normal Lewis structure; Group 13 examples include \(\mathrm{B_2H_6}\) and \(\mathrm{AlH_3}\), and they behave as Lewis acids.
  • Electron-precise hydrides have the required number of electrons for normal covalent bonding. Group 14 hydrides such as \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{SiH_4}\), \(\mathrm{GeH_4}\), and \(\mathrm{SnH_4}\) are standard examples.
  • Electron-rich hydrides contain lone pairs in addition to bond pairs. Group 15 to 17 hydrides such as \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{HF}\), \(\mathrm{HCl}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2S}\) are electron-rich and behave as Lewis bases.
  • The most useful identification test is: incomplete octet suggests electron-deficient hydride, exact normal bonding suggests electron-precise hydride, and lone pairs on the central atom suggest electron-rich hydride.
  • Hydrides containing hydrogen bonded to highly electronegative atoms such as N, O, and F can show hydrogen bonding. This explains why \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), and \(\mathrm{HF}\) frequently appear in questions on electron-rich hydrides and physical properties.
  • Among hydrogen halides, \(\mathrm{HCl}\), \(\mathrm{HBr}\), and \(\mathrm{HI}\) show increasing boiling point down the group because of increasing van der Waals forces, but \(\mathrm{HF}\) has the highest boiling point because of strong hydrogen bonding: \(\mathrm{HCl} < \mathrm{HBr} < \mathrm{HI} < \mathrm{HF}\).
  • \(\mathrm{BeH_2}\) and \(\mathrm{MgH_2}\) are important exceptions among lighter metal hydrides: they show significant covalent character and polymeric structures. In contrast, \(\mathrm{MgH_2}\) should not be treated as a typical interstitial hydride.
  • Metallic or non-stoichiometric hydrides are formed by many \(d\)- and \(f\)-block elements. They are often hydrogen-deficient, such as \(\mathrm{LaH_{2.87}}\) and \(\mathrm{YbH_{2.55}}\), and are important in catalytic reduction, hydrogenation, and hydrogen storage.
  • Interstitial hydrides are associated mainly with transition metals and some \(f\)-block elements, where hydrogen occupies interstitial spaces in the metal lattice. \(\mathrm{LaH}\) is treated as an interstitial hydride in exam usage.
  • For alkali metal hydrides, stability decreases down the group: \(\mathrm{LiH} > \mathrm{NaH} > \mathrm{KH} > \mathrm{RbH} > \mathrm{CsH}\). \(\mathrm{LiH}\) is also noted as relatively unreactive to oxygen at moderate temperatures.

    Water: structure and physical properties
  • Water is a covalent compound in which two hydrogen atoms are bonded to one oxygen atom. Because oxygen is highly electronegative, the water molecule is polar, with partial negative charge on oxygen and partial positive charge on hydrogen.
  • Intermolecular hydrogen bonding in water occurs between the hydrogen atom of one water molecule and the oxygen atom of another. This hydrogen bonding is responsible for many unusual properties of water.
  • Ice has an open three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded structure in which each oxygen atom is associated with four hydrogen atoms: two by covalent bonds and two by hydrogen bonds. This open tetrahedral arrangement makes ice less dense than liquid water, so ice floats on water.
  • Ordinary water has maximum density near \(3.98^\circ\mathrm{C}\), approximately \(4^\circ\mathrm{C}\). This value is important in comparison questions with heavy water.
  • Heavy water \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) has maximum density near \(11.2^\circ\mathrm{C}\). Therefore, the difference between the maximum-density temperatures of \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is about \(7.2\,\mathrm{K}\), numerically the same as \(7.2^\circ\mathrm{C}\).
  • For temperature differences, conversion between Celsius and Kelvin does not change the numerical difference. This is useful when a question asks for \((x-y)\) in kelvin after giving values usually remembered in degree Celsius.

    Chemical properties of water
  • Water is amphoteric: it can act both as an acid and as a base. With ammonia, water donates a proton: \(\mathrm{H_2O+NH_3\rightleftharpoons OH^-+NH_4^+}\); with hydrogen sulphide, water accepts a proton: \(\mathrm{H_2O+H_2S\rightleftharpoons H_3O^++HS^-}\).
  • Water undergoes auto-protolysis: \(\mathrm{H_2O+H_2O\rightleftharpoons H_3O^++OH^-}\). This supports its acid-base behaviour and links water chemistry to equilibrium and ionic reactions.
  • With electropositive metals such as sodium, water liberates hydrogen gas: \(\mathrm{2H_2O+2Na\rightarrow 2NaOH+H_2}\). Here hydrogen in water changes from \(+1\) to \(0\), so water is reduced.
  • Water is oxidised to oxygen in photosynthesis: \(\mathrm{6CO_2+6H_2O\rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6+6O_2}\). The oxygen evolved in photosynthesis comes from water.
  • Water is also oxidised by fluorine: \(\mathrm{2F_2+2H_2O\rightarrow 4H^++4F^-+O_2}\). Here oxygen in water changes from \(-2\) to \(0\).
  • In redox questions involving water, track the atom from water that appears in the product. Formation of \(\mathrm{H_2}\) indicates reduction of water, while formation of \(\mathrm{O_2}\) indicates oxidation of water.
  • Water causes hydrolysis of many compounds due to its polarity and high dielectric constant. Examples include: \(\mathrm{P_4O_{10}+6H_2O\rightarrow 4H_3PO_4}\), \(\mathrm{SiCl_4+2H_2O\rightarrow SiO_2+4HCl}\), and \(\mathrm{N^{3-}+3H_2O\rightarrow NH_3+3OH^-}\).
  • Water also forms hydrated salts. It may be present as coordinated water, interstitial water, or hydrogen-bonded water, as seen in hydrated salts such as \(\mathrm{CuSO_4.5H_2O}\).

    Hard and soft water
  • Hardness of water is mainly due to dissolved \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) ions. Temporary hardness is caused by bicarbonates, while permanent hardness is caused mainly by chlorides and sulphates of calcium and magnesium.
  • Temporary hardness can be removed by boiling. Calcium bicarbonate forms calcium carbonate: \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2\rightarrow CaCO_3\downarrow+CO_2+H_2O}\).
  • Magnesium bicarbonate forms magnesium hydroxide on boiling: \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2\rightarrow Mg(OH)_2\downarrow+2CO_2}\). The exam-useful memory rule is: calcium gives carbonate, magnesium gives hydroxide.
  • Clark's method removes temporary hardness by adding lime, \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\). The key identifying reaction is: \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2+Ca(OH)_2\rightarrow 2CaCO_3+2H_2O}\).
  • Calgon is sodium hexametaphosphate, commonly represented in hardness questions through phosphate complex formation. It removes hardness by forming soluble complexes with \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\), preventing them from causing scale or soap wastage.
  • In Calgon-type equations, \(\mathrm{M^{2+}}\) represents hardness-causing ions such as \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\). A phosphate complex in the product is the direct clue for Calgon's method.
  • The zeolite or permutit method works through ion exchange: \(\mathrm{2NaZ+Ca^{2+}\rightarrow CaZ+2Na^+}\), where \(Z\) represents the zeolite framework. The presence of \(\mathrm{NaZ}\) is the direct clue for this method.
  • The ion-exchange process is the most effective water-softening method when complete removal of hardness-causing ions is required. Boiling removes only temporary hardness, whereas ion exchange can remove dissolved ions much more completely.
  • Sodium hexametaphosphate is also used for removal of permanent hardness because it complexes \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\). In name-based questions, remember: Calgon = sodium hexametaphosphate, not sodium hexametaphosphite.

    Heavy water
  • Heavy water is \(\mathrm{D_2O}\), in which ordinary hydrogen is replaced by deuterium. Because deuterium is heavier than protium, heavy water shows different physical properties from ordinary water.
  • Heavy water is obtained by electrolysis of ordinary water. Since \(\mathrm{O-H}\) bonds are broken more rapidly than \(\mathrm{O-D}\) bonds, ordinary hydrogen is liberated faster and the remaining water becomes enriched in \(\mathrm{D_2O}\).
  • Heavy water freezes at about \(3.8^\circ\mathrm{C}\), higher than ordinary water. It also has maximum density near \(11.2^\circ\mathrm{C}\), compared with about \(4^\circ\mathrm{C}\) for ordinary water.
  • Heavy water is used as a moderator in nuclear reactors, where it slows down fast neutrons. The direct match \(\mathrm{D_2O}\rightarrow\) moderator is especially important.
  • Heavy water is also used in studying reaction mechanisms involving hydrogen and as a starting material for preparing deuterium compounds.

    Hydrogen peroxide
  • Hydrogen peroxide, \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), is prepared by the action of dilute mineral acids such as \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) on metallic peroxides such as barium peroxide or sodium peroxide. It can also be prepared by electrolysis of \(50\%\) sulphuric acid followed by distillation.
  • Hydrogen peroxide is a colourless, syrupy liquid with sharp odour. It is miscible with water, alcohol, and ether.
  • In \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), the oxidation state of oxygen is \(-1\), which lies between oxygen in \(\mathrm{O_2}\) \((0)\) and oxygen in water \((-2)\). Therefore, hydrogen peroxide can act both as an oxidising agent and as a reducing agent.
  • The decomposition reaction of hydrogen peroxide is: \(\mathrm{2H_2O_2\rightarrow 2H_2O+O_2}\). This equation is the basis for volume-strength questions.
  • “\(x\)-volume” \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) means 1 volume of solution liberates \(x\) volumes of oxygen at STP. For \(x\)-volume hydrogen peroxide, the useful conversion is: \((w/v)\%=\defrac{17x}{56}\).
  • If \(1\,\mathrm{mL}\) of solution gives \(20\,\mathrm{mL}\) oxygen at STP, the solution is \(20\)-volume \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), and \((w/v)\%=\defrac{17\times20}{56}\approx 6.06\%\).
  • Hydrogen peroxide has a non-planar open-book structure. The dihedral angle is about \(111.5^\circ\) in gaseous \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) and \(90.2^\circ\) in solid \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\).

          • The term deionised water means water from which dissolved ions have been removed.

            • \((Ca^{2+})\), \((Mg^{2+})\), \((Na^+)\) as cations

          • If we pass water through only an anion exchanger, only the negative ions are removed. The positive ions still remain.
          • Therefore, to obtain truly deionised water, we must remove both types of ions.
          • Hence hard water is passed through:
            • a cation exchange resin first
            • and an anion exchange resin next
          • So the correct choice is option (d).
        • Concept used / theory behind it
          • Ion-exchange method is used for preparing demineralised water.
          • The cation exchange resin replaces metal ions such as \((Ca^{2+})\), \((Mg^{2+})\), \((Na^+)\) with \((H^+)\).
          • The anion exchange resin replaces anions such as \((Cl^-)\), \((SO_4^{2-})\), \((NO_3^-)\) with \((OH^-)\).
          • Then \((H^+)\) and \((OH^-)\) combine to form water:
            • \((H^+ + OH^- \rightarrow H_2O)\)
          • This is why the final water becomes almost free from dissolved salts.
          • This process is different from simple softening. Softening mainly removes hardness-causing ions, while deionisation removes nearly all ions.

            • Deionised water means all ions removed.

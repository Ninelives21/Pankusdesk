    Solution:

    We need the compound \(X\) in which hydrogen has oxidation state \(-1\), and the compound \(Y\) in which hydrogen has oxidation state \(+1\).

    1. Recall the basic oxidation-state rule for hydrogen
      • In most compounds, hydrogen has oxidation state \(+1\).
      • In metal hydrides, hydrogen has oxidation state \(-1\).

      This is the key NCERT/JEE idea being tested.

    2. Check \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\)

      Let oxidation state of each hydrogen be \(x\).

      For neutral \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\):

      \((+1) + (+3) + 4x = 0\)

      \(4 + 4x = 0\)

      \(x = -1\)

      So in \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), hydrogen is \(-1\).

    3. Check \(\mathrm{H_2O}\)

      Let oxidation state of hydrogen be \(x\).

      For neutral \(\mathrm{H_2O}\):

      \(2x + (-2) = 0\)

      \(2x = 2\)

      \(x = +1\)

      So in \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), hydrogen is \(+1\).

    4. Therefore

      \(X = \mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) and \(Y = \mathrm{H_2O}\)

      Hence the correct option is \((a)\).

    Why the other options fail:

    • \((b)\ \mathrm{NH_3,\ NaH}\)

      In \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), hydrogen is \(+1\), not \(-1\).

      In \(\mathrm{NaH}\), hydrogen is \(-1\), not \(+1\).

      So the order is wrong.

    • \((c)\ \mathrm{CH_4,\ H_2O}\)

      In \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), hydrogen is \(+1\), because hydrogen attached to a non-metal generally has \(+1\).

      So \(X\) is not correct.

    • \((d)\ \mathrm{H_2S,\ NaBH_4}\)

      In \(\mathrm{H_2S}\), hydrogen is \(+1\), not \(-1\).

      In \(\mathrm{NaBH_4}\), hydrogen is \(-1\), not \(+1\).

      Again the order is wrong.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Hydrogen usually shows two common oxidation states:

      \(+1\) and \(-1\)

    • Hydrogen is \(+1\) when bonded to more electronegative non-metals such as:

      \(\mathrm{O,\ N,\ S,\ Cl}\)

      Examples: \(\mathrm{H_2O,\ NH_3,\ HCl,\ H_2S}\)

    • Hydrogen is \(-1\) in ionic or covalent hydrides of electropositive elements / metals.

      Examples: \(\mathrm{NaH,\ CaH_2,\ LiAlH_4,\ NaBH_4}\)

    • In oxidation-number questions, first identify:

      whether hydrogen is bonded to a metal/electropositive element or to a non-metal.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Do not start calculating every option in full.
    • First use the shortcut:

      Metal hydride \(\Rightarrow\) \(H = -1\)

      Non-metal hydride / common covalent compound \(\Rightarrow\) \(H = +1\)

    • So immediately:

      \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) behaves as a hydride \(\Rightarrow H = -1\)

      \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is a normal covalent compound \(\Rightarrow H = +1\)

    • Hence \((a)\) can be identified very quickly.
    • Use full oxidation-number calculation only to confirm if needed.

    Solution:

    1. Identify reaction A

      \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2 \longrightarrow Mg(OH)_2 \downarrow + 2CO_2 \uparrow}\)

      This is removal of temporary hardness by boiling. On heating, magnesium bicarbonate decomposes and insoluble \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\) separates out.

      So, A \(\rightarrow\) III.

    2. Identify reaction B

      \(\mathrm{M^{2+} + Na_4P_6O_{18}^{2-} \longrightarrow [Na_2MP_6O_{18}]^{2-} + 2Na^+}\)

      Here \(\mathrm{M^{2+}}\) represents hardness-causing ions like \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\). They are converted into a soluble complex by sodium hexametaphosphate, called Calgon.

      So, B \(\rightarrow\) IV.

    3. Identify reaction C

      \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2 + Ca(OH)_2 \longrightarrow 2CaCO_3 \downarrow + 2H_2O}\)

      Addition of \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\) removes bicarbonate hardness by precipitating \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\). This is Clark's method.

      So, C \(\rightarrow\) I.

    4. Identify reaction D

      \(\mathrm{2NaZ + Ca^{2+}(aq) \longrightarrow 2Na^+ + CaZ}\)

      \(\mathrm{(Z = Zeolite)}\)

      Sodium zeolite exchanges its \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) ions with hardness-producing \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) ions. This is the ion exchange method.

      So, D \(\rightarrow\) II.

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{\text{A-III, B-IV, C-I, D-II}}\)

    \(\boxed{(a)}\)

    Why this question is easy once the methods are recognized:

    • Boiling removes temporary hardness due to bicarbonates.
    • Clark's method uses \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\) to precipitate bicarbonate hardness.
    • Calgon's method does not precipitate ions; it forms soluble complexes.
    • Ion exchange / zeolite method works by exchange of ions like \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) with \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\).

    JEE / NCERT theory you should know:

    • Hard water: Water that does not easily form lather with soap due to dissolved \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) salts.
    • Temporary hardness: Caused by bicarbonates such as \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2}\). It can be removed by boiling.
    • Permanent hardness: Caused by chlorides and sulphates of calcium and magnesium; it is not removed by ordinary boiling.
    • Clark's method: Lime \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\) is added to remove temporary hardness.

      \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2 + Ca(OH)_2 \longrightarrow 2CaCO_3 \downarrow + 2H_2O}\)

      \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2 + 2Ca(OH)_2 \longrightarrow Mg(OH)_2 \downarrow + 2CaCO_3 \downarrow + 2H_2O}\)

    • Calgon method: Sodium hexametaphosphate \(\mathrm{Na_2[Na_4(PO_3)_6]}\) or written in simplified form using \(\mathrm{Na_4P_6O_{18}^{2-}}\), complexes \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\), preventing them from causing hardness.
    • Zeolite / ion exchange method:

      \(\mathrm{2NaZ + Ca^{2+} \longrightarrow CaZ + 2Na^+}\)

      \(\mathrm{2NaZ + Mg^{2+} \longrightarrow MgZ + 2Na^+}\)

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Look for the keyword bicarbonate + heating \(\rightarrow\) boiling.
    • Look for \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\) added externally \(\rightarrow\) Clark's method.
    • Look for a complex ion formed with phosphate species \(\rightarrow\) Calgon method.
    • Look for \(\mathrm{NaZ}\) or zeolite \(\rightarrow\) ion exchange.
    • In matching questions, once two pairs are clear, eliminate the remaining options quickly.

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Confusing Clark's method with boiling because both remove temporary hardness.
    2. Thinking Calgon precipitates hardness ions; it actually forms soluble complexes.
    3. Missing the clue \(\mathrm{Z = Zeolite}\), which directly points to ion exchange.
    4. Assuming all hardness-removal methods work by precipitation; some work by complex formation or exchange.

    Quick memory map:

    • Boiling \(\rightarrow\) bicarbonate decomposes
    • Clark \(\rightarrow\) lime added
    • Calgon \(\rightarrow\) complex formation
    • Ion exchange \(\rightarrow\) zeolite / resin swaps ions

    The correct answer is \((c)\ \mathrm{HCl,\ H_2S}\) because these are electron-rich hydrides.

    In electron-rich hydrides, the central atom has more electrons than the minimum required to form single covalent bonds with the surrounding atoms. This usually happens because the central atom contains one or more lone pairs.

    1. Understand the classification of hydrides

      In JEE / NCERT, covalent hydrides are commonly classified into three types on the basis of electron count around the central atom:

      • Electron-deficient hydrides: the central atom has less than an octet.
      • Electron-precise hydrides: the central atom has exactly the required number of electrons, usually a complete octet.
      • Electron-rich hydrides: the central atom has lone pairs in addition to bond pairs.
    2. Check option \((a)\ \mathrm{B_2H_6,\ AlH_3}\)

      Both \(\mathrm{B_2H_6}\) and \(\mathrm{AlH_3}\) are well-known electron-deficient hydrides.

      • Boron and aluminium do not get a complete octet in these hydrides.
      • Therefore, option \((a)\) is not correct.
    3. Check option \((d)\ \mathrm{CH_4,\ SiH_4}\)

      In \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) and \(\mathrm{SiH_4}\), the central atom forms four single covalent bonds and gets exactly a complete octet.

      • These are electron-precise hydrides.
      • So option \((d)\) is not correct.
    4. Check option \((c)\ \mathrm{HCl,\ H_2S}\)

      Now look at the lone pairs on the central atom.

      • In \(\mathrm{HCl}\), chlorine has \(7\) valence electrons. It forms one bond with hydrogen and still has \(3\) lone pairs left.
      • In \(\mathrm{H_2S}\), sulfur has \(6\) valence electrons. It forms two bonds with two hydrogen atoms and still has \(2\) lone pairs left.
      • Since the central atoms contain lone pairs in addition to bond pairs, both are electron-rich hydrides.

      Hence, the correct option is \((c)\).

    5. About option \((b)\ \mathrm{NaH,\ MgH_2}\)

      These are mainly classified as ionic / saline hydrides rather than the usual covalent electron-rich hydrides considered in this classification.

      So they are not the correct choice here.

    Why \(\mathrm{HCl}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2S}\) are electron-rich:

    • In \(\mathrm{HCl}\), chlorine has one bond pair and three lone pairs.
    • In \(\mathrm{H_2S}\), sulfur has two bond pairs and two lone pairs.
    • The presence of lone pairs on the central atom makes these hydrides electron-rich.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

      • Electron-deficient hydrides: Central atom has fewer electrons than required for a stable octet after bond formation. Example: \(\mathrm{B_2H_6,\ AlH_3}\).
      • Electron-precise hydrides: Central atom has exactly the required number of electrons for normal covalent bonding after bond formation. Example: \(\mathrm{CH_4,\ SiH_4}\).
      • Electron-rich hydrides: Central atom has lone pair(s) even after bond formation. Example: \(\mathrm{HCl,\ H_2S,\ NH_3,\ H_2O}\).
      Hydride type Typical examples
      Electron-deficient hydrides \(\mathrm{B_2H_6,\ AlH_3}\)
      Electron-precise hydrides \(\mathrm{CH_4,\ SiH_4}\)
      Electron-rich hydrides \(\mathrm{H_2O,\ NH_3,\ H_2S,\ HCl}\)
    • Electron-deficient hydrides: \(\mathrm{B_2H_6,\ AlH_3}\)
    • Electron-precise hydrides: \(\mathrm{CH_4,\ SiH_4}\)
    • Electron-rich hydrides: hydrides in which the central atom has lone pairs, such as \(\mathrm{NH_3,\ H_2O,\ HCl,\ H_2S}\)

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Check whether the central atom has lone pairs after bonding with hydrogen.
    • If lone pairs are present, the hydride is usually electron-rich.
    • If the central atom has exact octet only, it is usually electron-precise.
    • If the octet is incomplete, it is electron-deficient.
    • So in this question, seeing lone pairs on \(\mathrm{Cl}\) and \(\mathrm{S}\) is enough to quickly mark \((c)\).

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Confusing electron-rich with simply “more electronegative”.
    2. Ignoring lone pairs on the central atom.
    3. Marking \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) or \(\mathrm{SiH_4}\) as electron-rich even though they are electron-precise.
    4. Forgetting that \(\mathrm{B_2H_6}\) and \(\mathrm{AlH_3}\) are standard examples of electron-deficient hydrides.

    We are asked to find the approximate difference between the temperature of maximum density of ordinary water \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) and heavy water \(\mathrm{D_2O}\).

    1. Recall the standard values
      • Temperature of maximum density of \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) \(\approx 3.98^\circ \mathrm{C}\)
      • Temperature of maximum density of \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) \(\approx 11.2^\circ \mathrm{C}\)

      \(\Delta T = 11.2 - 3.98 = 7.22^\circ \mathrm{C}\)

    3. Convert idea to Kelvin difference

      \(7.22\ \mathrm{K}\) is nearest to \(7.0\).

    Why this works:

    • Water shows anomalous expansion, so its density does not increase uniformly on cooling.
    • Ordinary water has maximum density at about \(4^\circ \mathrm{C}\).
    • Heavy water \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) has the corresponding maximum at a higher temperature, around \(11.2^\circ \mathrm{C}\).
    • Since only the difference is required, we simply subtract the two temperatures.

    • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) shows anomalous behaviour because of hydrogen bonding.
    • Maximum density of \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) occurs near \(4^\circ \mathrm{C}\).
    • Heavy water \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) has slightly different physical properties from ordinary water because deuterium is heavier than protium.
    • Many physical properties such as melting point, boiling point, and temperature of maximum density are slightly higher for \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) than for \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).

      \(1^\circ \mathrm{C} = 1\ \mathrm{K}\)

    Solution:

    This question is from hardness of water, especially temporary hardness.

    Temporary hardness is caused by bicarbonates such as \( \mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2} \) and \( \mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2} \). On boiling, these decompose and form insoluble substances, which come out as precipitates.

    1. Decomposition of calcium bicarbonate

      \( \mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 \downarrow + CO_2 \uparrow + H_2O} \)

      So calcium bicarbonate gives \( \mathrm{CaCO_3} \), which is insoluble and precipitates out.

    2. Decomposition of magnesium bicarbonate

      On boiling, magnesium bicarbonate finally gives \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2} \) as precipitate.

      \( \mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2 \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2 \downarrow + 2CO_2 \uparrow} \)

      Water is also involved in the overall change, and the key thing to remember for exams is: magnesium bicarbonate gives \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2} \), not \( \mathrm{MgCO_3} \), on boiling.

    3. So the two precipitates are

      \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2} \) and \( \mathrm{CaCO_3} \)

      Hence the correct option is \( \boxed{(c)} \).

    Why does Mg give hydroxide but Ca gives carbonate?

    • In this chapter, you are expected to remember the standard result:

      \( \mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2 \xrightarrow{boiling} CaCO_3 \downarrow} \)

      \( \mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2 \xrightarrow{boiling} Mg(OH)_2 \downarrow} \)

    • Calcium carbonate is the stable insoluble precipitate for calcium.
    • For magnesium, the final insoluble product on boiling is taken as \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2} \).
    • This is a very standard memory-based result in questions on temporary hardness.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Temporary hardness is due to bicarbonates of calcium and magnesium.
    • Boiling removes temporary hardness because insoluble precipitates are formed.
    • \( \mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2} \) gives \( \mathrm{CaCO_3 \downarrow} \)
    • \( \mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2} \) gives \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2 \downarrow} \)
    • These solids settle out, so the dissolved hardness-causing ions are removed from water.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • See \( \mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2} \) and \( \mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2} \) → think temporary hardness.
    • On boiling:

      \( \mathrm{Ca} \rightarrow \mathrm{CaCO_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{Mg} \rightarrow \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2} \)

    • So directly mark:

      \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2,\ CaCO_3} \)

    • Easy memory line:

      Calcium gives carbonate, magnesium gives hydroxide.

    Final exam-style answer:

    On boiling, temporary hardness caused by bicarbonates is removed because insoluble precipitates are formed.

    \( \mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 \downarrow + CO_2 + H_2O} \)

    \( \mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2 \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2 \downarrow + 2CO_2} \)

    Therefore, the precipitates formed are \( \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2} \) and \( \mathrm{CaCO_3} \).

    \(\boxed{(c)\ \mathrm{Mg(OH)_2,\ CaCO_3}}\)

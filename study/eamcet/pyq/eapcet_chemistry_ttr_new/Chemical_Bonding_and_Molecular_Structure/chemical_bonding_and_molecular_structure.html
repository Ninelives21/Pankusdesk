Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

    Exam pattern map
  • Most repeated cluster: shape / hybridisation / lone-pair questions via VSEPR.
  • Second major cluster: MOT bond order, magnetism, existence / non-existence.
  • Then: dipole moment / polarity / bond angle order based on symmetry + lone pairs.
  • Also repeated: Fajans rule, Lewis acidity, formal charge, hydrogen bonding, \(\sigma:\pi\) counting.

    VSEPR solve-fast map
  • 2 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp\): \(BeCl_2,\ CO_2,\ XeF_2\) molecular shape often linear.
  • 3 domains \(\rightarrow sp^2\): \(BF_3,\ SO_3\) trigonal planar; \(SO_2,\ O_3,\ SnCl_2\) bent if one lone pair.
  • 4 domains \(\rightarrow sp^3\): \(CH_4\) tetrahedral, \(NH_3\) trigonal pyramidal, \(H_2O\) bent.
  • 5 domains \(\rightarrow sp^3d\): \(PCl_5\) trigonal bipyramidal, \(SF_4\) see-saw, \(ClF_3\) T-shaped, \(XeF_2 / I_3^-\) linear.
  • 6 domains \(\rightarrow sp^3d^2\): \(SF_6\) octahedral, \(BrF_5\) square pyramidal, \(XeF_4\) square planar.
  • If shape is asked, first count total electron domains, then subtract lone pairs.

    Lone-pair quick rules
  • \(ClF_3\): 2 lone pairs on central atom.
  • \(NF_3\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(SF_4\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(XeF_4\): 2 lone pairs.
  • \(BrF_5\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(XeF_6\): 1 lone pair in textbook VSEPR treatment.
  • \(NH_3\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(H_2O\): 2 lone pairs.
  • \(SO_2\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(O_3\): 1 lone pair on central atom.
  • \(BrF_3\): 2 lone pairs.
  • \(I_3^-\): 3 lone pairs on central atom.
  • If question asks “no lone pair”, test usual set: \(HgCl_2,\ SO_3,\ SF_6,\ BeCl_2,\ BF_3,\ PCl_5\).

    MOT quick sheet
  • \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\)
  • Higher bond order \(\Rightarrow\) shorter bond, greater bond enthalpy, usually lower bond length.
  • Paramagnetic \(\Rightarrow\) unpaired electrons present; diamagnetic \(\Rightarrow\) all electrons paired.
  • \(O_2: 2\), \(O_2^+: 2.5\), \(O_2^-: 1.5\), \(O_2^{2-}: 1\), \(O_2^{2+}: 3\).
  • \(B_2: 1\), \(C_2: 2\), \(N_2: 3\), \(He_2: 0\).
  • If species has bond order \(0\), treat it as non-existent in MOT questions.

    Dipole / polarity / symmetry rules
  • Do not judge polarity from bond polarity alone; shape + cancellation decide final dipole moment.
  • Common zero-dipole symmetric molecules: \(CO_2,\ BF_3,\ CCl_4,\ CS_2,\ CH_4,\ 1,4\)-dichlorobenzene.
  • Bent / pyramidal molecules are often polar: \(H_2O,\ NH_3,\ NF_3,\ H_2S,\ SO_2,\ O_3\).
  • For similar central atoms, lone-pair repulsion distorts angle and affects dipole.
  • Symmetry can make strongly polar bonds give zero molecular dipole: \(BF_3,\ CO_2,\ CCl_4\).

    Bond-angle order shortcuts
  • Linear \(>\) trigonal planar \(>\) tetrahedral \(>\) pyramidal \(>\) bent, unless multiple-bond effects increase angle.
  • \(BeCl_2(180^\circ) > BF_3(120^\circ) > SiCl_4(109.5^\circ) > SF_6(90^\circ)\).
  • \(H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2\) is the repeatedly tested style.
  • Multiple bonds compress electron density directionally and can increase bond angle, as in \(SO_2\).
  • Lone pair–lone pair \(>\) lone pair–bond pair \(>\) bond pair–bond pair repulsion.

    Hydrogen bonding / boiling point
  • Intermolecular H-bonding raises boiling point and often melting point.
  • \(H_2O\) shows extensive 3D H-bonding network, so boiling point exceeds \(HF\) and \(NH_3\).
  • \(HF\) has much higher boiling point than other hydrogen halides due to H-bonding.
  • Intramolecular H-bonding lowers association between molecules, often lowering melting / boiling point.
  • \(o\)-hydroxybenzaldehyde and 2-hydroxybenzoic acid are classic intramolecular H-bond examples.

    Fajans / Lewis acid / formal charge
  • Covalent character increases with smaller cation, higher cation charge, larger anion.
  • So \(LiF\) has more covalent character than \(KF\); higher oxidation state like \(SnCl_4\) is more covalent than \(SnCl_2\).
  • \(BF_3,\ AlCl_3,\ SnCl_4,\ Ag^+\) behave as Lewis acids because they can accept electron pairs.
  • \(BF_3\) is the weakest Lewis acid among boron trihalides; order is \(BI_3 > BBr_3 > BCl_3 > BF_3\).
  • Formal charge formula: \(\text{FC} = \text{valence e}^- - \text{nonbonding e}^- - \dfrac{\text{bonding e}^-}{2}\).

    Sigma / pi / resonance counting
  • Every single bond is one \(\sigma\)-bond.
  • Double bond \(= 1\sigma + 1\pi\); triple bond \(= 1\sigma + 2\pi\).
  • Ozone Lewis formula: \(2\sigma,\ 1\pi\); total lone pairs in the molecule \(= 6\).
  • Resonance equalises bond order, so carbonate C–O bonds become equivalent and intermediate in length.
  • If \(\sigma:\pi\) ratio is asked, draw the full skeleton first, then count bond-by-bond.

    Common traps
  • Same geometry does not always mean same hybridisation.
  • Hybridisation is based on electron domains, not just molecular shape.
  • Dipole moment order is not always bond-polarity order; symmetry can cancel everything.
  • Do not forget MOT trend: removing antibonding electron increases bond order.
  • Intramolecular H-bonding reduces intermolecular attraction; do not reverse it.
  • For Fajans rule, smaller cation means more polarising power, hence more covalent character.

    Kossel-Lewis approach and octet rule
  • Chemical bonding is explained through electron transfer or electron sharing to attain stable noble-gas-like configuration.
  • Lewis symbols represent valence electrons; Lewis structures show bond pairs and lone pairs.
  • Octet rule works best for simple second-period compounds.
  • Formal charge helps compare resonance contributors: \(\text{FC} = \text{valence e}^- - \text{nonbonding e}^- - \dfrac{\text{bonding e}^-}{2}\).
  • Frequently tested Lewis-structure applications here: \(O_3\), \(CO_3^{2-}\), lone-pair counting, formal charges.
  • Limitations of octet rule: incomplete octet (\(BF_3\)), expanded octet (\(PCl_5,\ SF_6,\ XeF_4\)), odd-electron species.

    Ionic bond, lattice enthalpy, Fajans rule
  • Ionic bond forms by electron transfer followed by electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  • Formation is favoured by low ionisation enthalpy of cation former, high electron affinity of anion former, and high lattice enthalpy.
  • Lattice enthalpy rises with higher ionic charge and smaller ionic size.
  • Fajans rule explains covalent character in ionic compounds: smaller and highly charged cation + larger anion \(\Rightarrow\) greater polarisation \(\Rightarrow\) more covalent character.
  • PYQ-relevant consequences: \(LiF\) is more covalent than \(KF\); higher oxidation state chlorides like \(SnCl_4\) are more covalent than \(SnCl_2\).
  • General ionic-compound properties: high melting point, brittleness, conductivity in molten / aqueous state, often water solubility.

    Bond parameters
  • Bond length decreases as bond order increases.
  • Bond enthalpy generally increases with bond order.
  • Bond angle depends on geometry, lone-pair repulsion, and sometimes multiple-bond character.
  • Resonance equalises bond lengths, as in \(CO_3^{2-}\) and \(O_3\).
  • Dipole moment depends on both bond polarity and molecular geometry.
  • High-yield comparisons repeatedly asked: bond angle order, dipole moment order, bond length order, bond order order.

    VSEPR theory
  • Electron pairs around central atom repel and arrange to minimise repulsion.
  • Repulsion strength: lone pair–lone pair \(>\) lone pair–bond pair \(>\) bond pair–bond pair.
  • Always distinguish electron-pair geometry from molecular shape.
  • Key textbook mappings:
    • \(AX_2\): linear
    • \(AX_3\): trigonal planar
    • \(AX_2E\): bent
    • \(AX_4\): tetrahedral
    • \(AX_3E\): trigonal pyramidal
    • \(AX_2E_2\): bent
    • \(AX_5\): trigonal bipyramidal
    • \(AX_4E\): see-saw
    • \(AX_3E_2\): T-shaped
    • \(AX_2E_3\): linear
    • \(AX_6\): octahedral
    • \(AX_5E\): square pyramidal
    • \(AX_4E_2\): square planar

    Hybridisation and standard shapes
  • \(sp\): \(BeCl_2,\ CO_2,\ XeF_2,\ I_3^-\) type arrangements.
  • \(sp^2\): \(BF_3,\ SO_3\); bent \(sp^2\) examples include \(SO_2,\ O_3\).
  • \(sp^3\): \(CH_4,\ NH_3,\ H_2O\).
  • \(sp^3d\): \(PCl_5,\ SF_4,\ ClF_3,\ XeF_2,\ I_3^-\).
  • \(sp^3d^2\): \(SF_6,\ BrF_5,\ XeF_4,\ XeOF_4\).
  • PYQ favorite: same shape does not always mean same hybridisation, and adding/removing a lone pair can change shape sharply.

    Orbital overlap and sigma-pi bonds
  • \(\sigma\)-bond forms by head-on overlap; \(\pi\)-bond forms by sidewise overlap.
  • \(\sigma\)-bonds are stronger than \(\pi\)-bonds due to greater overlap.
  • Single bond \(= 1\sigma\), double bond \(= 1\sigma + 1\pi\), triple bond \(= 1\sigma + 2\pi\).
  • \(\sigma:\pi\) counting questions are best solved only after drawing the whole structure.
  • Ozone and carbonate are standard resonance systems where counting and equivalent bond order both matter.

    Valence bond theory and covalent bond formation
  • Covalent bond forms by overlap of half-filled atomic orbitals having electrons of opposite spins.
  • Greater overlap \(\Rightarrow\) stronger bond and usually shorter bond.
  • Directional character of covalent bonds explains molecular shapes and fixed bond angles.
  • Factors favouring covalent bond formation include comparable electronegativities and effective orbital overlap.

    Molecular orbital theory
  • MOs form by linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO).
  • Conditions for AO combination: comparable energies, proper symmetry, and effective overlap.
  • Bonding MO has lower energy; antibonding MO has higher energy.
  • \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\)
  • Bond order \(>0\) implies existence; bond order \(=0\) implies non-existence in textbook MOT questions.
  • Paramagnetism arises from unpaired electrons in molecular orbitals.
  • High-value species to memorise: \(He_2,\ B_2,\ C_2,\ N_2,\ O_2,\ O_2^\pm,\ O_2^{2\pm},\ CN^-,\ NO^+\).

    MOT memory list
  • \(He_2\): bond order \(0\), does not exist.
  • \(B_2\): bond order \(1\), paramagnetic.
  • \(C_2\): bond order \(2\), diamagnetic.
  • \(N_2\): bond order \(3\), diamagnetic.
  • \(O_2\): bond order \(2\), paramagnetic.
  • \(O_2^+\): bond order \(2.5\), paramagnetic.
  • \(O_2^-\): bond order \(1.5\), paramagnetic.
  • \(O_2^{2-}\): bond order \(1\), diamagnetic.
  • \(O_2^{2+}\): bond order \(3\), diamagnetic.

    Polarity and dipole moment
  • Bond polarity depends on electronegativity difference.
  • Molecular polarity depends on vector sum of bond dipoles.
  • Symmetric molecules often have zero dipole moment despite polar bonds.
  • Repeated PYQ set with zero dipole: \(CO_2,\ BF_3,\ CCl_4,\ CS_2,\ 1,4\)-dichlorobenzene.
  • Polar examples from this chapter row: \(NH_3,\ NF_3,\ H_2O,\ H_2S,\ SO_2,\ O_3,\ CHCl_3\).

    Lewis acid-base and coordinate bond
  • Lewis acid accepts electron pair; Lewis base donates electron pair.
  • Coordinate bond is formed when both shared electrons come from the same atom.
  • \(BF_3,\ AlCl_3,\ SnCl_4,\ Ag^+\) are standard Lewis acids.
  • \(NH_3,\ H_2O,\ OH^-\) are standard Lewis bases.
  • Boron compounds are frequently tested because electron deficiency drives Lewis acidity and adduct formation.

    Hydrogen bonding
  • Occurs when H attached to highly electronegative atom (\(F,\ O,\ N\)) interacts with lone pair on another electronegative atom.
  • Intermolecular H-bonding increases association, boiling point, viscosity, and often melting point.
  • Intramolecular H-bonding reduces intermolecular attraction and can lower melting / boiling point.
  • Core examples: \(H_2O,\ HF,\ NH_3\) intermolecular; \(o\)-hydroxybenzaldehyde, salicylic acid intramolecular.
  • This row repeatedly links hydrogen bonding to boiling-point order and ortho/para property differences.

    Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure TTR

    Solution:

    We need molecules in which the central atom is \(sp^2\)-hybridised and the shape is bent.

    1. Check \(\mathrm{H_2O}\)

      In \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), the central atom is oxygen.

      Oxygen has:

      • 2 bond pairs
      • 2 lone pairs

      So total electron pairs around central atom \(= 4\).

      Hence hybridisation is:

      \(sp^3\)

      Its molecular shape is bent, but the hybridisation is not \(sp^2\).

      So \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) does not satisfy the condition.

    2. Check \(\mathrm{SO_2}\)

      In \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), the central atom is sulphur.

      Around sulphur, there are:

      • 2 bonding regions
      • 1 lone pair

      So steric number \(= 3\).

      Hence hybridisation is:

      \(sp^2\)

      Electron-pair geometry is trigonal planar, but because one position is occupied by a lone pair, the molecular shape becomes bent.

      So \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) satisfies the condition.

    3. Check \(\mathrm{O_3}\)

      In ozone, the central oxygen has:

      • 2 bonding regions
      • 1 lone pair

      So steric number \(= 3\).

      Hence hybridisation is:

      \(sp^2\)

      Its electron-pair arrangement is trigonal planar, and due to one lone pair, the actual shape is bent.

      So \(\mathrm{O_3}\) also satisfies the condition.

    4. Check \(\mathrm{N_2O}\)

      \(\mathrm{N_2O}\) is a linear molecule.

      The central nitrogen is generally taken as:

      \(sp\)

      So it does not have bent geometry and does not satisfy the condition.

    Therefore:

    The correct pair is:

    \(\boxed{\mathrm{SO_2,\ O_3}}\)

    \(\boxed{(b)\ \mathrm{SO_2,\ O_3}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • The easiest way is to use steric number, that is:

      \(\text{Steric number} = \text{bond pairs + lone pairs on central atom}\)

    • If steric number \(= 3\), the hybridisation is usually:

      \(sp^2\)

    • For \(sp^2\), the electron-pair geometry is:

      trigonal planar

    • If one of the three positions is occupied by a lone pair, the molecular shape becomes:

      bent

    • That is exactly what happens in \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_3}\).

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • \(sp\) hybridisation:

      steric number \(= 2\), linear arrangement

    • \(sp^2\) hybridisation:

      steric number \(= 3\), trigonal planar electron geometry

    • \(sp^3\) hybridisation:

      steric number \(= 4\), tetrahedral electron geometry

    • Bent molecules can arise from:
      • \(sp^2\) with one lone pair, such as \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\)
      • \(sp^3\) with two lone pairs, such as \(\mathrm{H_2O}\)
    • So “bent shape” alone is not enough; the hybridisation must also be checked.

    1-minute solving trick:

    • Immediately eliminate \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), because central oxygen has:

      2 bond pairs + 2 lone pairs \(\Rightarrow sp^3\)

    • Immediately eliminate \(\mathrm{N_2O}\), because it is linear.
    • That leaves only:

      \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_3}\)

    • So the answer is directly:

      \((b)\)

    1. Thinking every bent molecule is \(sp^2\)-hybridised.

    Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure TTR

    Solution:

    1. First compare \(O_2\) and \(O_2^{2+}\) using molecular orbital theory

      For oxygen molecule, the relevant valence MO filling is:

      \(\sigma_{2s}^2\ \sigma_{2s}^{*2}\ \sigma_{2p_z}^2\ \pi_{2p_x}^2\ \pi_{2p_y}^2\ \pi_{2p_x}^{*1}\ \pi_{2p_y}^{*1}\)

      So in \(O_2\), there are two electrons in antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals.

      Bond order is:

      \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\)

      For \(O_2\):

      \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{8-4}{2}=2\)

    2. Now form \(O_2^{2+}\)

      \(O_2^{2+}\) is obtained by removing two electrons from the highest occupied molecular orbitals of \(O_2\), that is, from the antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals.

      Therefore the configuration becomes:

      \(\sigma_{2s}^2\ \sigma_{2s}^{*2}\ \sigma_{2p_z}^2\ \pi_{2p_x}^2\ \pi_{2p_y}^2\)

      Now antibonding electrons decrease by 2.

      So bond order becomes:

      \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{8-2}{2}=3\)

      Hence bond order increases from \(2\) to \(3\).

      So statement I is incorrect.

    3. Magnetic property check

      In \(O_2\), two unpaired electrons are present in \(\pi^*\) orbitals, so \(O_2\) is paramagnetic.

      In \(O_2^{2+}\), those two electrons are removed, so all electrons become paired.

      Therefore \(O_2^{2+}\) is diamagnetic.

      So magnetic property does change.

      Hence statement II is incorrect.

    4. Bond length check

      Higher bond order means stronger bond and smaller bond length.

      Since bond order changes from \(2\) in \(O_2\) to \(3\) in \(O_2^{2+}\), bond length decreases.

      Therefore statement III is correct.

    5. Now check statement IV: \(O_2^{2-}\) and \(B_2\) have same bond order

      \(O_2^{2-}\) has two extra electrons compared to \(O_2\), so both antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals become fully occupied.

      Thus for \(O_2^{2-}\):

      \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{8-6}{2}=1\)

      For \(B_2\), molecular orbital configuration gives:

      \(\sigma_{2s}^2\ \sigma_{2s}^{*2}\ \pi_{2p_x}^1\ \pi_{2p_y}^1\)

      So for \(B_2\):

      \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{2-0}{2}=1\)

      Therefore \(O_2^{2-}\) and \(B_2\) do have the same bond order.

      Hence statement IV is correct.

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(c)\ \text{III and IV only}}\)

    Why this works:

    • Molecular orbital theory determines bond order from bonding and antibonding electrons:

      \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\)

    • Removing electrons from antibonding orbitals increases bond order.
    • Increasing bond order generally causes:
      • bond strength to increase,
      • bond length to decrease.
    • Unpaired electrons decide magnetic behaviour:
      • unpaired electrons \(\Rightarrow\) paramagnetic
      • all paired electrons \(\Rightarrow\) diamagnetic

    2. \(\mathrm{BF_3}\) is non-polar.

      • Reason: three identical \(\mathrm{B-F}\) bond dipoles are symmetrically arranged and cancel out
    3. \(\mathrm{NF_3}\) is polar.

      • Reason: lone pair on \(\mathrm{N}\), so net dipole moment is not zero

      • Reason: bent geometry prevents cancellation of dipoles

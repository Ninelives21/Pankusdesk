    Energetics of Ionic Formation
  • Ionisation enthalpy is energy absorbed; electron affinity/electron gain is energy released when taken with the usual sign convention.
  • For \(\mathrm{Mg+2F\rightarrow MgF_2}\), include both ionisation enthalpies of Mg and two electron-affinity terms for fluorine.
  • Useful calculation pattern: \(\Delta H=(IE_1+IE_2)-2(EA)=737+1451-2(328)=1532\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}\).

    Fajans’ Rule and Covalent Character
  • Fajans’ rule explains why an ionic compound may show partial covalent character due to polarisation of the anion by the cation.
  • Covalent character increases when the cation is small, highly charged, and has strong polarising power.
  • Covalent character also increases when the anion is large and easily polarised.
  • Same anion: compare cations. Smaller or more highly charged cation gives more covalent character; hence \(\mathrm{LiF > KF}\) and \(\mathrm{VF_5}\) is more covalent than lower-valent fluorides.
  • Same cation: compare anions. Larger anion gives more covalent character; hence \(\mathrm{KF < KI}\).
  • Higher oxidation state increases polarising power; hence \(\mathrm{SnCl_4}\) is more covalent than \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\).
  • Cations with pseudo-noble-gas configuration often polarise more strongly; hence \(\mathrm{CuCl}\) is more covalent than \(\mathrm{NaCl}\).
  • Common mistake: smaller cation does not mean “more ionic”; smaller cation means stronger polarisation and therefore more covalent character.

    Ionic and Covalent Character
  • Fajans’ rule: covalent character increases with smaller cation, higher cation charge and larger/more polarizable anion.
  • Same anion: smaller or more highly charged cation gives greater covalent character; hence \(\mathrm{LiF > KF}\) in covalent character.
  • Same cation: larger anion gives greater covalent character; e.g., \(\mathrm{KF < KI}\).
  • Higher oxidation state increases polarising power; \(\mathrm{SnCl_4}\) is more covalent than \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\), and \(\mathrm{VF_5}\) is more covalent than lower-valent fluorides in the given set.
  • Cations with pseudo-noble-gas configuration can increase covalent character; \(\mathrm{CuCl}\) is more covalent than \(\mathrm{NaCl}\).
  • Halogen + alkali metal generally forms ionic compound by electron transfer.

    Bond Parameters and Bond Angle
  • Bond length is inversely related to bond order: higher bond order means shorter and stronger bond.
  • For C–O bonds: \(\mathrm{CO}\) has bond order \(3\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) has bond order \(2\), and \(\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}\) has average bond order \(\frac{4}{3}\); therefore \(\mathrm{CO < CO_2 < CO_3^{2-}}\) in bond length.
  • Bond angle depends on hybridisation, lone-pair repulsion and multiple-bond effects; do not use hybridisation alone.
  • Repeated bond-angle pattern: \(\mathrm{H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2}\).
  • Another useful order: \(\mathrm{NO_2 > O_3 > H_2O}\), because \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is most compressed by two lone pairs.
  • Repulsion order: LP–LP > LP–BP > BP–BP.

    Hydrogen Bonding and Boiling Point
  • Hydrogen bonding occurs when H is attached to highly electronegative \(\mathrm{F}\), \(\mathrm{O}\), or \(\mathrm{N}\).
  • Intermolecular hydrogen bonding raises boiling point; \(\mathrm{HF}\) has abnormally high boiling point among hydrogen halides.
  • Boiling point order from hydrogen bonding: \(\mathrm{H_2O > HF > NH_3 > PH_3}\).
  • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) has an extensive 3D hydrogen-bonded network, so it exceeds HF despite HF having very strong individual H-bonds.
  • Do not apply only molar mass/dispersion trend when hydrogen bonding is present.

    Sigma, Pi and Resonance Counting
  • Single bond \(=1\sigma\); double bond \(=1\sigma+1\pi\); triple bond \(=1\sigma+2\pi\).
  • Every multiple bond has only one \(\sigma\)-bond; the extra bonds are \(\pi\)-bonds.
  • For \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\), use skeleton \(\mathrm{N\equiv C-CH_2-C\equiv N}\); total \(\sigma=6\), \(\pi=4\), so \(\sigma:\pi=3:2\).
  • Ozone Lewis formula: \(2\sigma\), \(1\pi\), total lone pairs \(=6\); LP:BP ratio \(=2:1\).
  • In resonance species, use one canonical structure for counting, but remember actual bonds become equivalent.

    VSEPR and Hybridisation Map
  • Steric number \(2 \rightarrow sp\): linear examples include \(\mathrm{BeF_2}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{CO}\).
  • Steric number \(3 \rightarrow sp^2\): \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), \(\mathrm{SO_3}\) trigonal planar; \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\), \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\) bent/angular due to one lone pair.
  • Steric number \(4 \rightarrow sp^3\): \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) tetrahedral, \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) trigonal pyramidal, \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) bent.
  • Steric number \(5 \rightarrow sp^3d\): \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\) trigonal bipyramidal, \(\mathrm{SF_4}\) see-saw, \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\) T-shaped, \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\) and \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\) linear.
  • Steric number \(6 \rightarrow sp^3d^2\): \(\mathrm{SF_6}\) octahedral, \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\) square pyramidal, \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\) square planar.
  • Same hybridisation need not mean same shape: \(\mathrm{SF_6}\) and \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\) are both \(sp^3d^2\), but their shapes are octahedral and square pyramidal respectively.
  • Same shape need not mean same hybridisation: \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\) is bent with \(sp^2\), while \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is bent with \(sp^3\).
  • In trigonal bipyramidal arrangements, lone pairs prefer equatorial positions; this explains see-saw \(\mathrm{SF_4}\), T-shaped \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\), and linear \(\mathrm{XeF_2/I_3^-}\).

    Lone Pair Quick Rules
  • \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\): 2 lone pairs on central chlorine; shape T-shaped.
  • \(\mathrm{NF_3}\): 1 lone pair on nitrogen; trigonal pyramidal and polar.
  • \(\mathrm{SF_4}\): 1 lone pair on sulfur; \(sp^3d\), see-saw.
  • \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\): 2 lone pairs on xenon; \(sp^3d^2\), square planar.
  • \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\): 1 lone pair on bromine; \(sp^3d^2\), square pyramidal.
  • \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\): 3 lone pairs on xenon; \(AX_2E_3\), linear, maximum LP–LP repulsion among common options.
  • \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\): 3 lone pairs on central iodine; linear despite five electron domains.
  • \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{PH_3}\): 1 lone pair; trigonal pyramidal type.
  • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\): 2 lone pairs; bent with smaller angle than \(\mathrm{NH_3}\).
  • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\), \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{PbCl_2}\): bent/angular due to lone pair on central atom.
  • \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{SO_3}\), \(\mathrm{SF_6}\), \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\), \(\mathrm{HgCl_2}\): no lone pair on central atom in the usual VSEPR treatment.
  • For one-lone-pair counting lists, remember: \(\mathrm{PbCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{PH_3}\), \(\mathrm{SF_4}\), \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\) each have one central lone pair; \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\) has two and \(\mathrm{BF_3}\) has zero.

    Standard Shapes to Memorise
  • \(\mathrm{SF_4}\rightarrow\) see-saw; \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\rightarrow\) T-shaped; \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\rightarrow\) square pyramidal; \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\rightarrow\) square planar.
  • \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\rightarrow\) angular; \(\mathrm{NH_3}\rightarrow\) trigonal pyramidal; \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\rightarrow\) linear; \(\mathrm{SO_3}\rightarrow\) trigonal planar.
  • \(\mathrm{XeOF_4}\rightarrow\) square pyramidal; \(\mathrm{XeO_3}\rightarrow\) pyramidal.
  • \(\mathrm{XeF_6+H_2O\rightarrow XeOF_4+2HF}\); after identifying \(\mathrm{XeOF_4}\), apply VSEPR: 5 bond regions + 1 lone pair gives square pyramidal shape.

    Dipole Moment and Polarity
  • Dipole moment is a vector quantity; final polarity depends on bond polarity, molecular shape and cancellation.
  • Symmetric molecules may be non-polar even with polar bonds: \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{CCl_4}\), \(\mathrm{SiF_4}\).
  • Common polar molecules: \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{NF_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2S}\), \(\mathrm{CHCl_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\).
  • Useful classification: \(\mathrm{NH_3,\ NF_3,\ H_2S,\ CHCl_3,\ H_2O}\) are polar; \(\mathrm{BF_3,\ CO_2,\ CH_4}\) are non-polar.
  • Important dipole order: \(\mathrm{BF_3 < NF_3 < NH_3}\); \(\mathrm{BF_3}\) is non-polar, while \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has larger dipole moment than \(\mathrm{NF_3}\).
  • Useful comparison: \(\mathrm{H_2O > HF > NH_3}\) in dipole moment; water’s bent geometry gives a slightly larger net dipole than HF.
  • Among hydrogen halides, \(\mathrm{HF}\) has the highest dipole moment in the commonly tested order; do not place HF last.
  • Dipole-induced dipole interaction occurs between one polar and one non-polar molecule, e.g., \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2}\).

    Molecular Orbital Theory: Bond Order and Magnetism
  • \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\), where \(N_b\) = bonding electrons and \(N_a\) = antibonding electrons.
  • Unpaired electrons make a species paramagnetic; all paired electrons make it diamagnetic.
  • For oxygen species: \(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}=3}\), \(\mathrm{O_2^+=2.5}\), \(\mathrm{O_2=2}\), \(\mathrm{O_2^-=1.5}\), \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}=1}\).
  • Removing electrons from antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals increases bond order; adding electrons to antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbitals decreases bond order.
  • \(\mathrm{O_2}\) is paramagnetic; \(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}}\) is diamagnetic and has shorter bond length than \(\mathrm{O_2}\); \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic.
  • \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) and \(\mathrm{B_2}\) both have bond order \(1\).
  • Highest bond order among common oxygen species \(\mathrm{O_2^+, O_2, O_2^-, O_2^{2-}}\) is \(\mathrm{O_2^+}\); the diamagnetic species among them is \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\).
  • Maximum oxygen-species bond-order difference occurs between \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}}\): \(3-1=2\).

    MOT Standard Species and Isoelectronic Shortcuts
  • Standard bond orders: \(\mathrm{He_2}=0\), \(\mathrm{B_2}=1\), \(\mathrm{C_2}=2\), \(\mathrm{N_2}=3\), \(\mathrm{O_2}=2\).
  • \(\mathrm{He_2}\) has bond order zero and is treated as non-existent/unstable in usual MOT questions.
  • \(\mathrm{C_2}\) is described as having two \(\pi\)-bonds only in the lighter-diatomic MO order; do not force a normal one-\(\sigma\), one-\(\pi\) picture.
  • Isoelectronic diatomic species generally have the same bond order in standard exam comparisons.
  • \(\mathrm{CN^-}\) and \(\mathrm{NO^+}\) each have 14 electrons and bond order \(3\).
  • Bond order \(2\) set: \(\mathrm{C_2}\), \(\mathrm{B_2^{2-}}\), \(\mathrm{N_2^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{CN^+}\), \(\mathrm{NO^-}\), \(\mathrm{O_2}\); \(\mathrm{C_2^+}\) has bond order \(1.5\).
  • For variable questions, first find the actual value of \(x\). If \(x=\text{B.O.}(\mathrm{O_2^+})=\frac52\), then \(\text{B.O.}(\mathrm{O_2^-})=\frac35x\) and \(\text{B.O.}(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}})=\frac65x\).

    Reaction-based Bonding Applications
  • Always identify the product first, then apply shape/hybridisation/MOT logic.
  • Sodium in excess oxygen forms peroxide: \(\mathrm{2Na+O_2\rightarrow Na_2O_2}\); peroxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic.
  • Potassium in excess oxygen forms superoxide: \(\mathrm{K+O_2\rightarrow KO_2}\); superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) is paramagnetic.
  • Urea hydrolysis: \(\mathrm{NH_2CONH_2+2H_2O\rightarrow (NH_4)_2CO_3}\), then \(\mathrm{(NH_4)_2CO_3\rightleftharpoons2NH_3+H_2O+CO_2}\); carbonate carbon is \(sp^2\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) carbon is \(sp\).
  • \(\mathrm{NH_3+H^+\rightarrow NH_4^+}\) changes lone pair into a bond pair but does not change nitrogen hybridisation; it remains \(sp^3\).
  • Lead nitrate decomposition gives gaseous \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\), both paramagnetic; \(\mathrm{N_2O}\) is diamagnetic in ordinary exam treatment.

    Common Pitfalls
  • Do not confuse electron-pair geometry with molecular shape; lone pairs affect shape but are not named as atoms.
  • Do not decide hybridisation from shape alone; count total electron domains.
  • Do not assume every bent molecule is \(sp^2\): \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is bent but \(sp^3\), while \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\), \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\) are bent/angular with \(sp^2\).
  • Do not assume every linear molecule is steric number 2; \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\) and \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\) are linear with steric number 5.
  • Do not judge dipole moment from electronegativity alone; symmetry and vector cancellation decide final polarity.
  • Do not forget: removing antibonding electrons increases bond order; adding antibonding electrons decreases it.
  • Do not count only central-atom lone pairs if the question asks total lone pairs in the molecule.
  • Do not reverse Fajans’ rule: smaller cation means greater polarising power and more covalent character.

    Kossel–Lewis Approach and Octet Rule
  • A chemical bond forms because the bonded arrangement has lower energy than the separated atoms. In most simple cases, atoms become stable by attaining the electronic configuration of the nearest noble gas.
  • Atoms may attain stability by losing electrons, gaining electrons or sharing electrons. This gives the three main bonding types: ionic/electrovalent bond, covalent bond and coordinate covalent bond.
  • Lewis symbols show valence electrons as dots around the element symbol. Only valence electrons normally participate in bond formation; inner shell electrons are not directly involved.
  • Please copy lewis_symbols_second_period.png into this topic’s images folder.
  • Octet rule: atoms tend to have eight electrons in the valence shell by loss, gain or sharing of electrons. Hydrogen and helium follow the duplet rule.
  • Octet rule is useful but not universal. Important exceptions are incomplete octet \((\mathrm{BeCl_2,\ BF_3})\), odd-electron molecules \((\mathrm{NO,\ NO_2})\), and expanded octet species \((\mathrm{PCl_5,\ SF_6})\).
  • For Lewis structures, first count total valence electrons, then form the skeleton, complete octets of terminal atoms, place remaining electrons on the central atom, and use multiple bonds only when needed to reduce formal charge or complete octet.

    Ionic Bond and Ionic Compounds
  • An ionic or electrovalent bond is the electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by electron transfer.
  • Ionic bond formation is favoured when the metal can lose electrons easily and the non-metal can gain electrons easily. Hence low ionisation enthalpy, high electron affinity/electron gain tendency, large lattice energy and large electronegativity difference favour ionic bonding.
  • Cation formation is favoured by low ionisation enthalpy, large atomic size, lower positive charge and stable inert gas configuration.
  • Anion formation is favoured by high electron affinity, smaller atomic size, lower negative charge and stable electronic configuration.
  • Lattice enthalpy is the enthalpy required to separate one mole of an ionic crystal into its gaseous ions. Large lattice enthalpy strongly stabilises ionic solids and can make the overall formation process exothermic.
  • Born–Haber cycle applies Hess’s law to ionic compound formation. It combines sublimation, ionisation, bond dissociation, electron gain and lattice formation steps.
  • For Born–Haber calculations, handle signs carefully: ionisation enthalpy is positive, sublimation/dissociation are usually positive, electron affinity/electron gain is energy released, and lattice formation is strongly negative.
  • For reactions such as \(\mathrm{Mg + 2F \rightarrow MgF_2}\), include both ionisation enthalpies of Mg and two electron-affinity contributions for fluorine: \(\Delta H=(IE_1+IE_2)-2(EA)\), before including lattice formation if the full cycle is being used.
  • Ionic compounds are crystalline, hard and brittle because ions are held in a regular three-dimensional lattice by strong electrostatic forces.
  • Ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points, are usually soluble in polar solvents, and conduct electricity in molten or aqueous state because ions become mobile.
  • Ionic bonds are non-directional; therefore ionic compounds generally do not show isomerism.

    Fajans’ Rule and Covalent Character
  • Fajans’ rule explains why an apparently ionic compound may show partial covalent character. A cation attracts and distorts the electron cloud of an anion; this distortion is called polarisation.
  • Covalent character increases when the cation has high polarising power: small size, high positive charge and pseudo-noble-gas configuration increase polarising power.
  • Covalent character increases when the anion is large and easily polarised. Larger anions have more diffuse electron clouds, so they are more easily distorted.
  • Same anion: compare cations. Smaller or more highly charged cation gives greater covalent character; hence \(\mathrm{LiF \gt KF}\) in covalent character.
  • Same cation: compare anions. Larger anion gives greater covalent character; hence \(\mathrm{KF \lt KI}\).
  • Higher oxidation state increases polarising power; hence \(\mathrm{SnCl_4}\) is more covalent than \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\), and \(\mathrm{VF_5}\) is more covalent than lower-valent fluorides in the given comparison.
  • Cations with pseudo-noble-gas configuration can polarise more strongly; hence \(\mathrm{CuCl}\) is more covalent than \(\mathrm{NaCl}\).
  • Common mistake: smaller cation does not mean “more ionic”; smaller cation usually means stronger polarisation and therefore greater covalent character.

    Covalent Bond, Coordinate Bond and Formal Charge
  • A covalent bond forms by sharing of one or more electron pairs between atoms. Each shared pair is a bond pair; electron pairs not involved in bonding are lone pairs.
  • Covalent compounds generally have lower melting and boiling points than ionic compounds, are poor conductors of electricity, and are often soluble in non-polar solvents.
  • A polar covalent bond forms when the shared electron pair is attracted more strongly by the more electronegative atom. Thus bond polarity depends on electronegativity difference.
  • Molecular polarity is not decided by bond polarity alone. It also depends on molecular geometry and vector cancellation of bond dipoles.
  • A coordinate covalent bond forms when both electrons of the shared pair are donated by one species. Examples include \(\mathrm{NH_3 + H^+ \rightarrow NH_4^+}\) and donor-acceptor bonding between \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) and \(\mathrm{BF_3}\).
  • Once a coordinate bond is formed, it behaves like an ordinary covalent bond; the difference lies only in the mode of formation.

  • Formal charge helps choose the better Lewis structure:
    \(\mathrm{FC = valence\ e^- - nonbonding\ e^- - \dfrac{bonding\ e^-}{2}}\)
  • Structures with smaller formal charges and minimum charge separation are generally more stable. Negative formal charge is preferably placed on the more electronegative atom.

    Bond Parameters and Resonance
  • Bond length is the internuclear distance between two bonded atoms. Bond enthalpy measures the strength of a bond.
  • Bond order is the effective number of bonds between two atoms. Higher bond order generally means shorter bond length, greater bond strength and higher bond enthalpy.
  • For C–O bonds: \(\mathrm{CO}\) has bond order \(3\), \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) has bond order \(2\), and \(\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}\) has average bond order \(\dfrac{4}{3}\). Therefore bond length increases as \(\mathrm{CO \lt CO_2 \lt CO_3^{2-}}\).
  • Resonance occurs when a molecule or ion cannot be represented accurately by a single Lewis structure. The actual structure is a resonance hybrid, not a rapidly switching mixture of canonical forms.
  • Resonance often makes equivalent bonds equal in length and gives fractional bond order. This is why all C–O bonds in \(\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}\) are equivalent.
  • In \(\mathrm{O_3}\), one canonical structure contains one single bond and one double bond; for counting in one Lewis form, it has \(2\sigma\)-bonds and \(1\pi\)-bond.
  • For ozone, total lone pairs are \(6\), and the lone pair : bond pair ratio is \(2:1\). Use one valid canonical structure for counting, but remember that the real molecule has delocalised bonding.

    Hydrogen Bonding and Intermolecular Effects
  • Hydrogen bonding is an attractive interaction between hydrogen bonded to a highly electronegative atom \((\mathrm{F,\ O,\ N})\) and a lone pair on another electronegative atom.
  • Although a hydrogen bond is weaker than a covalent bond, it is strong enough to affect boiling point, melting point, solubility, viscosity and molecular association.
  • Intermolecular hydrogen bonding occurs between different molecules. It increases association and usually raises boiling point and melting point.
  • Intramolecular hydrogen bonding occurs within the same molecule. It can reduce intermolecular association, often lowering boiling point and water solubility.
  • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) has an extensive hydrogen-bonded network, so it has a higher boiling point than \(\mathrm{HF}\), even though HF has strong individual hydrogen bonds.
  • Useful boiling point order: \(\mathrm{H_2O \gt HF \gt NH_3 \gt PH_3}\).
  • \(\mathrm{HF}\) has abnormally high boiling point among hydrogen halides due to hydrogen bonding; do not rank hydrogen halides only by molar mass.

    Valence Bond Theory and Orbital Overlap
  • Lewis structures show which atoms are connected, but they do not explain bonding using orbitals. Valence Bond Theory explains covalent bonding by overlap of atomic orbitals.
  • According to VBT, a covalent bond forms when two half-filled atomic orbitals overlap and the overlapping orbitals contain electrons of opposite spin.
  • The overlapping region has increased electron density between the two nuclei. This shared electron density attracts both nuclei and lowers the energy of the system.
  • Greater extent of orbital overlap gives a stronger bond and shorter bond length. Head-on overlap is usually stronger than sidewise overlap.
  • Since atomic orbitals are directional except \(s\)-orbitals, covalent bonds are directional. This directionality is one reason covalent molecules have definite shapes.
  • In \(\mathrm{H_2}\), two \(1s\) orbitals overlap to form a \(\sigma\)-bond. In \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\), two half-filled \(p\)-orbitals overlap head-on. In \(\mathrm{HCl}\), \(1s\) orbital of hydrogen overlaps with a half-filled \(p\)-orbital of chlorine.
  • VBT explains simple diatomic molecules well, but for polyatomic molecules with equivalent bonds, the ideas of excitation and hybridisation are needed.

    Sigma and Pi Bonds
  • A \(\sigma\)-bond forms by head-on overlap along the internuclear axis. The electron density is concentrated along the line joining the two nuclei.
  • A \(\pi\)-bond forms by sidewise overlap of parallel \(p\)-orbitals. The electron density lies above and below the internuclear axis.
  • A \(\sigma\)-bond is stronger than a \(\pi\)-bond because head-on overlap is more effective than sidewise overlap.
  • Single bond \(=1\sigma\); double bond \(=1\sigma+1\pi\); triple bond \(=1\sigma+2\pi\).
  • Every multiple bond contains only one \(\sigma\)-bond. The additional bonds are \(\pi\)-bonds.
  • For \(\mathrm{CH_2(CN)_2}\), use the skeleton \(\mathrm{N\equiv C-CH_2-C\equiv N}\). It has \(\sigma=6\), \(\pi=4\), hence \(\sigma:\pi=3:2\).
  • In resonance species, use one valid canonical structure for \(\sigma/\pi\)-counting, but remember that the actual molecule has delocalised electrons.

    Hybridisation and Shape Connection
  • Hybridisation is the mixing of atomic orbitals of comparable energy to form the same number of equivalent hybrid orbitals with definite orientation.
  • Hybridisation is needed because simple use of pure atomic orbitals may predict non-equivalent bonds, while experimentally molecules such as \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), and \(\mathrm{CH_4}\) have equivalent bonds.
  • Excitation may first create the required number of singly occupied orbitals; then these orbitals mix to form equivalent hybrid orbitals.
  • Hybridisation determines electron-pair geometry. Final molecular shape is obtained after considering how many of these positions are occupied by atoms and how many by lone pairs.
  • For exam purposes, hybridisation is usually obtained from steric number: \(2\rightarrow sp\), \(3\rightarrow sp^2\), \(4\rightarrow sp^3\), \(5\rightarrow sp^3d\), \(6\rightarrow sp^3d^2\).
  • Steric number \(=\) number of \(\sigma\)-bond regions + number of lone pairs on the central atom. Multiple bonds count as one electron region for hybridisation and VSEPR.

  • \(sp^3d^2\) hybridisation gives octahedral electron-pair geometry. \(\mathrm{SF_6}\), \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\), and \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\) show how lone pairs modify the final shape.

    VSEPR Theory and Molecular Shape
  • VSEPR theory predicts molecular shape using repulsions between electron pairs around the central atom. Electron pairs arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimise repulsion.
  • VSEPR gives shape from electron domains. A bond pair, lone pair, single bond, double bond or triple bond each occupies one region for shape prediction.
  • Repulsion order:
    \(\mathrm{LP-LP \gt LP-BP \gt BP-BP}\)
  • Electron-pair geometry includes both bond pairs and lone pairs. Molecular shape is named by considering only the positions of atoms.
  • Useful base geometries: steric number 2 = linear, 3 = trigonal planar, 4 = tetrahedral, 5 = trigonal bipyramidal, 6 = octahedral.
  • For steric number 4, \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) all have tetrahedral electron-pair geometry, but their molecular shapes are tetrahedral, trigonal pyramidal and bent respectively.
  • Bond angle decreases as the number of lone pairs increases: \(\mathrm{CH_4(109.5^\circ) \gt NH_3(107^\circ) \gt H_2O(104.5^\circ)}\).
  • For steric number 5, lone pairs prefer equatorial positions in a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement because this minimises strong repulsions.
    • \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\): \(\mathrm{AX_5}\), trigonal bipyramidal
    • \(\mathrm{SF_4}\): \(\mathrm{AX_4E}\), see-saw
    • \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\): \(\mathrm{AX_3E_2}\), T-shaped
    • \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\), \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\): \(\mathrm{AX_2E_3}\), linear
  • For steric number 6:
    • \(\mathrm{SF_6}\): \(\mathrm{AX_6}\), octahedral
    • \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\): \(\mathrm{AX_5E}\), square pyramidal
    • \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\): \(\mathrm{AX_4E_2}\), square planar
  • High-use lone-pair facts: \(\mathrm{ClF_3}\) has 2 LP, \(\mathrm{SF_4}\) has 1 LP, \(\mathrm{XeF_4}\) has 2 LP, \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\) has 3 LP, \(\mathrm{BrF_5}\) has 1 LP, \(\mathrm{NF_3}\) has 1 LP.
  • \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{SO_3}\), \(\mathrm{SF_6}\), \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\), and \(\mathrm{HgCl_2}\) have no lone pair on the central atom in the usual VSEPR treatment.
  • Do not assume every linear molecule is \(sp\): \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\) and \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\) are linear but have steric number \(5\).
  • Do not assume every bent molecule has the same hybridisation: \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is \(sp^3\), while \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\), and \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\) are \(sp^2\)-type.

    Bond Angle and Dipole Moment
  • Bond angle depends on electron-pair geometry, hybridisation, lone-pair repulsion, electronegativity of surrounding atoms and multiple-bond repulsion.
  • Lone pairs occupy more space than bond pairs, so they compress bond angles. This explains why \(\mathrm{CH_4 \gt NH_3 \gt H_2O}\) in bond angle.
  • Repeated angle order: \(\mathrm{H_2O \lt NH_3 \lt O_3 \lt SO_2}\). \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is most compressed by two lone pairs, while \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) has a larger angle due to \(sp^2\)-type arrangement and multiple-bond effects.
  • Another useful order: \(\mathrm{NO_2 \gt O_3 \gt H_2O}\).
  • Dipole moment \((\mu)\) is a vector quantity. It depends on both bond polarity and molecular geometry.
  • Symmetric molecules may have zero dipole moment even if individual bonds are polar. Examples: \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), \(\mathrm{BF_3}\), \(\mathrm{CH_4}\), \(\mathrm{CCl_4}\), and \(\mathrm{SiF_4}\).
  • Common polar molecules include \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{NF_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2S}\), \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\), and \(\mathrm{CHCl_3}\).
  • Useful order: \(\mathrm{BF_3 \lt NF_3 \lt NH_3}\). \(\mathrm{BF_3}\) is symmetric and non-polar; \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has a larger net dipole than \(\mathrm{NF_3}\) because bond-dipole and lone-pair effects combine differently.
  • Useful comparison: \(\mathrm{H_2O \gt HF \gt NH_3}\) in dipole moment. Water’s bent structure gives a large resultant dipole.
  • Among hydrogen halides, \(\mathrm{HF}\) has the highest dipole moment in the commonly tested order; do not place HF last by using only size or molar mass.
  • Dipole-induced dipole interaction occurs between a polar molecule and a non-polar molecule, e.g., \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2}\).

    Molecular Orbital Theory
  • Molecular Orbital Theory treats electrons as delocalised over the whole molecule, not confined between one pair of atoms.
  • Atomic orbitals combine by LCAO to form molecular orbitals. The number of molecular orbitals formed equals the number of atomic orbitals combined.
  • For effective combination, atomic orbitals must have comparable energy, proper symmetry and effective overlap.
  • Bonding molecular orbitals have lower energy than the original atomic orbitals and increase stability. Antibonding molecular orbitals have higher energy and reduce stability.
  • Bond order:
    \(\mathrm{Bond\ order=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}}\), where \(N_b\) = number of bonding electrons and \(N_a\) = number of antibonding electrons.
  • Higher bond order means greater stability, shorter bond length and usually greater bond enthalpy.
  • Bond order \(0\) means the molecule is not stable in the usual MOT treatment, e.g., \(\mathrm{He_2}\).
  • Magnetic nature is decided by electron pairing: species with unpaired electrons are paramagnetic; species with all electrons paired are diamagnetic.

    MOT of Common Diatomic Species
  • Standard bond orders to remember: \(\mathrm{He_2=0}\), \(\mathrm{B_2=1}\), \(\mathrm{C_2=2}\), \(\mathrm{N_2=3}\), \(\mathrm{O_2=2}\).
  • For oxygen species:
    \(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}=3,\ O_2^+=2.5,\ O_2=2,\ O_2^-=1.5,\ O_2^{2-}=1}\)
  • Adding electrons to antibonding \(\pi^*\)-orbitals decreases bond order; removing electrons from antibonding \(\pi^*\)-orbitals increases bond order.
  • \(\mathrm{O_2}\) is paramagnetic because it has two unpaired electrons. This is one of the important successes of MOT.
  • \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic; \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) and \(\mathrm{KO_2}\) are paramagnetic because superoxide contains an unpaired electron.
  • \(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}}\) is diamagnetic and has shorter bond length than \(\mathrm{O_2}\) because its bond order is higher.
  • Maximum oxygen-species bond-order difference among common ions occurs between \(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\): \(3-1=2\).
  • \(\mathrm{C_2}\) is a special exam case: in the lighter-diatomic MO order, the \(\pi 2p\) orbitals fill before \(\sigma 2p\), so \(\mathrm{C_2}\) is described as having two \(\pi\)-bonds and no \(\sigma 2p\)-bond.
  • Isoelectronic diatomic species generally have the same bond order in standard comparisons. Examples: \(\mathrm{CN^-}\) and \(\mathrm{NO^+}\) each have 14 electrons and bond order \(3\).
  • Bond order \(2\) species frequently tested include \(\mathrm{C_2}\), \(\mathrm{B_2^{2-}}\), \(\mathrm{N_2^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{CN^+}\), \(\mathrm{NO^-}\), and \(\mathrm{O_2}\); \(\mathrm{C_2^+}\) has bond order \(1.5\).
  • For variable questions, first find the actual value. If \(x=\text{B.O.}(\mathrm{O_2^+})=\dfrac{5}{2}\), then \(\text{B.O.}(\mathrm{O_2^-})=\dfrac{3}{5}x\) and \(\text{B.O.}(\mathrm{O_2^{2+}})=\dfrac{6}{5}x\).

    Reaction-based Bonding Applications
  • For reaction-linked bonding questions, first identify the actual product, then apply hybridisation, shape, magnetic nature or bond order.
  • \(\mathrm{XeF_6+H_2O\rightarrow XeOF_4+2HF}\); \(\mathrm{XeOF_4}\) has 5 bond regions and 1 lone pair, so its shape is square pyramidal.
  • \(\mathrm{XeO_3}\) has 3 bond regions and 1 lone pair, so it is pyramidal.
  • \(\mathrm{NH_2CONH_2+2H_2O\rightarrow (NH_4)_2CO_3}\), and \(\mathrm{(NH_4)_2CO_3\rightleftharpoons2NH_3+H_2O+CO_2}\). Carbon in carbonate is \(sp^2\); carbon in \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) is \(sp\).
  • \(\mathrm{NH_3+H^+\rightarrow NH_4^+}\): nitrogen remains \(sp^3\); the lone pair becomes a coordinate bond pair.
  • Sodium forms peroxide in excess oxygen: \(\mathrm{2Na+O_2\rightarrow Na_2O_2}\), containing diamagnetic \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\).
  • Potassium forms superoxide: \(\mathrm{K+O_2\rightarrow KO_2}\), containing paramagnetic \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).
  • Lead nitrate decomposition gives \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\), both paramagnetic gaseous products.

    Common Exam Pitfalls
  • Do not confuse molecular shape with electron-pair geometry.
  • Do not decide hybridisation from shape alone; count \(\sigma\)-bond regions and lone pairs on the central atom.
  • Do not assume every linear molecule is \(sp\); \(\mathrm{XeF_2}\) and \(\mathrm{I_3^-}\) are linear with steric number \(5\).
  • Do not assume every bent molecule has the same hybridisation; \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is \(sp^3\), while \(\mathrm{SO_2}\), \(\mathrm{O_3}\), and \(\mathrm{SnCl_2}\) are \(sp^2\)-type.
  • Do not rank dipole moment from electronegativity alone; check geometry and vector cancellation.
  • Do not count multiple bonds incorrectly: a double bond has one \(\sigma\) and one \(\pi\); a triple bond has one \(\sigma\) and two \(\pi\).
  • Do not treat resonance structures as rapidly interchanging real structures; the real molecule is a resonance hybrid.
  • Do not forget the MOT rule: adding antibonding electrons lowers bond order; removing antibonding electrons raises bond order.
  • Do not apply Fajans’ rule backwards: smaller/highly charged cation and larger anion increase covalent character, not ionic character.

Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

    Exam pattern map
  • Most repeated cluster: shape / hybridisation / lone-pair questions via VSEPR.
  • Second major cluster: MOT bond order, magnetism, existence / non-existence.
  • Then: dipole moment / polarity / bond angle order based on symmetry + lone pairs.
  • Also repeated: Fajans rule, Lewis acidity, formal charge, hydrogen bonding, \(\sigma:\pi\) counting.

    VSEPR solve-fast map
  • 2 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp\): \(BeCl_2,\ CO_2,\ XeF_2\) molecular shape often linear.
  • 3 domains \(\rightarrow sp^2\): \(BF_3,\ SO_3\) trigonal planar; \(SO_2,\ O_3,\ SnCl_2\) bent if one lone pair.
  • 4 domains \(\rightarrow sp^3\): \(CH_4\) tetrahedral, \(NH_3\) trigonal pyramidal, \(H_2O\) bent.
  • 5 domains \(\rightarrow sp^3d\): \(PCl_5\) trigonal bipyramidal, \(SF_4\) see-saw, \(ClF_3\) T-shaped, \(XeF_2 / I_3^-\) linear.
  • 6 domains \(\rightarrow sp^3d^2\): \(SF_6\) octahedral, \(BrF_5\) square pyramidal, \(XeF_4\) square planar.
  • If shape is asked, first count total electron domains, then subtract lone pairs.

    Lone-pair quick rules
  • \(ClF_3\): 2 lone pairs on central atom.
  • \(NF_3\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(SF_4\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(XeF_4\): 2 lone pairs.
  • \(BrF_5\): 1 lone pair.
  • \(XeF_6\): 1 lone pair in textbook VSEPR treatment.
  • If question asks “no lone pair”, test usual set: \(HgCl_2,\ SO_3,\ SF_6,\ BeCl_2,\ BF_3,\ PCl_5\).

    MOT quick sheet
  • \(\text{Bond order}=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\)
  • Higher bond order \(\Rightarrow\) shorter bond, greater bond enthalpy, usually lower bond length.
  • Paramagnetic \(\Rightarrow\) unpaired electrons present; diamagnetic \(\Rightarrow\) all electrons paired.
  • \(O_2: 2\), \(O_2^+: 2.5\), \(O_2^-: 1.5\), \(O_2^{2-}: 1\), \(O_2^{2+}: 3\).
  • \(B_2: 1\), \(C_2: 2\), \(N_2: 3\), \(He_2: 0\).
  • If species has bond order \(0\), treat it as non-existent in MOT questions.

    Dipole / polarity / symmetry rules
  • Do not judge polarity from bond polarity alone; shape + cancellation decide final dipole moment.
  • Common zero-dipole symmetric molecules: \(CO_2,\ BF_3,\ CCl_4,\ CS_2,\ CH_4,\ 1,4\)-dichlorobenzene.
  • Bent / pyramidal molecules are often polar: \(H_2O,\ NH_3,\ NF_3,\ H_2S,\ SO_2,\ O_3\).
  • For similar central atoms, lone-pair repulsion distorts angle and affects dipole.
  • Symmetry can make strongly polar bonds give zero molecular dipole: \(BF_3,\ CO_2,\ CCl_4\).

    Bond-angle order shortcuts
  • Linear \(>\) trigonal planar \(>\) tetrahedral \(>\) pyramidal \(>\) bent, unless multiple-bond effects increase angle.
  • \(BeCl_2(180^\circ) > BF_3(120^\circ) > SiCl_4(109.5^\circ) > SF_6(90^\circ)\).
  • \(H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2\) is the repeatedly tested style.
  • Multiple bonds compress electron density directionally and can increase bond angle, as in \(SO_2\).
  • Lone pair–lone pair \(>\) lone pair–bond pair \(>\) bond pair–bond pair repulsion.

    Hydrogen bonding / boiling point
  • Intermolecular H-bonding raises boiling point and often melting point.
  • \(H_2O\) shows extensive 3D H-bonding network, so boiling point exceeds \(HF\) and \(NH_3\).
  • \(HF\) has much higher boiling point than other hydrogen halides due to H-bonding.
  • Intramolecular H-bonding lowers association between molecules, often lowering melting / boiling point.
  • \(o\)-hydroxybenzaldehyde and 2-hydroxybenzoic acid are classic intramolecular H-bond examples.

    Fajans / Lewis acid / formal charge
  • Covalent character increases with smaller cation, higher cation charge, larger anion.
  • So \(LiF\) has more covalent character than \(KF\); higher oxidation state like \(SnCl_4\) is more covalent than \(SnCl_2\).
  • \(BF_3,\ AlCl_3,\ SnCl_4,\ Ag^+\) behave as Lewis acids because they can accept electron pairs.
  • \(BF_3\) is the weakest Lewis acid among boron trihalides; order is \(BI_3 > BBr_3 > BCl_3 > BF_3\).
  • Formal charge formula: \(\text{FC} = \text{valence e}^- - \text{nonbonding e}^- - \dfrac{\text{bonding e}^-}{2}\).

    Sigma / pi / resonance counting
  • Every single bond is one \(\sigma\)-bond.
  • Double bond \(= 1\sigma + 1\pi\); triple bond \(= 1\sigma + 2\pi\).
  • Ozone Lewis formula: \(2\sigma,\ 1\pi\); total lone pairs in the molecule \(= 6\).
  • Resonance equalises bond order, so carbonate C–O bonds become equivalent and intermediate in length.
  • If \(\sigma:\pi\) ratio is asked, draw the full skeleton first, then count bond-by-bond.

    Common traps
  • Same geometry does not always mean same hybridisation.
  • Hybridisation is based on electron domains, not just molecular shape.
  • Dipole moment order is not always bond-polarity order; symmetry can cancel everything.
  • Do not forget MOT trend: removing antibonding electron increases bond order.
  • Intramolecular H-bonding reduces intermolecular attraction; do not reverse it.
  • For Fajans rule, smaller cation means more polarising power, hence more covalent character.

    General introduction and Group 13 trends
  • Group 13 elements have the general valence-shell configuration \(ns^2np^1\), so \(+3\) is the expected oxidation state. Down the group, the \(ns^2\) pair becomes less available for bonding due to the inert pair effect, making \(+1\) increasingly stable.
  • The stability of the \(+1\) oxidation state increases down Group 13; a useful order is \(Tl > In > Ga\). Thallium is the key example where \(Tl^+\) is more stable than \(Tl^{3+}\), so statements calling \(Tl^+\) unstable should be rejected.
  • Atomic radius generally increases down the group, but gallium is slightly smaller than aluminium because poorly shielding \(3d\)-electrons increase effective nuclear charge. The important decreasing order is \(Tl > In > Al > Ga\), not a simple uninterrupted down-group order.
  • Boron has a strong covalent network structure, so it does not have the lowest melting point or boiling point among Group 13 elements. Do not assume that being a non-metal automatically means low melting and boiling points.
  • Gallium has an unusually low melting point, so the difference \(T_2 - T_1\), where \(T_2\) is boiling point and \(T_1\) is melting point, is maximum for \(Ga\) among the given Group 13 elements.
  • Ionisation and size trends in Group 13 often produce exception-based questions. The safest quick checks are: \(Ga < Al\) in size, \(Tl^+\) stability due to inert pair effect, and boron’s anomalous behaviour due to small size and absence of vacant \(d\)-orbitals.

    Oxides, acidic/basic nature and amphoterism
  • \(B_2O_3\) is acidic, while \(Al_2O_3\) and \(Ga_2O_3\) are amphoteric. In mixed oxide-counting questions, do not count neutral oxides like \(CO\) or amphoteric oxides as acidic.
  • In the commonly tested list, \(B_2O_3\), \(SiO_2\), and \(CO_2\) are acidic oxides, while \(CO\) is neutral. Oxides such as \(Al_2O_3\), \(Ga_2O_3\), \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\), and \(PbO_2\) are usually treated as amphoteric in such classification questions.
  • \(Al_2O_3\) is amphoteric because it reacts with both acids and bases. Therefore, it should not be marked as strongly basic in matching or statement-based questions.
  • Boron can be identified from the combination \(ns^2np^1\), black amorphous appearance, and formation of acidic \(B_2O_3\) on oxidation: \(4B + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2B_2O_3\). Aluminium and gallium do not fit this acidic oxide clue because their oxides are amphoteric.

    Boron: anomalous behaviour, covalency and Lewis acidity
  • Boron usually shows covalency 3, but it can show maximum covalency 4 by accepting a lone pair, as in \(BF_4^-\) or \([B(OH)_4]^-\). It does not expand to higher covalency like heavier elements because it lacks vacant \(d\)-orbitals.
  • Boron is electron deficient in many compounds and accepts lone pairs from Lewis bases such as \(H_2O\) or \(OH^-\). However, for EAPCET-level hydroxo species, boron is treated as forming tetrahedral \([B(OH)_4]^-\), not an octahedral hydroxo cation.
  • Boron trihalides are Lewis acids because boron has an incomplete octet. Their Lewis acidic strength follows \(BI_3 > BBr_3 > BCl_3 > BF_3\).
  • The Lewis acidity order of boron trihalides is controlled by back-bonding, not simple electronegativity. Back-bonding is strongest in \(BF_3\) because of effective \(2p-2p\) overlap, so \(BF_3\) is the weakest Lewis acid.
  • The back-bonding order is \(BF_3 > BCl_3 > BBr_3 > BI_3\), and the Lewis acidity order is the reverse. This pair is a high-yield direct-memory pattern.

    Borax, boric acid and boron minerals
  • Boric acid, \(H_3BO_3\), is a weak monobasic Lewis acid, not a tribasic acid. It does not donate three protons; instead, it accepts \(OH^-\) from water to form \([B(OH)_4]^-\).
  • Boric acid has a layered structure in the solid state. For quick revision, connect boric acid with: weak monobasic Lewis acid, tetrahydroxyborate formation, and layered solid structure.
  • Borax can be written structurally as \(Na_2[B_4O_5(OH)_4]\cdot 8H_2O\). If matched with \(Na_2[B_4O_5(OH)_x]\cdot yH_2O\), then \(x=4\), \(y=8\), and \(x+y=12\).
  • In the borax bead test, cobalt salts give a blue bead due to cobalt metaborate formation. This is a direct colour-memory point often paired with borax reactions.
  • Kernite is a boron mineral, while cryolite, \(Na_3AlF_6\), is an aluminium mineral. The quick mineral pair is: kernite \(\rightarrow\) boron and cryolite \(\rightarrow\) aluminium.

    Diborane, borohydride and borazine
  • Diborane, \(B_2H_6\), is an electron-deficient hydride with bridge hydrogens and \(3c-2e\) bonds. It should not be classified with electron-precise hydrides such as \(CH_4\).
  • Hydrogen in diborane and borohydride species is in the \(-1\) oxidation state. Do not mark hydrogen as \(+1\) in \(B_2H_6\) or \(BH_4^-\).
  • Diborane hydrolyses to boric acid with hydrogen evolution: \(B_2H_6 + 6H_2O \rightarrow 2H_3BO_3 + 6H_2\). This reaction links diborane, boric acid, and hydrogen gas evolution in one standard equation.
  • Diborane can be prepared by oxidation of sodium borohydride with iodine: \(2NaBH_4 + I_2 \rightarrow B_2H_6 + 2NaI + H_2\). This reaction also evolves hydrogen gas.
  • Hydrogen gas is evolved in \(NaBH_4 + I_2\) and diborane hydrolysis, but not in every diborane-related reaction. Oxidation of diborane gives \(B_2O_3\) and water, while reaction of \(BF_3\) with hydride sources may give diborane without free \(H_2\) depending on the reaction.
  • \(BF_3\) reacts with \(LiAlH_4\) in ether to form diborane, and diborane can react with \(NaH\) to form sodium borohydride, \(NaBH_4\). \(NaBH_4\) is a hydride donor and a good reducing agent, not a good oxidising agent.
  • Diborane undergoes Lewis-base cleavage. With \(CO\), it forms the borane carbonyl adduct \(BH_3\cdot CO\); do not write it as \(BH_3\cdot 2CO\).
  • Diborane reacts with ammonia to ultimately give borazine, \(B_3N_3H_6\). Borazine contains \(12\) \(\sigma\)-bonds and \(3\) \(\pi\)-bonds, so \(x+y=15\) when \(x\) is the number of \(\sigma\)-bonds and \(y\) is the number of \(\pi\)-bonds.
  • In borazine bond counting, include 6 ring \(\sigma\)-bonds, 3 \(B-H\) \(\sigma\)-bonds, 3 \(N-H\) \(\sigma\)-bonds, and 3 \(\pi\)-bonds. Do not count only the ring bonds or treat all ring bonds as separate double bonds.

    Aluminium compounds and reactions
  • Aluminium is amphoteric and reacts with both dilute acids and aqueous alkalis to liberate hydrogen gas. With dilute hydrochloric acid: \(2Al + 6HCl \rightarrow 2AlCl_3 + 3H_2\).
  • With excess aqueous sodium hydroxide, aluminium forms soluble tetrahydroxoaluminate and hydrogen: \(2Al + 2NaOH + 6H_2O \rightarrow 2Na^+[Al(OH)_4]^- + 3H_2\). Therefore, the gas from aluminium + aqueous \(NaOH\) is \(H_2\), not \(O_2\), \(O_3\), or \(D_2\).
  • In excess alkali, \(Al(OH)_3\) does not remain as the final product; it dissolves to form \([Al(OH)_4]^-\). This is the key elimination point in product-identification questions.
  • \(Al(OH)_3 + NaOH \rightarrow Na[Al(OH)_4]\), so aluminium has covalency 4 in the tetrahydroxoaluminate species. Similarly, \(Be(OH)_2\) in excess \(NaOH\) gives \([Be(OH)_4]^{2-}\), where beryllium also has covalency 4.
  • \(AlCl_3\) is electron deficient and gains stability by forming the dimer \(Al_2Cl_6\). This is a standard contrast with \(BCl_3\), which is also electron deficient but is commonly tested as a monomeric Lewis acid.
  • In excess water, \(AlCl_3\) gives the hydrated aluminium ion \([Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+}\) and \(3Cl^-\). Do not write only bare \(Al^{3+}+3Cl^-\) when hydration in excess water is the focus.
  • Aqueous \(AlCl_3\) is acidic because it is effectively a salt of strong acid \(HCl\) and weak base \(Al(OH)_3\). In matching questions, pair \(AlCl_3\) solution with acidic character.
  • Aluminium becomes passive in concentrated nitric acid because a protective oxide film forms on its surface. This passivation fact is often tested along with Group 13 trends and oxidation states.
  • \(E^\circ_{Al^{3+}/Al}\) is strongly negative, approximately \(-1.66\,V\), not positive. A statement giving a positive value for aluminium reduction potential should be treated as incorrect.

    Cross-linked quick matching facts
  • \(CH_3COONa\) solution is basic because it is derived from weak acid \(CH_3COOH\) and strong base \(NaOH\). \(KCl\) solution is highly conductive because it dissociates well as a strong electrolyte.
  • The standard matching pattern is: aqueous \(AlCl_3\) \(\rightarrow\) acidic, \(CH_3COONa\) \(\rightarrow\) basic, aqueous \(KCl\) \(\rightarrow\) highly conductive, \(Al_2O_3\) \(\rightarrow\) amphoteric.
  • In silicon hexahalide-type comparison questions, \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists but \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) does not, because six large chloride ions cannot be accommodated around small silicon. This is a size and steric crowding rule, not a Group 13 rule, but it appeared alongside the p-block set.

    Variables used
  • \(T_1\): melting point of the element in kelvin.
  • \(T_2\): boiling point of the element in kelvin.
  • \(E^\circ_{Al^{3+}/Al}\): standard reduction potential for the \(Al^{3+}/Al\) electrode.
  • \(x,y\): placeholders used in PYQs for counting structural groups or bonds, such as hydroxyl groups and waters of crystallisation in borax, or \(\sigma\)- and \(\pi\)-bonds in borazine.

    General introduction to Group 13
  • Group 13 contains boron, aluminium, gallium, indium and thallium. Their general outer electronic configuration is \(ns^2np^1\), so the expected common oxidation state is \(+3\).
  • Boron is a non-metal, aluminium is a metal, and metallic character increases down the group. This follows the general p-block trend: atomic size increases down a group, ionisation enthalpy decreases, and loss of electrons becomes easier.
  • Aluminium is one of the abundant p-block elements in nature and occurs mainly in combined form. Important aluminium minerals include bauxite and cryolite, while important boron minerals include borax, kernite and colemanite.

    Atomic size, ionisation enthalpy and electronegativity
  • Atomic radius generally increases down Group 13 because a new shell is added at each step. The exam-important exception is gallium: \(Ga\) is slightly smaller than \(Al\) because \(3d\)-electrons shield the nuclear charge poorly.
  • A safe decreasing order for Group 13 atomic radius in PYQ-style questions is \(Tl > In > Al > Ga > B\). Do not apply a simple uninterrupted down-group trend where \(Ga\) is placed larger than \(Al\).
  • Ionisation enthalpy is the energy needed to remove the most loosely held electron from an isolated gaseous atom. In general, it decreases down a group due to increasing atomic size, but irregularities appear because of poor shielding by inner \(d\)- and \(f\)-electrons.
  • Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom to attract a shared electron pair. Across a period it increases, while down a group it generally decreases; boron is therefore more electronegative and less metallic than the heavier Group 13 elements.
  • Gallium has an unusually low melting point and remains liquid over a wide temperature range. If \(T_2\) is boiling point and \(T_1\) is melting point, the difference \(T_2-T_1\) is maximum for gallium among the commonly compared Group 13 elements.

    Oxidation states and inert pair effect
  • The \(+3\) oxidation state is common in Group 13 because the three valence electrons, \(ns^2np^1\), can participate in bonding. Boron and aluminium mostly show \(+3\), while heavier elements increasingly show \(+1\).
  • The inert pair effect is the reluctance of the \(ns^2\) electron pair to participate in bonding. It becomes more important down the group, so the stability of the \(+1\) state increases in the order \(Ga < In < Tl\).
  • Thallium is the most important example: \(Tl^+\) is more stable than \(Tl^{3+}\). Therefore, any statement claiming that the \(+1\) state of thallium is unstable should be treated as incorrect.
  • The higher oxidation state becomes less stable down the group because promotion of \(ns^2\) electrons becomes less favourable and the larger atoms form weaker bonds. This is the practical consequence behind many inert-pair-effect questions.

    Anomalous behaviour of boron
  • Boron shows anomalous behaviour because of its very small size, high ionisation enthalpy, high electronegativity and absence of vacant \(d\)-orbitals. These factors make boron more covalent and less metallic than the rest of the group.
  • The maximum covalency of boron is 4. It can accept a lone pair to form tetrahedral species such as \(BF_4^-\) and \([B(OH)_4]^-\), but it cannot expand its octet beyond this.
  • Boron forms electron-deficient compounds because it often has only six electrons around it after forming three covalent bonds. This explains the Lewis acidic nature of boron trihalides and the special bonding in diborane.
  • Boron has a strong covalent network structure, so its melting point and boiling point are high. Do not assume that boron must have the lowest melting point simply because it is a non-metal.

    Oxides and amphoteric behaviour
  • In p-block oxides, acidic character generally increases across a period and basic character increases down a group. For Group 13, \(B_2O_3\) is acidic, while \(Al_2O_3\) and \(Ga_2O_3\) are amphoteric.
  • Boron burns in oxygen to form boron oxide: \(4B + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2B_2O_3\). The acidic nature of \(B_2O_3\) is a useful clue for identifying boron in statement-based questions.
  • \(Al_2O_3\) is amphoteric because it reacts with both acids and bases. It should not be marked as strongly basic in matching questions.
  • Mixed oxide-classification questions often combine Group 13 with Group 14 oxides. Remember: \(CO\) is neutral, \(CO_2\), \(SiO_2\) and \(B_2O_3\) are acidic, while \(Al_2O_3\), \(Ga_2O_3\), \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\) and \(PbO_2\) are amphoteric.

    Ortho boric acid, \(H_3BO_3\)
  • Ortho boric acid, \(H_3BO_3\) or \(B(OH)_3\), is prepared by treating a concentrated solution of borax with sulphuric acid: \(Na_2B_4O_7 + H_2SO_4 + 5H_2O \rightarrow Na_2SO_4 + 4H_3BO_3\).
  • Boric acid is a white crystalline solid, soluble in water. It is a weak monobasic Lewis acid, not a protonic tribasic acid.
  • Its acidity is due to acceptance of \(OH^-\) from water: \(B(OH)_3 + 2H_2O \rightarrow [B(OH)_4]^- + H_3O^+\). Therefore, the number of hydrogens in the formula must not be used to call it tribasic.
  • On heating, boric acid first gives metaboric acid and finally boric oxide: \(H_3BO_3 \xrightarrow{375K} HBO_2 + H_2O\); \(2HBO_2 \xrightarrow{\text{red heat}} B_2O_3 + H_2O\).
  • In the solid state, \(B(OH)_3\) units are linked by hydrogen bonding into two-dimensional sheets. Weak van der Waals forces between sheets explain the flaky nature of boric acid crystals.
  • Uses of boric acid include antiseptic applications, food preservation, and preparation of enamels, pottery glazes and glass.

    Borax, \(Na_2B_4O_7\cdot 10H_2O\)
  • Borax is sodium tetraborate decahydrate, \(Na_2B_4O_7\cdot 10H_2O\). In crude form it occurs as tincal in dried-up lakes and can also be prepared from colemanite using sodium carbonate.
  • For structural formula questions, borax may be written as \(Na_2[B_4O_5(OH)_4]\cdot 8H_2O\). Hence, in \(Na_2[B_4O_5(OH)_x]\cdot yH_2O\), \(x=4\), \(y=8\), and \(x+y=12\).
  • Borax loses water of crystallisation on heating. It is used as an alkaline buffer, preservative, flux, and in optical glass, borosilicate glass, pottery glazes and tiles.
  • In the borax bead test, cobalt salts give a blue bead due to cobalt metaborate formation. This is a direct memory point and should be linked with borax reactions.

    Diborane, \(B_2H_6\)
  • Diborane is the most important hydride of boron. It is prepared by the action of lithium aluminium hydride on boron trichloride: \(4BCl_3 + 3LiAlH_4 \rightarrow 2B_2H_6 + 3AlCl_3 + 3LiCl\).
  • It can also be prepared using lithium hydride and boron trifluoride: \(8BF_3 + 6LiH \rightarrow B_2H_6 + 6LiBF_4\). In PYQ reaction sequences, hydride sources such as \(LiAlH_4\), \(LiH\), \(NaH\) and \(NaBH_4\) should be tracked carefully.
  • Diborane is electron deficient. It has four normal terminal \(B-H\) bonds and two bridging \(B-H-B\) bonds, described as \(3c-2e\) bonds.
  • In \(B_2H_6\), the two boron atoms and four terminal hydrogens lie in one plane, while two bridge hydrogens lie above and below this plane. The absence of enough electrons for eight normal two-centre bonds is the reason for multicentre bonding.
  • Diborane burns in oxygen with large energy release: \(B_2H_6 + 3O_2 \rightarrow B_2O_3 + 3H_2O\). This oxidation does not produce free hydrogen gas.
  • Diborane is readily hydrolysed: \(B_2H_6 + 6H_2O \rightarrow 2H_3BO_3 + 6H_2\). This is a high-frequency equation because it connects diborane, boric acid and hydrogen gas evolution.
  • Sodium borohydride can be oxidised by iodine to diborane: \(2NaBH_4 + I_2 \rightarrow B_2H_6 + 2NaI + H_2\). Hydrogen in \(BH_4^-\) and \(B_2H_6\) is in the \(-1\) oxidation state.
  • \(NaBH_4\) is a hydride donor and reducing agent, not an oxidising agent. Diborane also undergoes Lewis-base cleavage, for example with carbon monoxide to give \(BH_3\cdot CO\).
  • Diborane reacts with ammonia to give borazine, \(B_3N_3H_6\), under suitable conditions. In borazine, count 6 ring \(\sigma\)-bonds, 3 \(B-H\) \(\sigma\)-bonds, 3 \(N-H\) \(\sigma\)-bonds and 3 \(\pi\)-bonds.

    Boron trifluoride and boron trihalides
  • Boron forms trihalides \(BX_3\), where \(X = F, Cl, Br, I\). These are electron-deficient compounds because boron has only six electrons around it after forming three covalent bonds.
  • \(BF_3\) acts as a Lewis acid because boron has an incomplete octet. It forms donor-acceptor complexes with Lewis bases such as \(NH_3\) and ethers.
  • The \(B-F\) bond in \(BF_3\) has partial multiple-bond character due to back-bonding from filled fluorine orbitals to the vacant \(p\)-orbital of boron.
  • The back-bonding order is \(BF_3 > BCl_3 > BBr_3 > BI_3\). Stronger back-bonding reduces electron deficiency on boron.
  • Therefore, Lewis acidic strength is the reverse: \(BI_3 > BBr_3 > BCl_3 > BF_3\). Do not use simple electronegativity logic to mark \(BF_3\) as the strongest Lewis acid.
  • \(BF_3\) is used as a catalyst in Friedel-Crafts alkylation, acylation and polymerisation reactions because of its Lewis acid character.

    Aluminium: reactions and compounds
  • Aluminium is an amphoteric metal. It reacts with acids as well as alkalies and commonly liberates hydrogen gas in both types of reactions.
  • With dilute hydrochloric acid: \(2Al + 6HCl \rightarrow 2AlCl_3 + 3H_2\). Therefore, aluminium with dilute acid gives \(H_2\), not \(Cl_2\).
  • With aqueous sodium hydroxide, aluminium forms aluminate or tetrahydroxoaluminate species and liberates hydrogen: \(2Al + 2NaOH + 6H_2O \rightarrow 2Na^+[Al(OH)_4]^- + 3H_2\).
  • In excess alkali, \(Al(OH)_3\) dissolves to form \([Al(OH)_4]^-\). Therefore, \(Al(OH)_3\) should not be treated as the final product when excess \(NaOH\) is given.
  • \(Al(OH)_3 + NaOH \rightarrow Na[Al(OH)_4]\), so aluminium has covalency 4 in the tetrahydroxoaluminate ion. Similar amphoteric hydroxide behaviour is seen for \(Be(OH)_2\), which forms \([Be(OH)_4]^{2-}\).
  • Aluminium becomes passive in concentrated \(HNO_3\) because a thin protective oxide layer forms on its surface and prevents further reaction.
  • \(E^\circ_{Al^{3+}/Al}\) is strongly negative, approximately \(-1.66\,V\). A positive value for this electrode potential is incorrect.

    Aluminium trichloride, \(AlCl_3\)
  • Anhydrous aluminium chloride exists as \(Al_2Cl_6\) at room temperature and as monomeric \(AlCl_3\) at high temperature. It is prepared by passing hydrogen chloride or chlorine over heated aluminium under anhydrous conditions.
  • Preparation equations: \(2Al + 6HCl \rightarrow Al_2Cl_6 + 3H_2\); \(2Al + 3Cl_2 \rightarrow Al_2Cl_6\).
  • Monomeric \(AlCl_3\) is electron deficient because aluminium has only six electrons around it after forming three covalent bonds. Dimerisation allows aluminium atoms to complete octets through chloride bridge donation.
  • In \(Al_2Cl_6\), two chlorine atoms act as bridges and the arrangement around each aluminium atom is approximately tetrahedral. This dimerisation is a standard comparison with electron-deficient boron halides.
  • In excess water, \(AlCl_3\) forms hydrated aluminium ions: \(AlCl_3 + 6H_2O \rightarrow [Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+} + 3Cl^-\). This is more accurate than writing only bare \(Al^{3+}\) in aqueous solution.
  • Aqueous \(AlCl_3\) is acidic because it behaves as a salt of strong acid \(HCl\) and weak base \(Al(OH)_3\). In matching questions, pair \(AlCl_3\) solution with acidic character.
  • Anhydrous aluminium chloride is a Lewis acid and is used as a catalyst in Friedel-Crafts reactions.

    Alums and other aluminium uses
  • Double salts are formed when two independently existing salts crystallise together, but give their constituent ions in solution. Potash alum is the important aluminium double salt.
  • Potash alum is commonly written as \(KAl(SO_4)_2\cdot 12H_2O\), or as \(K_2SO_4\cdot Al_2(SO_4)_3\cdot 24H_2O\). It is used as a mordant in dyeing and in water purification.
  • Aluminium is used in electrical cables, trays, picture frames and as a deoxidiser in metallurgy. Aluminium powder mixed with ammonium nitrate is called ammonal and is used in explosives.
  • Important aluminium alloys include magnalium, aluminium bronze, duralumin and Y-alloy. Duralumin and Y-alloy are especially associated with aircraft or aeroplane parts.

    Uses of boron, aluminium and their compounds
  • Borax is used as an alkaline buffer, preservative, flux, and in the manufacture of optical glass, borosilicate glass, glazes and tiles.
  • Boric acid is used as an antiseptic, food preservative, and in making enamels, pottery glazes and glass.
  • Boron trifluoride is used as a Lewis acid catalyst in Friedel-Crafts reactions and polymerisation reactions.
  • Aluminium is used in electrical cables, utensils, trays, photo frames and alloys. Cryolite, \(Na_3AlF_6\), is important in aluminium extraction because it helps lower the melting point of alumina and improves conductivity.

  • \(BCl_3\): electron-deficient Lewis acid; \(AlCl_3\): electron-deficient and dimerises to \(Al_2Cl_6\); \(BF_3\): weakest Lewis acid among boron trihalides because of strongest back-bonding.
  • \(H_3BO_3\): weak monobasic Lewis acid; \(B_2H_6\): electron-deficient hydride with \(3c-2e\) bonds; \(NaBH_4\): reducing agent and hydride donor.
  • Kernite \(\rightarrow\) boron; cryolite \(\rightarrow\) aluminium; bauxite \(\rightarrow\) chief ore of aluminium; borax \(\rightarrow\) boron compound used as flux and buffer.
  • \(Al +\) acid \(\rightarrow H_2\); \(Al +\) aqueous alkali \(\rightarrow [Al(OH)_4]^-\) and \(H_2\); concentrated \(HNO_3\) passivates aluminium by oxide-film formation.

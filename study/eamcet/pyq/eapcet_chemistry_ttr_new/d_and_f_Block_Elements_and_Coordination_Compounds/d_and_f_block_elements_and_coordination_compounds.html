d and f Block Elements and Coordination Compounds

    d-block electronic configuration and special stability
  • Cation electronic configuration rule
    • For transition-metal cations, remove electrons from the outer \(ns\) orbital before the \((n-1)d\) orbital.
    • This rule is essential for deciding \(d\)-count, magnetic moment, oxidation-state stability and crystal-field configuration.
  • Diamagnetic \(d^0\) and \(d^{10}\) ions
    • Completely filled and empty \(d\)-subshells are diamagnetic: \(d^0\) and \(d^{10}\) ions have zero magnetic moment.
    • Common zero-moment ions include \(\mathrm{Sc^{3+}}\), \(\mathrm{Ti^{4+}}\), \(\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Cu^+}\).
    • Group 12 metals have \(d^{10}\) configuration in the \(+2\) state:
      • \(\mathrm{Zn^{2+}=3d^{10}}\).
      • \(\mathrm{Cd^{2+}=4d^{10}}\).
      • \(\mathrm{Hg^{2+}=5d^{10}}\).
    • Do not include \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}}\), which are \(d^9\) and \(d^8\) respectively.
  • Half-filled stability
    • Half-filled configurations are especially stable.
    • \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}=3d^5}\) is more stable than \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}=3d^6}\).
    • \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}=3d^5}\) is more stable than \(\mathrm{Mn^{3+}=3d^4}\).
    • \(\mathrm{Mo}\) has anomalous configuration \(\mathrm{[Kr]\,4d^5\,5s^1}\), giving a half-filled \(4d\)-subshell.
    • In assertion-reason framing, the direct explanation is the extra stability of half-filled configuration; exchange energy supports that stability but should not be treated as the whole explanation by itself.
  • Same \(d\)-configuration, different redox behaviour
    • Same \(d\)-configuration does not guarantee same redox behaviour.
    • \(\mathrm{Cr^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mn^{3+}}\) are both \(d^4\).
    • \(\mathrm{Cr^{2+}}\) is reducing because it tends to form stable \(\mathrm{Cr^{3+}}\).
    • \(\mathrm{Mn^{3+}}\) is oxidising because it tends to form stable \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\).

    Transition-metal physical properties and electrode trends
  • Melting point and metallic bonding
    • Transition metals generally have high melting points because many \((n-1)d\) and \(ns\) electrons participate in interatomic metallic bonding.
    • More delocalised bonding electrons produce stronger metallic bonds and higher melting points.
    • Among \(\mathrm{Cr}\), \(\mathrm{Mo}\), and \(\mathrm{W}\), the standard melting-point order is \(\mathrm{W>Mo>Cr}\).
    • Tungsten should be placed highest in such comparison questions.
  • Electrode-potential and oxidation-state clues
    • \(\mathrm{Mn}\) gives a highly favoured \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\) ion because \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}=3d^5}\) is half-filled.
    • This explains why \(\mathrm{Mn}\) is singled out in \(\mathrm{M/M^{2+}}\) electrode-potential comparisons.
    • \(\mathrm{Ti(IV)}\) is more stable than \(\mathrm{Ti(III)}\) and \(\mathrm{Ti(II)}\) in the usual \(3d\)-series trend.
    • In the same region, copper is the notable metal with positive \(\mathrm{M^{2+}/M}\) reduction potential.
  • Oxoanion oxidising power
    • Oxidising power of transition-metal oxoanions rises with the oxidation state and oxidising tendency of the metal centre in the given species.
    • The tested order is: \(\mathrm{VO_2^+<Cr_2O_7^{2-}<MnO_4^-}\).
    • Compare the actual oxoanion, not just the metal position.

    Colours, magnetic properties and common aqueous reactions
  • Spin-only magnetic moment formula and shortcuts
    • Spin-only magnetic moment is calculated by \(\mu=\sqrt{n(n+2)}\ \mathrm{BM}\), where \(n\) is the number of unpaired electrons.
    • For quick comparison, count \(n\) first; exact calculation is needed only when values are asked.
    • \(\mu\approx2.86\ \mathrm{BM}\) corresponds to \(n=2\).
    • Therefore tetrahedral \((\mathrm{NiCl_4})^{2-}\), with \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}=d^8}\) and weak-field \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\), matches this value.
  • Aqua complexes and high-spin magnetic moments
    • Aqua complexes with weak-field \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) are usually high spin.
    • \([\mathrm{Ti(H_2O)_6}]^{3+}\) has \(\mathrm{Ti^{3+}=d^1}\), so it has the lowest spin-only magnetic moment among common aquated ions such as \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Co^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}}\).
    • \([\mathrm{Cr(H_2O)_6}]^{2+}\) is high-spin \(d^4\), and \([\mathrm{Fe(H_2O)_6}]^{2+}\) is high-spin \(d^6\).
    • Both have \(n=4\), so both have the same spin-only magnetic moment, \(\sqrt{24}\ \mathrm{BM}\).
  • Common aqueous ion colours
    • \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) is pale green.
    • \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) is blue.
    • \(\mathrm{V^{3+}}\) is green.
    • \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) is yellow/yellow-brown.
    • \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) is not pink.
  • Common aqueous reactions and precipitates
    • \(\mathrm{FeCl_3}\) with aqueous ammonia gives brown \(\mathrm{Fe(OH)_3}\) precipitate.
    • \(\mathrm{AgCl}\) dissolves in ammonia to form a colourless soluble ammine complex.
    • \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) with excess ammonia gives deep blue tetraamminecopper(II) complex.
  • Hydrated and hydroxo complexes
    • \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\) in excess water forms the hydrated ion \(\mathrm{[Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+}}\), not bare \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\).
    • Small, highly charged cations are strongly hydrated in aqueous solution.
    • \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) and \(\mathrm{Al(OH)_3}\) dissolve in excess alkali to form tetrahydroxo complexes: \(\mathrm{[Be(OH)_4]^{2-}}\) and \(\mathrm{[Al(OH)_4]^-}\).
    • The covalency of Be and Al in these products is \(4\) in the standard reaction.

    Potassium dichromate, potassium permanganate and oxoanions
  • Dichromate and permanganate heating reactions
    • Ammonium dichromate decomposes on heating as \(\mathrm{(NH_4)_2Cr_2O_7\xrightarrow{\Delta}N_2+Cr_2O_3+4H_2O}\).
    • The nitrogen product is \(\mathrm{N_2}\), and the chromium product is \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_3}\).
    • On heating potassium permanganate around \(513\ \mathrm{K}\), the reaction is \(\mathrm{2KMnO_4\xrightarrow{513\,K}K_2MnO_4+MnO_2+O_2}\).
    • Among the manganese products, manganate has Mn in higher oxidation state \(+6\), while \(\mathrm{MnO_2}\) has Mn in \(+4\).
  • Manganate vs permanganate
    • \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\) is green and has \(\mathrm{Mn^{6+}=3d^1}\), so it is paramagnetic.
    • \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) is permanganate and is purple.
    • \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\) is manganate and is green.
  • Oxoanion oxidising power order
    • For oxoanion oxidising power, remember the tested order \(\mathrm{VO_2^+<Cr_2O_7^{2-}<MnO_4^-}\).
    • The usual reasoning is that higher oxidation state and stronger tendency to reduce make the oxoanion a stronger oxidising agent.

    Lanthanoids, actinoids and f-block patterns
  • Lanthanoid contraction and consequences
    • Lanthanoid contraction is the gradual decrease in atomic and ionic radii from La to Lu due to poor shielding by \(4f\)-electrons.
    • It increases effective nuclear charge and pulls the electron cloud inward.
    • Consequences of lanthanoid contraction are frequently tested:
      • Covalent character of lanthanoid hydroxides increases from La to Lu.
      • Basicity decreases.
      • Chemical reactivity decreases.
      • Separation of lanthanoids is difficult because their properties are very similar.
    • \(\mathrm{La(OH)_3}\) is more basic than \(\mathrm{Lu(OH)_3}\).
    • As lanthanoid ionic size decreases, polarising power increases and hydroxides become less ionic and less basic.
  • 4f filling and size effects
    • Zr and Hf have nearly the same radius because lanthanoid contraction reduces the size of Hf after the \(4f\)-series is filled.
    • This is a standard consequence of filling \(4f\) orbitals before \(5d\) orbitals.
    • The small increase in atomic radii in the \(5d\) transition series is due to filling of \(4f\) orbitals before \(5d\) orbitals.
    • Therefore \(X=4f\) and \(Y=5d\) in this pattern.
  • Lanthanoid ion radii and configurations
    • Ionic radii of lanthanoid \(+3\) ions decrease from \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) to \(\mathrm{Lu^{3+}}\).
    • In the tested comparison, the increasing ionic radius order is \(\mathrm{Y^{3+}<Lu^{3+}<Eu^{3+}<La^{3+}}\).
    • For lanthanoid ions, remove outer \(6s\) electrons first, then \(5d\) if present.
    • Thus \(\mathrm{Gd=[Xe]\,4f^7\,5d^1\,6s^2}\) gives \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}=[Xe]\,4f^7}\).
    • \(\mathrm{Yb=[Xe]\,4f^{14}\,6s^2}\) gives \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}=[Xe]\,4f^{14}}\).
  • Lanthanoid oxidation states and magnetism
    • Lanthanoids mainly prefer the \(+3\) oxidation state.
    • \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Tb^{4+}}\) behave as oxidising agents because they tend to reduce to \(+3\).
    • \(\mathrm{Eu^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) behave as reducing agents because they tend to oxidise to \(+3\).
    • \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) are diamagnetic because both have \([\mathrm{Xe}]\) configuration.
    • \(\mathrm{Eu^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}}\) have the same number of electrons and are paramagnetic due to unpaired \(f\)-electrons.
  • Half-filled \(f\)-subshell and misch metal
    • Half-filled \(f\)-subshell means \(f^7\).
    • Commonly tested examples with half-filled \(f\)-orbitals include \(\mathrm{Eu}\), \(\mathrm{Gd}\), and \(\mathrm{Cm}\) in their ground-state configurations.
    • Misch metal is mostly lanthanoid metal mixture, about \(95\%\) lanthanoid metals and about \(5\%\) iron.
    • Do not reverse these percentages.

    Werner theory, coordination sphere and AgNO3 test
  • Werner theory: primary vs secondary valency
    • In Werner's theory, primary valency corresponds to oxidation state and is ionisable.
    • Secondary valency corresponds to coordination number, meaning the number of ligand donor atoms directly bonded to the central metal ion.
    • Do not equate precipitated halide count with secondary valency.
    • Precipitated halide count gives free ions outside the coordination sphere; secondary valency counts donor atoms inside the coordination sphere.
  • AgNO3 test and coordination sphere
    • In \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\) tests, only halide ions outside the coordination sphere precipitate as \(\mathrm{AgCl}\).
    • Coordinated chloride inside the bracket is not immediately precipitated.
    • If a cobalt ammine complex gives three moles of \(\mathrm{AgCl}\) per mole of complex, all three chlorides are outside the coordination sphere.
    • For cobalt(III) ammine complexes with coordination number \(6\), the formula becomes \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]Cl_3}\).
    • Hydrate or ionisation-sphere formulas can also be fixed from \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\) data.
    • If \(\mathrm{CoH_{12}O_6Cl_3}\) gives three moles of \(\mathrm{AgCl}\), the correct formula is \(\mathrm{[Co(H_2O)_6]Cl_3}\).
  • Oxidation state and coordination number
    • Oxidation state and coordination number must be calculated separately.
    • In \(\mathrm{K_2[PtCl_6]}\), the complex ion is \(\mathrm{[PtCl_6]^{2-}}\), so Pt is \(+4\) and coordination number is \(6\); their sum is \(10\).
    • Platinum dissolves in aqua regia to form \(\mathrm{[PtCl_6]^{2-}}\).
    • In this complex, \(x+6(-1)=-2\), so the oxidation state of Pt is \(+4\).

    Ligands, denticity and spectrochemical series
  • Ambidentate vs bidentate ligands
    • An ambidentate ligand has two possible donor atoms but coordinates through only one at a time.
    • Common examples are \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), \(\mathrm{SCN^-}\), \(\mathrm{NCS^-}\), and \(\mathrm{NO_2^-}\).
    • \(\mathrm{SO_4^{2-}}\) is not treated as an ambidentate ligand in the tested classification.
    • Do not confuse ambidentate and bidentate ligands.
    • Ambidentate ligands have alternative donor atoms but bind through one donor atom at a time.
    • Bidentate ligands such as ethane-1,2-diamine \((en)\) or oxalate use two donor atoms simultaneously.
  • Spectrochemical series and linkage effect
    • Ligand field strength follows the spectrochemical series.
    • In the tested set, the decreasing field strength is \(\mathrm{en>NCS^->SCN^->S^{2-}}\).
    • For thiocyanate linkage, donor atom matters: nitrogen-bonded \(\mathrm{NCS^-}\) gives stronger field than sulphur-bonded \(\mathrm{SCN^-}\).
    • Do not treat these two notations as equivalent in crystal-field questions.
  • Cobalt complex crystal-field splitting order
    • For cobalt complexes with the same metal ion, crystal-field splitting mainly follows ligand strength.
    • The increasing order is \(\mathrm{(CoF_6)^{3-}<(Co(H_2O)_6)^{3+}<(Co(NH_3)_6)^{3+}<(Co(CN)_6)^{3-}}\).

    Isomerism in coordination compounds
  • Standard geometrical-isomerism patterns
    • Tetrahedral \(\mathrm{MA_4}\) complexes do not show geometrical isomerism.
    • Octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_4B_2}\) complexes show cis-trans isomerism.
    • Octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_3B_3}\) complexes show fac-mer isomerism.
    • Octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_5B}\) complexes do not show geometrical isomerism.
    • In counting problems, use the standard totals:
      • \(\mathrm{MA_4}\) tetrahedral \(=0\).
      • \(\mathrm{MA_4B_2}\) octahedral \(=2\).
      • \(\mathrm{MA_3B_3}\) octahedral \(=2\).
      • \(\mathrm{MA_5B}=0\).
  • Specific cobalt complex examples
    • \(\mathrm{[CoCl_2(en)_2]^+}\) is an octahedral \([M(en)_2a_2]\)-type complex and shows cis-trans geometrical isomerism.
    • The two chloride ligands may be adjacent or opposite.
    • \(\mathrm{[Co(en)_3]^{3+}}\) may show optical isomerism but not geometrical isomerism.
    • Do not mark it as geometrically isomeric merely because \(en\) is bidentate.
  • Hydrate and ionisation isomerism clue
    • Hydrate isomerism and ionisation isomerism are often tested through the number of ions or precipitated chloride ions.
    • Use the coordination sphere first; place free ions only after checking experimental data such as \(\mathrm{AgCl}\) precipitation.

    Valence bond theory, hybridisation and magnetic nature
  • VBT hybridisation rules
    • In VBT, inner-orbital octahedral complexes use \(d^2sp^3\) hybridisation.
    • Outer-orbital octahedral complexes use \(sp^3d^2\).
    • Strong-field ligands can cause pairing and create vacant inner \(d\)-orbitals.
  • Cobalt complexes: low spin vs high spin
    • \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}}\) has \(\mathrm{Co^{3+}=3d^6}\).
    • With \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), cobalt(III) forms a low-spin inner-orbital complex with \(d^2sp^3\) hybridisation, \(\mathrm{t_{2g}^6e_g^0}\), \(0\) unpaired electrons and diamagnetic nature.
    • \(\mathrm{[Co(ox)_3]^{3-}}\) is diamagnetic because \(\mathrm{Co^{3+}=d^6}\) is paired in the oxalate complex.
    • In contrast, \(\mathrm{[CoF_6]^{3-}}\) is high-spin and paramagnetic because \(\mathrm{F^-}\) is weak field.
  • Nickel complexes
    • \(\mathrm{[NiCl_4]^{2-}}\) is tetrahedral and paramagnetic with two unpaired electrons.
    • \(\mathrm{[Ni(CN)_4]^{2-}}\) is square planar and diamagnetic.
    • \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\) is tetrahedral \(d^{10}\) and diamagnetic.
  • Weak-field and strong-field ligand memory
    • Weak-field ligands such as \(\mathrm{F^-}\), \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) usually give high-spin complexes.
    • Strong-field ligands such as \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), \(\mathrm{CO}\), and often \(en\) promote pairing.

    Crystal field theory and electronic configurations
  • Octahedral and tetrahedral splitting
    • In octahedral complexes, \(d\)-orbitals split into lower \(\mathrm{t_{2g}}\) and higher \(\mathrm{e_g}\) sets.
    • In tetrahedral complexes, the notation is often written as lower \(e\) and higher \(\mathrm{t_2}\), with smaller splitting than octahedral complexes.
  • High-spin and low-spin configurations
    • \(\mathrm{t_{2g}^3e_g^2}\) represents high-spin octahedral \(d^5\).
    • Therefore \(\mathrm{[Fe(H_2O)_6]^{3+}}\), with \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}=d^5}\) and weak-field \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), matches this configuration.
    • \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}}\) is low-spin \(d^6\), so its configuration is \(\mathrm{t_{2g}^6e_g^0}\).
    • \(\mathrm{[CoF_6]^{3-}}\) is high-spin \(d^6\), so its configuration is \(\mathrm{t_{2g}^4e_g^2}\).
    • \(\mathrm{[Fe(CN)_6]^{3-}}\) is low-spin \(d^5\), giving \(\mathrm{t_{2g}^5e_g^0}\).
    • \(\mathrm{[Mn(CN)_6]^{3-}}\) is low-spin \(d^4\) with two unpaired electrons in the tested treatment.
  • \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\) tetrahedral configuration
    • \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\) has \(\mathrm{Ni^0}\), effectively \(d^{10}\), and in tetrahedral splitting it is represented as \(\mathrm{t_2^4e^6}\).
    • Since all electrons are paired, it is diamagnetic.
  • Unpaired-electron and magnetic-moment order
    • Increasing unpaired-electron order must be found by oxidation state, \(d\)-count, ligand strength and geometry together.
    • For example, \(\mathrm{[Co(C_2O_4)_3]^{3-}}\) has \(0\), \(\mathrm{[Fe(CN)_6]^{3-}}\) has \(1\), \(\mathrm{[Mn(CN)_6]^{3-}}\) has \(2\), and \(\mathrm{[CoF_6]^{3-}}\) has \(4\) unpaired electrons.
    • For magnetic-moment order, use \(n\) directly: low-spin \(d^6\) may be \(n=0\), low-spin \(d^5\) may be \(n=1\), \(d^3\) is \(n=3\), and tetrahedral high-spin \(d^5\) is \(n=5\).
  • Absorbed colour vs observed colour
    • A complex absorbs a particular colour and appears in the complementary colour.
    • If a complex absorbs orange light, it appears green-blue.
    • Do not write the absorbed colour as the observed colour.

    Metal carbonyls and applications
  • CO ligand bonding
    • Carbon monoxide is a strong-field ligand and forms metal carbonyls through synergic bonding.
    • Synergic bonding involves \(\sigma\)-donation from CO to metal and \(\pi\)-back donation from metal to CO.
  • Terminal and bridging carbonyls
    • Carbonyl ligands may be terminal or bridging.
    • Bridging carbonyls are often represented as \(\mu\)-CO and bind to more than one metal atom.
    • \(\mathrm{[Co_2(CO)_8]}\), \(\mathrm{[Fe_3(CO)_{12}]}\), and \(\mathrm{[Fe_2(CO)_9]}\) contain bridged CO ligands.
    • \(\mathrm{[Mn_2(CO)_{10}]}\) contains terminal carbonyls only and is the standard exception in the tested set.
  • \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\) magnetic nature
    • \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\) is diamagnetic because nickel is in zero oxidation state with a filled \(d^{10}\)-type electron arrangement.
    • Do not classify it as paramagnetic just because it is tetrahedral.

    Position and electronic configuration of d-block elements
  • d-block elements occupy groups 3 to 12, between the s-block and p-block. They are called transition elements when the atom or at least one common ion has a partially filled \(d\)-subshell.
  • The general electronic configuration of transition elements is \(\mathrm{(n-1)d^{1-10}ns^{1-2}}\). In the first transition series, the \(3d\)-subshell is filled after \(4s\) begins filling.
  • Chromium and copper show anomalous ground-state configurations because half-filled and completely filled \(d\)-subshells are especially stable: \(\mathrm{Cr=[Ar]\,3d^5\,4s^1}\) and \(\mathrm{Cu=[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^1}\).
  • During ion formation, electrons are removed first from the outer \(ns\) orbital and then from the \((n-1)d\) orbital. For example, \(\mathrm{V=[Ar]\,3d^3\,4s^2}\), but \(\mathrm{V^{2+}=[Ar]\,3d^3}\).
  • This ionisation rule is essential for all \(d\)-count questions. For example, \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}=3d^5}\), \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}=3d^5}\), \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}=3d^8}\), \(\mathrm{Cu^+=3d^{10}}\), and \(\mathrm{Zn^{2+}=3d^{10}}\).
  • Zinc, cadmium and mercury are usually studied with d-block elements but are not true transition elements in the strict sense, because their atoms and common ions do not contain partially filled \(d\)-subshells.
  • Completely empty and completely filled \(d\)-subshells are diamagnetic. Thus \(d^0\) ions such as \(\mathrm{Sc^{3+}}\), \(\mathrm{Ti^{4+}}\), and \(d^{10}\) ions such as \(\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Cu^+}\), \(\mathrm{Cd^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Hg^{2+}}\) have zero magnetic moment.
  • Half-filled \(d^5\) configurations have special stability. This explains why \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\) is more stable than \(\mathrm{Mn^{3+}}\), and why \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) is more stable than expected compared with \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\).
  • \(\mathrm{Mo}\) has the anomalous configuration \(\mathrm{[Kr]\,4d^5\,5s^1}\). The main exam-useful reason is the stability of the half-filled \(4d\)-subshell.

    Physical properties and atomic-size trends
  • Transition elements are metallic, hard, malleable and ductile, with good electrical and thermal conductivity. Mercury is the major exception because it is liquid at room temperature.
  • They generally have high melting and boiling points because many \((n-1)d\) and \(ns\) electrons participate in strong interatomic metallic bonding. This is why assertion-reason questions link high melting points with strong metallic bonding.
  • Among \(\mathrm{Cr}\), \(\mathrm{Mo}\), and \(\mathrm{W}\), the standard melting-point order is \(\mathrm{W>Mo>Cr}\). Tungsten is placed highest because of very strong metallic bonding.
  • Across a transition series, atomic radii decrease slowly at first because nuclear charge increases and \(d\)-electrons shield poorly. Near the end, the decrease becomes very small and may show slight irregularity due to increased electron-electron repulsion.
  • In a group, atomic radius usually increases from \(3d\) to \(4d\), but the \(4d\) and \(5d\) elements are often nearly equal in size because of lanthanoid contraction. The classic pair is \(\mathrm{Zr}\) and \(\mathrm{Hf}\).
  • Transition metals have high densities because of small atomic size, high atomic mass and close-packed metallic structures. The density generally increases across a transition series and reaches high values near groups 8 to 10.

    Oxidation states and electrode-potential logic
  • Transition elements show variable oxidation states because \(ns\) and \((n-1)d\) orbitals are close in energy, so both can participate in bonding. The \(+2\) state is common in the \(3d\)-series because it usually arises from loss of two \(4s\) electrons.
  • Higher oxidation states are stabilised mainly by highly electronegative atoms such as oxygen and fluorine. Oxoanions such as \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\) and \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) are therefore important examples of high oxidation states.
  • As oxidation state increases, the covalent character and acidic character of oxides generally increase. For example, \(\mathrm{MnO}\) is basic, while \(\mathrm{Mn_2O_7}\) is acidic.
  • Compounds of transition metals in high oxidation states often behave as oxidising agents because the metal centre tends to accept electrons and move to a lower, more stable oxidation state.
  • \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\) is especially stable because it is \(3d^5\). This stability explains its importance in \(\mathrm{M^{2+}/M}\) electrode-potential comparisons.
  • Copper is the notable \(3d\)-series metal with positive \(\mathrm{M^{2+}/M}\) reduction potential. It does not liberate hydrogen from ordinary protic acids and reacts mainly with oxidising acids such as nitric acid and hot concentrated sulphuric acid.
  • Same \(d\)-configuration need not mean same redox behaviour. \(\mathrm{Cr^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mn^{3+}}\) are both \(d^4\), but \(\mathrm{Cr^{2+}}\) is reducing because it tends to form stable \(\mathrm{Cr^{3+}}\), while \(\mathrm{Mn^{3+}}\) is oxidising because it tends to form stable \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\).
  • The oxidising power order repeatedly used for oxoanions is \(\mathrm{VO_2^+<Cr_2O_7^{2-}<MnO_4^-}\). Compare the actual oxidation state and oxoanion, not just the position of the metal in the periodic table.

    Magnetic properties and coloured ions
  • Paramagnetism arises due to unpaired electrons, while diamagnetic species have no unpaired electrons. The spin-only magnetic moment is \(\mu=\sqrt{n(n+2)}\ \mathrm{BM}\), where \(n\) is the number of unpaired electrons.
  • The most useful quick values are: \(n=0\Rightarrow\mu=0\), \(n=1\Rightarrow\mu=\sqrt{3}\), \(n=2\Rightarrow\mu=\sqrt{8}\approx2.83\ \mathrm{BM}\), \(n=3\Rightarrow\mu=\sqrt{15}\), \(n=4\Rightarrow\mu=\sqrt{24}\), and \(n=5\Rightarrow\mu=\sqrt{35}\).
  • \(\mu\approx2.86\ \mathrm{BM}\) corresponds to \(n=2\). A common example is tetrahedral \(\mathrm{[NiCl_4]^{2-}}\), where \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}=3d^8}\) and weak-field \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\) gives two unpaired electrons.
  • Aquated ions are usually high-spin because \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is a weak-field ligand. \(\mathrm{[Ti(H_2O)_6]^{3+}}\) has \(\mathrm{Ti^{3+}=d^1}\), so it has the lowest magnetic moment among common aquated ions such as \(\mathrm{Mn^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Co^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}}\).
  • \(\mathrm{[Cr(H_2O)_6]^{2+}}\) is high-spin \(d^4\), and \(\mathrm{[Fe(H_2O)_6]^{2+}}\) is high-spin \(d^6\). Both have four unpaired electrons, so both have \(\mu=\sqrt{24}\ \mathrm{BM}\).
  • Colours of transition-metal ions generally arise from \(d-d\) transitions. Ions with \(d^0\) or \(d^{10}\) configurations are usually colourless because \(d-d\) transitions are not possible.
  • Common hydrated-ion colours worth remembering are: \(\mathrm{Ti^{3+}}\) violet, \(\mathrm{V^{3+}}\) blue/green depending on context, \(\mathrm{Cr^{3+}}\) green, \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) pale green, \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) yellow or yellow-brown, \(\mathrm{Co^{2+}}\) pink, \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}}\) green, and \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) blue.
  • If a complex absorbs a particular colour, it appears in the complementary colour. Thus a complex absorbing orange light appears green-blue.

    Complex formation, catalysis, interstitial compounds and alloys
  • Transition metals form many complexes because they have small size, high charge density, variable oxidation states and vacant orbitals that can accept lone pairs from ligands.
  • In aqueous solution, highly charged metal ions often exist as hydrated complexes rather than bare ions. For example, \(\mathrm{AlCl_3+6H_2O\rightarrow[Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+}+3Cl^-}\).
  • Transition metals and their compounds are good catalysts mainly because they show variable oxidation states and can adsorb reactants on their surfaces. Important examples include \(\mathrm{V_2O_5}\) in the contact process, finely divided Fe in the Haber process, and Ni in hydrogenation.
  • Transition metals form interstitial compounds with small atoms such as \(\mathrm{H}\), \(\mathrm{C}\), and \(\mathrm{N}\). These atoms occupy holes in the metal lattice and make the material hard and rigid.
  • They also form alloys because many transition metals have similar atomic sizes. Examples include brass \((\mathrm{Cu+Zn})\), bronze \((\mathrm{Cu+Sn})\), gun metal and bell metal.
  • Amphoteric hydroxides such as \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) and \(\mathrm{Al(OH)_3}\) dissolve in excess alkali to form tetrahydroxo complexes: \(\mathrm{[Be(OH)_4]^{2-}}\) and \(\mathrm{[Al(OH)_4]^-}\). In these products, Be and Al have covalency \(4\).

    Potassium dichromate, potassium permanganate and oxoanions
  • Potassium dichromate and potassium permanganate are important oxidising agents of transition-metal chemistry. Their strong oxidising nature is linked to high oxidation states of chromium and manganese.
  • Chromate ion is \(\mathrm{CrO_4^{2-}}\) and dichromate ion is \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\). Chromate is yellow and dichromate is orange; the interconversion depends on pH: chromate is favoured in alkaline medium, dichromate in acidic medium.
  • In \(\mathrm{CrO_4^{2-}}\) and \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}\), chromium is in the \(+6\) oxidation state. In dichromate, two tetrahedral chromate units share one oxygen atom through a \(\mathrm{Cr-O-Cr}\) bridge.
  • Ammonium dichromate decomposes on heating as \(\mathrm{(NH_4)_2Cr_2O_7\xrightarrow{\Delta}N_2+Cr_2O_3+4H_2O}\). The chromium product is \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_3}\), and the nitrogen product is \(\mathrm{N_2}\).
  • Potassium permanganate contains \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\), where Mn is in \(+7\) oxidation state. It is purple and has no \(d\)-electron on Mn, so the colour is mainly due to charge-transfer transition.
  • On heating \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) around \(513\ \mathrm{K}\), the reaction is \(\mathrm{2KMnO_4\xrightarrow{513\,K}K_2MnO_4+MnO_2+O_2}\). The product with higher manganese oxidation state is manganate, \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\), where Mn is \(+6\).
  • Manganate ion \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\) is green and has \(\mathrm{Mn^{6+}=3d^1}\), so it is paramagnetic. Permanganate \(\mathrm{MnO_4^-}\) is purple, while \(\mathrm{MnO_2}\) contains Mn in \(+4\).

  • Lanthanoids involve filling of the \(4f\)-subshell. Their general configuration may be written as \(\mathrm{[Xe]\,4f^{1-14}\,5d^{0-1}\,6s^2}\), with small irregularities due to the stability of \(f^0\), \(f^7\), and \(f^{14}\) arrangements.
  • For lanthanoid ion formation, remove \(6s\) electrons first and then \(5d\) if present. Thus \(\mathrm{Gd=[Xe]\,4f^7\,5d^1\,6s^2}\) gives \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}=[Xe]\,4f^7}\), while \(\mathrm{Yb=[Xe]\,4f^{14}\,6s^2}\) gives \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}=[Xe]\,4f^{14}}\).
  • Lanthanoids mainly show \(+3\) oxidation state. The \(+2\) and \(+4\) states occur when they lead to especially stable \(4f^0\), \(4f^7\), or \(4f^{14}\) configurations.
  • \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Tb^{4+}}\) act as oxidising agents because they tend to reduce to the common \(+3\) state. \(\mathrm{Eu^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) act as reducing agents because they tend to oxidise to \(+3\).
  • \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) are diamagnetic because both have the \(\mathrm{[Xe]}\) configuration. \(\mathrm{Eu^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}}\) have the same number of electrons and are paramagnetic because of unpaired \(f\)-electrons.
  • Half-filled \(f\)-subshell means \(f^7\). Important ground-state examples include \(\mathrm{Eu}\), \(\mathrm{Gd}\), and \(\mathrm{Cm}\) in the tested set.
  • Lanthanoid ions are often coloured because of \(f-f\) transitions. \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Lu^{3+}}\) are colourless because they lack unpaired \(f\)-electrons, while \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) can show intense colour due to charge transfer.
  • Lanthanoid contraction is the regular decrease in atomic and ionic radii from La to Lu. It occurs because \(4f\)-electrons shield the increasing nuclear charge poorly.
  • Consequences of lanthanoid contraction are highly testable: covalent character increases from La to Lu, basicity of hydroxides decreases, chemical reactivity decreases, separation of lanthanoids is difficult, and \(\mathrm{Zr}\) and \(\mathrm{Hf}\) become nearly equal in size.
  • In radius comparisons, remember that \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) is larger than later lanthanoid ions. In the tested order, \(\mathrm{Y^{3+}<Lu^{3+}<Eu^{3+}<La^{3+}}\).
  • The small increase in atomic radii in the \(5d\)-series is due to poor shielding by the filled \(4f\)-subshell before the \(5d\)-elements. Therefore, the key pair is \(4f\)-filling before \(5d\)-filling.
  • Misch metal is mostly a mixture of lanthanoid metals, about \(95\%\), with about \(5\%\) iron. This percentage order should not be reversed.

    Actinoids and comparison with lanthanoids
  • Actinoids involve filling of \(5f\)-orbitals and extend from thorium to lawrencium. Their configurations show greater irregularity because \(5f\), \(6d\), and \(7s\) orbitals are close in energy.
  • Actinoids show actinoid contraction, a gradual decrease in size across the series. It is similar to lanthanoid contraction but more irregular because \(5f\)-electrons shield poorly and bonding is more complex.
  • Actinoids show a wider range of oxidation states than lanthanoids. Early actinoids show \(+3\), \(+4\), \(+5\), \(+6\), and \(+7\) states because \(5f\), \(6d\), and \(7s\) electrons can participate in bonding.
  • Lanthanoids mainly prefer \(+3\), with limited \(+2\) and \(+4\) exceptions. Actinoids also often show \(+3\), but their chemistry is more variable, especially in the first half of the series.
  • Actinoids are generally radioactive and more reactive than lanthanoids. They react with water, acids and non-metals more readily, though some may be protected by oxide layers in nitric acid.
  • Magnetic behaviour in both lanthanoids and actinoids is related to unpaired \(f\)-electrons, but actinoid magnetic behaviour is more complicated due to stronger involvement of \(5f\)-orbitals in bonding.

    Applications of d-block and f-block elements
  • Iron is widely used in construction because of its strength, availability and alloy-forming ability.
  • Chromium, manganese and nickel form important strong alloys with carbon and other metals. Stainless steel, alloy steel, brass, bronze, gun metal and bell metal are standard examples of practical transition-metal alloy chemistry.
  • Titanium compounds are used in pigments, and transition-metal compounds are used as catalysts in several industrial processes.
  • Lanthanoids and their alloys have important uses in pyrophoric alloys, glass polishing, special steels and magnetic materials. Misch metal is a common lanthanoid alloy mixture used in lighter flints.

    Coordination compounds: basic ideas and Werner theory
  • A coordination compound contains a central metal atom or ion surrounded by ligands. The central metal ion acts as a Lewis acid, while ligands act as Lewis bases by donating electron pairs.
  • A double salt dissociates completely into simple ions in water, while a coordination compound retains a complex ion. For example, \(\mathrm{K_4[Fe(CN)_6]}\) gives \(\mathrm{K^+}\) and \(\mathrm{[Fe(CN)_6]^{4-}}\), not free \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{CN^-}\).
  • Werner's theory states that metals show primary and secondary valencies. Primary valency corresponds to oxidation state and is ionisable; secondary valency corresponds to coordination number and is non-ionisable.
  • In complexes such as \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]Cl_3}\), \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_5Cl]Cl_2}\), and \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_4Cl_2]Cl}\), the number of chloride ions outside the coordination sphere decides how many \(\mathrm{AgCl}\) moles are precipitated with \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\).
  • Only free halide ions outside the square bracket precipitate immediately with \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\). Coordinated chloride inside the bracket satisfies secondary valency and does not behave as free chloride.
  • If one mole of a cobalt complex gives three moles of \(\mathrm{AgCl}\), all three chlorides are outside the coordination sphere. Examples include \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]Cl_3}\) and \(\mathrm{[Co(H_2O)_6]Cl_3}\).
  • Secondary valency must not be confused with the number of precipitated halides. Precipitated halides tell the number of ionisable chloride ions; secondary valency tells the number of donor atoms directly attached to the metal.

    Ligands, coordination sphere and oxidation state
  • A ligand is an ion or molecule directly attached to the central metal atom or ion. The atom of the ligand that donates the lone pair is called the donor atom.
  • Monodentate ligands donate through one donor atom. Examples are \(\mathrm{H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\), and \(\mathrm{CN^-}\).
  • Bidentate ligands donate through two donor atoms at the same time. Examples are ethane-1,2-diamine \((en)\) and oxalate \((\mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}})\).
  • Polydentate ligands such as EDTA can attach through several donor atoms and form chelate rings. Chelation generally increases complex stability.
  • Ambidentate ligands have two possible donor atoms but coordinate through only one donor atom at a time. Important examples are \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), \(\mathrm{SCN^-}\), \(\mathrm{NCS^-}\), and \(\mathrm{NO_2^-}\). \(\mathrm{SO_4^{2-}}\) is not treated as ambidentate in the usual classification.
  • For thiocyanate linkage, donor atom matters. \(\mathrm{NCS^-}\) is nitrogen-bonded and generally gives stronger ligand field than sulphur-bonded \(\mathrm{SCN^-}\).
  • Coordination number is the number of donor atoms directly bonded to the central metal. For example, \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}}\) has coordination number \(6\), while \(\mathrm{[PtCl_6]^{2-}}\) also has coordination number \(6\).
  • Oxidation state is calculated from ligand charges and complex charge. In \(\mathrm{[PtCl_6]^{2-}}\), \(x+6(-1)=-2\), so \(\mathrm{Pt}=+4\). In \(\mathrm{K_2[PtCl_6]}\), the sum of oxidation state and coordination number is \(4+6=10\).

    Nomenclature of coordination compounds
  • In naming coordination compounds, name the cation before the anion. Inside the coordination sphere, name ligands first in alphabetical order, then the metal.
  • Anionic ligands usually end in "o": \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\) is chloro, \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) is hydroxo, \(\mathrm{CN^-}\) is cyano, \(\mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}}\) is oxalato, and \(\mathrm{SCN^-}\) is thiocyanato.
  • Important neutral ligand names are: \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) ammine, \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) aqua, and \(\mathrm{CO}\) carbonyl.
  • Use di, tri, tetra for simple ligands. Use bis, tris and tetrakis when the ligand name already contains a numerical prefix or is complex, as in bis(ethylenediamine).
  • The oxidation state of the metal is written in Roman numerals after the metal name. For anionic complexes, the metal name ends in "-ate", as in ferrate, cuprate, nickelate, chromate, manganate and platinate.
  • Useful examples are \(\mathrm{[Co(H_2O)_6]Cl_3}\), hexaaquacobalt(III) chloride; \(\mathrm{K_2[PtCl_6]}\), potassium hexachloroplatinate(IV); and \(\mathrm{[Co(en)_2Cl_2]Cl}\), dichlorobis(ethylenediamine)cobalt(III) chloride.

    Isomerism in coordination compounds
  • Geometrical isomerism depends on the relative positions of ligands around the metal. It is common in square planar and octahedral complexes, but tetrahedral \(\mathrm{MA_4}\) complexes do not show geometrical isomerism.
  • Octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_4B_2}\) complexes show cis-trans isomerism, while octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_3B_3}\) complexes show fac-mer isomerism. Octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_5B}\) complexes do not show geometrical isomerism.
  • In counting questions, remember the standard totals: tetrahedral \(\mathrm{MA_4}=0\), octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_4B_2}=2\), octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_3B_3}=2\), and octahedral \(\mathrm{MA_5B}=0\).
  • \(\mathrm{[CoCl_2(en)_2]^+}\) is an octahedral \([M(en)_2a_2]\)-type complex and shows cis-trans geometrical isomerism. The two chloride ligands may be adjacent or opposite.
  • \(\mathrm{[Co(en)_3]^{3+}}\) may show optical isomerism but not geometrical isomerism. Optical isomerism arises when a complex is non-superimposable on its mirror image.
  • Linkage isomerism occurs with ambidentate ligands, such as \(\mathrm{NO_2^-}\), \(\mathrm{SCN^-}\), and \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), because the ligand can attach through different donor atoms.
  • Ionisation isomerism occurs when the counter ion and a coordinated ligand exchange positions. Hydrate isomerism is a special case involving different positions of water molecules inside or outside the coordination sphere.

    Valence bond theory and hybridisation
  • Valence bond theory explains coordination compounds by hybridisation of vacant orbitals on the metal ion and donation of lone pairs from ligands into these hybrid orbitals.
  • Six-coordinate complexes are usually octahedral. Inner-orbital octahedral complexes use \(\mathrm{d^2sp^3}\) hybridisation, while outer-orbital octahedral complexes use \(\mathrm{sp^3d^2}\) hybridisation.
  • \(\mathrm{[CoF_6]^{3-}}\) has \(\mathrm{Co^{3+}=3d^6}\), but \(\mathrm{F^-}\) is weak field, so it does not force pairing. The complex is outer-orbital, high-spin and paramagnetic with four unpaired electrons.

  • \(\mathrm{[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}}\) also has \(\mathrm{Co^{3+}=3d^6}\), but \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) with \(\mathrm{Co^{3+}}\) gives pairing. The complex is inner-orbital, low-spin, \(\mathrm{d^2sp^3}\), and diamagnetic.

  • \(\mathrm{[Fe(CN)_6]^{4-}}\) has \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}=d^6}\). Because \(\mathrm{CN^-}\) is strong field, it forms a low-spin inner-orbital octahedral complex with no unpaired electron.

  • \(\mathrm{[Fe(CN)_6]^{3-}}\) has \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}=d^5}\). With strong-field \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), it is low-spin, inner-orbital and has one unpaired electron.

  • Four-coordinate complexes may be tetrahedral \(\mathrm{sp^3}\) or square planar \(\mathrm{dsp^2}\). \(\mathrm{[NiCl_4]^{2-}}\) is tetrahedral and paramagnetic, while \(\mathrm{[Ni(CN)_4]^{2-}}\) is square planar and diamagnetic.

  • \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\) is tetrahedral and diamagnetic because nickel is in zero oxidation state with a filled \(d^{10}\)-type arrangement. It should not be grouped with paramagnetic tetrahedral \(\mathrm{[NiCl_4]^{2-}}\).


    Crystal field theory
  • Crystal field theory treats the metal-ligand interaction as electrostatic. Ligands approaching the metal ion remove the degeneracy of the five \(d\)-orbitals and split them into sets of different energy.
  • In an octahedral field, the ligands approach along the axes. The \(\mathrm{d_{x^2-y^2}}\) and \(\mathrm{d_{z^2}}\) orbitals point towards ligands and form the higher-energy \(\mathrm{e_g}\) set; \(\mathrm{d_{xy}}\), \(\mathrm{d_{yz}}\), and \(\mathrm{d_{xz}}\) form the lower-energy \(\mathrm{t_{2g}}\) set.
  • The octahedral splitting energy is \(\Delta_o\). Each \(\mathrm{t_{2g}}\) electron is stabilised by \(0.4\Delta_o\), while each \(\mathrm{e_g}\) electron is destabilised by \(0.6\Delta_o\).
  • In tetrahedral complexes, the splitting is smaller and the order is reversed: \(e\) is lower and \(\mathrm{t_2}\) is higher. The useful relation is \(\Delta_t=\dfrac{4}{9}\Delta_o\), so tetrahedral complexes are usually high-spin.
  • Ligand strength controls the magnitude of splitting when the metal ion is fixed. The useful order from the PYQ pattern is \(\mathrm{F^-<H_2O<NH_3<CN^-}\), and more generally weak-field ligands give smaller splitting while strong-field ligands give larger splitting.
  • For cobalt complexes with the same metal ion, increasing splitting is \(\mathrm{[CoF_6]^{3-}<[Co(H_2O)_6]^{3+}<[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}<[Co(CN)_6]^{3-}}\).
  • In another tested ligand order, decreasing field strength is \(\mathrm{en>NCS^->SCN^->S^{2-}}\). Nitrogen-bonded thiocyanate is stronger field than sulphur-bonded thiocyanate.
  • High-spin and low-spin configurations depend on whether splitting energy is smaller or larger than pairing energy. Weak-field ligands such as \(\mathrm{F^-}\), \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) favour high spin; strong-field ligands such as \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), \(\mathrm{CO}\), and \(en\) favour pairing.
  • Important configurations include: high-spin octahedral \(d^5=\mathrm{t_{2g}^3e_g^2}\), low-spin \(d^6=\mathrm{t_{2g}^6e_g^0}\), high-spin \(d^6=\mathrm{t_{2g}^4e_g^2}\), and low-spin \(d^5=\mathrm{t_{2g}^5e_g^0}\).
  • A complex is coloured when it absorbs visible light corresponding to the energy gap between split \(d\)-levels. The observed colour is complementary to the absorbed colour.
  • Limitations of CFT include its electrostatic-only treatment of bonding and limited explanation of covalency. However, it explains magnetic behaviour, colour and orbital splitting more effectively than VBT.

    Bonding in metal carbonyls
  • Carbon monoxide is a strong-field ligand and forms metal carbonyls, often with metals in low or zero oxidation state.
  • Bonding in metal carbonyls is synergic. The ligand donates a lone pair from carbon to the metal by \(\sigma\)-bonding, and the metal donates electron density back into the antibonding orbital of CO by \(\pi\)-back bonding.
  • This synergic effect strengthens the metal-carbon bond and weakens the carbon-oxygen bond. It explains why CO is a strong-field ligand and why metal carbonyls are usually low-spin.
  • Carbonyl groups may be terminal or bridging. Bridging carbonyls are represented by \(\mu\)-CO and connect more than one metal atom.
  • The tested bridged carbonyl set is: \(\mathrm{[Co_2(CO)_8]}\), \(\mathrm{[Fe_3(CO)_{12}]}\), and \(\mathrm{[Fe_2(CO)_9]}\) contain bridged CO ligands, while \(\mathrm{[Mn_2(CO)_{10}]}\) contains terminal CO ligands only.

    Stability and applications of coordination compounds
  • Stability of a coordination compound in solution refers to the extent of association between metal ion and ligands. A larger stability constant means a larger proportion of complex exists in solution.
  • Stepwise stability constants are written for successive ligand addition: \(\mathrm{M+L\rightleftharpoons ML}\), \(\mathrm{ML+L\rightleftharpoons ML_2}\), and so on. For \(\mathrm{M+4L\rightleftharpoons ML_4}\), the overall stability constant is \(\beta_4=K_1K_2K_3K_4\).
  • The dissociation constant is the reciprocal of the formation constant, so \(K_d=\dfrac{1}{\beta_n}\). Higher \(\beta_n\) means greater stability and smaller dissociation constant.
  • Chelating ligands usually form more stable complexes than comparable monodentate ligands because they bind through multiple donor atoms and form ring structures.
  • Coordination compounds are important in extraction and purification of metals. Gold and silver can be extracted through cyanide complex formation, and nickel can be purified through volatile \(\mathrm{Ni(CO)_4}\).
  • In qualitative analysis, complex formation helps identify or separate ions. Examples include \(\mathrm{AgCl}\) dissolving in ammonia to give \(\mathrm{[Ag(NH_3)_2]^+}\), \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) giving deep blue \(\mathrm{[Cu(NH_3)_4]^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Ni^{2+}}\) forming a red complex with dimethylglyoxime.
  • Biological examples include chlorophyll as a magnesium complex, haemoglobin as an iron complex, and vitamin \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) as a cobalt complex.

    Common exam workflow for coordination compounds
  • For any coordination compound, first calculate oxidation state, then \(d\)-count, then ligand strength, then geometry, and finally magnetic behaviour or isomerism. This order prevents most common mistakes.
  • For magnetic questions, determine \(n\), the number of unpaired electrons, before using \(\mu=\sqrt{n(n+2)}\ \mathrm{BM}\). The identity of the metal alone is not enough; ligand strength and geometry can change the spin state.
  • For isomerism questions, first identify the geometry and ligand pattern. Then match with standard cases such as \(\mathrm{MA_4B_2}\), \(\mathrm{MA_3B_3}\), \(\mathrm{MA_5B}\), and \(\mathrm{[M(en)_2a_2]}\).
  • For \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\) precipitation questions, count only ionisable chlorides outside the coordination sphere. Then use coordination number to place remaining ligands inside the bracket.
  • For CFT configuration questions, do not write the configuration before deciding whether the ligand is weak field or strong field. \(\mathrm{F^-}\), \(\mathrm{Cl^-}\), and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) usually give high spin; \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), \(\mathrm{CO}\), \(en\), and often oxalate with high-charge metal ions favour pairing.

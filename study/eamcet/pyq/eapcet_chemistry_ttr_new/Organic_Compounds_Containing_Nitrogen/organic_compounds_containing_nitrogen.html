    The given compound is benzamide, \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CONH_2}\).

    1. Formation of \(P\) with \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) followed by \(\mathrm{H_2O}\)

      \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) is a strong reducing agent. It reduces amides \(\mathrm{RCONH_2}\) to amines \(\mathrm{RCH_2NH_2}\).

      So:

      \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CONH_2 \xrightarrow[\ H_2O\ ]{LiAlH_4} C_6H_5CH_2NH_2}\)

      Therefore, \(P\) is benzylamine, \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CH_2NH_2}\).

    2. Formation of \(Q\) with \(\mathrm{Br_2/NaOH}\)

      This is the Hofmann bromamide reaction (also called Hofmann degradation).

      In this reaction, a primary amide \(\mathrm{RCONH_2}\) gives a primary amine \(\mathrm{RNH_2}\) with loss of one carbon atom.

      So:

      \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CONH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/NaOH} C_6H_5NH_2}\)

      Therefore, \(Q\) is aniline, \(\mathrm{C_6H_5NH_2}\).

    3. Hence the correct option is

      \(\mathrm{P = C_6H_5CH_2NH_2,\quad Q = C_6H_5NH_2}\)

    Reaction summary:

    • \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CONH_2 \xrightarrow[\ H_2O\ ]{LiAlH_4} C_6H_5CH_2NH_2}\)
    • \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CONH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/NaOH} C_6H_5NH_2}\)

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{(b)\ \mathrm{P = C_6H_5CH_2NH_2;\ Q = C_6H_5NH_2}}\)

    Why this method works:

    • \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) reduces the carbonyl group of an amide to a methylene group.
    • So the amide carbonyl carbon remains in the molecule, giving:

      \(\mathrm{RCONH_2 \rightarrow RCH_2NH_2}\)

    • In Hofmann bromamide reaction, the carbonyl carbon is removed from the chain.
    • So:

      \(\mathrm{RCONH_2 \rightarrow RNH_2}\)

    • That is why the two reagents give different amines from the same amide.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • Reduction of amides:

      \(\mathrm{RCONH_2 \xrightarrow[\ H_2O\ ]{LiAlH_4} RCH_2NH_2}\)

    • Hofmann bromamide reaction:

      \(\mathrm{RCONH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/NaOH} RNH_2}\)

    • Hofmann bromamide reaction gives a primary amine with one carbon less than the amide.
    • \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) does not remove the carbonyl carbon; it reduces it.
    • This is a very common comparison in amines conversion questions.

    Helpful memory trick:

    • \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\): keeps the carbon chain, converts \(\mathrm{CO}\) into \(\mathrm{CH_2}\)
    • \(\mathrm{Br_2/NaOH}\): removes one carbon

    1. Confusing reduction of amide with Hofmann bromamide reaction.
    2. Forgetting that Hofmann reaction gives amine with one carbon less.

    The reaction sequence has three standard named/conceptual steps. We identify each intermediate one by one.

    1. Step 1: Diazonium salt to chlorobenzene

      The starting compound is benzenediazonium chloride, \(\mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^-}\).

      Therefore, \(X =\) chlorobenzene.

    2. Step 2: Chlorobenzene to toluene

      Now chlorobenzene reacts with \(\mathrm{CH_3Cl}\) in presence of \(\mathrm{Na/dry\ ether}\).

      This is the Wurtz-Fittig reaction, in which an aryl halide and an alkyl halide couple together.

      General form:

      \(\mathrm{ArX + RX + 2Na \rightarrow ArR + 2NaX}\)

      So here:

      \(\mathrm{C_6H_5Cl + CH_3Cl + 2Na \rightarrow C_6H_5CH_3 + 2NaCl}\)

      Thus, \(Y =\) toluene.

    3. Step 3: Chlorination of toluene in dark with \(\mathrm{Cl_2/Fe}\)

      The reagent \(\mathrm{Cl_2/Fe}\) in dark indicates electrophilic aromatic substitution on the benzene ring, not free-radical side-chain chlorination.

      The \(\mathrm{-CH_3}\) group is an activating, ortho-para directing group.

      Therefore chlorination occurs mainly at the ortho and para positions, with the para product preferred here.

      So:

      \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 \xrightarrow[\text{dark}]{Cl_2/Fe} p\text{-}chlorotoluene}\)

      Hence \(Z =\) \(p\)-chlorotoluene.

    \(\boxed{(b)\ \text{\(p\)-chlorotoluene}}\)

    • \(\mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow[HCl]{Cu_2Cl_2} C_6H_5Cl + N_2}\)

    • \(\mathrm{Cl_2/Fe}\) or \(\mathrm{Cl_2/FeCl_3}\) is the condition for electrophilic substitution on the aromatic ring.

      Coupling of an aryl halide and an alkyl halide using sodium in dry ether.

      \(\mathrm{ArX + RX + 2Na \rightarrow ArR + 2NaX}\)

      \(\mathrm{Cl_2/Fe}\) or \(\mathrm{Cl_2/FeCl_3}\) gives electrophilic aromatic substitution.

    • Side-chain halogenation:

      \(\mathrm{Cl_2/h\nu}\) acts at the benzylic position by radical substitution.

    Solution:

    1. What is being asked?

      This question is asking which reagent helps us tell apart 1°, 2° and 3° amines.

      The standard test for this is the Hinsberg test, which uses Hinsberg’s reagent (benzene sulphonyl chloride, usually written as \(C_6H_5SO_2Cl\)) in the presence of alkali.

    2. Why Hinsberg’s reagent works

      Amines behave differently depending on how many alkyl groups are attached to nitrogen.

      • Primary amine \((1^\circ)\):

        It has two hydrogens not fully replaced in the sense that nitrogen still has one removable hydrogen in the product formed after reaction.

        It reacts with Hinsberg’s reagent to form a sulphonamide that still contains an acidic hydrogen.

        So this product dissolves in alkali.

      • Secondary amine \((2^\circ)\):

        It also forms a sulphonamide, but now there is no acidic hydrogen left on nitrogen.

        So the product is not soluble in alkali.

      • Tertiary amine \((3^\circ)\):

        It has no hydrogen attached to nitrogen, so it does not form sulphonamide with Hinsberg’s reagent.

        So it behaves differently from both primary and secondary amines.

    3. Easy comparison table
      • Primary amine:

        Reacts and gives product soluble in alkali

      • Secondary amine:

        Reacts and gives product insoluble in alkali

      • Tertiary amine:

        Does not react normally in this test

    4. Why the other options are wrong
      • Fehling’s reagent:

        Mainly used to test aldehydes, especially aliphatic aldehydes.

      • Tollen’s reagent:

        Used for aldehydes; gives silver mirror test.

      • Lucas reagent:

        Used to distinguish primary, secondary and tertiary alcohols, not amines.

      • Hinsberg’s reagent:

        This is the correct one for distinguishing amines.

    Core theory you should know:

    • Hinsberg reagent:

      \(C_6H_5SO_2Cl\) (benzene sulphonyl chloride)

    • Purpose:

      Used to distinguish \(1^\circ\), \(2^\circ\), and \(3^\circ\) amines.

    • Primary amine reaction:

      \(RNH_2 + C_6H_5SO_2Cl \rightarrow C_6H_5SO_2NHR\)

      The product has one acidic H, so it dissolves in alkali.

    • Secondary amine reaction:

      \(R_2NH + C_6H_5SO_2Cl \rightarrow C_6H_5SO_2NR_2\)

      No acidic H is left, so it does not dissolve in alkali.

    • Tertiary amine:

      \(R_3N\) has no H on nitrogen, so it does not form the same sulphonamide in this test.

    Simple way to remember:

    • Lucas \(\rightarrow\) alcohols
    • Tollen’s / Fehling’s \(\rightarrow\) aldehydes
    • Hinsberg \(\rightarrow\) amines

    1-minute solving trick:

    • See the words primary, secondary, tertiary amines.
    • Immediately think: Hinsberg test.
    • Do not get confused with Lucas reagent, because that belongs to alcohols.

Organic Chemistry Some Basic Principles Techniques and Hydrocarbons

    Pattern map from PYQs
  • Repeated 2025–2023 clusters: nomenclature, carbocation stability, hyperconjugation / resonance effects, aromaticity, peroxide effect, ozonolysis backward-tracing, Friedel–Crafts alkylation/acylation, Fries rearrangement, Kjeldahl / Lassaigne / phosphorus tests.
  • 2023 strengthened three especially frequent paths: alkene / alkyne oxidation products, benzylic oxidation \(\rightarrow\) nitration / named reactions, and aldehyde/alcohol-priority nomenclature.
  • Very frequent solve-path: hydrocarbon / haloalkane \(\rightarrow\) elimination \(\rightarrow\) alkene or alkyne \(\rightarrow\) addition / ozonolysis / aromatic conversion.
  • Aromatic side-chain and ring chemistry remain very high ROI: activating / deactivating groups, Friedel–Crafts, Etard, Fries, meta-direction, benzylic oxidation, substituted benzene naming.
  • Physical-property PYQs repeatedly reduce to just two drivers: hydrogen bonding and branching.

    Fast comparison rules
  • Boiling point: alcohols \(>\) ethers / alkanes because of H-bonding; among isomeric alcohols, less branching \( \Rightarrow \) higher b.p.
  • Alkanes: more branching \( \Rightarrow \) lower boiling point.
  • Carbocation stability shortcut: benzylic / allylic \(>\) \(3^\circ\) \(>\) \(2^\circ\) \(>\) \(1^\circ\) \(>\) vinylic \(>\) ethynyl.
  • \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) reactivity follows carbocation stability after leaving-group loss.
  • Acidity of hydrocarbons: \(sp\) carbon \(>\) \(sp^2\) carbon \(>\) \(sp^3\) carbon.
  • Terminal alkyne has acidic H; internal alkyne does not.

    Nomenclature and isomer-count shortcuts
  • In naming, suffix priority beats unsaturation locant priority: \(-CN\) or \(>C{=}O\) or \(-OH\) or \(-CHO\) can force numbering direction.
  • For unsaturated alcohols, \(-OH\) gets the lower locant before double bond.
  • For aldehydes, the aldehyde carbon must be included in the parent chain and gets locant 1 automatically.
  • For multifunctional compounds, ketone suffix outranks alcohol and amino prefixes.
  • For substituted benzene, use lowest set of locants; if parent group exists, fix it first at position 1 where required.
  • For isomer-count questions, first count structural isomers, then separately test chirality / geometrical isomerism.

    Electronic effects and resonance shortcuts
  • Hyperconjugation needs at least one \(\beta\)-C–H bond next to a \(\pi\)-system / empty \(p\)-orbital; if that adjacent carbon has no H, hyperconjugation is absent.
  • Hyperconjugation of alkyl C–H bonds with benzene \(\pi\)-electrons is still hyperconjugation, not inductive or electromeric effect.
  • Resonance-contributor stability order: complete octet + minimum charge separation + negative charge on more electronegative atom.
  • So, in standard carbonyl resonance sets: neutral form \(>\) charged form with \(O^-\) \(>\) charged form with \(O^+\).

    Question-to-reagent mapping
  • If question says \(O_3/Zn,H_2O\), split \(C=C\) into carbonyl fragments and work backward.
  • If question says hot acidic \(KMnO_4\), use oxidative-cleavage rule: no H on alkene carbon \(\Rightarrow\) ketone; one H \(\Rightarrow\) acid / aldehyde depending on conditions; terminal \(=CH_2\) under strong oxidation \(\Rightarrow CO_2\).
  • If question says \(HBr\) + peroxide, immediately think anti-Markovnikov radical addition.
  • If question says \(HBr\) without peroxide, think normal Markovnikov addition.
  • If question says alcoholic KOH, think elimination to alkene.
  • If question says alc. KOH then \(NaNH_2\) on vic-dihalide, think double dehydrohalogenation to alkyne.
  • If question says \(Hg^{2+}/H^+, H_2O\) on terminal alkyne, think enol \(\rightarrow\) keto tautomerism.
  • If question says dry ether + Na, think Wurtz coupling.
  • If question says \(CH_3Cl/AlCl_3\), think Friedel–Crafts alkylation; if \(CH_3COCl/AlCl_3\), think acylation.
  • If question says aryl ester + \(AlCl_3\), think Fries rearrangement to hydroxy aryl ketone.
  • If question says \(CrO_2Cl_2/CS_2\) on toluene, think Etard oxidation to benzaldehyde.
  • If question says \(Zn(Hg)/HCl\), think Clemmensen reduction of \(C{=}O\) to \(CH_2\).

    Element tests and analysis
  • Lassaigne test: Prussian blue \( \Rightarrow \) nitrogen present.
  • \( \mathrm{Na + C + N \rightarrow NaCN} \)
  • Kjeldahl works for ordinary amine-type nitrogen; be cautious with exceptions such as ring / nitro-type forms.
  • Phosphorus in organic compound on oxidation gives phosphate; with \(HNO_3\) and ammonium molybdate, yellow precipitate confirms phosphate.
  • Back-titration pattern: initial acid − leftover acid \(=\) acid consumed by liberated \(NH_3\).

    Absolute-must reaction equations from PYQs
  • \( \mathrm{CH_2{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/CCl_4} BrCH_2{-}CH_2Br} \)
  • \( \mathrm{BrCH_2{-}CH_2Br \xrightarrow[(ii)\ NaNH_2]{(i)\ alc.\ KOH} HC{\equiv}CH} \)
  • \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH \xrightarrow{H_2O,\ Hg^{2+}/H^+} CH_2{=}CHOH \rightleftharpoons CH_3CHO} \)
  • \( \mathrm{RC{\equiv}CH \xrightarrow{H_2O,\ Hg^{2+}/H^+} enol \rightleftharpoons ketone} \)
  • \( \mathrm{3HC{\equiv}CH \rightarrow C_6H_6} \)
  • \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow{HBr/ROOR} CH_3CH_2CH_2Br} \)
  • \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + C_2H_5Cl \xrightarrow{AlCl_3} C_6H_5C_2H_5 + HCl} \)
  • \( \mathrm{ArOCOPh \xrightarrow{AlCl_3} o/p\text{-}HO{-}Ar{-}COPh} \) (Fries rearrangement)
  • \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 \xrightarrow{CrO_2Cl_2/CS_2,\ H_3O^+} C_6H_5CHO} \) (Etard reaction)
  • \( \mathrm{ArCOCH_3 \xrightarrow{Zn(Hg)/HCl} ArCH_2CH_3} \) (Clemmensen reduction)
  • \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow{Zn,\ \Delta} C_6H_6} \)
  • \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow{CHCl_3/NaOH,\ then\ H^+} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4CHO} \) (Reimer–Tiemann)
  • \( \mathrm{C_6H_5ONa \xrightarrow{CO_2,\ then\ H^+} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COOH} \) (Kolbe–Schmitt)

    Aromatic rules and traps
  • Aromaticity check: cyclic + planar + continuous conjugation + \((4n+2)\pi\) electrons.
  • \(-R\) groups such as \(-COCH_3,\ -COOCH_3,\ -SO_3H,\ -CHO,\ -NO_2,\ -COOH\) deactivate ring.
  • \(+R\)-donating groups such as \(-OH,\ -NH_2,\ -OR\) and acylamino-type groups activate ring.
  • Primary carbocation generated in Friedel–Crafts alkylation can rearrange; normal propyl source can still give cumene.
  • \(-CHO\) and \(-COOH\) are meta-directing; nitration after Etard oxidation or benzylic oxidation often gives meta product.
  • Do not confuse electrophilic substitution with electrophilic addition.

    Common traps / mistakes
  • Do not let alkene locant outrank a higher-priority suffix group in IUPAC naming.
  • Do not forget that hyperconjugation needs an adjacent C–H bond.
  • Do not treat every oxidation of toluene / alkyl benzene as controlled oxidation; reagent matters.
  • Do not forget that HBr alone and HBr/peroxide give different regioselectivity and mechanism.
  • Do not count only structural isomers when the question says “including stereoisomers”.
  • In soda-lime decarboxylation, product alkane has one carbon less than the sodium carboxylate.
  • When resonance structures are compared, neutral form usually dominates over charged forms.

    Structural representations, classification and nomenclature
  • Organic structures can appear as complete structural formulae, condensed formulae and bond-line formulae; this topic repeatedly tests recognition across all three.
  • Classify hydrocarbons first into alkanes, alkenes, alkynes and aromatic hydrocarbons.
  • In IUPAC naming, first identify the principal functional group, then choose the parent chain / ring, then assign numbering to give the suffix group the lowest possible locant.
  • Important PYQ-led rule: \(-OH\) outranks unsaturation in numbering of unsaturated alcohols.
  • Important PYQ-led rule: aldehyde carbon must be part of the parent chain and fixes numbering at carbon 1.
  • Important PYQ-led rule: ketone suffix outranks alcohol and amino groups in multifunctional compounds.
  • For substituted benzene nomenclature, apply lowest set of locants and correct parent-name choice.

    Electronic effects and intermediates
  • Inductive effect is permanent \(\sigma\)-bond polarization; resonance effect is delocalisation through conjugation.
  • Hyperconjugation involves delocalisation of a \(\sigma\)(C–H) bond adjacent to a positive charge or \(\pi\)-system.
  • 2023 PYQ-led refinement: if the carbon next to the \(\pi\)-system / aromatic ring / empty \(p\)-orbital has no hydrogen, hyperconjugation is absent.
  • \(-R\) effect was directly tested using benzaldehyde-type resonance structures.
  • Carbocation stability increases with resonance and hyperconjugation: benzylic / allylic \(>\) \(3^\circ\) \(>\) \(2^\circ\) \(>\) \(1^\circ\) \(>\) vinylic \(>\) ethynyl.
  • Resonance-contributor stability order follows: complete octet \(>\) minimum charge separation \(>\) negative charge on more electronegative atom.

    Element analysis and tests
  • Lassaigne sodium fusion converts covalent nitrogen into ionic \(\mathrm{CN^-}\); Prussian blue confirms nitrogen.
  • Kjeldahl estimation converts suitable nitrogen compounds into \(NH_3\), then uses absorption and titration.
  • PYQ-led addition: phosphorus in organic compounds on oxidation gives phosphate; with \(HNO_3\) and ammonium molybdate it gives a yellow phosphomolybdate precipitate.
  • syllabus-supported, not PYQ-led: Quantitative elemental analysis beyond N/P should still be revised from NCERT examples.

    Purification methods
  • Steam distillation is used for steam-volatile, water-immiscible or sparingly soluble organic compounds.
  • Reduced-pressure distillation is used for high-boiling liquids that decompose near their normal boiling point.
  • PYQ-led trap: do not choose steam distillation for fully miscible liquid pairs.

    Alkanes
  • Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons; physical properties depend strongly on size and branching.
  • Boiling-point rule among alkane isomers: straight chain \(>\) branched isomer.
  • Wurtz reaction couples alkyl halides using sodium in dry ether to form higher alkanes.
  • Controlled oxidation of alkanes is an important factual area: current PYQs tested methane \(\rightarrow\) methanol and methane \(\rightarrow\) methanal catalyst mapping.
  • 2023 PYQ-led addition: aromatisation of higher alkanes to aromatic compounds is a directly used conversion in objective chains.
  • syllabus-supported, not PYQ-led: revise halogenation free-radical mechanism, pyrolysis and isomerisation separately.

    Alkenes
  • Alkenes undergo electrophilic addition because the \(\pi\)-bond is electron-rich.
  • Without peroxide, \(HBr\) addition follows Markovnikov rule; with peroxide, \(HBr\) shows anti-Markovnikov addition by free-radical mechanism.
  • \(O_3/Zn,H_2O\) cleaves the double bond into carbonyl compounds and is one of the most repeated reverse-tracing tools in PYQs.
  • Hot acidic \(KMnO_4\) oxidative cleavage pattern is high value: no H on alkene carbon \(\Rightarrow\) ketone; H present \(\Rightarrow\) acid / aldehyde depending on conditions; terminal \(=CH_2\) under strong oxidation \(\Rightarrow CO_2\).
  • Alcohol dehydration commonly gives alkene; subsequent addition / ozonolysis often forms multi-step PYQ sequences.
  • Geometrical isomerism in alkenes requires different groups on each double-bond carbon.

    Alkynes
  • Terminal alkynes have acidic hydrogen; internal alkynes do not.
  • Acidity order depends on hybridisation: \(sp > sp^2 > sp^3\).
  • Vicinal dihalides can form alkynes by successive dehydrohalogenation.
  • Hydration of terminal alkynes with \(Hg^{2+}/H^+\) first gives enol, then tautomerises to carbonyl compound.
  • Ethyne hydration finally gives ethanal; propyne hydration finally gives acetone.
  • Trimerisation of acetylene to benzene is a very high-yield conversion and appears repeatedly in multistep reaction chains.

    Aromatic hydrocarbons and substitutions
  • Aromaticity requires cyclic, planar, conjugated system with \((4n+2)\pi\) electrons.
  • Benzene usually undergoes electrophilic substitution, not addition.
  • Activating / deactivating groups strongly influence ring reactivity and orientation.
  • PYQ-led examples: \(-OH,\ -NH_2,\ -OR\) activate; \(-CHO,\ -COOH,\ -NO_2,\ -SO_3H\) deactivate.
  • \(-CHO\) and \(-COOH\) are meta-directing, so nitration after benzylic oxidation often gives meta product.
  • Substituted benzene naming and lowest-locant choice are repeatedly tested in this topic.

    Friedel-Crafts, Fries and benzylic chemistry
  • Friedel–Crafts alkylation introduces an alkyl group using alkyl halide / \(AlCl_3\).
  • Primary carbocation rearrangement can occur during Friedel–Crafts alkylation, so normal propyl source can give isopropylbenzene (cumene).
  • Friedel–Crafts acylation introduces \(RCO-\) group through acylium ion electrophile and does not rearrange like a simple carbocation.
  • Fries rearrangement: aryl ester \(+\mathrm{AlCl_3}\rightarrow\) ortho/para hydroxy aryl ketone.
  • Benzylic oxidation is highly important: controlled oxidation can stop at benzaldehyde; stronger oxidation gives benzoic acid.
  • Etard reaction specifically means toluene \(+\ CrO_2Cl_2 \rightarrow\) benzaldehyde after hydrolysis.
  • Clemmensen reduction uses \(Zn(Hg)/HCl\) to reduce aryl ketone carbonyl to methylene.

    Named aromatic and substitution sequences
  • Reimer–Tiemann reaction: phenol \(+\ CHCl_3/NaOH \rightarrow\) salicylaldehyde after workup.
  • Kolbe–Schmitt reaction: sodium phenoxide \(+\ CO_2 \rightarrow\) salicylic acid after acidification.
  • Phenol with zinc dust gives benzene.
  • Diazotisation followed by Sandmeyer replacement can convert amino group to chloro group on aromatic ring.
  • PYQ-led chain to remember: benzoic acid \(\rightarrow\) m-nitrobenzoic acid \(\rightarrow\) m-aminobenzoic acid \(\rightarrow\) m-chlorobenzoic acid.

    Carbonyl and follow-up sequences
  • Grignard reagent adds carbon skeleton to carbonyl compound and, after hydrolysis, gives alcohol.
  • Primary alcohol on Cu / 573 K gives aldehyde by dehydrogenation; secondary alcohol gives ketone.
  • Aldehydes with \(\alpha\)-hydrogen undergo aldol condensation in dilute base and, on heating, form \(\alpha,\beta\)-unsaturated aldehydes / ketones.
  • Current PYQs repeatedly combine hydroboration-oxidation, Cu dehydrogenation, ozonolysis and aldol condensation in one chain.

    Physical properties and composition calculations
  • Boiling-point comparisons in this topic reduce mainly to hydrogen bonding, molar mass and branching.
  • Alcohols boil much higher than ethers and alkanes because of intermolecular H-bonding.
  • Once organic product is identified, percentage-composition questions use: \( \mathrm{\%\ element = \dfrac{mass\ of\ element\ in\ one\ mole}{molar\ mass\ of\ compound}\times100} \).
  • PYQ-led example: ethylbenzene from Friedel–Crafts alkylation gives \(90.6\%\) carbon.

    Final exam cue
  • If the question is conversion-based, identify the reagent class first: elimination, Markovnikov / anti-Markovnikov addition, oxidation, ozonolysis, Friedel–Crafts, Fries, hydroboration-oxidation, Cu dehydrogenation, aldol, Etard, Clemmensen or Sandmeyer.
  • If the question is comparison-based, ask whether the driver is branching, H-bonding, carbocation stability, aromaticity, hybridisation acidity, or functional-group priority.
  • If the question is test-based, directly map: Prussian blue \(\Rightarrow N\), yellow phosphomolybdate \(\Rightarrow P\), Tollen’s \(\Rightarrow\) aldehyde, 2,4-DNP \(\Rightarrow C{=}O\), iodoform \(\Rightarrow CH_3CO-\) or suitable alcohol precursor.

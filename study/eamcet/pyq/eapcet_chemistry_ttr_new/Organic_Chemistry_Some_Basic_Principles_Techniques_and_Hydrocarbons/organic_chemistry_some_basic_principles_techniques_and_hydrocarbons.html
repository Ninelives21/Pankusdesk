Organic Chemistry Some Basic Principles Techniques and Hydrocarbons

    Pattern map from PYQs
  • Repeated 2025–2022 clusters: nomenclature, carbocation / radical stability, hyperconjugation / resonance effects, aromaticity, peroxide effect, ozonolysis backward-tracing, Friedel–Crafts alkylation/acylation, Fries rearrangement, Kjeldahl / Lassaigne / phosphorus tests.
  • 2022 strengthens three more recurring clusters: Wurtz / dry ether, partial reduction of alkynes (Lindlar vs \(Na/NH_3\)), and electrophilic reactivity comparisons of aromatic rings.
  • Very frequent solve-path: hydrocarbon / haloalkane \(\rightarrow\) elimination \(\rightarrow\) alkene or alkyne \(\rightarrow\) addition / oxidation / aromatic conversion.
  • Aromatic side-chain and ring chemistry remain very high ROI: activating / deactivating groups, Friedel–Crafts, Etard, Fries, meta-direction, benzylic oxidation, substituted benzene naming.
  • Physical-property PYQs repeatedly reduce to hydrogen bonding, branching, hybridisation or electron-donating/withdrawing effects.

    Fast comparison rules
  • Boiling point: alcohols \(>\) ethers / alkanes because of H-bonding; among isomeric alcohols, less branching \( \Rightarrow \) higher b.p.
  • Alkanes: more branching \( \Rightarrow \) lower boiling point.
  • Carbocation stability shortcut: benzylic / allylic \(>\) \(3^\circ\) \(>\) \(2^\circ\) \(>\) \(1^\circ\) \(>\) vinylic \(>\) ethynyl.
  • Radical stability follows a similar hyperconjugation/resonance trend: benzylic / allylic \(>\) \(3^\circ\) \(>\) \(2^\circ\) \(>\) \(1^\circ\) \(>\) methyl.
  • \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) reactivity follows carbocation stability after leaving-group loss.
  • Acidity of hydrocarbons: \(sp\) carbon \(>\) \(sp^2\) carbon \(>\) \(sp^3\) carbon.
  • Acidic strength reminder from 2022: alkynyl-substituted carboxylic acid \(>\) alkenyl-substituted acid \(>\) alkyl carboxylic acid \(>\) alcohol.
  • Terminal alkyne has acidic H; internal alkyne does not.

    Nomenclature and isomer-count shortcuts
  • In naming, suffix priority beats unsaturation locant priority: \(-CN\) or \(>C{=}O\) or \(-OH\) or \(-CHO\) can force numbering direction.
  • For unsaturated alcohols, \(-OH\) gets the lower locant before double bond.
  • For aldehydes, the aldehyde carbon must be included in the parent chain and gets locant 1 automatically.
  • For multifunctional compounds, ketone suffix outranks alcohol and amino prefixes.
  • For substituted benzene, use lowest set of locants; if parent group exists, fix it first at position 1 where required.
  • For branched alkanes, choose the real longest chain first; do not prefer the “prettier” branching pattern.
  • For isomer-count questions, first count structural isomers, then separately test chirality / geometrical isomerism.

    Electronic effects and resonance shortcuts
  • Hyperconjugation needs at least one \(\beta\)-C–H bond next to a \(\pi\)-system / empty \(p\)-orbital; if that adjacent carbon has no H, hyperconjugation is absent.
  • Hyperconjugation of alkyl C–H bonds with benzene \(\pi\)-electrons is still hyperconjugation, not inductive or electromeric effect.
  • Resonance-contributor stability order: complete octet + minimum charge separation + negative charge on more electronegative atom.
  • So, in standard carbonyl resonance sets: neutral form \(>\) charged form with \(O^-\) \(>\) charged form with \(O^+\).

    Question-to-reagent mapping
  • If question says \(O_3/Zn,H_2O\), split \(C=C\) into carbonyl fragments and work backward.
  • If question says hot acidic \(KMnO_4\), use oxidative-cleavage rule: no H on alkene carbon \(\Rightarrow\) ketone; one H \(\Rightarrow\) acid / aldehyde depending on conditions; terminal \(=CH_2\) under strong oxidation \(\Rightarrow CO_2\).
  • If question says cold dilute alkaline \(KMnO_4\), think vicinal dihydroxylation; count new \(-OH\) groups carefully.
  • If question says \(HBr\) + peroxide, immediately think anti-Markovnikov radical addition.
  • If question says \(HBr\) without peroxide, think normal Markovnikov addition.
  • If question says alcoholic KOH, think elimination to alkene.
  • If question says alc. KOH then \(NaNH_2\) on vic-dihalide, think double dehydrohalogenation to alkyne.
  • If question says \(Hg^{2+}/H^+, H_2O\) on terminal alkyne, think enol \(\rightarrow\) keto tautomerism.
  • If question says Lindlar catalyst, think partial reduction to cis-alkene.
  • If question says \(Na/liquid\ NH_3\), think dissolving-metal reduction to trans-alkene.
  • If question says dry ether + Na, think Wurtz coupling.
  • If question says \(CH_3Cl/AlCl_3\), think Friedel–Crafts alkylation; if \(CH_3COCl/AlCl_3\), think acylation.
  • If question says aryl ester + \(AlCl_3\), think Fries rearrangement to hydroxy aryl ketone.
  • If question says \(CrO_2Cl_2/CS_2\) on toluene, think Etard oxidation to benzaldehyde.
  • If question says \(Zn(Hg)/HCl\), think Clemmensen reduction of \(C{=}O\) to \(CH_2\).
  • If question says excess \(Cl_2\), \(h\nu\) on benzene, think BHC \((C_6H_6Cl_6)\), not substitution.

    Element tests and analysis
  • Lassaigne test: Prussian blue \( \Rightarrow \) nitrogen present.
  • \( \mathrm{Na + C + N \rightarrow NaCN} \)
  • Kjeldahl works for ordinary amine-type nitrogen; be cautious with exceptions such as ring / nitro-type forms.
  • Phosphorus in organic compound on oxidation gives phosphate; with \(HNO_3\) and ammonium molybdate, yellow precipitate confirms phosphate.
  • Back-titration pattern: initial acid − leftover acid \(=\) acid consumed by liberated \(NH_3\).

    Aromatic rules and traps
  • Aromaticity check: cyclic + planar + continuous conjugation + \((4n+2)\pi\) electrons.
  • \(-R\) groups such as \(-COCH_3,\ -COOCH_3,\ -SO_3H,\ -CHO,\ -NO_2,\ -COOH\) deactivate ring.
  • \(+R\)-donating groups such as \(-OH,\ -NH_2,\ -OR\) and acylamino-type groups activate ring.
  • Electrophilic substitution reactivity shortcut: phenol \(>\) toluene \(>\) benzene \(>\) chlorobenzene.
  • Primary carbocation generated in Friedel–Crafts alkylation can rearrange; normal propyl source can still give cumene.
  • \(-CHO\) and \(-COOH\) are meta-directing; nitration after Etard oxidation or benzylic oxidation often gives meta product.
  • Do not confuse electrophilic substitution with electrophilic addition.

    Common traps / mistakes
  • Do not let alkene locant outrank a higher-priority suffix group in IUPAC naming.
  • Do not forget that hyperconjugation needs an adjacent C–H bond.
  • Do not treat every oxidation of toluene / alkyl benzene as controlled oxidation; reagent matters.
  • Do not forget that HBr alone and HBr/peroxide give different regioselectivity and mechanism.
  • Do not count only structural isomers when the question says “including stereoisomers”.
  • In soda-lime decarboxylation, product alkane has one carbon less than the sodium carboxylate.
  • When resonance structures are compared, neutral form usually dominates over charged forms.
  • Under UV, benzene can undergo addition with chlorine to BHC instead of normal substitution.

    Variables used
  • \(R, R', Ar\): alkyl / another alkyl / aryl (usually phenyl) group
  • \(X\): halogen atom
  • \(\mathrm{Nu^-}\) or Nu:: nucleophile, an electron-rich species that donates an electron pair
  • \(E^+\): electrophile, an electron-deficient species that accepts an electron pair
  • \(\sigma\)-bond, \(\pi\)-bond: sigma bond and pi bond
  • \(sp^3, sp^2, sp\): hybridisation states of carbon
  • \(h\nu\): light energy, usually sunlight / UV light
  • \(\Delta\): heating
  • \(\mathrm{aq.}\), anhy.: aqueous and anhydrous
  • \([O]\), \([H]\): oxidation and reduction shorthand
  • \(o\)-, \(m\)-, \(p\)-: ortho, meta, para positions in benzene

    Organic framework and classification
  • Organic chemistry mainly deals with compounds of carbon. The huge number of organic compounds comes from catenation and the tetravalency of carbon, which lets carbon form long chains, branched chains and rings.
  • Hydrocarbons are the parent compounds of organic chemistry and are classified first into open-chain (aliphatic) and closed-chain (cyclic) compounds.
  • Open-chain compounds may be saturated (alkanes) or unsaturated (alkenes, alkynes).
  • Closed-chain compounds are divided into alicyclic, aromatic and heterocyclic classes.
  • For quick exam sorting: single bonds only \(\rightarrow\) alkane; one or more \(\mathrm{C=C}\) bonds \(\rightarrow\) alkene; one or more \(\mathrm{C \equiv C}\) bonds \(\rightarrow\) alkyne; benzene-type conjugated ring \(\rightarrow\) aromatic.

    Structural representation and tetravalency of carbon
  • Carbon is tetravalent, so it forms four covalent bonds and gives stable chain and ring structures.
  • In saturated compounds, carbon is usually \(sp^3\)-hybridised with tetrahedral arrangement; in alkenes it is usually \(sp^2\)-hybridised and planar around the double bond; in alkynes it is \(sp\)-hybridised and linear.
  • Use condensed formulae, expanded structures and line-angle structures comfortably; in hydrocarbon chapters, MCQs often expect instant recognition from compact skeletal forms.
  • Very common hybridisation clue from PYQs: benzene ring carbons are \(sp^2\), carbonyl carbon is \(sp^2\), saturated alkyl carbon is \(sp^3\), and carbon atoms of a triple bond are \(sp\).

    IUPAC nomenclature high-yield rules
  • Build the name in this order: prefix(es) + word root + primary suffix + position(s), with substituent positions written by lowest locants.
  • Word root depends on number of carbons: meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-, hept-, oct-, non-, dec-.
  • Primary suffix: -ane for saturated hydrocarbons, -ene for double bond, -yne for triple bond.
  • Longest chain rule: choose the longest chain containing the multiple bond if present.
  • Lowest locant rule: number from the end that gives the multiple bond first priority, then substituents the lowest set.
  • If two main chains are possible, prefer the one containing more substituents when length is equal.
  • Identical substituents use di-, tri-, tetra-, but alphabetical order ignores these multiplicative prefixes.
  • Cyclic compounds use the prefix cyclo-.
  • Benzene derivatives are often named as substituted benzenes; for disubstituted benzenes, remember \(o\)-, \(m\)-, \(p\)- naming.

    Isomerism
  • Structural isomerism means same molecular formula but different connectivity. The big ones here are chain isomerism, position isomerism and functional isomerism.
  • For alkenes and alkynes, position isomerism of the multiple bond is very important.
  • Geometrical isomerism arises in alkenes due to restricted rotation about \(\mathrm{C=C}\); cis/trans or E/Z ideas come from fixed arrangement.
  • Alkanes mainly show chain isomerism; ethane conformations are the required conformational part, with staggered more stable than eclipsed.
  • Very common counting trick from PYQs: in terminal alkynes the acidic hydrogen is present only when the triple bond is at the end, i.e. \(\mathrm{R-C \equiv C-H}\).

    Bond fission, reactive species and electron movement
  • Homolytic fission gives free radicals; each atom takes one electron. This is the bond-breaking pattern in free-radical halogenation of alkanes.
  • Heterolytic fission gives ions; one atom takes both electrons. This gives carbocations and carbanions.
  • Nucleophiles are electron-rich species such as \(\mathrm{OH^-}\), \(\mathrm{CN^-}\), \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).
  • Electrophiles are electron-deficient species such as \(\mathrm{H^+}\), \(\mathrm{NO_2^+}\), \(\mathrm{Br^+}\)-type attacking species and acylium ion \(\mathrm{RCO^+}\).
  • In reaction arrows, curved full-headed arrow shows movement of an electron pair; fish-hook arrow shows movement of a single electron in radical mechanisms.

    Electronic effects in covalent bonds
  • Inductive effect (\(+I\), \(-I\)): permanent displacement of \(\sigma\)-electrons due to electronegativity difference; it operates through single bonds and decreases with distance.
  • Resonance / mesomeric effect: delocalisation of \(\pi\)-electrons or lone pairs over adjacent p-orbitals. This strongly stabilises allylic, benzylic and aromatic systems.
  • Electromeric effect: temporary complete transfer of a \(\pi\)-electron pair in presence of an attacking reagent.
  • Hyperconjugation: delocalisation of \(\sigma(\mathrm{C-H})\) electrons of an alkyl group into an adjacent \(\pi\)-system or vacant p-orbital. This is the “no-bond resonance” idea repeatedly tested in MCQs.
  • Steric hindrance: crowding slows or prevents reaction, especially important in substitution and addition patterns.
  • Fast stability order often used in PYQs: resonance stabilisation \(>\) hyperconjugation alone; benzylic and allylic carbocations are unusually stable; vinylic carbocations are very unstable.
  • For benzene substitution, alkyl groups are generally ortho/para-directing because hyperconjugation and electron release activate the ring.

    Types of organic reactions
  • Substitution: one atom or group is replaced by another. Aliphatic compounds often undergo nucleophilic substitution; benzene typically undergoes electrophilic substitution.
  • Addition: reagent adds across a multiple bond. This is the core chemistry of alkenes and alkynes.
  • Elimination: removal of a small molecule such as \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) or HX to form a multiple bond.
  • Rearrangement: internal reorganisation of a molecular skeleton; less central here than substitution/addition/elimination, but still part of the general toolkit.
  • PYQ pattern clue: benzene + Lewis acid catalyst such as \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\) or \(\mathrm{FeCl_3}\) usually signals electrophilic aromatic substitution.

    Purification and qualitative / quantitative analysis
  • Purification of solids: crystallisation, fractional crystallisation, sublimation.
  • Purification of liquids: simple distillation, fractional distillation, distillation under reduced pressure, steam distillation.
  • Chromatography works on selective adsorption / partition between stationary and mobile phases; TLC commonly uses silica gel or alumina.
  • In qualitative analysis, carbon and hydrogen are detected by oxidation to \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).
  • Lassaigne’s test is the key test for nitrogen, sulphur and halogens.
  • Kjeldahl’s method estimates nitrogen when nitrogen is in ordinary amino-type forms, but not in nitro, azo, diazonium and many ring-nitrogen systems; this is directly PYQ-tested.
  • Dumas method is another nitrogen estimation method; Carius method is used for halogens.

    Alkanes essentials: structure, nomenclature and isomerism
  • Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with general formula \(\mathrm{C_nH_{2n+2}}\).
  • All carbon atoms in alkanes are \(sp^3\)-hybridised; bond angle is about \(109^\circ 28'\).
  • They mainly show chain isomerism. Branching lowers surface area, so it usually lowers boiling point.
  • Conformations of ethane: staggered is more stable because torsional strain is minimum; eclipsed is less stable.
  • Physical trend worth remembering: lower alkanes are gases, mid members are liquids, higher members are solids.

    Preparation of alkanes: must-know methods and equations
  • Reduction of haloalkanes: \(\mathrm{R-X \rightarrow R-H}\)
  • Wurtz reaction: \(\mathrm{2R-X + 2Na \xrightarrow[dry\ ether]{} R-R + 2NaX}\)
  • From Grignard reagent with active hydrogen compounds: \(\mathrm{R-MgX + H_2O \rightarrow R-H + Mg(OH)X}\)
  • Hydrogenation of alkenes / alkynes: addition of \(\mathrm{H_2}\) in presence of \(\mathrm{Ni/Pt/Pd}\) gives alkane.
  • Soda-lime decarboxylation: \(\mathrm{RCOONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[CaO]{\Delta} RH + Na_2CO_3}\)
  • Kolbe electrolysis: \(\mathrm{2RCOO^- \xrightarrow[electrolysis]{} R-R + 2CO_2 + 2e^-}\)
  • Kolbe usually gives symmetrical alkanes, especially useful for even-carbon products.
  • PYQ-anchor: sodium acetate on Kolbe electrolysis gives ethane and carbon dioxide at the anode.

    Properties and reactions of alkanes
  • Alkanes are comparatively less reactive because they contain only strong \(\sigma\)-bonds.
  • Halogenation is the signature substitution reaction; chlorination / bromination proceeds by free-radical mechanism.
  • Free-radical chlorination has three steps: initiation, propagation, termination.
  • Controlled oxidation can convert alkane to oxygen-containing compounds under suitable catalysts and conditions.
  • Combustion: \(\mathrm{C_xH_y + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O}\); limited oxygen gives incomplete combustion and may form CO.
  • Isomerisation: straight-chain alkane \(\rightarrow\) branched alkane under suitable catalyst.
  • Aromatization: higher straight-chain alkanes can form aromatic compounds under catalytic dehydrogenation conditions; PYQ pattern: hexane \(\rightarrow\) benzene.
  • Pyrolysis / cracking: large alkanes on strong heating break into smaller alkanes and alkenes.
  • Reaction with steam is industrially important for synthesis gas / hydrogen-related processes, though less frequently tested than halogenation, pyrolysis and aromatization.

    Alkenes essentials: structure and preparation
  • Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons with general formula \(\mathrm{C_nH_{2n}}\) for one open-chain double bond.
  • Carbon atoms of the double bond are \(sp^2\)-hybridised and trigonal planar; rotation about \(\mathrm{C=C}\) is restricted, giving geometrical isomerism.
  • Preparation: dehydrohalogenation of alkyl halides, dehydration of alcohols, and dehalogenation of vicinal dihalides are the big routes.
  • Ethene is the model molecule for understanding alkene structure and reactivity.

    Chemical reactions of alkenes: the highest-yield block
  • Because the \(\pi\)-bond is weaker and electron-rich, alkenes undergo electrophilic addition easily.
  • Hydrogenation: \(\mathrm{RCH=CHR' + H_2 \xrightarrow{Ni/Pt/Pd} RCH_2-CH_2R'}\)
  • Halogenation: bromine / chlorine add across the double bond; decolourisation of bromine solution is a quick test for unsaturation.
  • Addition of water: alkene \(\rightarrow\) alcohol under acidic conditions.
  • Addition of sulphuric acid: alkene + \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) gives alkyl hydrogen sulphate, which on hydrolysis gives alcohol.
  • Addition of HX follows Markovnikov rule in normal ionic addition: in unsymmetrical alkene, H attaches to the carbon already having more H atoms.
  • Peroxide effect / Kharasch effect is the anti-Markovnikov addition of HBr only, in presence of peroxide.
  • Oxidation: mild oxidation with alkaline \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) gives vicinal diols; stronger oxidation can cleave the bond.
  • Ozonolysis breaks the double bond and helps locate its position by the carbonyl products formed.
  • Polymerization: alkene molecules add repeatedly to form polymers; ethene \(\rightarrow\) polyethene is the standard example.
  • Exam warning: anti-Markovnikov is not a general “smaller compound rule”; it is specifically the peroxide effect with HBr.

    Markovnikov, peroxide effect and ozonolysis in one place
  • Markovnikov rule: in addition of an unsymmetrical reagent to an unsymmetrical alkene, the negative part goes to the carbon with fewer hydrogens.
  • Anti-Markovnikov / peroxide effect: only with \(\mathrm{HBr}\) in presence of peroxide, due to radical pathway.
  • Ozonolysis: \(\mathrm{C=C \xrightarrow{O_3} ozonide \xrightarrow{Zn/H_2O} aldehydes/ketones}\)
  • Ozonolysis is one of the cleanest ways to infer where the double bond was present in the original alkene.

    Alkynes essentials: structure, preparation and acidity
  • Alkynes have general formula \(\mathrm{C_nH_{2n-2}}\) for one open-chain triple bond.
  • The two carbons of \(\mathrm{C \equiv C}\) are \(sp\)-hybridised and linear.
  • Acetylene (ethyne) is the model alkyne and very important.
  • Preparation of ethyne: \(\mathrm{CaC_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow HC \equiv CH + Ca(OH)_2}\)
  • Alkynes may also be prepared by double dehydrohalogenation of vicinal or geminal dihalides.
  • Terminal alkynes are acidic because the hydrogen is attached to \(sp\)-hybridised carbon, which has high s-character.
  • So terminal alkynes react with strong bases and also with ammoniacal \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\) and ammoniacal cuprous chloride to give precipitates.
  • PYQ anchor: for a given formula, count terminal alkynes separately from internal alkynes; only terminal alkynes have acidic hydrogen.

    Reactions of alkynes
  • Like alkenes, alkynes undergo addition of \(\mathrm{H_2}\), halogens, HX and water, but often in two stages because of two \(\pi\)-bonds.
  • Hydrogenation: complete hydrogenation gives alkane.
  • Addition of halogens and HX: first forms alkene derivative, then further addition can give saturated product.
  • Hydration of ethyne finally gives acetaldehyde after enol-keto tautomerism.
  • Polymerization of ethyne can give benzene under special conditions; this is a classic conversion to remember.
  • Oxidation can cleave the triple bond and give acids / carbon dioxide depending on the substrate.

    How to distinguish alkane, alkene and alkyne
  • \(\mathrm{Br_2/CCl_4}\): alkene and alkyne decolourise it; alkane does not under ordinary conditions.
  • Alkaline \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\): unsaturated hydrocarbons decolourise it; alkane does not.
  • Ammoniacal \(\mathrm{AgNO_3}\) and ammoniacal \(\mathrm{Cu_2Cl_2}\): terminal alkynes give characteristic precipitates; alkenes and alkanes do not.
  • So if three jars contain alkane, alkene and alkyne: first use bromine or \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) to separate saturated vs unsaturated, then ammoniacal silver / cuprous test to identify the terminal alkyne.

    Aromatic hydrocarbons and benzene
  • Benzene is the central aromatic hydrocarbon. The real structure is not alternating fixed single and double bonds; it is a resonance hybrid.
  • This delocalisation gives aromatic stability, which is why benzene prefers substitution over addition.
  • Each carbon in benzene is \(sp^2\)-hybridised, and the ring is planar.
  • Aromaticity is the reason benzene is unusually stable compared with expected reactivity of a normal triene.

    Preparation and properties of benzene
  • Benzene is obtained industrially from petroleum / coal-related sources; the text also notes destructive distillation of coal as a source.
  • It is a colourless liquid, burns with a sooty flame and is less reactive toward addition than ordinary unsaturated compounds because aromaticity would be lost.
  • In hydrocarbon conversions, benzene can also be reached by aromatization of higher alkanes and by polymerization / trimerization of ethyne under special conditions.

    Electrophilic substitution of benzene: must-remember reactions
  • General idea: benzene first generates / encounters an electrophile, forms a sigma-complex, then loses \(\mathrm{H^+}\) to regain aromaticity.
  • Nitration: \(\mathrm{C_6H_6 + HNO_3 \xrightarrow[conc.]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} C_6H_5NO_2 + H_2O}\)
  • Sulphonation: \(\mathrm{C_6H_6 + H_2SO_4(fuming) \rightarrow C_6H_5SO_3H + H_2O}\)
  • Halogenation: \(\mathrm{C_6H_6 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{FeCl_3/AlCl_3} C_6H_5Cl + HCl}\)
  • Friedel–Crafts alkylation: \(\mathrm{C_6H_6 + RCl \xrightarrow{AlCl_3} C_6H_5R + HCl}\)
  • Friedel–Crafts acylation: \(\mathrm{C_6H_6 + RCOCl \xrightarrow{AlCl_3} C_6H_5COR + HCl}\)
  • PYQ anchor: benzene with \(\mathrm{CH_3COCl/AlCl_3}\) gives acetophenone; remember the electrophile is the acylium ion.

    Directive influence in monosubstituted benzene
  • Groups already attached to benzene control where the next substituent enters.
  • Ortho/para directors generally donate electron density to the ring, making ortho and para positions more reactive.
  • Meta directors generally withdraw electron density strongly and make meta attack relatively favoured.
  • Alkyl groups are usually ortho/para-directing and activating.
  • Nitro group is a classic meta-directing, deactivating group.
  • This block matters a lot in product prediction MCQs.

      \(P_{\text{total}} = P_{\text{atm}}\)

Organic Chemistry Some Basic Principles Techniques and Hydrocarbons

    Exam pattern map
  • Most repeated asks here: nomenclature, electron effects, reaction-sequence decoding, alkyne acidity, Markovnikov / peroxide effect, benzene reactions, and named reactions.
  • Many questions are hidden conversion chains. First identify the reaction type, then the product.
  • If the question is image-based, first mark: functional group, unsaturation, benzylic position, and presence/absence of \(\alpha\)-H.

    Nomenclature and parent-chain rules
  • Choose the longest chain containing the principal functional group and maximum unsaturation.
  • Priority in numbering: principal functional group \(>\) multiple bond \(>\) substituents.
  • \(-OH\) gets lower locant than \(C=C\) or \(C\equiv C\).
  • Use lowest set of locants, not just lowest first number.
  • Alphabetise substituents in the final name.

    Carbon hybridisation and shape quick rules
  • \(sp\): linear, 50% \(s\)-character.
  • \(sp^2\): trigonal planar.
  • \(sp^3\): tetrahedral.
  • Benzene-ring carbons are all \(sp^2\).
  • Carbonyl carbon is \(sp^2\).
  • Both carbons of a triple bond are \(sp\).
  • If counting \(sp/sp^2/sp^3\), classify carbon-by-carbon; do not infer from formula alone.

    Electron effects quick map
  • Inductive effect acts through \(\sigma\)-bonds and decreases with distance.
  • Resonance needs conjugation and p-orbital overlap.
  • Hyperconjugation = delocalisation of adjacent \(\sigma(C-H)\) electrons.
  • \(-I\) groups usually increase acidity; \(+I\) groups usually decrease acidity.
  • Carbocation stability usually follows resonance-stabilised \(>\) \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ\).
  • If the option shows electron donation from adjacent \(C-H\) bond to \(C=C\) or \(C^+\), think hyperconjugation.

    Acidity and stability shortcuts
  • For hydrocarbon C-H acidity, remember:
    \[ sp > sp^2 > sp^3 \]
  • So:
    \[ \text{terminal alkyne} > \text{alkene} > \text{alkane} \]
  • Terminal alkynes have acidic hydrogen; internal alkynes do not.
  • Benzylic and allylic carbocations / radicals are resonance-stabilised.
  • If \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) reactivity is asked, check benzylic / benzhydryl stabilization first.

    Isomerism and counting rules
  • For alkyne counting, first list all carbon skeletons, then all distinct triple-bond positions.
  • Separate terminal vs internal alkynes only after generating all constitutional isomers.
  • Do not double-count mirror-equivalent chain drawings.
  • For alkene questions, check chain isomerism + position isomerism before geometrical isomerism.

    Must-remember equations and reagent triggers
  • Calcium carbide to acetylene:
    \[ \mathrm{CaC_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow HC\equiv CH + Ca(OH)_2} \]
  • With heavy water:
    \[ \mathrm{CaC_2 + 2D_2O \rightarrow DC\equiv CD + Ca(OD)_2} \]
  • Wurtz reaction:
    \[ \mathrm{2R-X + 2Na \xrightarrow[dry\ ether]{} R-R + 2NaX} \]
  • Markovnikov addition:
    \[ \mathrm{RCH=CH_2 + HX \rightarrow RCHX-CH_3} \]
  • Peroxide effect with HBr:
    \[ \mathrm{RCH=CH_2 + HBr \xrightarrow[peroxide]{} RCH_2-CH_2Br} \]
  • Cold alkaline \(KMnO_4\):
    \[ \mathrm{RCH=CHR \rightarrow RCH(OH)-CH(OH)R} \]
  • Dacron clue:
    \[ \mathrm{HO-CH_2-CH_2-OH + HOOC-C_6H_4-COOH \rightarrow polyester} \]

    Alkane reaction mapping
  • \(\mathrm{Na/dry\ ether}\) on haloalkane \(\rightarrow\) Wurtz reaction.
  • Free-radical halogenation is substitution, not addition.
  • Kolbe-electrolysis type clue: carboxylate salt on electrolysis gives hydrocarbon at anode.
  • Controlled oxidation / aromatization / pyrolysis questions are often conversion-sequence questions, not direct fact questions.

    Alkene reaction mapping
  • Normal HX addition follows Markovnikov’s rule.
  • Peroxide effect applies only to HBr.
  • \(\mathrm{H_2/Ni}\) adds across double bond.
  • \(\mathrm{Br_2/CCl_4}\) adds across double bond and decolourises bromine.
  • \(\mathrm{cold\ alkaline\ KMnO_4}\) gives vicinal glycol.
  • \(\mathrm{O_3}\) followed by work-up gives ozonolysis fragments.

    Alkyne reaction mapping
  • Terminal alkyne forms metal acetylide with strong base / active metal reagents.
  • Hydration of acetylene gives carbonyl compound after enol-keto tautomerism.
  • Addition of HX / \(X_2\) can occur once or twice depending on reagent excess.
  • If isotope reagent like \(D_2O\) is present, track every incoming D carefully.

    Aromatic chemistry quick rules
  • Benzene mainly undergoes electrophilic substitution.
  • Friedel-Crafts alkylation:
    \[ \mathrm{C_6H_6 + RCl \xrightarrow[ ]{AlCl_3} C_6H_5R + HCl} \]
  • Friedel-Crafts acylation:
    \[ \mathrm{C_6H_6 + RCOCl \xrightarrow[ ]{AlCl_3} C_6H_5COR + HCl} \]
  • Acylation usually does not rearrange; alkylation may rearrange.
  • Etard oxidation clue: side-chain methyl on benzene \(\rightarrow\) aldehyde.
  • Benzylic positions are unusually reactive because intermediates are resonance-stabilised.

    Named reaction trigger list
  • Etard oxidation:
    \[ \mathrm{Ar-CH_3 \xrightarrow[CS_2]{CrO_2Cl_2} Ar-CHO} \]
  • Cannizzaro reaction: aldehyde without \(\alpha\)-H + strong base.
  • Clemmensen reduction:
    \[ \mathrm{R-CO-R' \xrightarrow[ ]{Zn(Hg)/HCl} R-CH_2-R'} \]
  • If you see aldehyde with no \(\alpha\)-H, think Cannizzaro immediately.
  • If you see acylation followed by Zn(Hg)/HCl, think alkylbenzene formation.

    Purification and analysis triggers
  • Kjeldahl estimates nitrogen only when N can be converted to ammonium sulfate during digestion.
  • Reject nitro / azo / diazo-type exceptions first in MCQs.
  • These questions often test the exception rather than the normal case.

    If question asks X, immediately use Y
  • If acidity of hydrocarbon C-H is asked, immediately use:
    \[ sp > sp^2 > sp^3 \]
  • If HX addition with peroxide is asked, immediately use anti-Markovnikov and HBr-only rule.
  • If benzene + acyl chloride/\(AlCl_3\) is asked, immediately use Friedel-Crafts acylation.
  • If aldehyde + strong base and no \(\alpha\)-H is asked, immediately use Cannizzaro.
  • If carbide + water / heavy water is asked, immediately think acetylene / deuterated acetylene.
  • If an \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) substrate is compared, immediately check benzylic resonance stabilization.

    Common traps
  • Giving lower number to multiple bond when \(-OH\) should get priority.
  • Applying peroxide effect to HCl or HI.
  • Forgetting only terminal alkynes have acidic hydrogen.
  • Missing “no \(\alpha\)-H” clue for Cannizzaro.
  • Confusing Etard oxidation with full oxidation to acid.
  • Confusing hyperconjugation with resonance.
  • Failing to track isotope labels like D in product structure.

    General introduction and tetravalency of carbon
  • Carbon is tetravalent and forms stable covalent bonds with itself and many other elements.
  • This causes catenation, chain formation, branching, ring formation, and huge structural diversity.
  • Shapes depend on hybridisation: \(sp\) linear, \(sp^2\) trigonal planar, \(sp^3\) tetrahedral.
  • PYQs repeatedly connect shape and hybridisation with acidity, nomenclature, and reaction outcome.

    Structural representation and classification
  • Organic compounds may be represented by expanded, condensed, bond-line, and Lewis-type structures.
  • Classification: open-chain / cyclic; saturated / unsaturated; aliphatic / aromatic; functional-group based classes.
  • Hydrocarbons are classified into alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, and aromatic hydrocarbons.

    Nomenclature of organic compounds
  • Choose the parent chain containing the principal functional group and maximum unsaturation.
  • Number to give the principal functional group the lowest possible locant.
  • Then minimise locants for multiple bond and substituents.
  • Alphabetise substituents in the final name.
  • Live PYQs strongly reinforce alcohol-priority numbering over alkene numbering.

    Isomerism
  • Structural isomerism includes chain, position, functional, metamerism, and ring-chain cases where relevant.
  • Hydrocarbons commonly test chain and position isomerism, plus terminal vs internal alkyne distinction.
  • Geometrical isomerism occurs in alkenes when restricted rotation and suitable substitution are present.
  • Ethane conformations are limited to staggered and eclipsed forms in this syllabus block.

    Bond fission and reaction intermediates
  • Homolytic fission gives radicals; heterolytic fission gives cations and anions.
  • Nucleophiles are electron-rich species; electrophiles are electron-deficient species.
  • Arrow pushing tracks electron movement and is essential for mechanism recognition.
  • Organic reactions commonly proceed through carbocation, carbanion, radical, or cyclic transition-state pathways.

    Electron displacement effects
  • Inductive effect operates through \(\sigma\)-bonds and decreases with distance.
  • Resonance / mesomeric effect requires conjugation and orbital overlap.
  • Electromeric effect is temporary and appears only in presence of attacking reagent.
  • Hyperconjugation is delocalisation of \(\sigma(C-H)\) electrons adjacent to \(\pi\)-system or positive centre.
  • Repeated PYQ use-cases: acidity order, stability order, carbocation preference, and diagram identification.

    Purification and elemental analysis
  • Main purification methods: crystallisation, sublimation, distillation, steam distillation, differential extraction, chromatography.
  • Qualitative analysis detects \(N, S, P\), halogens after suitable conversion.
  • Quantitative analysis includes estimation of C, H, N and other elements by standard methods.
  • Kjeldahl method estimates nitrogen in suitable compounds but not all nitrogen-containing functional classes.

    Alkanes
  • General formula \(C_nH_{2n+2}\); saturated hydrocarbons with only \(\sigma\)-bonds.
  • Preparation includes hydrogenation of unsaturated hydrocarbons and Wurtz reaction.
  • Main reactions: halogenation by free-radical mechanism, combustion, controlled oxidation, isomerisation, aromatisation, steam reforming, pyrolysis.
  • Wurtz reaction and controlled oxidation both appeared in the live-read PYQs.

    Alkenes
  • General formula \(C_nH_{2n}\); contain one \(C=C\) double bond.
  • They undergo electrophilic addition reactions.
  • Addition of \(H_2\), \(X_2\), \(H_2O\), \(H_2SO_4\), and HX are standard.
  • Markovnikov addition is the default for HX; peroxide effect gives anti-Markovnikov addition only with HBr.
  • Oxidation, ozonolysis, polymerisation, and glycol formation by cold alkaline \(KMnO_4\) are important.

    Alkynes
  • General formula \(C_nH_{2n-2}\); contain triple bond and \(sp\)-hybridised carbons.
  • Terminal alkynes possess acidic hydrogen.
  • Acidity order among hydrocarbons follows \(sp > sp^2 > sp^3\).
  • Acetylene preparation from calcium carbide is very high yield in exams.
  • Addition of \(H_2\), halogen, HX, and water are standard; polymerisation is also important.

    Aromatic hydrocarbons
  • Benzene is planar, cyclic, conjugated, and aromatic; resonance stabilisation is central.
  • Electrophilic substitution is the signature reaction pattern.
  • Main reactions: nitration, sulphonation, halogenation, Friedel-Crafts alkylation, Friedel-Crafts acylation.
  • Alkylation may rearrange; acylation usually does not.
  • Acylation followed by reduction is a very common sequence in problem solving.

    Directive influence and side-chain chemistry
  • Electron-donating groups generally activate ring and direct ortho/para.
  • Strong electron-withdrawing groups generally deactivate and direct meta, except halogens which are deactivating but ortho/para-directing.
  • Benzylic position is special because radicals and carbocations there are resonance stabilised.
  • This helps explain high \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) reactivity of benzylic halides in PYQs.

    Named reactions and sequence-solving
  • Wurtz: coupling of alkyl halides with Na in dry ether.
  • Etard: oxidation of toluene side chain to benzaldehyde using \(CrO_2Cl_2\).
  • Cannizzaro: aldehydes without \(\alpha\)-hydrogen disproportionate in strong base.
  • Clemmensen: carbonyl to methylene using Zn(Hg)/HCl.
  • Hydroboration-oxidation: anti-Markovnikov alcohol formation.
  • Dacron formation: ethylene glycol + terephthalic acid / derivative.

Biomolecules

    Carbohydrates: monosaccharides and glucose reactions
  • Glucose is an aldohexose with open-chain structure \(\mathrm{CHO-(CHOH)_4-CH_2OH}\). Bromine water selectively oxidises the aldehyde group \(\mathrm{-CHO}\) to \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), giving gluconic acid.
  • Strong oxidation of glucose with concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) oxidises both terminal groups, \(\mathrm{-CHO}\) and \(\mathrm{-CH_2OH}\), to \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), giving saccharic acid. Do not assign these oxidations to the internal \(\mathrm{>CHOH}\) groups.
  • Prolonged heating of glucose with \(\mathrm{HI}\) gives \(n\)-hexane, proving that glucose has a straight six-carbon chain. If this \(n\)-hexane is further treated with \(\mathrm{Mo_2O_3}\) at \(773\ \mathrm{K}\) and \(10\text{-}20\ \mathrm{atm}\), it undergoes aromatization to benzene.
  • In Wurtz backtracking, \(n\)-hexane is obtained by coupling two straight-chain propyl halide molecules, so the required alkyl halide is \(\mathrm{CH_3CH_2CH_2Cl}\), not branched \(\mathrm{(CH_3)_2CHCl}\).
  • Kiliani-Fischer type cyanohydrin formation followed by hydrolysis increases an aldose chain by one carbon. A six-carbon aldose framework can therefore give a seven-carbon hydroxy acid such as \(2,3,4,5,6,7\)-hexahydroxyheptanoic acid.

    D/L configuration, optical rotation and cyclic sugar identification
  • In Fischer projections of sugars, D/L configuration is decided by the \(\mathrm{OH}\) group on the chiral carbon farthest from the carbonyl group: \(\mathrm{OH}\) on the right gives D-form, and \(\mathrm{OH}\) on the left gives L-form.
  • D/L configuration and \(+\)/\(-\) optical rotation are different ideas. D does not mean dextrorotatory: D-\((+)\)-glucose is dextrorotatory, but D-\((-)\)-fructose is levorotatory.
  • For fructose, the carbonyl group is at \(\mathrm{C_2}\), so D/L is decided using the lowest chiral carbon, not the carbonyl carbon. In Fischer projection, D-fructose has the reference \(\mathrm{OH}\) on the right, while L-fructose has it on the left.
  • In Haworth projection, a six-membered sugar ring is pyranose and a five-membered sugar ring is furanose. A six-membered glucose ring should not be identified as fructofuranose.
  • For D-glucose in Haworth form, \(\mathrm{CH_2OH}\) is drawn upward; the \(\alpha\)-anomer has the anomeric \(\mathrm{OH}\) downward, while the \(\beta\)-anomer has it upward. Thus six-membered ring + D orientation + downward anomeric \(\mathrm{OH}\) identifies \(\alpha\)-D-\((+)\)-glucopyranose.

    Disaccharides: sucrose, maltose and lactose
  • The three most important disaccharide compositions are: sucrose = glucose + fructose, maltose = glucose + glucose, and lactose = glucose + galactose.
  • Sucrose on acid hydrolysis gives one mole of glucose and one mole of fructose: \(\mathrm{C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}+H_2O\rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6+C_6H_{12}O_6}\). Since one mole of sucrose gives one mole of glucose, \(342\ \mathrm{g}\) sucrose is required to produce one mole glucose.
  • Sucrose is made of \(\alpha\)-D-glucose and \(\beta\)-D-fructose. Hydrolysis of sucrose gives invert sugar because the optical rotation changes after formation of glucose and fructose mixture.
  • Maltose is made of two \(\alpha\)-D-glucose units joined through an \(\alpha(1\rightarrow4)\)-glycosidic linkage. It is a reducing sugar because one anomeric carbon remains free.
  • A statement saying maltose contains glucose and galactose is incorrect; glucose + galactose identifies lactose. A statement saying maltose contains glucose and fructose is also incorrect; that belongs to sucrose.
  • Lactose is milk sugar and is made of \(\beta\)-D-galactose and \(\beta\)-D-glucose. The “milk sugar” clue is not enough; the \(\beta\)-D-galactose unit must also be remembered correctly.

    Polysaccharides: starch, cellulose and glycogen
  • Starch is a plant storage polysaccharide made of \(\alpha\)-D-glucose units. Its two components are amylose and amylopectin.
  • Amylose is linear or unbranched and mainly contains \(\alpha(1\rightarrow4)\)-glycosidic linkages. Amylopectin is branched, so “amylose is branched” is incorrect.
  • Cellulose is a straight-chain polysaccharide made of \(\beta\)-D-glucose units joined by \(\beta(1\rightarrow4)\)-glycosidic linkages. Starch is based on \(\alpha\)-D-glucose, while cellulose is based on \(\beta\)-D-glucose.
  • The recurring comparison is: starch = \(\alpha\)-glucose storage polymer; cellulose = \(\beta\)-glucose straight-chain structural polymer; amylose = unbranched; amylopectin = branched.

    Amino acids: structure, side chains and zwitter ions
  • A standard \(\alpha\)-amino acid contains an amino group \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\), a carboxyl group \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), hydrogen and a side chain \(R\) on the same \(\alpha\)-carbon. The side chain decides the identity and classification of the amino acid.
  • In aqueous solution, amino acids exist mainly as zwitter ions, \(\mathrm{R-CH(NH_3^+)-COO^-}\), because \(\mathrm{-COOH}\) donates a proton and \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) accepts it. This is due to internal acid-base behaviour, not due to L-configuration.
  • Most naturally occurring amino acids have L-configuration, but this stereochemical fact does not explain zwitter ion formation. In assertion-reason questions, both statements may be true while the reason is not the explanation.
  • Asparagine has the usual \(\alpha\)-amino acid groups \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) and \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), and its side chain is \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CONH_2}\). Therefore, its functional groups include amino, carboxyl and amide \(\mathrm{-C(=O)-NH_2}\).
  • Important side-chain recognition: tyrosine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-C_6H_4-OH}\), cysteine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-SH}\), asparagine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CONH_2}\), glutamine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CH_2-CONH_2}\), serine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-OH}\), and methionine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CH_2-S-CH_3}\).
  • Tyrosine contains a phenolic \(\mathrm{-OH}\) because the \(\mathrm{-OH}\) is directly attached to an aromatic ring. Serine and threonine contain alcoholic \(\mathrm{-OH}\), not phenolic \(\mathrm{-OH}\).

    Proteins and denaturation
  • Proteins are polymers of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. The primary structure is the amino acid sequence, while secondary and tertiary structures describe folding and three-dimensional shape.
  • Denaturation disturbs secondary and tertiary structures and causes loss of native shape and biological activity. Ordinary denaturation does not break peptide bonds, so the primary structure is generally not destroyed.
  • Curdling of milk is a standard example of protein denaturation or coagulation. This statement is true, but it does not imply that the primary amino acid sequence is destroyed.
  • In assertion-reason questions on denaturation, reject any assertion saying all \(1^\circ\), \(2^\circ\) and \(3^\circ\) structures are destroyed. The \(1^\circ\) structure usually remains intact.

    Enzymes and biomolecule-based reagents
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts with high specificity. A key tested example is diastase, present in malt, which converts starch into maltose.
  • Do not confuse diastase with maltase. Diastase acts on starch to give maltose, while maltase converts maltose into glucose.
  • Aspartame is an artificial sweetener that is a methyl ester of a dipeptide containing aspartic acid and phenylalanine. It should not be confused with saccharin or sucralose.
  • Sucralose is related to sucrose chemistry, but aspartame is identified by the aspartic acid + phenylalanine clue.

    Vitamins: names, sources and deficiency diseases
  • Fat-soluble vitamins are A, D, E and K. Vitamin D is associated with egg yolk and sunlight exposure, and its deficiency causes rickets in children.
  • Vitamin A deficiency causes xerophthalmia or night blindness. This should not be confused with vitamin D deficiency, which affects bone mineralisation.
  • Vitamin C deficiency causes scurvy. If the disease clue is scurvy, do not choose a fat-soluble vitamin.
  • Vitamin \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\), also called cobalamin, is required for normal red blood cell maturation. Its deficiency causes pernicious anaemia.
  • Vitamin \(\mathrm{B_6}\) is pyridoxine. Do not confuse \(\mathrm{B_6}\) with \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\): pyridoxine = \(\mathrm{B_6}\), cobalamin = \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\).
  • High-yield vitamin pairs: vitamin A \(\rightarrow\) xerophthalmia/night blindness; vitamin C \(\rightarrow\) scurvy; vitamin D \(\rightarrow\) rickets; vitamin \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) \(\rightarrow\) pernicious anaemia; vitamin \(\mathrm{B_6}\) \(\rightarrow\) pyridoxine.

    Hormones and biologically important molecules
  • Hormones may be classified by chemical nature. Insulin is a polypeptide hormone, while epinephrine is amine-derived, and estrogen and androgen are steroid hormones.
  • Do not classify all hormones as proteins. The chemical class must be recognised from the specific hormone: insulin is peptide/polypeptide, sex hormones are steroids, and epinephrine is an amine derivative.

    Nucleic acids: low-frequency but syllabus-linked reminders
  • Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides. Each nucleotide contains a nitrogenous base, a pentose sugar and phosphate group.
  • DNA contains deoxyribose sugar and bases A, G, C and T, while RNA contains ribose sugar and bases A, G, C and U. DNA fingerprinting is based on differences in DNA sequence among individuals.
  • Although nucleic acids were not strongly represented in this PYQ set, their composition and biological function remain direct syllabus points.

    Biomolecules overview
  • Biomolecules are complex organic molecules required for growth, maintenance and biological functions. For this chapter, the exam focus is mainly on carbohydrates, amino acids, proteins, enzymes, vitamins, nucleic acids and hormones.
  • The strongest PYQ pattern is structure-based recognition: sugar identity, D/L and \(+\)/\(-\) distinction, glycosidic linkage, amino acid functional groups, protein denaturation, vitamin deficiency diseases and hormone class.

    Carbohydrates: classification and key monosaccharides
  • Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or substances that give such compounds on hydrolysis. Glucose is an aldohexose; fructose is a ketohexose.
  • Monosaccharides cannot be hydrolysed into simpler carbohydrates. Disaccharides give two monosaccharide units on hydrolysis, while polysaccharides give many monosaccharide units.
  • D-glucose, D-fructose, D-ribose and 2-deoxy-D-ribose are the key monosaccharides in the IPE theory. D-ribose occurs in RNA, while 2-deoxy-D-ribose occurs in DNA.
  • Glucose has the open-chain structure \(\mathrm{CHO-(CHOH)_4-CH_2OH}\). The \(\mathrm{-CHO}\) group explains mild oxidation to gluconic acid, while the terminal \(\mathrm{-CH_2OH}\) group is also oxidised during strong oxidation to saccharic acid.
  • \(\mathrm{Br_2/H_2O}\) oxidises only the aldehyde group of glucose to \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), giving gluconic acid. Concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) oxidises both \(\mathrm{-CHO}\) and \(\mathrm{-CH_2OH}\), giving saccharic acid.
  • Prolonged heating of glucose with \(\mathrm{HI}\) gives \(n\)-hexane, showing that glucose has a straight six-carbon chain. Further aromatisation of \(n\)-hexane with \(\mathrm{Mo_2O_3}\), \(773\ \mathrm{K}\), \(10\text{-}20\ \mathrm{atm}\), gives benzene.
  • For Wurtz backtracking, straight-chain \(n\)-hexane comes from two molecules of straight-chain propyl halide, \(\mathrm{CH_3CH_2CH_2Cl}\), not from branched \(\mathrm{(CH_3)_2CHCl}\).
  • Kiliani-Fischer chain extension increases an aldose chain by one carbon through cyanohydrin formation followed by hydrolysis. A six-carbon aldose framework therefore gives a seven-carbon hydroxy acid.

    D/L configuration, optical rotation and cyclic sugar recognition
  • D/L configuration is assigned by comparison with glyceraldehyde. In a sugar Fischer projection, the \(\mathrm{OH}\) group on the chiral carbon farthest from the carbonyl group decides D or L.
  • If the reference \(\mathrm{OH}\) is on the right, the sugar is D-form; if it is on the left, the sugar is L-form. For fructose, because the carbonyl group is at \(\mathrm{C_2}\), the lowest chiral carbon is the deciding carbon.
  • D/L configuration and optical rotation are independent. D does not mean dextrorotatory: D-\((+)\)-glucose is dextrorotatory, but D-\((-)\)-fructose is levorotatory.
  • Monosaccharides commonly exist as cyclic structures. Glucose preferentially forms a six-membered pyranose ring; a five-membered sugar ring is called furanose.
  • The \(\alpha\)- and \(\beta\)-forms of glucose are anomers because they differ only in the arrangement of the \(\mathrm{OH}\) group at the anomeric carbon \(\mathrm{C_1}\).
  • In Haworth projection of D-glucose, \(\mathrm{CH_2OH}\) is drawn upward. The \(\alpha\)-anomer has anomeric \(\mathrm{OH}\) downward, while the \(\beta\)-anomer has anomeric \(\mathrm{OH}\) upward.
  • A six-membered ring with D-glucose orientation and downward anomeric \(\mathrm{OH}\) identifies \(\alpha\)-D-\((+)\)-glucopyranose. A six-membered glucose ring should not be identified as fructofuranose.

    Disaccharides: sucrose, maltose, lactose and invert sugar
  • Disaccharides are formed by condensation of two monosaccharides with loss of water. The bond joining the two units is called a glycosidic linkage.
  • The most important composition map is: sucrose = glucose + fructose, maltose = glucose + glucose, and lactose = glucose + galactose.
  • Sucrose is common table sugar. On hydrolysis, \(1\) mole sucrose gives \(1\) mole glucose and \(1\) mole fructose: \(\mathrm{C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}+H_2O\rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6+C_6H_{12}O_6}\).
  • Since the molar mass of sucrose is \(342\ \mathrm{g\ mol^{-1}}\), \(342\ \mathrm{g}\) sucrose is required to produce \(1\) mole glucose by hydrolysis. Do not use \(180\ \mathrm{g}\), which is the molar mass of glucose.
  • Sucrose is made of \(\alpha\)-D-glucose and \(\beta\)-D-fructose. Its hydrolysis product is called invert sugar because the optical rotation changes after glucose and fructose are formed.
  • Maltose is made of two \(\alpha\)-D-glucose units joined by an \(\alpha(1\rightarrow4)\)-glycosidic linkage. It is a reducing sugar because one anomeric carbon remains free.
  • Lactose is milk sugar and contains \(\beta\)-D-galactose and \(\beta\)-D-glucose. A glucose + galactose statement belongs to lactose, not maltose.
  • The fastest elimination rule is: glucose + fructose = sucrose; glucose + glucose = maltose; glucose + galactose = lactose.

    Polysaccharides: starch, cellulose and glycogen
  • Polysaccharides give a large number of monosaccharide units on hydrolysis. Starch, glycogen and cellulose are the major syllabus examples.
  • Starch is the main food storage polysaccharide of plants and is a polymer of \(\alpha\)-D-glucose. It contains two components: amylose and amylopectin.
  • Amylose is the water-soluble, linear or unbranched fraction of starch and mainly contains \(\alpha(1\rightarrow4)\)-glycosidic linkages.
  • Amylopectin is the water-insoluble, branched fraction of starch. In statement questions, “amylose is branched” is incorrect; the branched component is amylopectin.
  • Glycogen is the animal storage carbohydrate. It is a polymer of \(\alpha\)-glucose and has a structure similar to amylopectin.
  • Cellulose is the main structural polysaccharide of plant cell walls. It is a straight-chain polymer of \(\beta\)-D-glucose units joined by \(\beta(1\rightarrow4)\)-glycosidic linkages.
  • The recurring comparison is: starch = \(\alpha\)-glucose storage polymer; cellulose = \(\beta\)-glucose straight structural polymer; amylose = unbranched; amylopectin = branched.

    Biological importance of carbohydrates
  • Carbohydrates act as storage molecules: starch in plants and glycogen in animals.
  • D-ribose and 2-deoxy-D-ribose are sugar components of RNA and DNA respectively.
  • Cellulose forms the cell wall material of plants and bacteria. Humans cannot digest cellulose because the required enzymes are absent.
  • Some carbohydrates occur in glycoproteins and glycolipids, where they perform recognition and structural functions.

    Amino acids: structure, side-chain recognition and zwitter ions
  • Proteins are polymers of \(\alpha\)-amino acids. A standard \(\alpha\)-amino acid has \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\), \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), hydrogen and an \(R\)-group on the same \(\alpha\)-carbon.
  • The \(R\)-group determines the identity and properties of an amino acid. Asparagine has side chain \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CONH_2}\), so it contains amino, carboxyl and amide functional groups.
  • Important side-chain recognition: tyrosine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-C_6H_4-OH}\), cysteine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-SH}\), asparagine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CONH_2}\), glutamine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CH_2-CONH_2}\), serine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-OH}\), and methionine has \(\mathrm{-CH_2-CH_2-S-CH_3}\).
  • Tyrosine contains phenolic \(\mathrm{-OH}\), because the \(\mathrm{-OH}\) is directly attached to an aromatic ring. Serine and threonine contain alcoholic \(\mathrm{-OH}\), not phenolic \(\mathrm{-OH}\).
  • In aqueous solution, amino acids exist mainly as zwitter ions, \(\mathrm{R-CH(NH_3^+)-COO^-}\), due to internal proton transfer from \(\mathrm{-COOH}\) to \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\).
  • Most naturally occurring amino acids have L-configuration, but L-configuration does not explain zwitter ion formation. Zwitter ion formation depends on the presence of acidic and basic groups in the same molecule.

    Proteins: peptide linkage and levels of structure
  • Proteins are high molecular mass polymers of amino acids. Amino acids are joined by peptide linkages, which are amide bonds formed between \(\mathrm{-COOH}\) of one amino acid and \(\mathrm{-NH_2}\) of another with loss of water.
  • A dipeptide contains two amino acid units and one peptide linkage; a tripeptide contains three amino acid units and two peptide linkages. Larger chains are polypeptides, and proteins are biologically active large polypeptides.
  • The amino acid unit with the free amino group is the N-terminal residue, and the one with the free carboxyl group is the C-terminal residue. Protein sequences are conventionally written from N-terminal to C-terminal.
  • Primary structure is the sequence of amino acids in the protein chain. This sequence is held by peptide bonds and is essential for biological activity.
  • Secondary structure arises from regular folding of the chain due to hydrogen bonding between \(\mathrm{C=O}\) and \(\mathrm{N-H}\) groups. The main forms are \(\alpha\)-helix and \(\beta\)-pleated sheet.
  • Tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional folding of one protein chain. Quaternary structure describes the association of multiple protein chains into a functional complex.

    Protein classification and denaturation
  • Simple proteins give only amino acids on hydrolysis. Conjugated proteins give amino acids plus a non-amino-acid prosthetic group.
  • Fibrous proteins are generally water-insoluble and thread-like; examples include collagen, elastin and keratin. Globular proteins are compact and generally soluble; examples include albumin, globulin and haemoglobin.
  • Denaturation occurs when changes in pH, temperature or chemical environment disturb the weak interactions maintaining protein shape. The protein loses its native shape and biological activity.
  • Ordinary denaturation disrupts secondary and tertiary structures but usually does not break peptide bonds; therefore, primary structure is generally not destroyed.
  • Curdling of milk and coagulation of egg white are standard examples of protein denaturation or coagulation. Curdling is true evidence of denaturation, but it does not mean the amino acid sequence is destroyed.
  • In assertion-reason questions, reject any assertion saying denaturation destroys all \(1^\circ\), \(2^\circ\) and \(3^\circ\) structures. The \(1^\circ\) structure usually remains intact.

    Enzymes and enzyme action
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up biochemical reactions under mild conditions. Almost all enzymes are globular proteins.
  • Enzymes are highly specific. Their names often end in “-ase”, such as maltase and esterase.
  • Enzymes lower activation energy and act through an enzyme-substrate complex. The substrate fits into the active site, the product is formed, and the enzyme is regenerated.
  • Diastase, present in malt, converts starch into maltose. Maltase converts maltose into glucose. Invertase converts sucrose into glucose and fructose.
  • Aspartame is a methyl ester of a dipeptide containing aspartic acid and phenylalanine. It is identified by these amino acid components and should not be confused with saccharin or sucralose.

    Vitamins: classification, sources and deficiency diseases
  • Vitamins are organic compounds required in minute quantities for normal health. Their absence causes deficiency diseases.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins are A, D, E and K. Water-soluble vitamins include vitamin C and the B-complex vitamins.
  • Vitamin A is retinol. Its deficiency causes night blindness and xerophthalmia.
  • Vitamin D is calciferol. It is fat-soluble, associated with sources such as egg yolk, milk, butter and sunlight exposure, and its deficiency causes rickets in children and osteomalacia in adults.
  • Vitamin C is ascorbic acid. Its deficiency causes scurvy and delayed wound healing.
  • Vitamin \(\mathrm{B_1}\) is thiamine, \(\mathrm{B_2}\) is riboflavin, \(\mathrm{B_6}\) is pyridoxine, and \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) is cobalamin.
  • Vitamin \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) deficiency causes pernicious anaemia. Do not confuse \(\mathrm{B_6}\) pyridoxine with \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) cobalamin in anaemia questions.
  • High-yield pairs: A \(\rightarrow\) xerophthalmia/night blindness; C \(\rightarrow\) scurvy; D \(\rightarrow\) rickets; \(\mathrm{B_6}\) \(\rightarrow\) pyridoxine; \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) \(\rightarrow\) pernicious anaemia.

    Nucleic acids: composition, DNA and RNA
  • Nucleic acids are polynucleotides responsible for heredity and protein synthesis. The two major types are DNA and RNA.
  • Each nucleotide contains three components: a nitrogenous base, a pentose sugar and phosphoric acid.
  • DNA contains 2-deoxyribose sugar and the bases adenine, guanine, cytosine and thymine. RNA contains ribose sugar and the bases adenine, guanine, cytosine and uracil.
  • DNA is double-stranded and helical. Specific base pairing holds the strands together: adenine pairs with thymine, and guanine pairs with cytosine.
  • RNA is generally single-stranded and occurs mainly as mRNA, rRNA and tRNA. mRNA carries information from DNA, tRNA brings amino acids, and rRNA participates in protein assembly.
  • DNA contains the coded message for protein synthesis, while RNA helps carry out protein synthesis. DNA fingerprinting is based on differences in DNA sequences among individuals.

    Hormones
  • Hormones are chemical messengers produced in endocrine tissues and transported to target organs, where they regulate biological activity.
  • Hormones may be classified by chemical nature. Insulin and glucagon are protein or polypeptide hormones, epinephrine is amine-derived, and estrogen and androgen are steroid hormones.
  • Insulin is a polypeptide hormone. Do not classify all hormones as proteins; sex hormones are steroids, and epinephrine is an amine derivative.
  • Abnormal hormone activity causes diseases because hormones regulate growth, metabolism, reproduction and homeostasis. For this syllabus, the main exam need is to identify the chemical class and biological role of common hormones.

  • D/L configuration = stereochemistry; \(+\)/\(-\) = optical rotation.
  • Sucrose = glucose + fructose; maltose = glucose + glucose; lactose = glucose + galactose.
  • Starch = \(\alpha\)-D-glucose polymer; cellulose = \(\beta\)-D-glucose polymer.
  • Amylose = linear; amylopectin = branched.
  • Amino acid zwitter ion = \(\mathrm{R-CH(NH_3^+)-COO^-}\).
  • Denaturation affects folding and activity, not the primary amino acid sequence in ordinary cases.
  • Diastase converts starch to maltose; maltase converts maltose to glucose.
  • Vitamin D deficiency causes rickets; vitamin \(\mathrm{B_{12}}\) deficiency causes pernicious anaemia.

    Group 1: Alkali metals — physical trends, flame colour and reducing nature
  • Alkali metals vs alkaline earth metals
    • Ionisation enthalpy: Alkali metals have lower ionisation enthalpy than the corresponding alkaline earth metals because they have one loosely held \(ns^1\) electron and larger atomic size in the same period.
    • Physical nature: They are softer, usually have lower melting/boiling points, and form larger cations than Group 2 metals.
  • Flame colour logic
    • Alkali metals: Alkali metals and their salts give characteristic flame colours because low ionisation enthalpy allows easy excitation of the valence electron in a flame.
    • Visible colour: De-excitation then emits visible light of characteristic colour. This explains common flame-test logic for Li, Na, K and related salts.
    • Be and Mg exception: \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg}\) do not impart colour to the flame because their electrons are strongly held and are not easily excited by ordinary flame energy.
    • Assertion-reason use: In assertion-reason questions, this reason correctly explains the absence of flame colour.
  • Melting point order
    • Main deciding factor: Melting point depends strongly on metallic bonding, not simply atomic mass.
    • Group comparison: Group 2 metals have stronger metallic bonding than Group 1 metals, and melting point generally decreases down a group.
    • Selected order: \(\mathrm{Be > Mg > Li > Na}\).
  • Aqueous electrode potential and reducing nature
    • Lithium anomaly: In aqueous electrode-potential questions, lithium behaves anomalously because \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) has very high hydration enthalpy.
    • Electrode potential: Lithium has the lowest/most negative \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{M^+/M}}\), while sodium has the highest value in the usual alkali-metal comparison.
    • Among Li, K, Na: The negative potential order is \(\mathrm{Li > K > Na}\).
    • Reducing nature rule: Reducing nature in aqueous medium is not decided by ionisation enthalpy alone; hydration enthalpy modifies the trend.
    • Exam takeaway: Lithium is strongly reducing due to high hydration enthalpy, while sodium is treated as least reducing among alkali metals in the given aqueous comparison.

    Alkali metals with oxygen, air and water
  • Oxygen products depend on cation size
    • Lithium: \(\mathrm{Li}\) forms oxide \(\mathrm{Li_2O}\).
    • Sodium: \(\mathrm{Na}\) forms peroxide \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\).
    • Potassium, rubidium and caesium: \(\mathrm{K}\), \(\mathrm{Rb}\), and \(\mathrm{Cs}\) form superoxides \(\mathrm{MO_2}\).
    • Reason: Larger cations stabilise the larger superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).
  • Peroxide vs superoxide: magnetism and examples
    • Sodium in excess oxygen: Sodium forms \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\), which contains peroxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\).
    • Peroxide magnetism: \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic.
    • Potassium in excess oxygen: Potassium forms \(\mathrm{KO_2}\), which contains superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).
    • Superoxide magnetism: \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) is paramagnetic due to one unpaired electron.
    • Caesium check: Caesium forms superoxide \(\mathrm{CsO_2}\), while sodium forms peroxide \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\). Do not mark both as peroxide.
  • Superoxide stability, colour and reaction with water
    • Stability order: The stability of alkali metal superoxides increases down the group: \(\mathrm{KO_2 < RbO_2 < CsO_2}\).
    • Reason: This follows the increasing ability of larger cations to stabilise \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).
    • Colour and magnetism: Alkali metal superoxides are coloured and paramagnetic due to the \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) ion; they are not colourless.
    • Water reaction clue: Potassium superoxide reacts with water to give \(\mathrm{K^+}\), \(\mathrm{OH^-}\), \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\), and \(\mathrm{O_2}\).
    • Product-set trap: Choose the option containing both \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\), not \(\mathrm{K_2O}\).
  • Burning in air: oxides and nitrides
    • Metals counted: When metals are burnt in air, the metals counted for forming both oxides and nitrides are \(\mathrm{Li, Be, Mg, Sr, Ba}\).
    • Lithium: \(\mathrm{Li}\) forms \(\mathrm{Li_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{Li_3N}\).
    • Alkaline earth metals: Be, Mg, Sr, and Ba form oxides and nitrides.
    • Trap: \(\mathrm{Na}\) and \(\mathrm{K}\) are not counted for nitride formation in this pattern.

    Alkali metal halides: lattice enthalpy, hydration enthalpy and melting point
  • Solubility: lattice enthalpy vs hydration enthalpy
    • General rule: Solubility of ionic halides depends on the balance between lattice enthalpy and hydration enthalpy.
    • \(\mathrm{LiF}\): \(\mathrm{LiF}\) has low solubility because small \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) and \(\mathrm{F^-}\) give very high lattice enthalpy.
    • \(\mathrm{CsI}\): \(\mathrm{CsI}\) also has low solubility, but for the opposite reason: both ions are very large, so hydration enthalpy is small.
    • High-yield contrast: \(\mathrm{LiF}\rightarrow\) high lattice enthalpy and \(\mathrm{CsI}\rightarrow\) low hydration enthalpy.
    • Lithium anomaly: \(\mathrm{LiF}\) is less soluble than \(\mathrm{NaF}\) because the very high lattice enthalpy of \(\mathrm{LiF}\) dominates.
  • Melting point and enthalpy of formation
    • Lithium halides: For lithium halides with the same cation \(\mathrm{Li^+}\), melting point follows lattice energy.
    • Anion-size logic: Since anion size increases from \(\mathrm{F^-}\) to \(\mathrm{I^-}\), the order is: \(\mathrm{LiF > LiCl > LiI}\).
    • Statement check: Alkali metal halides have high negative enthalpies of formation.
    • Common grouping: This is usually tested in statement questions along with beryllium halide covalency and superoxide colour/magnetism.

    Anomalous behaviour of lithium and diagonal relationship with magnesium
  • Cause of lithium anomaly
    • Lithium shows anomalous behaviour because of its very small size, high polarising power, and high hydration enthalpy.
    • It often resembles magnesium through diagonal relationship.
    • \(\mathrm{Li}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg}\) both form nitrides, a repeatedly tested diagonal-relationship point.
    • Exam trap: Statement sets often pair \(\mathrm{Li}\) with \(\mathrm{Mg}\), not simply with other alkali metals.
  • Lithium carbonate and carbonate stability
    • Thermal decomposition: \(\mathrm{Li_2CO_3}\) decomposes more readily on heating than other alkali metal carbonates because the small \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) ion has high polarising power.
    • Important reaction: \(\mathrm{Li_2CO_3 \rightarrow Li_2O + CO_2}\).
    • Other alkali carbonates: Other alkali metal carbonates such as \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\) are thermally stable and do not readily give \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) on heating.
    • Question clue: If a question asks which alkali carbonate gives \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) readily, select lithium carbonate.
    • Trend: Alkali metal carbonate thermal stability generally increases down the group.
    • Trap: An order placing \(\mathrm{Li_2CO_3}\) as most stable is incorrect; lithium carbonate is the least stable among the common alkali carbonates.

    Nitrates, carbonates and other oxo-salts
  • Alkali metal nitrates
    • Normal alkali metal nitrates: Alkali metal nitrates except lithium decompose on heating to nitrite and oxygen.
    • Example: \(\mathrm{2KNO_3 \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2KNO_2 + O_2}\).
    • Potassium nitrate trap: Potassium nitrate does not liberate \(\mathrm{NO_2}\).
    • Lithium nitrate exception: Lithium nitrate decomposes like alkaline earth nitrates.
    • Lithium nitrate reaction: \(\mathrm{4LiNO_3 \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2Li_2O + 4NO_2 + O_2}\).
    • Products from \(\mathrm{LiNO_3}\): It gives \(\mathrm{Li_2O}\), \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), and \(\mathrm{O_2}\), not \(\mathrm{LiNO_2}\).
    • Incorrect reaction: If a reaction shows \(\mathrm{LiNO_3\rightarrow LiNO_2+O_2}\), mark it incorrect.
    • Na vs Li: \(\mathrm{NaNO_3}\) can give nitrite and oxygen, but \(\mathrm{LiNO_3}\) cannot be treated like sodium nitrate.
  • Alkaline earth and heavy metal nitrates
    • Alkaline earth and many heavy metal nitrates decompose to metal oxide, \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), and \(\mathrm{O_2}\).
    • Examples: \(\mathrm{Pb(NO_3)_2}\) and \(\mathrm{Ba(NO_3)_2}\).
    • Contrast: This contrasts with normal alkali metal nitrates.
  • Carbonates and sulphates of alkaline earth metals
    • Carbonate thermal stability: For alkaline earth metals, carbonate thermal stability increases down the group because larger cations have less polarising power.
    • Carbonate solubility: Carbonate solubility decreases down the group.
    • Sulphate solubility: Alkaline earth sulphate solubility decreases down the group.
    • \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\): \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\) is relatively soluble because the small \(\mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) ion has high hydration enthalpy.
    • Heavier sulphates: Heavier sulphates such as \(\mathrm{BaSO_4}\) are poorly soluble.

    Important compounds of sodium
  • Sodium carbonate and Solvay process
    • Product: Sodium carbonate \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\), or soda ash, is generally prepared by the Solvay process.
    • Key intermediate: Sodium hydrogen carbonate.
    • Intermediate-forming reaction: \(\mathrm{NaCl+NH_3+CO_2+H_2O\rightarrow NaHCO_3+NH_4Cl}\).
    • Heating reaction: \(\mathrm{2NaHCO_3\rightarrow Na_2CO_3+CO_2+H_2O}\).
    • Connection: This conversion is the central reaction connecting \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\) and \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\).
  • Industrial process names
    • Solvay process: prepares sodium carbonate.
    • Castner–Kellner process: prepares sodium hydroxide.
    • Nelson cell process: prepares sodium hydroxide.
    • Deacon's process: associated with chlorine.
  • Castner–Kellner process
    • Starting material: Brine is electrolysed to manufacture \(\mathrm{NaOH}\).
    • Anode: Carbon/graphite is the anode.
    • Cathode: Mercury is the cathode.
    • Anode reaction: Chlorine is liberated at the anode: \(\mathrm{2Cl^- \rightarrow Cl_2 + 2e^-}\).
    • Cathode product: At the mercury cathode, sodium forms sodium amalgam.
    • Amalgam reaction: \(\mathrm{2Na(Hg)+2H_2O\rightarrow 2NaOH+H_2+2Hg}\).
    • Common error: Reversing mercury and carbon electrodes.
  • Soda lime and lime water clues
    • Soda lime: \(\mathrm{NaOH+CaO}\) is soda lime.
    • Soda-lime decarboxylation: Benzoic acid \(\mathrm{C_6H_5COOH}\) gives benzene.
    • Product clue: Benzene as product points to benzoic acid rather than phenylacetic acid.
    • Lime water: \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\), used in water softening, gives cloudy \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\) with \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • Clue-based matching: Lime water/cloudiness points to \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\), and pH \(14\) product often points to \(\mathrm{NaOH}\).

    Water softening and Calgon
  • Calgon identity and function
    • Name: Calgon is sodium hexametaphosphate.
    • Formula: It is commonly written as \((\mathrm{NaPO_3})_6\).
    • Not this: It is not \(\mathrm{NaAlSiO_4}\).
    • Use: Sodium hexametaphosphate removes permanent hardness of water.
    • Method: It complexes \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\), preventing the ions from causing hardness and scale formation.
  • Linked statement checks
    • Photosynthesis: Water is oxidised to \(\mathrm{O_2}\) in photosynthesis.
    • Permanent hardness: Sodium hexametaphosphate removes permanent hardness.
    • Hydride trap: \(\mathrm{MgH_2}\) is not an interstitial hydride.
    • Reason: Interstitial hydrides are mainly transition-metal or \(f\)-block type.

    Group 2: alkaline earth compounds and hydration trends
  • Oxides and carbonates
    • Basic strength: Basic strength of alkaline earth oxides increases down the group as ionic character increases.
    • Water reaction trap: The blanket statement that \(\mathrm{MgO}\), \(\mathrm{CaO}\), \(\mathrm{SrO}\), and \(\mathrm{BaO}\) are all insoluble in water is not correct because heavier oxides react with water more readily.
    • Carbonates: Alkaline earth carbonates are generally insoluble in water.
    • Common statement set: This fact is often tested along with beryllium halide covalency and superoxide properties.
  • Hydration tendency of Group 2 cations
    • Trend: Hydration tendency of alkaline earth cations decreases down the group because cation size increases and charge density decreases.
    • Order: \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+} > Ca^{2+} > Sr^{2+} > Ba^{2+}}\).
    • Reason: Smaller \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) attracts water molecules more strongly due to higher charge density.
    • Chloride hydrate clue: For chlorides, \(\mathrm{MgCl_2}\) forms the most highly hydrated hydrate among \(\mathrm{MgCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{CaCl_2}\), \(\mathrm{SrCl_2}\), and \(\mathrm{BaCl_2}\).

    Anomalous behaviour of beryllium and diagonal relationship with aluminium
  • Cause and general behaviour
    • Cause: Beryllium shows anomalous behaviour due to very small size, high ionisation enthalpy, and high polarising power.
    • General behaviour: Its compounds often show covalent and amphoteric behaviour unlike the more ionic/basic compounds of heavier alkaline earth metals.
  • Amphoteric oxide, hydroxide and hydroxo-complexes
    • \(\mathrm{BeO}\): \(\mathrm{BeO}\) is amphoteric, not completely basic.
    • \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\): \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) is also amphoteric and reacts with both acids and alkalis.
    • With acid: \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2+2HCl\rightarrow BeCl_2+2H_2O}\).
    • With alkali: \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2+2NaOH\rightarrow Na_2BeO_2+2H_2O}\).
    • Excess alkali: Amphoteric hydroxides form hydroxo-complexes.
    • Beryllium complex: \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) forms \(\mathrm{Na_2[Be(OH)_4]}\).
    • Aluminium complex: \(\mathrm{Al(OH)_3}\) forms \(\mathrm{Na[Al(OH)_4]}\).
    • Covalency: Both Be and Al have covalency \(4\) in these complexes.
  • Beryllium hydride and chloride
    • \(\mathrm{BeH_2}\) preparation: \(\mathrm{BeH_2}\) is not prepared by direct combination of beryllium with hydrogen.
    • Indirect preparation: It is prepared indirectly from \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) and \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\).
    • \(\mathrm{BeH_2}\) nature: It is covalent/polymeric.
    • \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) character: \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) has appreciable covalent character.
    • \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) solubility: It is soluble in organic solvents.
    • \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) vapour phase: It exists as a chloro-bridged dimer in vapour phase.
    • Exam use: These facts are common beryllium-anomaly markers.
  • Beryllium sulphate and carbonate
    • \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\): \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\) is readily soluble in water because high hydration enthalpy of small \(\mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) helps overcome lattice enthalpy.
    • Trap: \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\) is not the least soluble sulphate among alkaline earth metals.
    • \(\mathrm{BeCO_3}\): \(\mathrm{BeCO_3}\) is unstable and is kept in an atmosphere of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • Pattern: This fits the broader pattern that beryllium compounds often behave differently from heavier Group 2 compounds.

    Calcium compounds, lime and cement
  • Cement composition
    • Main composition: Ordinary cement is mainly composed of lime \((\mathrm{CaO})\) and silica \((\mathrm{SiO_2})\).
    • Trap: Alumina and ferric oxide are important but are not the major pair.
    • Major Portland cement phase: The major phase, around \(51\%\), is tricalcium silicate, \(\mathrm{Ca_3SiO_5}\).
    • Other names: Tricalcium silicate is also written as \(3\mathrm{CaO\cdot SiO_2}\) or \(C_3S\).
    • Gypsum role: Gypsum \(\mathrm{CaSO_4\cdot 2H_2O}\) controls setting but is not the major ingredient.
  • Lime water and calcium hydroxide
    • Compound: Calcium hydroxide, \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\), is linked with water softening and lime-water tests.
    • Test with \(\mathrm{CO_2}\): It gives \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\) turbidity with \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • Identification clue: This turbidity is a useful identification clue.

    s-Block elements: position and electronic configuration
  • The \(s\)-block elements are placed in Groups 1 and 2 because their valence electrons enter the \(s\)-subshell. Group 1 elements have the general outer configuration \(ns^1\), while Group 2 elements have \(ns^2\).
  • Group 1 elements are called alkali metals: \(\mathrm{Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr}\). They readily lose one electron and form \(\mathrm{M^+}\) ions with oxidation state \(+1\).
  • Group 2 elements are called alkaline earth metals: \(\mathrm{Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra}\). They lose two electrons to form \(\mathrm{M^{2+}}\) ions with oxidation state \(+2\).
  • Both groups are highly electropositive, but alkali metals are more electropositive and more reactive than the corresponding alkaline earth metals because removal of one \(ns^1\) electron is easier than removal of two \(ns^2\) electrons.

    Group 1: alkali metals — physical trends
  • Atomic and ionic radii increase down Group 1 because a new shell is added at each step: \(\mathrm{Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs}\). Hence the outer electron is farther from the nucleus down the group.
  • Ionisation enthalpy decreases down the group: \(\mathrm{Li > Na > K > Rb > Cs}\). This explains why alkali metals become more electropositive and generally more reactive from Li to Cs.
  • Electronegativity decreases and metallic character increases down the group. The single valence electron becomes easier to lose as atomic size increases.
  • Melting and boiling points generally decrease down Group 1 because increasing atomic size weakens metallic bonding: \(\mathrm{Li > Na > K > Rb > Cs}\).
  • Alkali metals are softer and generally have lower melting/boiling points than alkaline earth metals because Group 1 metals have weaker metallic bonding than Group 2 metals.
  • In mixed Group 1 and Group 2 melting-point comparisons, remember that Group 2 metallic bonding is stronger. A useful exam order is: \(\mathrm{Be > Mg > Li > Na}\).
  • Alkali metals and their salts impart characteristic flame colours because their low ionisation enthalpy allows the valence electron to be excited easily by flame energy. When the electron returns to a lower level, visible light is emitted.
  • Common flame colours are: Li = crimson red, Na = yellow, K = pale violet/lilac, Rb = violet, Cs = violet. Sodium’s yellow colour is especially important and is also linked to sodium street lamps.

    Group 1: chemical properties and reducing nature
  • Alkali metals are strong reducing agents because they lose their outer electron easily: \(\mathrm{M \rightarrow M^+ + e^-}\). Reactivity generally increases down the group due to decreasing ionisation enthalpy.
  • In aqueous solution, reducing power is affected by hydration enthalpy as well as ionisation enthalpy. Lithium is the strongest reducing agent in aqueous medium because \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) has very high hydration enthalpy.
  • Standard electrode potential \(E^\circ_{\mathrm{M^+/M}}\) values of alkali metals are highly negative. Lithium has the lowest/most negative value due to very high hydration enthalpy, while sodium is treated as the highest in the usual alkali-metal comparison.
  • Do not arrange aqueous electrode potentials simply by atomic size. For negative standard potential values among \(\mathrm{Li, K, Na}\), the useful order is: \(\mathrm{Li > K > Na}\), where “greater” refers to magnitude of negative reducing tendency in the exam pattern.
  • Alkali metals react with water vigorously to form hydroxides and hydrogen: \(\mathrm{2M+2H_2O\rightarrow 2MOH+H_2}\). Lithium reacts less vigorously than sodium despite its very negative \(E^\circ\), because its small size and high ionisation enthalpy reduce its reaction rate.
  • Alkali metals combine with hydrogen at high temperature to form ionic hydrides: \(\mathrm{2M+H_2\rightarrow 2MH}\). These hydrides contain \(\mathrm{H^-}\), so the bonding is ionic.
  • Alkali metals react with halogens to form ionic halides: \(\mathrm{2M+X_2\rightarrow 2MX}\), where \(X=\mathrm{F,Cl,Br,I}\).
  • In liquid ammonia, alkali metals form deep blue conducting solutions due to solvated electrons. Dilute solutions are paramagnetic; concentrated solutions may become bronze coloured and diamagnetic.

    Alkali metal oxides, peroxides and superoxides
  • Alkali metals form different oxygen compounds depending mainly on cation size. \(\mathrm{Li}\) forms oxide \(\mathrm{Li_2O}\), \(\mathrm{Na}\) forms peroxide \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\), while \(\mathrm{K, Rb, Cs}\) form superoxides \(\mathrm{MO_2}\).
  • The size rule is central: small \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) stabilises the small oxide ion \(\mathrm{O^{2-}}\), \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) stabilises peroxide \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\), and large \(\mathrm{K^+}\), \(\mathrm{Rb^+}\), \(\mathrm{Cs^+}\) stabilise the larger superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).
  • In excess oxygen, sodium forms sodium peroxide: \(\mathrm{2Na+O_2\rightarrow Na_2O_2}\). Potassium forms potassium superoxide: \(\mathrm{K+O_2\rightarrow KO_2}\).
  • Peroxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic because all electrons are paired. Superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) is paramagnetic because it has one unpaired electron.
  • Alkali metal superoxides are coloured and paramagnetic. They should not be described as colourless; pure oxides and peroxides are colourless, but superoxides are yellow or orange.
  • Stability of alkali metal superoxides increases down the group: \(\mathrm{KO_2 < RbO_2 < CsO_2}\). Larger cations stabilise the large \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) ion more effectively.
  • Hydrolysis of alkali metal oxides, peroxides and superoxides gives hydroxide, but peroxide and superoxide also give oxygen-containing products: \(\mathrm{M_2O+H_2O\rightarrow 2M^+ +2OH^-}\), \(\mathrm{M_2O_2+2H_2O\rightarrow 2M^+ +2OH^-+H_2O_2}\), \(\mathrm{2MO_2+2H_2O\rightarrow 2M^+ +2OH^-+H_2O_2+O_2}\).
  • For \(\mathrm{KO_2}\) hydrolysis, select the product set containing both \(\mathrm{H_2O_2}\) and \(\mathrm{O_2}\). \(\mathrm{K_2O}\) is not the expected hydrolysis product.
  • On burning in air, metals that form both oxides and nitrides include \(\mathrm{Li}\) and the alkaline earth metals \(\mathrm{Be, Mg, Sr, Ba}\). Sodium and potassium mainly form oxides/peroxides/superoxides and are not counted for nitride formation in this pattern.

    Alkali metal halides
  • Alkali metal halides \(\mathrm{MX}\) are generally colourless crystalline ionic solids. They are commonly prepared by reacting the corresponding oxide, hydroxide or carbonate with a hydrohalic acid.
  • The melting and boiling points of alkali metal halides decrease from fluoride to iodide for the same metal because lattice enthalpy decreases as halide size increases: \(\mathrm{fluoride > chloride > bromide > iodide}\).
  • For lithium halides, the important melting-point order is: \(\mathrm{LiF > LiCl > LiI}\). This follows lattice energy, not formula mass.
  • Solubility of ionic compounds depends on the balance between lattice enthalpy and hydration enthalpy. \(\mathrm{LiF}\) is sparingly soluble because the small \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) and \(\mathrm{F^-}\) ions produce very high lattice enthalpy.
  • \(\mathrm{CsI}\) is also less soluble, but for a different reason: \(\mathrm{Cs^+}\) and \(\mathrm{I^-}\) are large ions with low charge density, so their hydration enthalpy is small.
  • The frequently reversed comparison is: \(\mathrm{LiF}\rightarrow\) high lattice enthalpy; \(\mathrm{CsI}\rightarrow\) small hydration enthalpy.
  • \(\mathrm{LiF}\) is less soluble than \(\mathrm{NaF}\) because lattice enthalpy dominates in \(\mathrm{LiF}\). This is part of lithium’s anomalous behaviour.
  • Alkali metal halides have high negative enthalpies of formation, but the enthalpy of formation generally becomes less negative from fluoride to iodide.

    Alkali metal salts of oxoacids
  • Alkali metals form salts with oxoacids such as carbonic acid and sulphuric acid. Their carbonates \(\mathrm{M_2CO_3}\) and bicarbonates \(\mathrm{MHCO_3}\) are generally stable to heat, with lithium as the important exception.
  • Thermal stability of alkali metal carbonates increases down the group because electropositive character increases and the cation polarising power decreases. Therefore, \(\mathrm{Li_2CO_3}\) is the least stable among common alkali metal carbonates.
  • Lithium carbonate decomposes readily on heating: \(\mathrm{Li_2CO_3\rightarrow Li_2O+CO_2}\). Other alkali metal carbonates such as \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\) are much more thermally stable.
  • Alkali metal nitrates except lithium decompose to nitrites and oxygen on heating: \(\mathrm{2KNO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}2KNO_2+O_2}\). Therefore potassium nitrate does not liberate \(\mathrm{NO_2}\).
  • Lithium nitrate is exceptional and decomposes like alkaline earth metal nitrates: \(\mathrm{4LiNO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}2Li_2O+4NO_2+O_2}\). It forms oxide, nitrogen dioxide and oxygen, not lithium nitrite.
  • A reaction showing \(\mathrm{LiNO_3\rightarrow LiNO_2+O_2}\) is incorrect. Sodium nitrate may follow the nitrite pattern, but lithium nitrate does not.

    Anomalous behaviour of lithium and diagonal relationship with magnesium
  • Lithium is anomalous because \(\mathrm{Li}\) and \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) are very small and have high polarising power. This increases covalent character in lithium compounds and affects solubility, thermal stability and reducing behaviour.
  • Lithium is harder and less reactive than other alkali metals, but it is the strongest reducing agent in aqueous medium due to the very high hydration enthalpy of \(\mathrm{Li^+}\).
  • Lithium forms only monoxide \(\mathrm{Li_2O}\) with oxygen, unlike sodium which forms peroxide and potassium/rubidium/caesium which form superoxides.
  • \(\mathrm{LiCl}\) is deliquescent and crystallises as \(\mathrm{LiCl\cdot 2H_2O}\), while many other alkali metal chlorides do not form such hydrates. \(\mathrm{LiCl}\) is also soluble in ethanol due to appreciable covalent character.
  • Lithium hydrogen carbonate is not obtained in solid form, unlike hydrogen carbonates of the other alkali metals.
  • Lithium shows a diagonal relationship with magnesium because their atomic and ionic sizes are similar. Both are harder and lighter than other elements in their respective groups.
  • \(\mathrm{Li}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg}\) both form nitrides: \(\mathrm{6Li+N_2\rightarrow 2Li_3N}\) and \(\mathrm{3Mg+N_2\rightarrow Mg_3N_2}\). This similarity is repeatedly useful in statement questions.
  • Both lithium and magnesium form monoxides, and their carbonates decompose on heating to give oxides and carbon dioxide: \(\mathrm{Li_2CO_3\rightarrow Li_2O+CO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{MgCO_3\rightarrow MgO+CO_2}\).

    Important compounds of sodium
  • Sodium carbonate, \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3\cdot 10H_2O}\), is washing soda. It is prepared industrially by the Solvay process from brine, ammonia and carbon dioxide.
  • In the Solvay process, sodium hydrogen carbonate first precipitates: \(\mathrm{NaCl+NH_3+CO_2+H_2O\rightarrow NaHCO_3+NH_4Cl}\). On heating: \(\mathrm{2NaHCO_3\rightarrow Na_2CO_3+CO_2+H_2O}\).
  • The process names must be kept distinct: Solvay process prepares sodium carbonate; Castner–Kellner and Nelson cell processes prepare sodium hydroxide; Deacon’s process is associated with chlorine.
  • Sodium hydrogen carbonate, \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\), is baking soda. It reduces acidity in the stomach and is used in fire extinguishers because it can liberate \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
  • Sodium chloride is obtained from sea water or rock salt. It is used as common salt, food preservative, and for de-icing roads and sidewalks.
  • Sodium hydroxide, \(\mathrm{NaOH}\), is caustic soda and is prepared by the Castner–Kellner process. Brine is electrolysed using mercury as cathode and carbon/graphite as anode.
  • In Castner–Kellner cell, chloride ions are oxidised at the anode: \(\mathrm{2Cl^-\rightarrow Cl_2+2e^-}\). Sodium forms sodium amalgam at the mercury cathode.
  • Sodium amalgam reacts with water to give sodium hydroxide and hydrogen: \(\mathrm{2Na(Hg)+2H_2O\rightarrow 2NaOH+H_2+2Hg}\). The common statement error is reversing mercury and carbon electrodes.
  • \(\mathrm{NaOH+CaO}\) is soda lime. Soda-lime decarboxylation of benzoic acid \(\mathrm{C_6H_5COOH}\) gives benzene; benzene as product points to benzoic acid, not phenylacetic acid.

    Group 1 uses and biological importance of sodium and potassium
  • Lithium is the lightest metal and is used in alloys, electrochemical cells and lithium batteries. Lithium batteries are important because lithium has low atomic mass and a highly negative electrode potential.
  • Liquid sodium is used as a coolant in nuclear reactors because it transfers heat efficiently and remains liquid over a useful temperature range.
  • Potassium hydroxide is used in detergents and also acts as an absorbent for acidic gases such as \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
  • Caesium is used in photoelectric cells because it emits electrons readily.
  • Important direct pairs are: \(\mathrm{KOH}\rightarrow \mathrm{CO_2}\) absorbent, \(\mathrm{Na(l)}\rightarrow\) coolant, \(\mathrm{Li}\rightarrow\) electrochemical cells, \(\mathrm{Cs}\rightarrow\) photoelectric cells.
  • \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) and \(\mathrm{K^+}\) are biologically important because their distribution inside and outside cells creates electrical potential across cell membranes.
  • \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) is associated with movement of glucose into cells, while \(\mathrm{K^+}\) is important in glucose metabolism, protein synthesis and enzyme activation.

    Group 2: alkaline earth metals — physical trends
  • Alkaline earth metals have the general outer electronic configuration \(ns^2\). Their common oxidation state is \(+2\).
  • Atomic and ionic radii increase down Group 2: \(\mathrm{Be < Mg < Ca < Sr < Ba}\). Ionisation enthalpy decreases down the group: \(\mathrm{Be > Mg > Ca > Sr > Ba}\).
  • Alkaline earth metals are smaller than the corresponding alkali metals in the same period because the added nuclear charge pulls electrons more strongly.
  • Alkaline earth metals have higher melting and boiling points than alkali metals because they have smaller size and stronger metallic bonding. Down the group, melting-point trend is less regular because packing also matters.
  • Except \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg}\), alkaline earth metals impart characteristic flame colours. \(\mathrm{Ca}\) gives brick red, \(\mathrm{Sr}\) gives crimson red, and \(\mathrm{Ba}\) gives sea green.
  • \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg}\) do not show flame colour because their small size and high ionisation enthalpy keep electrons strongly bound, so ordinary flame energy cannot excite them effectively.

    Group 2: chemical properties
  • Alkaline earth metals are reactive, but less reactive than alkali metals. Their reactivity increases down the group as electropositive character increases.
  • Alkaline earth metals burn in oxygen to form oxides \(\mathrm{MO}\). Heavier members such as \(\mathrm{Sr}\), \(\mathrm{Ba}\), and \(\mathrm{Ra}\) can also form peroxides more readily because larger cations stabilise peroxide ions better.
  • Basic character of alkaline earth oxides increases down the group. \(\mathrm{BeO}\) is amphoteric, \(\mathrm{MgO}\) is weakly basic, and \(\mathrm{CaO}\), \(\mathrm{SrO}\), \(\mathrm{BaO}\) become more strongly basic.
  • A blanket statement that \(\mathrm{MgO, CaO, SrO, BaO}\) are all insoluble in water is not correct, because heavier oxides react with water more readily to form hydroxides.
  • Alkaline earth metals combine with hydrogen to form hydrides of formula \(\mathrm{MH_2}\), with halogens to form halides \(\mathrm{MX_2}\), and with dilute acids to liberate hydrogen: \(\mathrm{M+2HCl\rightarrow MCl_2+H_2}\).
  • \(\mathrm{Be}\) does not react with water or steam even at red heat and does not get oxidised in air below high temperature, whereas \(\mathrm{Ca}\), \(\mathrm{Sr}\), and \(\mathrm{Ba}\) react with cold water with increasing vigour.
  • Like alkali metals, alkaline earth metals dissolve in liquid ammonia to give coloured conducting solutions due to ammoniated electrons.

    Group 2 compounds: oxides, hydroxides, halides and oxo-salts
  • Oxides of alkaline earth metals are generally ionic and form hydroxides with water: \(\mathrm{MO+H_2O\rightarrow M(OH)_2}\). The exception is \(\mathrm{BeO}\), which is amphoteric.
  • Basic strength of hydroxides increases down the group from \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\) to \(\mathrm{Ba(OH)_2}\). These hydroxides are less basic than alkali metal hydroxides.
  • \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\), milk of magnesia, is a mild base used as an antacid. It neutralises excess stomach acid and is a common use-based match.
  • Except beryllium halides, alkaline earth metal halides are largely ionic. Beryllium halides are covalent and soluble in organic solvents because \(\mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) has high polarising power.
  • Alkaline earth carbonates are insoluble in water and can be precipitated by adding sodium or ammonium carbonate solution. Their solubility decreases down the group.
  • Alkaline earth carbonates decompose on heating to give oxide and carbon dioxide: \(\mathrm{MCO_3\rightarrow MO+CO_2}\). Thermal stability increases down the group because larger cations polarise \(\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}\) less strongly.
  • Alkaline earth sulphates are white solids and stable to heat. \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\) and \(\mathrm{MgSO_4}\) are readily soluble due to high hydration enthalpy, while solubility decreases down to \(\mathrm{BaSO_4}\).
  • Hydration tendency decreases down Group 2 because cation size increases and charge density decreases: \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+} > Ca^{2+} > Sr^{2+} > Ba^{2+}}\). Hence \(\mathrm{MgCl_2}\) can form the most highly hydrated chloride among the listed Group 2 chlorides.
  • Alkaline earth nitrates are obtained by dissolving carbonates in dilute nitric acid and decompose on heating to give the corresponding oxide, \(\mathrm{NO_2}\), and \(\mathrm{O_2}\).

    Anomalous behaviour of beryllium and diagonal relationship with aluminium
  • Beryllium is anomalous because of its very small atomic and ionic size, high ionisation enthalpy and high polarising power. Its compounds show greater covalent character than those of other alkaline earth metals.
  • \(\mathrm{BeO}\) and \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) are amphoteric, while oxides and hydroxides of heavier Group 2 elements are mainly basic.
  • \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) reacts with acids and alkalis: \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2+2HCl\rightarrow BeCl_2+2H_2O}\) and \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2+2NaOH\rightarrow Na_2BeO_2+2H_2O}\).
  • In excess alkali, \(\mathrm{Be(OH)_2}\) can form the hydroxo-complex \([Be(OH)_4]^{2-}\). In the analogous aluminium complex \([Al(OH)_4]^-\), both Be and Al show covalency \(4\) because four hydroxyl groups are attached to the central atom.
  • \(\mathrm{BeH_2}\) is not prepared by direct combination of beryllium with hydrogen. It is prepared indirectly from \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) and \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), and it is covalent/polymeric.
  • \(\mathrm{BeCl_2}\) is covalent, soluble in organic solvents, acts as a Lewis acid, and has a chlorine-bridged structure in vapour phase. This parallels aluminium chloride in the diagonal relationship.
  • \(\mathrm{BeSO_4}\) is relatively soluble in water because the high hydration enthalpy of small \(\mathrm{Be^{2+}}\) helps overcome lattice enthalpy. It is not the least soluble alkaline earth sulphate.
  • \(\mathrm{BeCO_3}\) is unstable and can be kept only in an atmosphere of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\). This is another marker of beryllium’s anomalous behaviour.
  • Beryllium shows diagonal relationship with aluminium: both have similar electronegativity and polarising power, both form covalent chlorides with bridged structures, both resist acids due to protective oxide films, and both form complexes.

    Important compounds of calcium
  • Calcium oxide, \(\mathrm{CaO}\), is quick lime. It is prepared by heating limestone: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3\xrightarrow{1070-1270\,K} CaO+CO_2}\). Removal of \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) helps the reaction proceed to completion.
  • Addition of water to quick lime is called slaking of lime: \(\mathrm{CaO+H_2O\rightarrow Ca(OH)_2}\). Quick lime also absorbs \(\mathrm{CO_2}\): \(\mathrm{CaO+CO_2\rightarrow CaCO_3}\).
  • Calcium hydroxide, \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\), is slaked lime. Its aqueous solution is lime water and its suspension in water is milk of lime.
  • Lime water turns milky when \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) is passed through it because insoluble calcium carbonate forms: \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2+CO_2\rightarrow CaCO_3+H_2O}\). In excess \(\mathrm{CO_2}\), the precipitate dissolves as calcium bicarbonate: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3+CO_2+H_2O\rightarrow Ca(HCO_3)_2}\).
  • Calcium carbonate, \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\), occurs as limestone, chalk and marble. It is insoluble in water and releases \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) with dilute acids: \(\mathrm{CaCO_3+2HCl\rightarrow CaCl_2+H_2O+CO_2}\).
  • Plaster of Paris is calcium sulphate hemihydrate: \(\mathrm{CaSO_4\cdot \defrac{1}{2}H_2O}\). It is prepared by heating gypsum \(\mathrm{CaSO_4\cdot 2H_2O}\) at about \(393\,\mathrm{K}\).
  • Plaster of Paris sets with water to form a hard mass. It is used for casts in bone fractures, dentistry, moulds, statues and construction.
  • Cement is made from limestone and clay. Strong heating gives cement clinker, which is mixed with \(2-3\%\) gypsum to slow down setting and allow proper hardening.
  • Ordinary cement is mainly composed of lime \((\mathrm{CaO})\) and silica \((\mathrm{SiO_2})\). The major Portland cement phase is tricalcium silicate, \(\mathrm{Ca_3SiO_5}\), also written as \(3\mathrm{CaO\cdot SiO_2}\) or \(C_3S\).

    Hard and soft water
  • Hard water does not give lather readily with soap. Its hardness is due to calcium and magnesium bicarbonates, chlorides and sulphates; iron salts may also contribute.
  • Temporary hardness is caused by bicarbonates of calcium and magnesium. It can be removed by boiling or by Clarke’s method using milk of lime.
  • On boiling, calcium bicarbonate gives insoluble calcium carbonate, while magnesium bicarbonate gives insoluble magnesium hydroxide: \(\mathrm{Ca(HCO_3)_2\rightarrow CaCO_3\downarrow+CO_2+H_2O}\), \(\mathrm{Mg(HCO_3)_2\rightarrow Mg(OH)_2\downarrow+2CO_2}\).
  • Clarke’s method uses a required quantity of milk of lime to remove temporary hardness. Excess lime should be avoided because it may introduce permanent hardness.
  • Permanent hardness is caused by chlorides and sulphates of calcium and magnesium. It cannot be removed by boiling because these salts do not decompose on boiling.
  • Permutit is artificial zeolite, chemically sodium aluminium orthosilicate. It softens hard water by replacing \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) ions with \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) ions.
  • A typical zeolite exchange equation is: \(\mathrm{2NaZ+Ca^{2+}\rightarrow CaZ+2Na^+}\), where \(Z\) represents the zeolite framework.
  • Calgon is sodium hexametaphosphate, represented as \(\mathrm{Na_6P_6O_{18}}\) or commonly \((\mathrm{NaPO_3})_6\). It removes hardness by forming complexes with \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\).
  • Calgon is not \(\mathrm{NaAlSiO_4}\). The name to remember is sodium hexametaphosphate, used for permanent hardness removal.
  • Ion-exchange resins can remove nearly all mineral ions from water to give deionised water. This is the most effective softening/deionising method among common options.
  • Hard water causes wastage of soap and scale formation in boilers, which wastes heat and damages boiler surfaces. The degree of hardness is expressed as parts per million of calcium carbonate equivalent.

    Uses, alloys and biological importance of Group 2 elements
  • Beryllium is used in alloys. Beryllium-copper alloy is used for high-strength springs because it has high strength, hardness, elasticity and wear resistance.
  • Magnesium forms alloys with aluminium, zinc, manganese and tin. Magnesium-aluminium alloys are used in aircraft construction.
  • Important alloy-use pairs are: Li–Pb = bearings for motor engines, Be–Cu = high-strength springs, Mg–Al = aircraft construction, and Na–Pb = tetraethyl lead.
  • \(\mathrm{Mg(OH)_2}\), milk of magnesia, is used as an antacid. Magnesium carbonate is used in toothpaste.
  • Calcium is used in extraction of metals from oxides when reduction with carbon is difficult. Calcium compounds such as \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\), \(\mathrm{CaO}\), \(\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}\), plaster of Paris and cement are important industrial materials.
  • \(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}\) is concentrated in animal cells, participates in ATP-related enzyme reactions, and is a constituent of chlorophyll.
  • \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) is present in bones and teeth as apatite, is necessary for blood clotting, helps maintain heartbeat, and is needed for muscle contraction.

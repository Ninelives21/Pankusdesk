    s-Block Elements TTR
    Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure TTR

    Solution:

    When alkali metals burn in excess oxygen, the nature of oxide formed depends on the size of the metal cation.

    1. Identify the compounds \(X\) and \(Y\)

      Sodium in excess oxygen forms sodium peroxide:

      \(\mathrm{2Na(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow Na_2O_2(s)}\)

      So:

      \(X = \mathrm{Na_2O_2}\)

      Potassium in excess oxygen forms potassium superoxide:

      \(\mathrm{K(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow KO_2(s)}\)

      So:

      \(Y = \mathrm{KO_2}\)

    2. Now determine the magnetic nature of \(X = \mathrm{Na_2O_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2}\) contains the peroxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\).

      In peroxide ion, all electrons are paired.

      Therefore:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic.

      Hence:

      \(X\) is diamagnetic.

    3. Now determine the magnetic nature of \(Y = \mathrm{KO_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{KO_2}\) contains the superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).

      In superoxide ion, one electron remains unpaired.

      Therefore:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) is paramagnetic.

      Hence:

      \(Y\) is paramagnetic.

    4. Final conclusion

      \(X\) is diamagnetic and \(Y\) is paramagnetic.

      So the correct option is \((b)\).

    Useful electron-species comparison:

    • \(\mathrm{O_2}\): paramagnetic
    • \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) (superoxide): paramagnetic
    • \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) (peroxide): diamagnetic

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • In alkali metals, the product formed with oxygen changes down the group:

      \(\mathrm{Li \rightarrow oxide}\)

      \(\mathrm{Na \rightarrow peroxide}\)

      \(\mathrm{K,\ Rb,\ Cs \rightarrow superoxide}\)

    • This trend is due to increasing cation size down the group.

      Larger cations stabilize larger anions like \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).

    • Peroxide ion:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\)

      All electrons are paired, so it is diamagnetic.

    • Superoxide ion:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\)

      Contains one unpaired electron, so it is paramagnetic.

    • Magnetic behavior depends on unpaired electrons:

      unpaired electrons \(\Rightarrow\) paramagnetic

      all electrons paired \(\Rightarrow\) diamagnetic

    Why sodium and potassium behave differently:

    • Sodium is smaller than potassium.
    • \(\mathrm{Na^+}\) stabilizes peroxide ion better than superoxide ion.
    • \(\mathrm{K^+}\), being larger, can stabilize the larger superoxide ion \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\).
    • So in excess oxygen:

      \(\mathrm{Na \rightarrow Na_2O_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{K \rightarrow KO_2}\)

    • Memorize the oxygen products of alkali metals:

      \(\mathrm{Li \rightarrow Li_2O}\)

      \(\mathrm{Na \rightarrow Na_2O_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{K \rightarrow KO_2}\)

    • Then memorize just one magnetic fact:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\) is diamagnetic

      \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\) is paramagnetic

    • So immediately:

      \(\mathrm{Na_2O_2 \Rightarrow diamagnetic}\)

      \(\mathrm{KO_2 \Rightarrow paramagnetic}\)

    • That gives option \((b)\) almost instantly.

    Quick intuitive memory hook:

    • Peroxide means more reduced oxygen:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^{2-}}\)

      electrons become fully paired \(\Rightarrow\) diamagnetic

    • Superoxide:

      \(\mathrm{O_2^-}\)

      one electron remains unpaired \(\Rightarrow\) paramagnetic

    Solution:

    1. Check Statement I

      Statement I says: Both \(\mathrm{LiF}\) and \(\mathrm{CsI}\) have low solubility in water.

      This is correct.

      • \(\mathrm{LiF}\) is sparingly soluble because its lattice enthalpy is very high.
      • \(\mathrm{CsI}\) is also less soluble because its ions are very large, so their hydration enthalpy is small.

      So, Statement I is correct.

    2. Check Statement II

      Statement II says that low solubility of \(\mathrm{LiF}\) is due to smaller hydration enthalpy and that of \(\mathrm{CsI}\) is due to high lattice enthalpy.

      This is reversed, so it is incorrect.

      • For \(\mathrm{LiF}\), the main reason is high lattice enthalpy, because \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) and \(\mathrm{F^-}\) are small ions and attract each other strongly.
      • For \(\mathrm{CsI}\), the main reason is small hydration enthalpy, because \(\mathrm{Cs^+}\) and \(\mathrm{I^-}\) are large ions and water cannot hydrate them strongly.

      So, Statement II is not correct.

    3. Hence the correct option

      Statement I is correct, but Statement II is not correct.

      Therefore, the correct answer is \(\boxed{(b)}\).

    Core theory behind this question:

    • Solubility of an ionic compound in water depends mainly on the balance between:

      \(\text{Lattice enthalpy}\)

      and

      \(\text{Hydration enthalpy}\)

    • Lattice enthalpy is the energy needed to separate oppositely charged ions in the crystal.
    • Hydration enthalpy is the energy released when ions get surrounded by water molecules.
    • If hydration enthalpy is sufficiently high to overcome lattice enthalpy, the salt dissolves more easily.
    • If lattice enthalpy is too high, or hydration enthalpy is too low, solubility becomes low.

    Why \(\mathrm{LiF}\) has low solubility:

    • \(\mathrm{Li^+}\) is very small.
    • \(\mathrm{F^-}\) is also small.
    • Small ions come very close to each other in the crystal.
    • So electrostatic attraction is very strong.
    • Therefore, \(\mathrm{LiF}\) has high lattice enthalpy.
    • This makes dissolution difficult.

    Why \(\mathrm{CsI}\) has low solubility:

    • \(\mathrm{Cs^+}\) and \(\mathrm{I^-}\) are both very large ions.
    • Large ions have low charge density.
    • So water molecules do not attract them strongly.
    • Therefore, their hydration enthalpy is small.
    • Hence dissolution is not strongly favored.

    Correct conceptual statement:

    Low solubility of \(\mathrm{LiF}\) in water is due to its high lattice enthalpy, and low solubility of \(\mathrm{CsI}\) in water is due to its smaller hydration enthalpy.

    JEE / NCERT takeaway:

    • In alkali metal halides, most salts are soluble, but \(\mathrm{LiF}\) is sparingly soluble because of very high lattice enthalpy.
    • Large cation + large anion combinations may show lower solubility because hydration enthalpy becomes too small.
    • Down the group, hydration enthalpy decreases because ionic size increases.
    • Smaller ions generally have:

      higher lattice enthalpy and higher hydration enthalpy

      but in \(\mathrm{LiF}\), lattice enthalpy dominates.

    • Larger ions generally have:

      lower lattice enthalpy and lower hydration enthalpy

      but for salts like \(\mathrm{CsI}\), very low hydration enthalpy becomes important.

    Intuition shortcut:

    • Small + small ions \(\rightarrow\) very strong crystal binding \(\rightarrow\) high lattice enthalpy.
    • Large + large ions \(\rightarrow\) weak interaction with water \(\rightarrow\) low hydration enthalpy.
    • So:

      \(\mathrm{LiF}\) \(\rightarrow\) think lattice enthalpy

      \(\mathrm{CsI}\) \(\rightarrow\) think hydration enthalpy

    1-minute solving trick:

    • See \(\mathrm{LiF}\): both ions are small \(\rightarrow\) high lattice enthalpy.
    • See \(\mathrm{CsI}\): both ions are large \(\rightarrow\) low hydration enthalpy.
    • If a statement swaps these two reasons, it is wrong.
    • So immediately mark:

      Statement I correct, Statement II incorrect \(\Rightarrow\) \(\boxed{(b)}\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Thinking all low-solubility salts have high lattice enthalpy only.
    2. Forgetting that low hydration enthalpy can also reduce solubility.
    3. Mixing up the reason for \(\mathrm{LiF}\) and \(\mathrm{CsI}\).
    4. Assuming large ions always mean greater solubility.

    Quick comparison table:

    Salt Main reason for low solubility
    \(\mathrm{LiF}\) High lattice enthalpy
    \(\mathrm{CsI}\) Small hydration enthalpy

    Useful trend summary:

    • Lattice enthalpy increases as ionic size decreases.
    • Hydration enthalpy increases as ionic size decreases.
    • Solubility depends on which effect dominates.

    Solution:

    1. Use the general trend of melting points in \(s\)-block elements

      Melting point generally decreases down a group.

      • In Group 1: \(\mathrm{Li > Na}\)
      • In Group 2: \(\mathrm{Be > Mg}\)
    2. Compare Group 1 and Group 2 elements

      Group 2 metals usually have higher melting points than Group 1 metals of the same period because they have:

      • smaller atomic size,
      • higher charge on the metal ion,
      • higher charge density,
      • stronger metallic bonding.

      Therefore, \(\mathrm{Be}\) and \(\mathrm{Mg}\) have higher melting points than \(\mathrm{Li}\) and \(\mathrm{Na}\).

    3. Hence the correct order is

      \(\mathrm{Be > Mg > Li > Na}\)

      So the correct option is \((c)\).

    Therefore:

    \(\boxed{\mathrm{Be > Mg > Li > Na}}\)

    \(\boxed{(c)}\)

    Why this order is correct:

    • Melting point depends on the strength of metallic bonding.
    • Stronger metallic bonding means more energy is needed to separate metal atoms, so melting point is higher.
    • Metallic bond becomes stronger when:
      • the atom is smaller,
      • the valence electrons are more effective in holding the lattice together,
      • the cation has higher charge density.

    JEE / NCERT theory to remember:

    • \(s\)-block elements include Group 1 and Group 2 elements.
    • Group 1 metals have only one valence electron, so metallic bonding is relatively weaker.
    • Group 2 metals have two valence electrons, so metallic bonding is stronger.
    • Hence, Group 2 elements usually have higher melting and boiling points than Group 1 elements.
    • Down a group, atomic size increases, metallic bond weakens, and melting point generally decreases.

    Quick comparison:

    Element Reason for relative melting point
    \(\mathrm{Be}\) Very small size, high charge density, strongest metallic bonding among these
    \(\mathrm{Mg}\) Group 2 metal, stronger metallic bonding than Group 1 metals
    \(\mathrm{Li}\) Highest melting point in Group 1 among these due to smaller size
    \(\mathrm{Na}\) Larger than Li, so weaker metallic bonding and lower melting point

    1-minute solving trick:

    • First remember: Group 2 \(>\) Group 1 in melting point.
    • Then remember: melting point decreases down the group.
    • So:
      • \(\mathrm{Be > Mg}\)
      • \(\mathrm{Li > Na}\)
    • Combining both gives:

      \(\mathrm{Be > Mg > Li > Na}\)

    Common mistakes to avoid:

    1. Assuming all Group 1 and Group 2 elements follow only atomic mass order.
    2. Forgetting that metallic bonding, not atomic mass, controls melting point here.
    3. Placing \(\mathrm{Li}\) above \(\mathrm{Mg}\) just because Li is smaller.
    4. Ignoring that Group 2 metals have stronger metallic bonding than Group 1 metals.

    Intuitive idea:

    • Smaller atom \(\rightarrow\) atoms packed more tightly
    • Higher charge density \(\rightarrow\) stronger attraction in metal lattice
    • Stronger metallic bond \(\rightarrow\) higher melting point

    2. Main idea of the Solvay process

      In this process, brine \(\left(\mathrm{NaCl}\right)\), ammonia \(\left(\mathrm{NH_3}\right)\), and carbon dioxide \(\left(\mathrm{CO_2}\right)\) are used to first form sodium hydrogen carbonate \(\left(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\right)\), which on heating gives sodium carbonate \(\left(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\right)\).

      On heating:

      \(\mathrm{2NaHCO_3 \rightarrow Na_2CO_3 + CO_2 + H_2O}\)

      Thus, sodium carbonate is obtained.

    3. Important raw materials in the Solvay process
      • Brine \(\left(\mathrm{NaCl}\right)\)
      • Ammonia \(\left(\mathrm{NH_3}\right)\)
      • Limestone \(\left(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\right)\)

      \(\mathrm{CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2}\)

      This \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) is then used in the process.

      The Solvay process is specifically associated with the manufacture of sodium carbonate, also called soda ash.

      • \((b)\) Castner-Kellner process: used for manufacture of sodium hydroxide \(\left(\mathrm{NaOH}\right)\), not sodium carbonate.

    General trends in Group 14
  • Group 14 elements have valence configuration \(ns^2np^2\), so \(+4\) and \(+2\) oxidation states are the most important. The \(+4\) state is favoured near the top of the group, while \(+2\) becomes more stable down the group because of the inert pair effect.
  • The stability of the \(+2\) state increases in the order \(Ge < Sn < Pb\). Hence \(Pb^{2+}\) is relatively stable and is not a good reducing agent.
  • \(Sn^{2+}\) acts as a reducing agent because it can be oxidised to \(Sn^{4+}\): \(Sn^{2+}\rightarrow Sn^{4+}+2e^-\). \(Pb^{4+}\) acts as an oxidising agent because it gets reduced to the more stable \(Pb^{2+}\): \(Pb^{4+}+2e^-\rightarrow Pb^{2+}\).
  • The most repeated redox-memory pair is: \(Sn^{2+}\) is reducing and \(Pb^{4+}\) is oxidising. Statements saying \(Pb^{2+}\) is a good reducing agent are incorrect.
  • Electronegativity generally decreases down a group, but Group 14 has a useful exception: among \(C\), \(Ge\), and \(Pb\), the order is \(C > Pb > Ge\). Poor shielding by inner \(d\)- and \(f\)-electrons makes \(Pb\) slightly more electronegative than expected.

    Melting point and bonding trends
  • \(C\), \(Si\), and \(Ge\) have covalent network structures, but bond strength decreases down the group as atomic size increases and orbital overlap becomes poorer. Therefore, the melting temperature order is \(C > Si > Ge\).
  • Among \(Si\), \(Ge\), \(Sn\), and \(Pb\), the useful melting point order is \(Si > Ge > Pb > Sn\). Hence tin has the lowest melting point among these four.
  • Group 14 melting points should not be treated as a smooth down-group trend. \(Si\) and \(Ge\) are high-melting covalent network solids, while \(Sn\) and \(Pb\) are metallic, giving an irregular order.

    Catenation and anomalous behaviour of carbon
  • Catenation is the ability of an element to form bonds with itself and produce chains, rings or networks. Carbon shows maximum catenation because it has small atomic size, strong \(C-C\) bond energy, and the ability to form single, double and triple bonds.
  • Catenation decreases down Group 14 as atomic size increases and element-element bond strength decreases. The useful trend is \(C > Si > Ge \approx Sn > Pb\), with \(Pb\) showing negligible catenation in this exam context.
  • If a question asks for maximum catenation, choose carbon first. If it asks for the element that does not show catenation among \(C\), \(Ge\), \(Sn\), and \(Pb\), choose lead.
  • Among the listed Group 14 choices in the PYQs, germanium is treated as the least abundant on earth. Thus the pair “maximum catenation + least abundance” becomes \(C, Ge\).

    Allotropes of carbon
  • Diamond has a three-dimensional network of directional covalent bonds. Each carbon is \(sp^3\)-hybridised, which explains its hardness and non-conducting nature.
  • Graphite has a layered structure. Each carbon is \(sp^2\)-hybridised and forms three \(\sigma\)-bonds in a planar sheet; the fourth electron is delocalised.
  • Graphite is used as a dry lubricant because strong covalent bonding exists within each layer, but weak van der Waals forces act between layers. These layers slide over one another under pressure, reducing friction.
  • In graphite, \(141.5\,pm\) is the \(C-C\) bond length within a layer, not the distance between adjacent layers. The adjacent layer separation is much larger, about \(335\,pm\).
  • Buckminster fullerene \((C_{60})\) has \(sp^2\)-hybridised carbon atoms and a delocalised \(\pi\)-electron system. Do not mark fullerene as \(sp^3\) merely because it is cage-shaped.
  • Graphite and fullerene both have \(sp^2\)-hybridised carbon, while diamond has \(sp^3\)-hybridised carbon. The quick hybridisation rule is: three bonds per carbon \(\rightarrow sp^2\); four bonds per carbon \(\rightarrow sp^3\).
  • Graphite is the thermodynamically most stable allotrope of carbon under ordinary conditions. It is the standard state, so \(\Delta H_f^\circ\) of graphite is \(0\).
  • The standard enthalpy of formation order for carbon allotropes is fullerene \(>\) diamond \(>\) graphite. Less stable allotropes have higher enthalpy, so the order is \(C_{60} > diamond > graphite\), or III \(>\) I \(>\) II.

    Hydrides and carbon monoxide
  • Group 14 hydrides such as \(CH_4\), \(SiH_4\), \(GeH_4\), and \(SnH_4\) are electron-precise hydrides. They have exactly enough electrons to form normal covalent bonds.
  • The fast hydride classification pattern is: Group 13 \(\rightarrow\) electron-deficient, Group 14 \(\rightarrow\) electron-precise, and Group 15/16 \(\rightarrow\) electron-rich due to lone pairs.
  • Formic acid gives carbon monoxide on dehydration with concentrated sulphuric acid at \(373\,K\): \(\mathrm{HCOOH \xrightarrow[\ 373\,K\ ]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} CO + H_2O}\).
  • Carbon in \(CO\) is \(sp\)-hybridised and \(CO\) is a neutral oxide. Do not confuse this dehydration reaction with oxidation to \(CO_2\), which is acidic.

    Oxides of Group 14 and related elements
  • \(CO\) is a neutral oxide, while \(CO_2\) is an acidic oxide. This distinction is repeatedly tested, especially in oxide-counting questions.
  • \(SiO_2\) is an acidic oxide and reacts with bases to form silicates, for example: \(\mathrm{SiO_2 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SiO_3 + H_2O}\).
  • \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\), and \(PbO_2\) are amphoteric oxides. They react with acids as well as bases, so they should not be counted as purely acidic oxides.
  • If both monoxide and dioxide are asked to be amphoteric in Group 14, the answer is \(Sn\) and \(Pb\). Carbon and silicon oxides are mainly acidic, so they do not satisfy this condition.
  • In mixed oxide lists, the acidic oxides commonly tested here are \(B_2O_3\), \(SiO_2\), and \(CO_2\). \(CO\) is neutral; \(Al_2O_3\), \(Ga_2O_3\), \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\), and \(PbO_2\) are amphoteric.
  • For the list \(\mathrm{CO,\ B_2O_3,\ SnO_2,\ PbO_2,\ Ga_2O_3,\ SnO,\ PbO,\ CO_2}\), the amphoteric oxides are \(\mathrm{SnO_2,\ PbO_2,\ Ga_2O_3,\ SnO,\ PbO}\), so the count is \(5\).
  • For the list \(\mathrm{CO,\ B_2O_3,\ SiO_2,\ CO_2,\ Al_2O_3,\ PbO_2,\ Tl_2O_3}\), the acidic oxides are \(\mathrm{B_2O_3,\ SiO_2,\ CO_2}\), so the count is \(3\).

    Halides and coordination species
  • \(CCl_4\) does not hydrolyse easily because carbon has no vacant \(d\)-orbitals and cannot expand its coordination number. This is the key contrast with \(SiCl_4\), which hydrolyses readily.
  • \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists because fluoride ions are small enough to fit around silicon. \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) does not exist because six large chloride ions cause steric crowding around small silicon.
  • Do not generalise that silicon cannot form six-coordinate species. The important deciding factor in these options is ligand size: \(F^-\) fits, \(Cl^-\) is too large.

    Silica, silicates, zeolites and silicones
  • Silica, \(SiO_2\), is an acidic oxide and reacts with bases to give silicates. It is also a key example in distinguishing acidic oxides from amphoteric oxides of tin and lead.
  • Glass is a man-made silicate. This is a direct factual point and should be linked with the wider silicates section.
  • Zeolites are hydrated aluminosilicates, commonly remembered as sodium aluminosilicates. The quick exam phrase is: zeolite = \(Na^+\) + \(Al^{3+}\) + silicate framework.
  • Do not identify zeolites as simple calcium or magnesium silicates just because \(Ca^{2+}\) and \(Mg^{2+}\) appear in water-hardness contexts. In composition questions, look for \(Na^+\) and \(Al^{3+}\).
  • Silicones are organosilicon polymers with a \(\mathrm{Si-O-Si}\) backbone. Their preparation from methyl chloride follows: \(2CH_3Cl + Si \xrightarrow[\ 573\,K\ ]{Cu} (CH_3)_2SiCl_2\).
  • Hydrolysis of \((CH_3)_2SiCl_2\) gives \((CH_3)_2Si(OH)_2\), which undergoes condensation polymerisation to form the repeating silicone unit \(\mathrm{-Si(CH_3)_2-O-}\). Do not choose structures with \(\mathrm{C-C}\) or \(\mathrm{CH_2-Si}\) as the backbone.

    Fast must-remember list
  • Oxidation states: Group 14 shows \(+4\) and \(+2\); \(+2\) stability increases down the group; \(Sn^{2+}\) is reducing and \(Pb^{4+}\) is oxidising.
  • Catenation: carbon is maximum; lead is negligible; catenation decreases down Group 14 due to weaker element-element bonding.
  • Allotropes: diamond \(sp^3\); graphite \(sp^2\), layered, conducting and lubricating; fullerene \(sp^2\); graphite is the standard state of carbon.
  • Oxides: \(CO\) neutral; \(CO_2\) and \(SiO_2\) acidic; \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\), \(PbO_2\) amphoteric.
  • Silicon compounds: \(CCl_4\) does not hydrolyse easily, \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists, \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) does not, zeolite is sodium aluminosilicate, and silicone has a \(\mathrm{Si-O-Si}\) backbone.

    General introduction and electronic configuration
  • Group 14 elements are carbon, silicon, germanium, tin and lead. Their general valence-shell configuration is \(ns^2np^2\), so four valence electrons are available for bonding.
  • The common oxidation states are \(+4\) and \(+2\). The \(+4\) state is more important for carbon and silicon, while the \(+2\) state becomes increasingly stable down the group due to inert pair effect.
  • Carbon and silicon are non-metals/metalloids with strong covalent chemistry, while tin and lead are metallic. This change in character explains why oxide nature, catenation, melting points and oxidation-state stability change sharply down the group.
  • Group 14 hydrides such as \(CH_4\), \(SiH_4\), \(GeH_4\) and \(SnH_4\) are electron-precise hydrides: they have just enough electrons to form normal covalent bonds.

    Atomic radii, ionisation enthalpy and electronegativity
  • Atomic radius generally increases down the group because a new shell is added at each step. As size increases, orbital overlap becomes poorer, so element-element bond strength decreases.
  • Ionisation enthalpy is the energy required to remove the outermost electron from an isolated gaseous atom. In general, it decreases down the group because the valence electrons are farther from the nucleus and less strongly held.
  • Electronegativity usually decreases down a group, but Group 14 shows useful irregularity among heavier elements. For \(C\), \(Ge\), and \(Pb\), the exam-useful order is \(C > Pb > Ge\), because poor shielding by inner \(d\)- and \(f\)-electrons makes \(Pb\) slightly more electronegative than expected.
  • Melting point is controlled not just by position in the group, but also by structure. \(C\), \(Si\), and \(Ge\) are network-type solids, so the order \(C > Si > Ge\) follows decreasing covalent bond strength.
  • Among \(Si\), \(Ge\), \(Sn\), and \(Pb\), the useful order is \(Si > Ge > Pb > Sn\). Thus tin has the lowest melting point among these four, and this order should be remembered directly.

    Oxidation states and inert pair effect
  • In Group 14, the expected higher oxidation state is \(+4\), because all four valence electrons can participate in bonding. However, down the group the \(ns^2\) pair becomes less available for bonding.
  • The reluctance of the \(ns^2\) pair to participate in bonding is called inert pair effect. As a result, the \(+2\) oxidation state becomes more stable down the group, while \(+4\) becomes less stable.
  • The stability of the \(+2\) state follows \(Ge < Sn < Pb\). Therefore \(Pb^{2+}\) is relatively stable and should not be treated as a good reducing agent.
  • \(Sn^{2+}\) acts as a reducing agent because it can be oxidised to \(Sn^{4+}\): \(Sn^{2+}\rightarrow Sn^{4+}+2e^-\). In contrast, \(Pb^{4+}\) acts as an oxidising agent because it tends to reduce to \(Pb^{2+}\): \(Pb^{4+}+2e^-\rightarrow Pb^{2+}\).
  • The high-recall redox pair is: \(Sn^{2+}\) is reducing and \(Pb^{4+}\) is oxidising. Statements saying \(Pb^{2+}\) is a good reducing agent are incorrect.

    Anomalous behaviour of carbon and catenation
  • Carbon differs strongly from the heavier members because of its small size, high electronegativity, high ionisation enthalpy and strong \(p\pi-p\pi\) multiple-bonding ability.
  • Catenation is the ability of an element to form bonds with itself to produce chains, rings and networks. Carbon shows maximum catenation because the \(C-C\) bond is strong and carbon can form single, double and triple bonds.
  • Catenation decreases down Group 14 as atomic size increases and element-element bond strength decreases. The useful order is \(C > Si > Ge \approx Sn > Pb\), with lead showing negligible catenation in EAPCET-style questions.
  • When “maximum catenation” is asked, carbon is the direct answer. When “does not show catenation” is asked among \(C\), \(Ge\), \(Sn\), and \(Pb\), choose lead.
  • Carbon forms a very large number of compounds because of catenation and multiple bonding. Silicon also shows some catenation, but \(Si-Si\) bonding is weaker than \(C-C\), so silicon forms far fewer chain compounds.

    Allotropes of carbon
  • Diamond and graphite are crystalline allotropes of carbon, but their structures and properties are different. Diamond has a three-dimensional covalent network, while graphite has layered hexagonal sheets.
  • In diamond, each carbon atom is \(sp^3\)-hybridised and bonded tetrahedrally to four other carbon atoms by strong directional covalent bonds. This explains its hardness and non-conducting nature.
  • In graphite, each carbon atom is \(sp^2\)-hybridised and bonded to three other carbon atoms in a hexagonal layer. The fourth electron is delocalised, which helps explain electrical conductivity.
  • Graphite is soft and useful as a dry lubricant because strong bonds exist within each layer, but adjacent layers are held by weak van der Waals forces. These layers can slide over one another under pressure.
  • In graphite, \(141.5\,pm\) is the \(C-C\) bond length within a layer, not the distance between adjacent layers. The interlayer separation is much larger, about \(335\,pm\).
  • Fullerene, especially \(C_{60}\), is a cage-like allotrope in which carbon atoms form a hollow soccer-ball type structure made from pentagons and hexagons. Carbon in fullerene is also mainly \(sp^2\)-hybridised.
  • The quick hybridisation rule is: four \(\sigma\)-bonds around carbon means \(sp^3\), while three \(\sigma\)-bonds with a delocalised \(\pi\)-system means \(sp^2\). Therefore, diamond is \(sp^3\), while graphite and fullerene are \(sp^2\).
  • Graphite is the thermodynamically most stable allotrope of carbon under ordinary conditions and is taken as the standard state. Hence \(\Delta H_f^\circ\) of graphite is \(0\).
  • Less stable allotropes have higher standard enthalpy of formation. The order is fullerene \(>\) diamond \(>\) graphite, i.e. \(C_{60} > diamond > graphite\).

    Uses of carbon
  • Diamond is used for cutting and grinding hard substances such as rocks and glass, and in dies for drawing wires. Its use follows directly from its very hard three-dimensional covalent network.
  • Graphite is used as electrodes, lubricant, in crucibles, in lead pencils and in heat-resistant paints. Its conductivity comes from delocalised electrons, while its lubricating nature comes from the easy sliding of layers.
  • Carbon monoxide is used as an industrial fuel and as a reducing agent. It is also a ligand in metal carbonyl chemistry, but for this topic its neutral oxide character and \(sp\)-hybridisation are more frequently tested.
  • Carbon dioxide is used as dry ice in refrigeration, in fire extinguishers and in the manufacture of urea. Its acidic oxide character is the key point for oxide-classification questions.

    Carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide
  • Carbon forms two important oxides: carbon monoxide, \(CO\), and carbon dioxide, \(CO_2\). \(CO\) is a neutral oxide, while \(CO_2\) is an acidic oxide.
  • In carbon monoxide, carbon and oxygen share three electron pairs. Carbon is treated as \(sp\)-hybridised, and the molecule is linear.
  • \(CO\) is colourless, odourless and poisonous because it forms a stable complex with haemoglobin, reducing oxygen transport in blood. It burns with a blue flame and acts as a reducing agent.
  • Formic acid gives \(CO\) on dehydration with concentrated sulphuric acid at \(373\,K\): \(\mathrm{HCOOH \xrightarrow[\ 373\,K\ ]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} CO + H_2O}\). This reaction should not be confused with oxidation to \(CO_2\).
  • Carbon dioxide is linear, \(O=C=O\), with carbon using \(sp\)-hybrid orbitals. It is an acidic oxide and reacts with bases to give carbonates or bicarbonates.
  • In oxide-classification questions, \(CO\) is neutral and \(CO_2\) is acidic. This single distinction prevents many counting mistakes.

    Oxides of Group 14 and oxide classification
  • In p-block chemistry, acidic character of oxides is usually associated with non-metals or higher oxidation states, while metallic and borderline elements often give basic or amphoteric oxides.
  • \(SiO_2\) is an acidic oxide. It reacts with bases to form silicates: \(\mathrm{SiO_2 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SiO_3 + H_2O}\).
  • \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\), and \(PbO_2\) are amphoteric oxides. They react with acids as well as bases, so they must not be counted as purely acidic oxides.
  • If both monoxide and dioxide are required to be amphoteric in Group 14, the standard answer is \(Sn\) and \(Pb\). Carbon and silicon oxides are mainly acidic, so they do not satisfy this condition.
  • For mixed oxide lists, remember: \(CO\) is neutral; \(CO_2\), \(SiO_2\), and \(B_2O_3\) are acidic; \(Al_2O_3\), \(Ga_2O_3\), \(SnO\), \(SnO_2\), \(PbO\), and \(PbO_2\) are amphoteric.
  • For the list \(\mathrm{CO,\ B_2O_3,\ SnO_2,\ PbO_2,\ Ga_2O_3,\ SnO,\ PbO,\ CO_2}\), the amphoteric oxides are \(\mathrm{SnO_2,\ PbO_2,\ Ga_2O_3,\ SnO,\ PbO}\), giving count \(5\).
  • For the list \(\mathrm{CO,\ B_2O_3,\ SiO_2,\ CO_2,\ Al_2O_3,\ PbO_2,\ Tl_2O_3}\), the acidic oxides are \(\mathrm{B_2O_3,\ SiO_2,\ CO_2}\), giving count \(3\).

    Silica and silicon dioxide, \(SiO_2\)
  • Silicon dioxide occurs widely as sand and quartz. It exists in several forms, including quartz and cristobalite, and has a giant covalent structure.
  • In \(SiO_2\), each silicon atom is tetrahedrally surrounded by four oxygen atoms, and each oxygen atom is bonded to two silicon atoms. This gives an infinite three-dimensional macromolecular structure and a high melting point.
  • \(SiO_2\) is an acidic oxide and is generally unreactive, but it reacts with alkalies and fused carbonates to form silicates. It also reacts with fluorine and hydrofluoric acid to form silicon tetrafluoride.
  • Important reactions of silica include: \(\mathrm{SiO_2 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SiO_3 + H_2O}\); \(\mathrm{SiO_2 + 2F_2 \rightarrow SiF_4 + O_2}\); \(\mathrm{SiO_2 + 4HF \rightarrow SiF_4 + 2H_2O}\).
  • Silica is used as sand in construction, as an acid flux in metallurgy, as coloured quartz for gems and as transparent quartz for lenses and optical instruments.

    Halides of carbon and silicon
  • Carbon and silicon form tetrahalides such as \(CCl_4\) and \(SiCl_4\). In these molecules, the central atom is \(sp^3\)-hybridised and the arrangement is tetrahedral.
  • \(CCl_4\) is not hydrolysed easily because carbon has no vacant \(d\)-orbitals and cannot expand its coordination number. This is a standard contrast with \(SiCl_4\).
  • \(SiCl_4\) is readily hydrolysed because silicon can accept a lone pair from water into vacant orbitals and then replace chlorine atoms by hydroxyl groups: \(\mathrm{SiCl_4 + 4H_2O \rightarrow Si(OH)_4 + 4HCl}\).
  • \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists because fluoride is small enough to fit around silicon. \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) does not exist because six large chloride ions create steric crowding around small silicon.
  • Do not generalise that silicon cannot form six-coordinate species. In these questions, ligand size is the key deciding factor: \(F^-\) fits, \(Cl^-\) is too large.

    Silicon carbide, silicates, glass and zeolites
  • Silicon carbide, \(SiC\), is called carborundum. It has a diamond-like three-dimensional covalent structure in which each silicon or carbon atom is tetrahedrally surrounded by four atoms of the other type.
  • \(SiC\) is extremely hard and chemically stable, so it is widely used as an abrasive. It is prepared by heating silica with excess carbon in an electric furnace.
  • Silicates contain strong \(Si-O\) bonds and occur in many materials such as granite, slate, bricks, cement, ceramics and glass. Glass is treated as a man-made silicate.
  • Zeolites are hydrated aluminosilicates, commonly remembered as sodium aluminosilicates. In composition questions, look for \(Na^+\), \(Al^{3+}\), and a silicate framework.
  • Zeolites have porous structures with cavities and channels, so they are useful in ion exchange, water softening and catalysis. Do not identify zeolite as a simple calcium or magnesium silicate merely because \(Ca^{2+}\) and \(Mg^{2+}\) are associated with hard water.

    Silicones
  • Silicones are polyorganosilicon compounds containing repeating \(Si-O-Si\) linkages. They may be linear, cyclic or branched.
  • A common preparation route begins with methyl chloride and silicon in the presence of copper at \(573\,K\): \(2CH_3Cl + Si \xrightarrow[\ 573\,K\ ]{Cu} (CH_3)_2SiCl_2\).
  • Hydrolysis of dimethyldichlorosilane gives dimethylsilanediol: \((CH_3)_2SiCl_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow (CH_3)_2Si(OH)_2 + 2HCl\).
  • Condensation polymerisation of \((CH_3)_2Si(OH)_2\) gives the silicone repeating unit \(\mathrm{-Si(CH_3)_2-O-}\). The backbone is \(Si-O-Si\), not \(C-C\) or \(CH_2-Si\).
  • Silicones are used in rubber preparation, waterproof clothes and papers, greases, aircraft lubricants, electrical insulation, paints and enamels because they withstand high temperatures.

    Final integrated comparison
  • Diamond: \(sp^3\), three-dimensional network, hardest natural substance, non-conductor. Graphite: \(sp^2\), layered, conductor, dry lubricant. Fullerene: cage-like \(C_{60}\), \(sp^2\), less stable than diamond and graphite.
  • \(CO\): neutral oxide, \(sp\)-hybridised carbon, poisonous, reducing agent. \(CO_2\): acidic oxide, linear, dry ice in solid state. \(SiO_2\): acidic oxide, giant covalent structure, forms silicates.
  • \(CCl_4\) does not hydrolyse easily, while \(SiCl_4\) hydrolyses readily. \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists, but \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) does not because chloride is too large for stable hexacoordination around silicon.
  • The most useful Group 14 memory set is: carbon shows maximum catenation, graphite is the standard state, \(Sn^{2+}\) is reducing, \(Pb^{4+}\) is oxidising, \(CO\) is neutral, \(CO_2\) and \(SiO_2\) are acidic, and \(Sn/Pb\) oxides are amphoteric.

  • Voids / Holes in crystal packing
    1. When atoms or ions are packed closely, some empty spaces remain between them. These empty spaces are called voids or holes.
    2. These holes are important because in ionic solids, usually one type of ion forms the main packing and the other type of ion occupies these holes.
  • Tetrahedral hole
    1. A tetrahedral hole is an empty space surrounded by 4 atoms.
    2. Easy picture:
      1. 3 atoms in one layer
      2. 1 atom above them
    3. The gap between them is a tetrahedral hole.
    4. It is called tetrahedral because the surrounding atoms form a tetrahedron.
  • Octahedral hole
    1. An octahedral hole is an empty space surrounded by 6 atoms.
    2. Easy picture:
      1. 3 atoms in one layer
      2. 3 atoms in another layer
    3. The gap between them is an octahedral hole.
    4. It is called octahedral because the surrounding atoms form an octahedron.
  • Important rule for CCP/FCC packing
    1. If \(n\) atoms form ccp/fcc packing, then:
      1. \(\text{Tetrahedral holes} = 2n\)
      2. \(\text{Octahedral holes} = n\)
    2. So if packed atoms \(= 4\), then:
      1. tetrahedral holes \(= 8\)
      2. octahedral holes \(= 4\)
  • NaCl structure
    1. In NaCl:
      1. \(\text{Cl}^-\) ions form FCC/CCP lattice
      2. \(\text{Na}^+\) ions occupy all octahedral holes
    2. Coordination:
      1. each \(\text{Na}^+\) is surrounded by 6 \(\text{Cl}^-\)
      2. each \(\text{Cl}^-\) is surrounded by 6 \(\text{Na}^+\)
    3. So coordination number is \(6:6\).
    4. Correct counting in one unit cell:
      1. For \(\text{Cl}^-\) ions:
        1. 8 corner \(\text{Cl}^-\) \(\rightarrow 8 \times \frac{1}{8} = 1\)
        2. 6 face-center \(\text{Cl}^-\) \(\rightarrow 6 \times \frac{1}{2} = 3\)
        3. Total \(\text{Cl}^- = 1 + 3 = 4\)
      2. For \(\text{Na}^+\) ions:
        1. 12 edge-center \(\text{Na}^+\) \(\rightarrow 12 \times \frac{1}{4} = 3\)
        2. 1 body-center \(\text{Na}^+\) \(\rightarrow 1\)
        3. Total \(\text{Na}^+ = 3 + 1 = 4\)
      3. So \(\text{Na}^+ : \text{Cl}^- = 4:4 = 1:1\).
      4. That is why the formula is NaCl.
    5. Important note:
      1. In diagrams, it may look like there are unequal numbers of ions, but that is because the drawing is only a representation.
      2. Ions must be counted by fractional contribution, not by direct visual counting.
      3. Useful fractions:
        1. corner \(= \frac{1}{8}\)
        2. face center \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
        3. edge center \(= \frac{1}{4}\)
        4. body center / fully inside \(= 1\)

  • Question: \(AB_2O_4\)
    1. Given:
      1. oxygen forms ccp
      2. A occupies \(\frac{1}{8}\) of tetrahedral holes
      3. find fraction of vacant octahedral holes
  • Step-by-step idea
    1. The formula is \(AB_2O_4\).
    2. This means the ratio is:
      1. \(A : B : O = 1 : 2 : 4\)
    3. So take:
      1. \(A = 1\)
      2. \(B = 2\)
      3. \(O = 4\)
  • Since oxygen forms CCP
    1. If oxygen atoms \(= 4\), then:
      1. \(\text{Tetrahedral holes} = 8\)
      2. \(\text{Octahedral holes} = 4\)
    2. So now we have:
      1. 4 oxygen atoms
      2. 8 tetrahedral holes
      3. 4 octahedral holes
  • A occupies \(\frac{1}{8}\) of tetrahedral holes
    1. Total tetrahedral holes \(= 8\).
    2. So number of A atoms:
      1. \(\frac{1}{8} \times 8 = 1\)
    3. This matches the formula.
  • Now find B occupancy
    1. From the formula \(AB_2O_4\), when \(A = 1\) and \(O = 4\), then:
      1. \(B = 2\)
    2. Total octahedral holes \(= 4\).
    3. B atoms occupy 2 of them.
    4. So occupied octahedral fraction:
      1. \(\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\)
    5. Therefore vacant octahedral fraction:
      1. \(1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\)
    6. Final answer:
      1. \(\boxed{\frac{1}{2}}\)
  • Super-simple way to think of this question
    1. With 4 oxygen atoms:
      1. tetrahedral holes \(= 8\)
      2. octahedral holes \(= 4\)
    2. A fills \(\frac{1}{8}\) of 8 tetrahedral holes:
      1. so \(A = 1\)
    3. Formula says if \(A = 1\), then \(B = 2\).
    4. So B fills 2 out of 4 octahedral holes.
    5. Hence:
      1. occupied octahedral holes \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
      2. vacant octahedral holes \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
  • Final memory points
    1. Tetrahedral hole \(\rightarrow\) surrounded by 4 atoms
    2. Octahedral hole \(\rightarrow\) surrounded by 6 atoms
    3. In ccp/fcc, if atoms \(= n\):
      1. tetrahedral holes \(= 2n\)
      2. octahedral holes \(= n\)
    4. In NaCl:
      1. \(\text{Cl}^-\) forms fcc
      2. \(\text{Na}^+\) occupies all octahedral holes
      3. coordination number \(= 6:6\)
    5. In \(AB_2O_4\) with oxygen in ccp:
      1. 4 O gives 8 tetrahedral holes and 4 octahedral holes
      2. A occupies 1 tetrahedral hole
      3. B occupies 2 octahedral holes
      4. vacant octahedral holes \(= \frac{1}{2}\)

Important Physical Chemistry Revision

    1. (Refer to 2025, May 2, Shift 1, Q 131)

    • Electron-deficient hydrides: Central atom has fewer electrons than required for a stable octet after bond formation. Example: \(\mathrm{B_2H_6,\ AlH_3}\).
    • Electron-precise hydrides: Central atom has exactly the required number of electrons for normal covalent bonding after bond formation. Example: \(\mathrm{CH_4,\ SiH_4}\).
    • Electron-rich hydrides: Central atom has lone pair(s) even after bond formation. Example: \(\mathrm{HCl,\ H_2S,\ NH_3,\ H_2O}\).
    Hydride type Typical examples
    Electron-deficient hydrides \(\mathrm{B_2H_6,\ AlH_3}\)
    Electron-precise hydrides \(\mathrm{CH_4,\ SiH_4}\)
    Electron-rich hydrides \(\mathrm{H_2O,\ NH_3,\ H_2S,\ HCl}\)
    2. (Quick Things to Remember)

    • Artificial sweetening agents: These are low calorie sweeteners used as sugar substitutes. Examples: \(\mathrm{Saccharin,\ Aspartame,\ Sucralose}\).
    • Food preservatives: These are chemical substances used to protect food against bacteria, yeasts and moulds. Examples: \(\mathrm{Table\ salt,\ Sugar\ syrup}\).
    • Antacids: These are drugs used to remove excess acid in the stomach and maintain normal pH. Examples: \(\mathrm{Omeprazole,\ Lansoprazole}\).
    • Antibiotics: These are drugs used to inhibit the growth of or destroy micro-organisms and are produced by micro-organisms. Examples: \(\mathrm{Penicillin,\ Chloramphenicol,\ Sulphadiazine}\).
    • Antiseptics: These are drugs used to kill or prevent the growth of micro-organisms. Examples: \(\mathrm{Dettol,\ Bithional,\ Tincture\ of\ iodine}\).
    • Antihistamines: These are drugs used to diminish the effect of histamine and prevent allergy. Examples: \(\mathrm{Dimetane\ (Dimetapp),\ Terfenadine\ (Seldane)}\).
    • Analgesics: These are drugs used to reduce or abolish pain without causing disturbances to the nervous system. Examples: \(\mathrm{Morphine,\ Codeine,\ Aspirin,\ Ibuprofen}\).
    • Tranquilizers: These are drugs mainly used in the treatment of psychoses and neuroses. Examples: \(\mathrm{Luminal,\ Seconal,\ Barbituric\ acid}\).

Important Organic Chemistry Revision

    1. (Quick revision cheat sheet for EAPCET)

    • Use this exact naming method every time:
      1. Find the principal functional group.
      2. Choose the main chain containing that group and the maximum number of multiple bonds.
      3. Number by: \(\mathrm{Principal\ FG > C=C > C\equiv C > substituents}\).
      4. Write substituents in alphabetical order.
      5. Final order: \(\mathrm{prefixes + parent + en/yn + suffix}\).
    • Functional-group priority ladder: \(\mathrm{COOH > SO_3H > Anhydride > Ester > COX > CONH_2 > CN > CHO > CO > OH > NH_2 > C=C > C\equiv C > substituents}\)
    • Numbering rule: Principal functional group gets first priority. If both double and triple bonds are present, \(\mathrm{ene}\) gets lower locant than \(\mathrm{yne}\) in a tie.
    • Useful instant prefixes: \(\mathrm{-OH \rightarrow hydroxy,\ -NH_2 \rightarrow amino,\ -CHO \rightarrow formyl,\ >C=O \rightarrow oxo,\ -CN \rightarrow cyano,\ -COOH \rightarrow carboxy,\ -OR \rightarrow alkoxy,\ -NO_2 \rightarrow nitro,\ -F/-Cl/-Br/-I \rightarrow fluoro/chloro/bromo/iodo}\)
    • High-yield traps: Do not choose the longest chain blindly; \(\mathrm{-OH}\) beats \(\mathrm{C=C}\) and \(\mathrm{C\equiv C}\); substituents are written in alphabetical order; in esters write \(\mathrm{alkyl\ first,\ alkanoate\ second}\); in \(\mathrm{-COOH}\), \(\mathrm{-CN}\), and \(\mathrm{-CHO}\), the functional carbon is usually carbon \(1\).

Solutions

    Exam map / pattern map
  • Solutions PYQs here cluster around five fast-solve zones: colligative-property comparisons, osmotic pressure, Henry’s law, Raoult’s law, and vapour-phase composition.
  • If the question compares boiling point or freezing point for equal concentration solutions, first think effective particles, not molar mass.
  • If electrolyte + non-electrolyte are mixed, count particles using van’t Hoff factor \(i\) before using any formula.
  • If gas solubility is asked through \(K_H\), remember: smaller Henry constant \(\Rightarrow\) greater solubility.
  • If total vapour pressure changes after mixing ideal liquids, write Raoult’s law for both states and solve simultaneously.
  • If vapour composition is asked, use partial pressure first, then \( y_i=\dfrac{p_i}{p_{\text{total}}} \).

    Core formulas
  • Raoult’s law for volatile component: \( p_A=x_Ap_A^0 \).
  • Total vapour pressure of ideal binary solution: \( p_{\text{total}}=x_Ap_A^0+x_Bp_B^0 \).
  • Vapour-phase mole fraction: \( y_A=\dfrac{p_A}{p_{\text{total}}} \).
  • Relative lowering of vapour pressure for non-volatile solute: \( \dfrac{p^0-p}{p^0}=x_{\text{solute}} \) for dilute solution.
  • Osmotic pressure: \( \pi=iCRT \).
  • Boiling-point elevation: \( \Delta T_b=iK_bm \).
  • Henry’s law: \( p=K_Hx \).

    Fast proportionality rules
  • Same molarity, stronger dissociation \(\Rightarrow\) larger \(i\) \(\Rightarrow\) larger \( \Delta T_b \), \( \Delta T_f \), \( \pi \).
  • Equal concentration urea vs KCl: KCl gives larger colligative effect because it produces more particles.
  • Lower \(K_H\) means gas dissolves more easily; do not reverse this.
  • For non-volatile solute, only solvent contributes to vapour pressure.
  • More volatile liquid has larger \(p^0\), so its vapour-phase mole fraction is higher than in liquid.
  • Tiny relative lowering of vapour pressure for fixed solute mass usually hints at high molar mass.

    Question-to-formula mapping
  • If the question says “osmotic pressure at given \(T\) and \(V\),” go straight to \( \pi=iCRT \).
  • If both electrolyte and non-electrolyte are present, first convert each into effective moles of particles.
  • If a table gives \(K_H\) values and asks gas-solubility order, sort by inverse order of \(K_H\).
  • If two ideal-solution vapour pressures are given before and after adding one component, set up two Raoult equations.
  • If vapour-phase composition is asked from liquid mole fraction, calculate \(p_A\) and \(p_B\) first; never equate vapour fraction with liquid fraction directly.
  • If molar mass is asked from vapour-pressure lowering, use relative lowering to get solute mole fraction, then moles, then molar mass.

    Common traps
  • Do not say boiling-point elevation is inversely proportional to molar mass in general; it depends directly on \(i\), \(K_b\), and molality.
  • Do not forget dissociation when using osmotic pressure for salts.
  • Do not rank gas solubility in the same order as \(K_H\); the relation is opposite.
  • Do not use mole fraction of solute when the question needs mole fraction of solvent for vapour pressure of non-volatile-solute solution.
  • Do not assume vapour mole fraction equals liquid mole fraction unless the components have equal volatility.
  • Do not force every grouped-list anchor into Solutions; a few live-page entries drift into nearby topics.

    Solve-fast reminders
  • Electrolyte question \(\Rightarrow\) compute \(i\) first.
  • Gas-solubility table \(\Rightarrow\) smallest \(K_H\) first.
  • Ideal liquid pair \(\Rightarrow\) Raoult + mole fraction.
  • Vapour composition \(\Rightarrow\) partial pressures first, ratio later.
  • Relative lowering \(\Rightarrow\) dilute solution means \(x_{\text{solute}}\) is the key bridge.

    Concentration terms
  • Common concentration expressions are mass %, volume %, mass-by-volume %, ppm, mole fraction, molarity, and molality.
  • Mole fraction is most useful in vapour-pressure questions.
  • Molality is preferred in boiling-point elevation and freezing-point depression because it is temperature independent.
  • Molarity is convenient in osmotic-pressure numericals through \( \pi=iCRT \).

    Solubility of gases and Henry’s law
  • For gases dissolved in liquids, Henry’s law is \( p=K_Hx \).
  • \(p\) is partial pressure of gas above solution, \(x\) is mole fraction dissolved, and \(K_H\) is Henry’s law constant.
  • At fixed temperature, smaller \(K_H\) means higher solubility of gas.
  • This is why gas-solubility ordering questions can often be solved directly from the \(K_H\) table without calculation.

    Vapour pressure and Raoult’s law
  • For an ideal binary liquid solution, \( p_A=x_Ap_A^0 \) and \( p_B=x_Bp_B^0 \).
  • Total vapour pressure is \( p_{\text{total}}=p_A+p_B=x_Ap_A^0+x_Bp_B^0 \).
  • For a non-volatile solute, only solvent contributes: \( p=x_{\text{solvent}}p_{\text{solvent}}^0 \).
  • Raoult’s law is central in PYQs asking pure-component vapour pressures, total vapour pressure, or pressure after adding one component.
  • For ideal solutions, intermolecular interactions \(A\!-\!A\), \(B\!-\!B\), and \(A\!-\!B\) are similar, so Raoult’s law is followed closely.

    Vapour composition
  • The vapour phase is usually richer in the more volatile component.
  • Vapour-phase mole fraction is found by Dalton’s relation: \( y_A=\dfrac{p_A}{p_{\text{total}}} \).
  • So the standard route is:
    • find each partial pressure using Raoult’s law
    • add them for total pressure
    • divide required partial pressure by total pressure
  • This directly explains benzene–toluene type PYQs where liquid mole fraction and vapour mole fraction are not equal.

    Colligative properties
  • Colligative properties depend on the number of solute particles, not on their chemical identity.
  • Main colligative properties:
    • relative lowering of vapour pressure
    • elevation of boiling point
    • depression of freezing point
    • osmotic pressure
  • Boiling-point elevation: \( \Delta T_b=iK_bm \).
  • Freezing-point depression: \( \Delta T_f=iK_fm \).
  • Relative lowering of vapour pressure for dilute solution: \( \dfrac{p^0-p}{p^0}=x_{\text{solute}} \).
  • Osmotic pressure: \( \pi=iCRT \).

    van’t Hoff factor
  • van’t Hoff factor \(i\) corrects for association or dissociation of solute.
  • For non-electrolytes like glucose or urea, \(i\approx 1\).
  • For electrolytes like NaCl or KCl, \(i>1\) because more particles are produced in solution.
  • This is the first thing to check in colligative-property comparison PYQs.
  • If dissociation is partial, effective particles must be counted using degree of dissociation.

    Determination of molar mass
  • Unknown molar mass is frequently determined from colligative-property data.
  • From relative lowering of vapour pressure, first obtain \(x_{\text{solute}}\), then solute moles, then molar mass from given mass.
  • From osmotic pressure, use \( \pi=iCRT \) to find concentration, then moles, then molar mass if required.
  • A very small vapour-pressure lowering for a known solute mass usually indicates a large molar mass.

    Common exam-use distinctions
  • Do not confuse liquid mole fraction \(x_i\) with vapour mole fraction \(y_i\).
  • Do not rank solubility in the same order as \(K_H\); the relation is inverse.
  • Do not ignore \(i\) for salts in osmotic-pressure or boiling-point questions.
  • Do not use mole fraction of solute when vapour pressure of solvent is being asked in a non-volatile-solute problem.
  • In ideal-solution numericals, two different mixing conditions often give two linear equations in \(p_A^0\) and \(p_B^0\).

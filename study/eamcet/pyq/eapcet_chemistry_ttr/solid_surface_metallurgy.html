SOLID STATE, SURFACE CHEMISTRY, METALLURGY

    1. Density of cubic unit cell

      \( \rho=\dfrac{ZM}{N_Aa^3} \)

      \( M=\dfrac{\rho N_Aa^3}{Z} \)

      \( Z=\dfrac{\rho N_Aa^3}{M} \)

      Use \(Z=4\) for fcc and rock-salt type structures.

      Convert edge length before cubing: \(1\,\mathrm{pm}=10^{-10}\,\mathrm{cm}\), \(1\,\text{\AA}=10^{-8}\,\mathrm{cm}\).

    2. Effective contribution of atoms in a unit cell

      Corner atom contribution \(=\dfrac{1}{8}\).

      Edge-centre atom contribution \(=\dfrac{1}{4}\).

      Face-centre atom contribution \(=\dfrac{1}{2}\).

      Body-centre atom contribution \(=1\).

      These contribution rules are the base for empirical-formula questions from unit-cell positions.

    3. Common cubic unit-cell anchors

      \( \mathrm{sc:\ Z=1,\ CN=6} \)

      \( \mathrm{bcc:\ Z=2,\ CN=8} \)

      \( \mathrm{fcc/ccp:\ Z=4,\ CN=12} \)

      Rock-salt \((\mathrm{NaCl})\)-type structure has \(4\) formula units per unit cell.

    4. Radius and edge-length relations

      Simple cubic: \( a=2r \).

      bcc: atoms touch along body diagonal, so \( \sqrt{3}a=4r \) and \( a=\dfrac{4r}{\sqrt{3}} \).

      fcc/ccp: atoms touch along face diagonal, so \( \sqrt{2}a=4r \) and \( a=2\sqrt{2}r \).

    5. Density ratio for same metal in two crystal forms

      For the same substance, \( d\propto\dfrac{Z}{a^3} \).

      \( \dfrac{d_1}{d_2}=\dfrac{Z_1}{Z_2}\times\dfrac{a_2^3}{a_1^3} \)

      Edge length must be cubed because unit-cell volume is \(a^3\).

    6. Voids in ccp/fcc packing

      If \(N\) particles form ccp/fcc packing:

      Octahedral voids \(=N\).

      Tetrahedral voids \(=2N\).

      For \(4\) oxide ions in ccp: octahedral voids \(=4\), tetrahedral voids \(=8\).

    7. Formula from occupied voids

      First count the lattice-forming ions.

      Then count the occupied tetrahedral and octahedral voids.

      For \(\mathrm{AB_2O_4}\) with ccp oxide lattice: \(O=4\), tetrahedral voids \(=8\), octahedral voids \(=4\).

      \(A=1\) means \(A\) occupies \(\dfrac{1}{8}\) of tetrahedral voids.

      \(B=2\) means \(B\) occupies \(\dfrac{1}{2}\) of octahedral voids.

    8. Vacant octahedral void fraction in \(\mathrm{AB_2O_4}\)

      With \(4\) oxide ions in ccp, total octahedral voids \(=4\).

      \(B=2\) occupies \(2\) octahedral voids.

      Occupied octahedral fraction \(=\dfrac{2}{4}=\dfrac{1}{2}\).

      Vacant octahedral fraction \(=\dfrac{1}{2}\).

    9. Formula from corners, edge centres and body centre

      If \(W\) is at \(8\) corners: \(W=8\times\dfrac{1}{8}=1\).

      If \(O\) is at \(12\) edge centres: \(O=12\times\dfrac{1}{4}=3\).

      If \(\mathrm{Na}\) is at body centre: \(\mathrm{Na}=1\).

      Formula \(=\mathrm{NaWO_3}\).

    10. Formula when corner atoms are missing

      If \(3\) out of \(8\) corner atoms are missing, only \(5\) corners contribute.

      Corner species contribution \(=5\times\dfrac{1}{8}=\dfrac{5}{8}\).

      Body-centred species contribution \(=1\).

      Multiply by \(8\) to get the simplest whole-number formula.

    11. Schottky defect

      Schottky defect involves equal number of cation and anion vacancies.

      Electrical neutrality is maintained.

      Stoichiometry is maintained.

      Density decreases because ions are missing from lattice sites.

    12. Frenkel defect and F-centres

      Frenkel defect is a dislocation defect where an ion leaves its normal lattice site and occupies an interstitial site.

      It is favoured by large cation-anion size difference.

      F-centres are electrons trapped in anion vacancies.

      F-centres are responsible for colour in many ionic crystals.

    13. Non-stoichiometric oxide with mixed oxidation states

      For \( \mathrm{M_{0.96}O} \), let \(x\) metal ions be \(\mathrm{M^{3+}}\).

      Remaining \(\mathrm{M^{2+}}\) ions \(=0.96-x\).

      Charge neutrality gives \( 3x+2(0.96-x)=2 \).

      After solving, fraction of \(\mathrm{M^{3+}}=\dfrac{x}{0.96}\).

    14. Crystal-system identification

      Tetragonal: \( a=b\neq c \), \( \alpha=\beta=\gamma=90^\circ \).

      Hexagonal: \( a=b\neq c \), \( \alpha=\beta=90^\circ,\ \gamma=120^\circ \).

      Cubic: \( a=b=c \), \( \alpha=\beta=\gamma=90^\circ \).

      The \(120^\circ\) basal angle is the quickest clue for the hexagonal system.

    15. Distance between octahedral voids in fcc

      In fcc, octahedral voids occur at body centre and edge centres.

      If body diagonal \(=x\), then \(x=\sqrt{3}a\), so \(a=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{3}}\).

      Nearest body-centre to edge-centre octahedral void distance \(=\dfrac{a}{\sqrt{2}}\).

      Therefore distance \(=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{6}}\).

    16. Surface-area scaling after cutting a cube

      If a cube of side \(a\) is divided into smaller cubes of side \(b\):

      \( \dfrac{\text{total surface area after cutting}}{\text{original surface area}}=\dfrac{a}{b} \)

      \(1\,\mathrm{cm}=10^7\,\mathrm{nm}\).

      Surface-area ratio is not the same as the number of small cubes.


    1. Freundlich adsorption isotherm

      \( \dfrac{x}{m}=kp^{1/n} \), where \(n>1\).

      \( \log\dfrac{x}{m}=\log k+\dfrac{1}{n}\log p \)

      Slope of \(\log\dfrac{x}{m}\) vs \(\log p\) graph \(=\dfrac{1}{n}\).

      Intercept \(=\log k\); if intercept \(=1\), then \(k=10\).

    2. Physical adsorption conditions

      Physisorption is due to weak van der Waals forces.

      It is favoured by high surface area, low temperature and high pressure.

      Adsorption of gases on solids is generally exothermic.

      In adsorption-versus-pressure curves, the curve with higher \(x/m\) at the same pressure corresponds to lower temperature.

    3. Hardy-Schulze rule for coagulation

      Coagulating power depends mainly on the valency of the oppositely charged ion.

      For negatively charged sols, cations cause coagulation.

      For positively charged sols, anions cause coagulation.

      For negative \(\mathrm{As_2S_3}\) or \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\) sol: \(\mathrm{Al^{3+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) is more effective than \(\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}\) or \(\mathrm{Na^+}\).

    4. Flocculating value

      Flocculating value \(=\) millimoles of electrolyte required to coagulate \(1\,\mathrm{L}\) of sol.

      If molarity and volume are given: millimoles \(=M\times\mathrm{mL}\).

      If mass is given: first convert mass to moles, then scale to \(1\,\mathrm{L}\).

      Lower flocculating value means greater coagulating power.

    5. Classification of colloids

      Sol \(=\) solid dispersed in liquid.

      Foam \(=\) gas dispersed in liquid or solid.

      Gel \(=\) liquid dispersed in solid.

      Aerosol \(=\) solid or liquid dispersed in gas.

      Examples: paint \(=\) sol, whipped cream \(=\) foam, butter \(=\) gel, cloud \(=\) aerosol.

    6. Tyndall effect

      Tyndall effect is scattering of light by colloidal particles.

      It distinguishes colloids from true solutions.

      It is prominent when dispersed phase and dispersion medium differ appreciably in refractive index.

      Examples showing Tyndall effect: milk, clouds, suspensions.

      Sugar solution is a true solution, so it does not show Tyndall effect appreciably.

    7. Brownian motion and stability of sols

      Brownian motion is random zig-zag motion of colloidal particles.

      It helps prevent settling and stabilises sols.

      Brownian motion becomes slower in high-viscosity media.

    8. Electrophoresis

      Electrophoresis is movement of charged colloidal particles under a DC electric field.

      Particles move towards the oppositely charged electrode.

      It is different from Brownian motion, which is random thermal motion.

    9. Bredig's arc method

      Bredig's arc method is an electrical dispersion method.

      It is used to prepare metal sols such as gold, silver and platinum sols.

      Metal sols prepared this way are treated as negatively charged in the PYQ pattern.

      It is not the standard method for metal sulphide sols such as \(\mathrm{As_2S_3}\).

    10. Gold number and protective colloids

      Gold number measures protective power of a lyophilic colloid.

      Lower gold number means better protective action.

      It is used in questions on protection of colloids from coagulation.

    11. Micelle formation

      Ionic micelles form from amphiphilic ionic surfactants above critical micelle concentration.

      A micelle-forming molecule has a long hydrophobic hydrocarbon tail and a hydrophilic ionic head.

      Sodium lauryl sulphate forms ionic micelles in water.

      Iodine, \(\mathrm{S_8}\) and starch are not standard ionic-micelle examples.

    12. Catalyst selectivity in \(\mathrm{CO}\) hydrogenation

      \( \mathrm{CO+H_2\xrightarrow{Cu}HCHO} \)

      \( \mathrm{CO+3H_2\xrightarrow{Ni}CH_4+H_2O} \)

      Copper gives partial hydrogenation to formaldehyde.

      Nickel gives complete hydrogenation/methanation to methane.

    13. Industrial catalysts

      Ostwald process: Pt gauze.

      Haber process: Fe.

      Deacon process: \(\mathrm{CuCl_2}\).

      Catalytic cracking of hydrocarbons: zeolites.

      Contact-process oxidation of \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) to \(\mathrm{SO_3}\): Pt in the PYQ summary pattern.

      Lead chamber oxidation: NO.

    14. Calcination versus roasting

      Calcination is heating ore in absence or limited supply of air.

      It commonly removes \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) or \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) from hydrated or carbonate ores.

      \( \mathrm{Fe_2O_3\cdot xH_2O\xrightarrow{\Delta}Fe_2O_3+xH_2O} \)

      Roasting is heating sulphide ore in oxygen to form oxide and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).

      Dehydration of hydrated ore is calcination, not roasting.


    1. Ore and metal identification anchors

      \(\mathrm{sphalerite=ZnS\rightarrow Zn}\)

      \(\mathrm{siderite=FeCO_3\rightarrow Fe}\)

      \(\mathrm{calamine=ZnCO_3\rightarrow Zn}\)

      \(\mathrm{malachite=CuCO_3\cdot Cu(OH)_2\rightarrow Cu}\)

      \(\mathrm{copper\ pyrites/chalcopyrite=CuFeS_2\rightarrow Cu}\)

      Malachite is a copper ore; calamine and sphalerite are zinc ores.

    2. Mineral-to-metal matching

      Kaolinite \(\rightarrow\) aluminium mineral.

      Calamine \(\rightarrow\) zinc ore.

      Sphalerite \(\rightarrow\) zinc ore.

      Siderite \(\rightarrow\) iron ore.

      Malachite \(\rightarrow\) copper ore.

      Calamine is the strong clue for zinc; kaolinite is the aluminium mineral clue.

    3. Concentration by leaching

      Bauxite is concentrated by leaching in Bayer's process using concentrated \(\mathrm{NaOH}\).

      Alumina dissolves while many impurities remain undissolved.

      Cyanide leaching of silver:

      \(\mathrm{4Ag+8CN^-+2H_2O+O_2\rightarrow4[Ag(CN)_2]^-+4OH^-}\)

      Leaching is chemical dissolution, not a physical concentration method like froth flotation or levigation.

    4. Calcination versus roasting

      Calcination: heating ore in absence or limited supply of air.

      Used for hydrated ores and carbonate ores.

      \(\mathrm{Fe_2O_3\cdot xH_2O\xrightarrow{\Delta}Fe_2O_3+xH_2O}\)

      Roasting: heating sulphide ore in oxygen to form oxide and \(\mathrm{SO_2}\).

      Sulphide ore heated in oxygen indicates roasting, not calcination.

    5. Copper extraction roasting reactions

      \(\mathrm{2Cu_2S+3O_2\xrightarrow{\Delta}2Cu_2O+2SO_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{2FeS+3O_2\xrightarrow{\Delta}2FeO+2SO_2}\)

      Copper matte is mainly \(\mathrm{Cu_2S+FeS}\).

      Matte is sulphide-rich; slag is silicate-rich.

    6. Copper extraction slag formation

      \(\mathrm{FeO+SiO_2\xrightarrow{\Delta}FeSiO_3}\)

      Silica flux removes iron impurity as ferrous silicate slag.

      \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\rightarrow CaSiO_3}\) belongs more naturally to iron extraction slag logic, not copper extraction.

    7. Copper self-reduction

      \(\mathrm{2Cu_2O+Cu_2S\xrightarrow{\Delta}6Cu+SO_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is the gaseous product and has bent/angular shape.

      This is the key self-reduction step in copper extraction from sulphide ore.

    8. Iron extraction flux and slag

      \(\mathrm{CaCO_3\xrightarrow{\Delta}CaO+CO_2}\)

      \(\mathrm{CaO+SiO_2\xrightarrow{\Delta}CaSiO_3}\)

      In blast furnace, \(\mathrm{SiO_2}\) is acidic gangue and \(\mathrm{CaCO_3}\) is added flux.

      \(\mathrm{CaSiO_3}\) is the slag.

    9. Ellingham diagram

      Ellingham diagram is a plot of \(\Delta G^\circ\) versus \(T\).

      It helps choose reducing agents for oxide reduction.

      Lower \(\Delta G^\circ\) means more stable oxide.

      The x-axis is \(T\), not \(1/T\).

    10. Refining by distillation and liquation

      Distillation is used for volatile metals such as \(\mathrm{Zn}\) and \(\mathrm{Hg}\).

      Distillation works when the metal has low boiling point and impurity has high boiling point.

      Liquation is used for metals with low melting point, such as \(\mathrm{Sn}\).

      Distillation is boiling-point based; liquation is melting-point based.

    11. Special refining methods

      Zone refining: ultra-pure metals such as \(\mathrm{Ga}\).

      Poling: \(\mathrm{Cu}\).

      Mond process: \(\mathrm{Ni}\).

      van Arkel process: \(\mathrm{Ti/Zr}\).

      Vapour phase refining: \(\mathrm{Ti}\) in the PYQ matching pattern.

      Mond and van Arkel are both vapour-phase type examples, but they are different processes.

    12. Alloy-use anchors

      \(\mathrm{Li-Pb}\) alloy: bearings for motor engines.

      \(\mathrm{Be-Cu}\) alloy: high-strength springs.

      \(\mathrm{Mg-Al}\) alloy: aircraft construction.

      \(\mathrm{Na-Pb}\) alloy: tetraethyl lead preparation.

      \(\mathrm{Li-Pb}\) and \(\mathrm{Na-Pb}\) are easy to interchange, so keep their uses separate.

Stoichiometry / Some Basic Concepts / Redox Reactions

    Exam map for this combined topic
  • The repeated PYQ buckets are: mole concept, equivalent weight, solution concentration, oxidation number, redox classification, balancing, reaction stoichiometry, combustion/gas-volume stoichiometry, and significant figures.
  • Before solving, first tag the question: mass–mole conversion / concentration / n-factor / oxidation state / redox type / balancing / gas-volume ratio / significant-figure rule.
  • Most questions reduce to one of three engines: \(n=\frac{w}{M}\), n-factor/equivalent-weight, or oxidation-number/electron balance.

    Mole concept and core conversions
  • \(n=\dfrac{w}{M}\), \(N=nN_A\), and for gases at same conditions, volume ratio = mole ratio.
  • Mass of solute in solution questions often use \(w = Molarity \times V(\text{L}) \times\) molar mass.
  • Same number of atoms questions require converting each sample into moles of atoms, not just moles of molecules.
  • Combustion and decomposition questions become easy once the balanced equation is written first.

    Equivalent weight and n-factor
  • \(\text{Equivalent weight}=\dfrac{\text{molar mass}}{n\text{-factor}}\).
  • For acids: n-factor = number of \(H^+\) actually furnished.
  • For bases: n-factor = number of \(OH^-\) actually furnished.
  • For salts, n-factor depends on the total positive or negative charge exchanged. Example: \(Na_2CO_3\) has n-factor \(=2\) because \(CO_3^{2-}\) carries charge 2.
  • For redox species: n-factor = number of electrons lost or gained per formula unit.
  • Very important: n-factor is reaction-dependent, especially in redox reactions.
  • For elements combining with oxygen, equivalent weight = mass combining with 8 g oxygen.
  • For acids/bases/salts, use the reaction-specific n-factor, not a memorized value from another context.
  • For oxidants/reductants, n-factor = electrons gained or lost per formula unit in that medium.
  • \(KMnO_4\) and \(K_2Cr_2O_7\) change equivalent weight with medium, so always check acidic/basic/neutral condition first.

    Oxidation number and redox identification
  • Use standard oxidation-number rules first: free element 0, oxygen usually \(-2\), hydrogen usually \(+1\), and total equals charge.
  • Lowest oxidation-state questions are pure bookkeeping once those rules are applied carefully.
  • Redox means oxidation number changes; non-redox means no oxidation-number change.
  • Disproportionation means the same element is both oxidized and reduced in the same reaction.

    Balancing redox reactions
  • In acidic/basic-medium redox, oxidation-number and half-reaction methods are both valid; choose the faster one.
  • Always balance atoms undergoing change first, then oxygen with \(H_2O\), hydrogen with \(H^+\), and finally charge with electrons; in basic medium, neutralize \(H^+\) using \(OH^-\).
  • After balancing, coefficient-sum questions are usually direct arithmetic.
  • If one mole of oxidant is given, compare total electrons accepted to electrons lost by the reducing species.

    Reaction stoichiometry shortcuts
  • Mixture heating questions often rely on only one component decomposing; use the observed mass loss to back-calculate that component.
  • Redox precipitation questions like \(Cu^{2+}+I^-\) require both electron transfer and product mole ratios.
  • Gas-volume combustion questions use Avogadro/Gay-Lussac directly: at same \(T,P\), volume ratio = coefficient ratio.
  • When both mass and volume data appear, convert everything to moles before comparing.

    Significant figures and rounding
  • All non-zero digits are significant.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
  • Leading zeros are not significant.
  • Trailing zeros to the right of a decimal are significant.
  • For addition/subtraction, round by decimal places; for multiplication/division, round by significant figures.

    Question-to-rule map
  • “Highest amount of solute” \(\rightarrow\) compare \(M \times V \times\) molar mass.
  • Equivalent-weight matching \(\rightarrow\) compute n-factor in the exact reaction context.
  • Oxidation-state question \(\rightarrow\) assign oxidation number to the target atom only, then compare.
  • Disproportionation question \(\rightarrow\) check whether one element moves simultaneously up and down in oxidation state.
  • Mass-loss mixture question \(\rightarrow\) identify the decomposing component and write its decomposition equation first.

    Common traps
  • Do not use a fixed equivalent weight for \(KMnO_4\) or dichromate without checking the medium.
  • Do not confuse mole ratio with mass ratio.
  • In significant-figure addition, do not round by total significant figures; use decimal-place rule instead.
  • Do not call every redox reaction disproportionation; disproportionation is a special subset.
  • For gas-volume stoichiometry, use same-condition volume ratios only after confirming the gases are compared under the same conditions.

    Some basic concepts and laws of chemical combination
  • Matter is measured using mass, moles, particles, and sometimes gas volume under stated conditions.
  • Important laws include conservation of mass, definite proportions, multiple proportions, Gay-Lussac’s law of gaseous volumes, and Avogadro’s law. These laws justify stoichiometric mole-ratio calculations.
  • Mole concept links microscopic particles to laboratory-scale masses through Avogadro’s number.

    Mole concept, molar mass, and equivalent weight
  • Moles: \(n=\dfrac{w}{M}\), where \(w\) is mass and \(M\) is molar mass.
  • Number of particles: \(N=nN_A\).
  • Equivalent weight \(=\dfrac{\text{molar mass}}{n\text{-factor}}\).
  • Equivalent weight depends on the reaction context, especially for oxidants, reductants, acids, and bases.
  • Permanganate and dichromate are classic reaction-context substances because their n-factor changes with medium.

    Percentage composition and empirical / molecular formulae
  • Percentage composition connects element mass data to formula determination.
  • Empirical formula is the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms.
  • Molecular formula is an integer multiple of the empirical formula.
  • Combustion data of hydrocarbons or organics often leads to empirical or molecular formula through mole ratios of \(CO_2\) and \(H_2O\).

    Stoichiometric calculations
  • Always start from the balanced chemical equation.
  • Convert given quantities into moles first, apply mole ratio from the equation, then convert back to the asked quantity.
  • Mixture problems often combine a chemical change with an observed mass loss or gas evolution.
  • For gas-volume stoichiometry under the same conditions, volume ratio equals mole ratio.

    Ways of expressing concentration
  • Mass percent, mole fraction, molarity, molality, and normality are all syllabus-listed concentration terms.
  • Molarity \(M=\dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{litres of solution}}\).
  • Mass of solute in a solution can be found by \(w = M \times V(\text{L}) \times\) molar mass.
  • This is the direct tool for “highest amount of solute” style PYQs.

    Oxidation, reduction, and oxidation number
  • Classically, oxidation means addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen, while reduction is the reverse.
  • Electron-transfer view: oxidation is loss of electrons and reduction is gain of electrons.
  • Oxidation number is a bookkeeping device used to track oxidation and reduction.
  • Common rules: elemental form \(=0\), oxygen usually \(-2\), hydrogen usually \(+1\), and sum of oxidation numbers equals the species charge.

    Types of redox reactions
  • Combination, decomposition, displacement, and disproportionation are specifically named in the syllabus.
  • Disproportionation occurs when the same element is simultaneously oxidized and reduced.
  • \(H_2O_2 \rightarrow H_2O + O_2\) and \(Cl_2 + NaOH\) are classic disproportionation examples.
  • Not every redox reaction is disproportionation, so the oxidation-state pattern must be checked explicitly.

    Balancing redox reactions
  • Two main methods are prescribed: oxidation-number method and half-reaction (ion-electron) method.
  • In acidic medium, use \(H^+\), \(H_2O\), and electrons appropriately.
  • In basic medium, complete the acidic balancing first if needed, then neutralize \(H^+\) by adding \(OH^-\) to both sides.
  • Coefficient-based PYQs often become simple once the electron balance is correct.

    Redox reactions in titrimetry
  • Permanganate and dichromate are standard oxidants in titrimetric redox calculations.
  • One mole of oxidant corresponds to a fixed number of moles of electrons accepted in the stated medium.
  • Questions such as “moles of \(Fe^{2+}\) oxidized by one mole of oxidant” are solved by electron-equivalent comparison.

    Significant figures and rounding
  • Leading zeros are not significant.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant.
  • Addition/subtraction is rounded by decimal places; multiplication/division by significant figures.
  • Many PYQs here are direct rule-recognition questions, so memorizing these four rules pays off quickly.

    How to solve this topic quickly
  • If mass/volume/concentration is given, convert to moles first.
  • If equivalent weight is asked, identify the exact n-factor for that reaction, not a generic one.
  • If oxidation/reduction is asked, assign oxidation numbers only to the changing atoms first.
  • If a redox balancing question appears, balance electron change before worrying about final coefficients.
  • If significant figures are involved, apply the relevant rounding rule only at the final step.

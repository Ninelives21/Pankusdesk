Hydrogen and Its Compounds

    Hydrides: exam-classification map
  • Ionic / saline hydrides are formed mainly by alkali metals and heavier alkaline earth metals; they contain \(H^-\).
  • Covalent hydrides may be electron-deficient, electron-precise, or electron-rich.
  • Electron-rich hydrides have lone pairs on the central atom; common examples: \(HCl,\ H_2S,\ NH_3,\ H_2O\).
  • Electron-deficient hydrides have fewer electrons than needed for normal two-centre two-electron bonds; example: \(B_2H_6\).
  • Polymeric hydrides repeatedly asked: \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\).
  • \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\) also show significant covalent character.

    Ionic hydrides: one-step recall
  • Molten ionic hydrides contain \(H^-\), so on electrolysis oxidation occurs at anode.
  • Anode process: \(2H^- \rightarrow H_2 + 2e^-\).
  • Fast trap: hydrogen is liberated at the anode, not at the cathode.
  • Stability trend of alkali metal hydrides increases down the group: \(LiH < NaH < KH < RbH < CsH\).

    Water and heavy water: direct-fact cluster
  • Heavy water is \(D_2O\).
  • Freezing point of \(D_2O\) is about \(3.8^\circ C\).
  • Temperature of maximum density: \(H_2O \approx 4^\circ C\), \(D_2O \approx 11.2^\circ C\).
  • So \((x-y)\) for maximum-density temperatures of \(D_2O\) and \(H_2O\) is nearly \(7\ \mathrm{K}\).
  • Reaction memory: \(CaC_2 + 2D_2O \rightarrow C_2D_2 + Ca(OD)_2\).

    Hardness of water: methods and mappings
  • Temporary hardness due to bicarbonates can be removed by boiling.
  • \(Mg(HCO_3)_2\) on boiling gives \(Mg(OH)_2\) precipitate.
  • \(Ca(HCO_3)_2\) on boiling gives \(CaCO_3\) precipitate.
  • Clark’s method uses \(Ca(OH)_2\) to remove bicarbonate hardness.
  • Calgon method complexes \(Ca^{2+}\) / \(Mg^{2+}\) ions and keeps them in solution.
  • Ion exchange / zeolite methods replace hardness-causing ions by \(Na^+\) or \(H^+\).
  • Deionised water needs both cationic and anionic exchangers, one after the other.

    \(H_2O_2\): structure, storage, and numerical shortcuts
  • \(H_2O_2\) is non-planar.
  • Dihedral angle is about \(111.5^\circ\) in gaseous phase and \(90.2^\circ\) in solid phase.
  • Store \(H_2O_2\) in a wax-lined container and keep it in the dark.
  • It decomposes in light and in presence of alkali, rough surfaces, or metal impurities.
  • \(2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2\).
  • \(x\)-volume means \(1\) volume of solution liberates \(x\) volumes of \(O_2\) at STP/NTP.
  • Useful shortcut: \((w/v)\% \approx \dfrac{x}{3.3}\).
  • So \(20\)-volume \(H_2O_2 \approx 6.06\%\ (w/v)\).
  • For \(H_2O_2\), normality \(N \approx \dfrac{\text{strength in g L}^{-1}}{17}\).
  • Hence \(20\)-volume \(H_2O_2\) has normality about \(3.57\ N\).

    Position of hydrogen and isotopes
  • Hydrogen occupies a unique position because it resembles both alkali metals and halogens in different ways.
  • Main isotopes are protium \((^1H)\), deuterium \((^2H\ or\ D)\), and tritium \((^3H\ or\ T)\).
  • Heavy water is \(D_2O\), formed by replacing ordinary hydrogen with deuterium.

    Hydrides: classification and recognition
  • Hydrides are compounds of hydrogen with other elements.
  • Ionic or saline hydrides are formed by highly electropositive metals and contain \(H^{-}\).
  • Covalent hydrides are commonly grouped as electron-deficient, electron-precise, and electron-rich.
  • Electron-deficient hydrides include \(B_2H_6\) and \(AlH_3\).
  • Electron-precise hydrides include \(CH_4\) and \(SiH_4\).
  • Electron-rich hydrides contain lone pairs on the central atom; examples include \(H_2O,\ NH_3,\ HCl,\ H_2S\).
  • Hydrogen usually shows oxidation state \(+1\), but in metal hydrides it shows \(-1\).

    Polymeric and covalent character in hydrides
  • \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\) are important polymeric hydrides repeatedly tested in PYQs.
  • They form bridged structures in the solid state instead of existing as simple discrete molecules.
  • Because \(Be^{2+}\) and \(Mg^{2+}\) are small and polarizing, these hydrides also show significant covalent character.

    Ionic hydrides and electrolysis
  • Molten ionic hydrides dissociate into metal cations and hydride ions.
  • During electrolysis, \(H^{-}\) migrates to the anode and is oxidized.
  • Anode reaction: \(2H^- \rightarrow H_2 + 2e^-\).
  • This is why hydrogen gas is liberated at the anode in molten ionic hydrides.

    Water and heavy water
  • Water shows unusual physical properties because of hydrogen bonding.
  • Heavy water differs from ordinary water because deuterium is heavier than protium.
  • The freezing point of heavy water is about \(3.8^\circ C\).
  • The temperature of maximum density is about \(4^\circ C\) for \(H_2O\) and \(11.2^\circ C\) for \(D_2O\).
  • The difference in these temperatures is therefore about \(7\ \mathrm{K}\).
  • Heavy water participates in isotope-exchange style reactions and introduces deuterium into products.
  • Example: \(CaC_2 + 2D_2O \rightarrow C_2D_2 + Ca(OD)_2\).

    Hard and soft water
  • Hardness of water is mainly due to dissolved salts of calcium and magnesium.
  • Temporary hardness is due to bicarbonates such as \(Ca(HCO_3)_2\) and \(Mg(HCO_3)_2\).
  • On boiling, \(Ca(HCO_3)_2\) gives \(CaCO_3\) precipitate.
  • On boiling, \(Mg(HCO_3)_2\) gives \(Mg(OH)_2\) precipitate.
  • Clark’s method removes temporary hardness by adding lime, \(Ca(OH)_2\).
  • Calgon method uses sodium hexametaphosphate to complex hardness-causing ions.
  • Ion-exchange and zeolite methods replace hardness-causing ions by exchangeable ions such as \(Na^+\).
  • Deionised water is obtained only after passing water through both cationic and anionic exchangers successively.

    Hydrogen peroxide: structure and properties
  • \(H_2O_2\) is a non-planar molecule with an open-book type arrangement.
  • Its dihedral angle is about \(111.5^\circ\) in gaseous phase and about \(90.2^\circ\) in solid phase.
  • It decomposes according to \(2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2\).
  • Its decomposition is accelerated by light, alkali, rough surfaces, and metal impurities.

    Hydrogen peroxide: storage and numerical conversions
  • Hydrogen peroxide is stored in wax-lined containers and kept in the dark.
  • Avoid metal vessels and alkaline contamination because they speed up decomposition.
  • \(x\)-volume means one unit volume of solution gives \(x\) unit volumes of oxygen.
  • From \(2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2\), 68 g \(H_2O_2\) liberate 22.4 L \(O_2\).
  • Using this, 20-volume hydrogen peroxide corresponds to about \(6.06\%\ (w/v)\).
  • The same 20-volume solution corresponds to about \(3.57\ N\).

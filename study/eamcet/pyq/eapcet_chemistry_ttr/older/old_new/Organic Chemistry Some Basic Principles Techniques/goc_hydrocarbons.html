Organic Chemistry : Some Basic Principles, Techniques

    Electron-displacement effect map
  • Hyperconjugation = delocalisation of \(\sigma\)(C–H) electrons of an alkyl group into an adjacent \(\pi\)-system or vacant \(p\)-orbital.
  • Typical PYQ cue: “\(\sigma\) electrons of C–H bond of alkyl group with benzene \(\pi\) electrons” \(\Rightarrow\) hyperconjugation.
  • \(-R\) / \(-M\) effect means electron withdrawal by resonance, common for groups like \(-CHO,\ -NO_2,\ -COOH\).
  • Electromeric effect is temporary and appears only in the presence of an attacking reagent.
  • If the structure shows permanent charge delocalisation by conjugation, it is resonance / resonance effect, not electromeric effect.

    Carbocation stability
  • General order: benzylic / allylic \(>\) \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ >\) methyl.
  • Vinylic carbocations such as \(\mathrm{CH_2=CH^+}\) are very unstable.
  • Resonance stabilisation beats simple alkyl substitution.
  • If the cation carbon is itself part of a double bond, expect the least stable choice.

    Nitrogen detection and estimation
  • Lassaigne’s test for nitrogen: sodium fusion converts covalent nitrogen into \(\mathrm{NaCN}\).
  • Boiling sodium extract with \(\mathrm{FeSO_4}\), then acidification, giving Prussian blue \(\Rightarrow\) nitrogen present.
  • Kjeldahl method estimates nitrogen only when nitrogen is convertible to ammonium sulphate on digestion.
  • Nitrogen in nitro, azo, diazo, ring nitrogen like pyridine/quinoline, etc. is generally not estimated directly by Kjeldahl method.
  • Amines and amides are standard positive-type Kjeldahl candidates.

    Distillation technique map
  • Steam distillation: for steam-volatile, water-immiscible or sparingly water-soluble, high-boiling organic compounds.
  • Classic PYQ-fit pair: aniline + water.
  • Reduced-pressure distillation: for high-boiling liquids that decompose near or below their boiling point.
  • Fractional distillation: for miscible liquids with close boiling points.
  • Do not choose steam distillation for two normal miscible hydrocarbons like \(n\)-hexane and \(n\)-heptane.

    Nomenclature and structure representation
  • In IUPAC naming, highest-priority functional group decides suffix and parent chain numbering.
  • Nitrile carbon is included in the parent chain and numbered as carbon-1.
  • For bond-line vs condensed vs complete structures, first identify how many carbons are explicitly shown and where hydrogens are suppressed.
  • Aromatic naming questions often test lowest set of locants and alphabetical listing of substituent prefixes.

    Ozonolysis and oxidative cleavage
  • Ozonolysis cleaves \(\mathrm{C=C}\) into carbonyl fragments.
  • Use the products to reconstruct the original alkene by joining the carbonyl carbons with a double bond.
  • \(\mathrm{KMnO_4/H^+}\) on alkenes can give oxidative cleavage products; terminal \(\mathrm{=CH_2}\) carbon often ends as \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) under vigorous conditions.
  • If ozonolysis gives acetone and 3-pentanone, rebuild the alkene around those two carbonyl carbons.

    Alkynes and acidic hydrogen
  • Only terminal alkynes \(\mathrm{R-C{\equiv}C-H}\) have acidic hydrogen.
  • Internal alkynes do not have acidic hydrogen on the triple-bond carbon.
  • For formula-based isomer counting, first list all alkyne skeletons, then separate terminal vs internal.

    Aromaticity and electrophilic substitution
  • Aromatic species must be cyclic, planar, conjugated, and follow \((4n+2)\pi\) electrons.
  • Benzene reactions like nitration, sulphonation, halogenation and Friedel–Crafts are electrophilic substitution reactions.
  • If the ring loses aromaticity without regaining it, the species is not aromatic.
  • Benzene \(\xrightarrow[\mathrm{AlCl_3}]{}\) toluene or acetophenone is a direct Friedel–Crafts clue.

  1. Structural representation of organic compounds
    • Organic compounds are commonly represented as complete structural formulae, condensed formulae, and bond-line formulae.
    • In bond-line notation, each line end or vertex represents a carbon atom unless another atom is shown explicitly.
    • Hydrogens attached to carbon are usually omitted in bond-line structures and must be inferred from carbon valency.
    • This is directly tested through comparison-type questions such as identifying the three representations of \(n\)-butane.

  2. Nomenclature basics
    • Choose the longest parent chain containing the principal functional group and maximum multiple bonds.
    • The principal functional group determines the suffix and gets the lowest possible locant.
    • Nitrile carbon is part of the parent chain and is counted as carbon-1.
    • Substituents are written as prefixes in alphabetical order; multiplicative prefixes like di-, tri- do not control alphabetising.
    • Aromatic naming often uses the ring as parent unless a higher-priority functional group changes the parent choice.

  3. Isomerism
    • Structural isomerism includes chain, position, functional, metameric and tautomeric variations in common exam framing.
    • Geometrical isomerism in alkenes requires restricted rotation and two different groups on each carbon of the double bond.
    • Branched chains generally have lower boiling points than corresponding straight-chain isomers because of lower surface area.
    • Terminal vs internal alkynes become a separate counting issue because only terminal alkynes carry acidic hydrogen.

  4. Electron displacement effects
    • Inductive effect is permanent polarisation through \(\sigma\)-bonds due to electronegativity difference.
    • Resonance effect \((+R/-R)\) is permanent electron donation or withdrawal through conjugation.
    • Electromeric effect is temporary and occurs only in the presence of an attacking reagent.
    • Hyperconjugation is delocalisation of \(\sigma\)(C–H) electrons adjacent to a multiple bond, carbocation, or aromatic ring.
    • Alkyl groups stabilise carbocations and substituted alkenes partly through hyperconjugation.

  5. Reaction intermediates and stability
    • Carbocation stability depends strongly on resonance and hyperconjugation.
    • Benzylic and allylic carbocations are resonance-stabilised.
    • Among simple alkyl carbocations, more substituted cations are generally more stable.
    • Vinylic carbocations are unusually unstable and appear as “least stable” traps in MCQs.

  6. Purification techniques
    • Simple distillation is used when boiling-point difference is large or one component is non-volatile.
    • Fractional distillation is used for miscible liquids with close boiling points.
    • Steam distillation is used for steam-volatile, water-insoluble or sparingly water-soluble, high-boiling organic compounds.
    • Reduced-pressure distillation is used when the liquid has high boiling point and decomposes near its boiling point.
    • These distinctions were directly tested through aniline/water and high-boiling decomposing-liquid questions.

  7. Qualitative analysis of organic compounds
    • In sodium fusion (Lassaigne’s test), covalently bonded N, S, and halogens are converted into ionic salts so that inorganic tests become possible.
    • Nitrogen gives \(\mathrm{NaCN}\), which with \(\mathrm{FeSO_4}\) and acid ultimately yields Prussian blue colour.
    • This confirms nitrogen, not sulphur, chlorine or phosphorus.
    • Kjeldahl method estimates nitrogen by converting it to ammonia, which is then absorbed in acid and back-titrated if required.
    • Compounds with nitro or azo nitrogen usually do not fit ordinary Kjeldahl estimation.

  8. Hydrocarbons, aromaticity, and electrophilic substitution
    • Aromatic compounds must be cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, and satisfy Hückel’s \((4n+2)\pi\)-electron rule.
    • Benzene undergoes electrophilic substitution rather than addition because aromaticity is preserved.
    • Friedel–Crafts alkylation and acylation use alkyl halides or acyl halides with anhydrous \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\).
    • Benzene to toluene suggests \(\mathrm{CH_3Cl/AlCl_3}\); benzene to acetophenone suggests \(\mathrm{CH_3COCl/AlCl_3}\).

  9. Ozonolysis, hydration, oxidation, and elimination pattern
    • Ozonolysis cleaves alkenes into aldehydes and/or ketones depending on substitution around the double bond.
    • Vigorous oxidation with \(\mathrm{KMnO_4/H^+}\) can cleave double bonds; terminal methylene may end up as \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).
    • Hydration of alkynes under \(\mathrm{Hg^{2+}/H^+}\) gives enols which tautomerise to carbonyl compounds.
    • Alcohol dehydration often gives the more substituted alkene under acidic conditions.
    • Many questions in this topic are solved by rebuilding structures backward from products.

  10. What this PYQ cluster repeatedly tests
    • Identification of electron-displacement effects from pictures or statements.
    • IUPAC naming under mixed substituent / multifunctional conditions.
    • Use of Lassaigne and Kjeldahl methods for nitrogen.
    • Correct selection of distillation technique.
    • Carbocation stability, aromaticity, and electrophilic substitution.
    • Product reconstruction from ozonolysis, oxidation, hydration and elimination sequences.

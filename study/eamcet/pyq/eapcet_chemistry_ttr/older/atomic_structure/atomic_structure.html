  • For hydrogen-like species, the most important radius formula is \[ r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z} \]
  • For hydrogen atom, orbit energy is \[ E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \] or \[ E_n = -\frac{2.18 \times 10^{-18}}{n^2}\ \text{J} \]
  • For emission or absorption, \[ \Delta E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]
  • Must-remember hydrogen spectral series:
    • Lyman \(\rightarrow n_f = 1\)
    • Balmer \(\rightarrow n_f = 2\)
    • Paschen \(\rightarrow n_f = 3\)
    • Brackett \(\rightarrow n_f = 4\)
    • Pfund \(\rightarrow n_f = 5\)
  • Rydberg equation: \[ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \]
  • For photoelectric effect, remember: \[ K_{\max} = h\nu - \phi = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - \phi \]
  • Very useful shortcut: \[ E(\text{eV}) = \frac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})} \]
  • Quantum model quick facts:
    • probability density \(= |\psi|^2\)
    • \(m_l\) gives orientation
    • number of orbitals in a subshell \(= 2l + 1\)
    • \(l = 0,1,2,3 \Rightarrow s,p,d,f\)
  • Rutherford model can explain the nucleus, but it cannot explain atomic stability.
  • EM-spectrum reminder:
    • X-rays have smaller wavelength than microwaves
    • microwaves have larger wavelength than X-rays
  • Last-minute checklist:
    • memorise \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
    • memorise \(E_n \propto -\dfrac{1}{n^2}\)

  1. What 2025 is repeatedly testing in Atomic Structure
    • In the 2025 papers, Atomic Structure is a very strong opening chapter.
    • The repeated exam pattern is not random. It strongly revolves around:
      • Bohr radius of hydrogen and hydrogen-like species
      • hydrogen spectral series
      • Rydberg equation
      • energy of orbits and \(\Delta E = h\nu\)
      • photoelectric effect numericals
      • basic quantum-number and wave-function facts
      • Rutherford-model limitation
    • So this chapter should be revised as a compact formula-and-concept toolkit, not as a long theory chapter.
  2. Bohr model of hydrogen and hydrogen-like species
    • For hydrogen-like species: \[ r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z} \]
    • So:
      • radius increases as \(n^2\)
      • radius decreases as nuclear charge \(Z\) increases
    • For ratio-based problems, it is often faster to use: \[ \frac{r_A}{r_B} = \frac{n_A^2/Z_A}{n_B^2/Z_B} \] instead of substituting actual \(a_0\).
    • This logic directly solves questions comparing H with \(\mathrm{He^+}\), \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), and \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\).
  3. Energy of orbits and excitation / emission
    • For hydrogen atom: \[ E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \] or \[ E_n = -\frac{2.18\times10^{-18}}{n^2}\ \text{J} \]
    • Lower orbit to higher orbit \(\rightarrow\) excitation \(\rightarrow\) energy absorbed.
    • Higher orbit to lower orbit \(\rightarrow\) de-excitation \(\rightarrow\) energy emitted.
    • In both cases: \[ \Delta E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]
    • If the question gives orbit radii instead of orbit numbers, first identify \(n\) from radius, then apply the energy formula.
  4. Hydrogen spectral series and Rydberg equation
    • Final orbit decides the series:
      • Lyman \(\rightarrow n_f = 1\)
      • Balmer \(\rightarrow n_f = 2\)
      • Paschen \(\rightarrow n_f = 3\)
      • Brackett \(\rightarrow n_f = 4\)
      • Pfund \(\rightarrow n_f = 5\)
    • Rydberg equation: \[ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \]
    • A common exam shortcut is:
      • if final orbit radius is given, first find \(n_f\)
      • then identify the spectral series
    • Another common pattern is comparing two emitted wavelengths by writing the Rydberg expression for each transition.
  5. Photoelectric effect
    • Main equation: \[ K_{\max} = h\nu - \phi = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - \phi \]
    • Very useful shortcut: \[ E(\text{eV}) = \frac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})} \]
    • Key meaning:
      • \(h\nu\) = photon energy
      • \(\phi\) = work function of metal
      • \(K_{\max}\) = maximum kinetic energy of emitted electron
    • Important ideas:
      • emission occurs only if photon energy \(\ge \phi\)
      • extra energy appears as kinetic energy
      • intensity mainly changes number of emitted electrons, not their maximum kinetic energy
    • Some questions give energy per particle, some per mole. Keep units consistent throughout the calculation.
  6. Quantum mechanical model and quantum numbers
    • Important quantum numbers:
      • \(n\) \(\rightarrow\) principal quantum number
      • \(l\) \(\rightarrow\) subshell / shape
      • \(m_l\) \(\rightarrow\) orientation
      • \(m_s\) \(\rightarrow\) spin
    • Subshell mapping:
      • \(l=0 \rightarrow s\)
      • \(l=1 \rightarrow p\)
      • \(l=2 \rightarrow d\)
      • \(l=3 \rightarrow f\)
    • Number of orbitals in a subshell: \[ 2l + 1 \]
    • Probability density is: \[ |\psi|^2 \] not \(|\psi|^3\).
    • This part of the chapter is often tested through small but tricky fact-based questions, not long derivations.
  7. Rutherford model limitation and electromagnetic spectrum
    • Rutherford model successfully explained the existence of a small dense nucleus.
    • But it failed to explain why electrons do not collapse into the nucleus.
    • According to classical theory, an accelerating charged particle should continuously radiate energy.
    • So a revolving electron should lose energy and spiral inward.
    • That is why Rutherford model cannot explain atomic stability.
    • EM-spectrum reminder:
      • microwaves have larger wavelength than infrared
      • X-rays have much smaller wavelength than microwaves
      • gamma rays have the smallest wavelength in the usual school-level ordering

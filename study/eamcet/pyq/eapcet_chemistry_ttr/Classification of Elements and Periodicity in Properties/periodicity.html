Classification of Elements and Periodicity in Properties

    Exam map for this topic
  • Most PYQs fall into a few buckets: atomic radius, ionic radius, ionisation enthalpy, electron gain enthalpy, metallic/non-metallic character, oxide acidity/basicity, and periodic-table position.
  • Before solving, first identify the bucket: trend order / isoelectronic comparison / exception / block-group-period / large I.E. jump.
  • Many questions are pure recall of one strong exception plus one standard trend.

    Atomic radius: fastest rules
  • Across a period, atomic radius generally decreases because effective nuclear charge increases.
  • Down a group, atomic radius generally increases because new shells are added.
  • Largest left-side drop in the same period is usually near the beginning, like Li to Be.
  • Lanthanoid contraction makes some \(4d\) and \(5d\) pairs nearly equal in size: \(Zr \approx Hf,\ Mo \approx W\).
  • If a radius order conflicts with both “across decreases” and “down increases,” reject it quickly.

    Ionic radius and isoelectronic shortcuts
  • For isoelectronic species, radius decreases as nuclear charge increases.
  • Quick rule: same electrons, more protons \(\Rightarrow\) smaller ion.
  • Among \(O^{2-},F^-,Na^+,Mg^{2+},Al^{3+}\), first count electrons, then sort by nuclear charge.
  • Anions are usually larger than parent atoms; cations are usually smaller than parent atoms.
  • Extra shell beats isoelectronic pull: \(K^+\) stays larger than the 10-electron ions.

    First ionisation enthalpy: trend plus classic exceptions
  • General trend across a period: \(IE_1\) increases.
  • Exception 1: \(Be > B\) and \(Mg > Al\) because filled \(s^2\) subshell is more stable.
  • Exception 2: \(N > O\) because half-filled \(p^3\) is more stable.
  • If asked for a missing period-3 value, place Al below Mg but above Na.
  • “Higher than both neighbours” in period 2 directly points to Be and N.

    Second and successive ionisation enthalpy
  • For \(IE_2\), do not study neutral atoms first — study the singly charged cations.
  • Stable cation before electron removal \(\Rightarrow\) very high next ionisation enthalpy.
  • Large jump after the \(n^{\text{th}}\) ionisation means the atom had \(n\) valence electrons.
  • Huge jump after third I.E. \(\Rightarrow\) group 13 pattern.

    Electron gain enthalpy: direct rules and must-know exceptions
  • More negative value means stronger tendency to accept an electron.
  • Across a period, EGE generally becomes more negative; down a group it generally becomes less negative.
  • Key exception: \(Cl\) has more negative EGE than \(F\).
  • Key exception: \(S\) has more negative EGE than \(P\).
  • Oxygen is less negative than sulphur because incoming electron enters compact \(2p\) orbital with more repulsion.
  • Noble gases show positive electron gain enthalpy because an extra electron must enter the next shell.

    Metallic character and oxide nature
  • Across a period, metallic character decreases; down a group, it increases.
  • More metallic means easier electron loss and usually lower ionisation enthalpy.
  • Oxides across a period move from basic \(\rightarrow\) amphoteric \(\rightarrow\) acidic.
  • Useful pattern: \(Li_2O\) basic, \(BeO\) amphoteric, \(B_2O_3/CO_2/N_2O_3\) acidic.
  • Acidic strength of non-metal oxides generally increases with non-metallic character.

    Block, group, period, and periodic-table position
  • Block is determined by the subshell receiving the differentiating electron.
  • \(s\)-block \(\Rightarrow ns\), \(p\)-block \(\Rightarrow np\), \(d\)-block \(\Rightarrow (n-1)d\), \(f\)-block \(\Rightarrow (n-2)f\).
  • For \(d\)-block elements, group number \(=\) \((n-1)d + ns\) electrons.
  • Period number is the highest principal quantum number present.
  • Atomic-number-to-position questions often reduce to identifying the element first, then reading its block/group/period.

    Electron counting mini-rules
  • For “same \(s:p\) ratio” or “count \(s\)- and \(p\)-electrons,” write full configuration and total each type.
  • For ions, add electrons for anions and subtract for cations before counting.
  • Isoelectronic species questions are just electron-count questions in disguise.

    Common traps
  • Do not apply neutral-atom trends directly to ions without checking isoelectronic relationships.
  • Do not forget period-trend exceptions: \(Be/B,\ Mg/Al,\ N/O,\ Cl/F,\ S/P\).
  • Do not treat “more negative electron gain enthalpy” and “larger numerical value” carelessly — sign matters.
  • In assertion-reason ionic size questions, “isoelectronic” alone does not mean “same radius.”

    Last-minute question-to-rule map
  • Atomic radius order \(\rightarrow\) use across decreases, down increases, then check exceptions like lanthanoid contraction.
  • Ionic radius order \(\rightarrow\) first count electrons, then compare nuclear charge or shell number.
  • Unknown ionisation pattern \(\rightarrow\) spot the exceptional stable configuration or the giant jump.
  • Electron gain enthalpy order \(\rightarrow\) start with trend, then apply \(Cl/F\), \(S/P\), \(O/S\), noble-gas exceptions.
  • Block/group/period \(\rightarrow\) write configuration, then read highest \(n\), differentiating subshell, and valence pattern.

    Need for classification and modern periodic law
  • As the number of known elements increased, a systematic arrangement became necessary to relate properties, predict trends, and study reactivity efficiently.
  • Modern periodic law states that the physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.
  • The modern periodic table arranges elements in increasing atomic number and groups elements with similar valence-shell patterns together.
  • Periods correspond broadly to principal shells being filled, while groups collect similar outer electronic configurations.

    Electronic configuration and the periodic table
  • The position of an element is strongly tied to its electronic configuration.
  • Period number is usually the highest principal quantum number \(n\) present in the valence shell.
  • Block is identified from the differentiating electron:
    • \(s\)-block: last electron enters \(s\)-subshell
    • \(p\)-block: last electron enters \(p\)-subshell
    • \(d\)-block: last electron enters \((n-1)d\)-subshell
    • \(f\)-block: last electron enters \((n-2)f\)-subshell
  • For many \(p\)-block elements, group number equals \(10 + (ns + np)\) valence electrons.
  • For many transition elements, group number is obtained from \((n-1)d + ns\) electrons.

    Types of elements: \(s\), \(p\), \(d\), and \(f\) blocks
  • \(s\)-block elements are generally highly electropositive and include alkali and alkaline earth metals.
  • \(p\)-block contains metals, metalloids, and non-metals, so it shows the widest variety of properties.
  • \(d\)-block elements are transition elements with characteristic variable oxidation states and complex formation tendencies.
  • \(f\)-block elements include lanthanoids and actinoids, often written separately for table compactness.
  • Many direct EAPCET questions simply test block recognition from configuration or family names.

    Atomic radius
  • Atomic radius generally decreases across a period because effective nuclear charge increases while electrons enter the same shell.
  • Atomic radius generally increases down a group because a new shell is added each step.
  • Across a period, shielding does not rise enough to cancel increased nuclear pull.
  • Down a group, the shell effect dominates and size increases.
  • Lanthanoid contraction reduces the expected size increase among later elements, causing pairs like \(Zr/Hf\) to become very similar.

    Ionic radius
  • Cations are smaller than their parent atoms because electron loss reduces repulsion and may remove an outer shell.
  • Anions are larger than their parent atoms because electron gain increases repulsion in the valence shell.
  • For isoelectronic species, ionic radius decreases as nuclear charge increases.
  • Classic 10-electron series:
    • \(O^{2-} > F^- > Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}\) in decreasing size
  • Questions on ionic radius are often really electron-count plus proton-count questions.

    Ionisation enthalpy
  • Ionisation enthalpy is the enthalpy required to remove an electron from an isolated gaseous atom.
  • First ionisation enthalpy generally increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Key reasons: atomic size, effective nuclear charge, shielding, and electronic configuration.
  • Important exceptions:
    • \(Be > B\) and \(Mg > Al\) because filled \(s^2\) is relatively stable
    • \(N > O\) because half-filled \(p^3\) is relatively stable
  • Successive ionisation enthalpies always increase, and a very large jump indicates entry into a core shell after valence electrons are removed.

    Electron gain enthalpy
  • Electron gain enthalpy is the enthalpy change when an isolated gaseous atom gains an electron.
  • More negative value means greater tendency to accept an electron.
  • General trend: more negative across a period, less negative down a group.
  • Important exceptions:
    • \(Cl\) is more negative than \(F\)
    • \(S\) is more negative than \(P\)
    • Oxygen is less negative than sulphur because of compact \(2p\) repulsion
    • Noble gases often show positive values due to stable filled shells

    Electronegativity and metallic/non-metallic character
  • Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom in a bonded state to attract shared electrons.
  • It generally increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Metallic character generally decreases across a period and increases down a group.
  • Non-metallic character follows the opposite trend.
  • Lower ionisation enthalpy and larger size usually support stronger metallic character.

    Oxides and periodic trends in chemical properties
  • Across a period, oxides typically change from basic to amphoteric to acidic.
  • \(Li_2O\) is strongly basic, \(BeO\) is amphoteric, and oxides like \(B_2O_3\), \(CO_2\), and \(N_2O_3\) are acidic.
  • Acidic strength of oxides generally rises with increasing non-metallic character.
  • This trend helps rank oxide acidity in many direct PYQs.

    Variation of size in inner transition elements
  • Lanthanoid contraction is the gradual decrease in size across the lanthanoid series.
  • It occurs because \(4f\)-electrons shield nuclear charge poorly.
  • Important consequences:
    • close similarity of some \(4d\) and \(5d\) elements
    • difficulty in separation of lanthanoids
    • increase in covalent character and related effects

    Nomenclature of elements with atomic number greater than 100
  • IUPAC temporary and modern naming conventions are part of the chapter’s factual component.
  • Questions may test symbol/name recognition of superheavy elements, such as Ds for element 110.
  • These are usually direct recall questions rather than trend questions.

    How to solve this chapter quickly in exams
  • If the question is an order question, first identify whether it is about atoms, ions, or electron-affinity type values.
  • If ions are involved, count electrons first and check for isoelectronic species.
  • If a period-trend order looks odd, test the standard exceptions immediately.
  • If a periodic-table position is asked, write configuration first, then extract block, period, and group.
  • If successive ionisation energies are given, locate the huge jump and infer valence-electron count from it.

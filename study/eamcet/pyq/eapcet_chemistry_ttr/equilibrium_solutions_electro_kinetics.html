EQUILIBRIUM, SOLUTIONS, ELECTROCHEMISTRY, CHEMICAL KINETICS

    1. Writing \(K_c\) and \(K_p\) from balanced equations

      For \(aA+bB \rightleftharpoons cC+dD\):

      \( K_c=\dfrac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b} \)

      \( K_p=\dfrac{(p_C)^c(p_D)^d}{(p_A)^a(p_B)^b} \)

      Trap: use powers from balanced coefficients; pure solids and pure liquids are omitted.

    2. \(K_p\) and \(K_c\) relation

      \( K_p=K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g} \)

      \( \Delta n_g=\text{moles of gaseous products}-\text{moles of gaseous reactants} \)

      For \( \mathrm{N_2+3H_2\rightleftharpoons2NH_3} \), \( \Delta n_g=2-4=-2 \).

      Trap: count only gaseous species in \(\Delta n_g\).

    3. Changing equation changes equilibrium constant

      Reverse reaction: \(K_{\text{new}}=\dfrac{1}{K}\).

      Multiply equation by \(n\): \(K_{\text{new}}=K^n\).

      Divide equation by \(n\): \(K_{\text{new}}=K^{1/n}\).

      Add equations: multiply their equilibrium constants.

      Trap: scale \(K\) the same way the equation is scaled, not the same way enthalpy is scaled.

    4. Equilibrium from ICE table

      For \( \mathrm{PCl_5\rightleftharpoons PCl_3+Cl_2} \):

      \( K_c=\dfrac{[\mathrm{PCl_3}][\mathrm{Cl_2}]}{[\mathrm{PCl_5}]} \)

      If product formed is \(x\), then equilibrium values are \(a-x,\ x,\ x\).

      Trap: write equilibrium moles first, then convert to concentration if volume is needed.

    5. Dissociation of \( \mathrm{N_2O_4} \)

      \( \mathrm{N_2O_4\rightleftharpoons2NO_2} \)

      \( K_p=\dfrac{4\alpha^2p}{1-\alpha^2} \)

      \( \alpha=\sqrt{\dfrac{K_p/p}{4+K_p/p}} \)

      Trap: this shortcut applies to pure \(\mathrm{N_2O_4}\) dissociation with total pressure \(p\).

    6. Pressure effect on gaseous equilibrium

      Increase in pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gas.

      Products are favoured by pressure increase when \( \Delta n_g<0 \).

      If \( \Delta n_g=0 \), pressure has no effect on composition.

      Trap: compare gaseous moles only; solids and liquids do not count.

    7. Equilibrium constant depends only on temperature

      At fixed temperature, \(K\) is unchanged by pressure, concentration, volume, or catalyst.

      Catalyst changes the rate of forward and reverse reactions equally.

      Trap: catalyst helps equilibrium reach faster; it does not change \(K\) or equilibrium position.

    8. Heterogeneous equilibrium

      Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from \(K_c\) and \(K_p\).

      For \( \mathrm{A(s)\rightleftharpoons B(s)+C(g)} \):

      \( K_c=[C] \)

      \( K_p=p_C \)

      Trap: do not include \(\mathrm{A(s)}\) or \(\mathrm{B(s)}\) in the equilibrium expression.

    9. Gibbs energy and equilibrium constant

      \( \Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K \)

      \( \Delta G^\circ=-2.303RT\log K \)

      Negative \(\Delta G^\circ\) means \(K>1\) and product-favoured equilibrium.

      Trap: convert kJ to J before using \(R=8.314\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}K^{-1}}\).

    10. Strong acid and strong base mixing

      \( \text{moles of }H^+=M_aV_a \)

      \( \text{moles of }OH^-=M_bV_b \)

      Subtract excess \(H^+\) or \(OH^-\), then divide by final total volume.

      Trap: never average pH values directly; always subtract moles first.

    11. Henderson-Hasselbalch equation for acidic buffer

      \( \mathrm{pH}=pK_a+\log\dfrac{[\text{salt}]}{[\text{acid}]} \)

      If \([\text{salt}]=[\text{acid}]\), then \( \mathrm{pH}=pK_a \).

      Equal dilution of acid and salt does not change the ratio.

      Trap: buffer exists only when weak acid/base and its conjugate salt remain together.

    12. Buffer formation trap

      Weak acid + strong base gives buffer only after partial neutralisation.

      Weak base + strong acid gives buffer only after partial neutralisation.

      Complete neutralisation in equivalent amount does not give a buffer.

      Trap: weak acid plus strong base in exact \(1:1\) amount leaves only salt, not buffer.

    13. Neutral water and temperature

      Neutral water means \( [H^+]=[OH^-] \).

      At \(25^\circ\mathrm{C}\), neutral water has \(\mathrm{pH}=7\).

      At higher temperature, \(K_w\) increases and neutral pH becomes less than \(7\).

      Trap: neutral does not always mean pH \(7\).

    14. Salt hydrolysis classification

      Strong acid + strong base salt \(\rightarrow\) neutral solution.

      Strong acid + weak base salt \(\rightarrow\) acidic solution.

      Weak acid + strong base salt \(\rightarrow\) basic solution.

      Example: \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\) acidic, \(\mathrm{CH_3COONa}\) basic, \(\mathrm{KCl}\) neutral.

      Trap: classify by parent acid and parent base, not by formula appearance alone.

    15. Lewis acids and Lewis bases

      Lewis acid \(=\) electron-pair acceptor.

      Lewis base \(=\) electron-pair donor.

      Lewis acids: \(\mathrm{AlCl_3,\ SnCl_4,\ CO_2,\ Ag^+}\).

      Lewis base: \(\mathrm{NH_3}\), due to lone pair on nitrogen.

      Trap: metal cations and electron-deficient central atoms are common Lewis acids.

    16. Basicity of oxyacids

      Basicity \(=\) number of ionisable hydrogens attached to oxygen as \(\mathrm{-OH}\).

      \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\) is dibasic.

      \(\mathrm{H_3PO_2}\) is monobasic.

      \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) is dibasic.

      Trap: do not count total hydrogens; \(\mathrm{P-H}\) hydrogen is non-ionisable.

    17. Acidic strength of hypohalous acids

      For \(\mathrm{HOX}\), acidity increases with electronegativity of \(X\).

      \( \mathrm{HClO>HBrO>HIO} \)

      Stronger \(-I\) effect weakens the \(\mathrm{O-H}\) bond more.

      Trap: for same type of oxyacid, compare electronegativity of central atom.

    18. Solubility product for simple salts

      For \( \mathrm{AB\rightleftharpoons A^++B^-} \):

      \( K_{sp}=s^2 \)

      For \( \mathrm{AB_2\rightleftharpoons A^{2+}+2B^-} \):

      \( K_{sp}=S(2S)^2=4S^3 \)

      Trap: include stoichiometric powers of ions in \(K_{sp}\).

    19. Common-ion effect in \(K_{sp}\)

      For \( \mathrm{Cd(OH)_2\rightleftharpoons Cd^{2+}+2OH^-} \):

      \( K_{sp}=[\mathrm{Cd^{2+}}][\mathrm{OH^-}]^2 \)

      In \(0.1\,\mathrm{M}\) KOH, use \( [\mathrm{OH^-}]\approx0.1\,\mathrm{M} \), so \(K_{sp}=s(0.1)^2\).

      Trap: if common ion concentration is large, treat it as fixed.

    20. Mixed saturated salts with common ion

      First find the common-ion concentration from the known salt.

      Example: \( K_{sp}(\mathrm{MgCO_3})=[\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}][\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}] \).

      Then use that \([\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}]\) in \( K_{sp}(\mathrm{Ag_2CO_3})=[\mathrm{Ag^+}]^2[\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}] \).

      Trap: do not solve both salts independently when they share a common ion.


    1. Raoult's law for ideal binary solutions

      \( p_A=x_Ap_A^\circ \)

      \( p_B=x_Bp_B^\circ \)

      \( p_{\text{total}}=x_Ap_A^\circ+x_Bp_B^\circ \)

      Trap: after adding more solute/solvent, recalculate mole fractions before using vapour pressures.

    2. Vapour-phase composition above liquid solution

      \( y_A=\dfrac{p_A}{p_{\text{total}}} \)

      \( p_A=x_Ap_A^\circ \)

      The vapour phase is richer in the more volatile component.

      Trap: vapour mole fraction is not the same as liquid mole fraction unless pure vapour pressures are equal.

    3. Relative lowering of vapour pressure

      \( \dfrac{p^\circ-p}{p^\circ}=x_{\text{solute}} \)

      For dilute non-volatile solute:

      \( \dfrac{p^\circ-p}{p^\circ}\approx\dfrac{n_2}{n_1}=\dfrac{w_2/M_2}{w_1/M_1} \)

      Trap: use moles of solvent, not solvent mass directly.

    4. Vapour pressure lowering by non-volatile solute

      \( p_{\text{solution}}=x_{\text{solvent}}p^\circ_{\text{solvent}} \)

      \( x_{\text{solvent}}=\dfrac{n_{\text{solvent}}}{n_{\text{solvent}}+n_{\text{solute}}} \)

      Trap: mole fraction of solvent must be calculated from total moles, not read directly from solvent amount alone.

    5. Henry's law

      \( p=K_Hx \)

      \( x=\dfrac{p}{K_H} \)

      At fixed pressure, gas solubility \( \propto \dfrac{1}{K_H} \).

      Trap: lower \(K_H\) means higher solubility; do not arrange solubility in the same order as \(K_H\).

    6. Henry's law with partial pressure of gas

      \( p_{\text{gas}}=y_{\text{gas}}P_{\text{total}} \)

      \( x_{\text{gas}}=\dfrac{p_{\text{gas}}}{K_H} \)

      For dissolved gas ratio:

      \( \dfrac{x_1}{x_2}=\dfrac{p_1/K_{H,1}}{p_2/K_{H,2}} \)

      Trap: use partial pressure, not total pressure of air.

    7. Validity of Henry's law

      Henry's law applies when gas dissolves physically without chemical reaction or strong binding.

      Valid exam examples: \(\mathrm{O_2}\) in water and \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) in water.

      Not simple Henry-law cases: \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) in water and \(\mathrm{O_2}\) in blood.

      Trap: reaction with solvent or binding to haemoglobin breaks the simple physical-dissolution model.

    8. Boiling point elevation

      \( \Delta T_b=iK_bm \)

      For non-electrolytes, \(i=1\).

      For ideal \(\mathrm{KCl}\), \(i\approx2\).

      Trap: same molarity does not mean same colligative effect if one solute dissociates.

    9. Freezing point depression

      \( \Delta T_f=iK_fm \)

      \( T_f(\text{solution})=T_f^\circ-\Delta T_f \)

      Highest freezing point means smallest \(\Delta T_f\).

      Trap: choose smallest \(i\), not largest, for highest freezing point among equimolal solutions.

    10. Same solution: boiling point and freezing point link

      \( \Delta T_b=K_bm \)

      \( \Delta T_f=K_fm \)

      For the same non-electrolyte solution:

      \( \dfrac{\Delta T_f}{\Delta T_b}=\dfrac{K_f}{K_b} \)

      Trap: first find elevation/depression, not absolute boiling or freezing point.

    11. Molal depression constant

      \( K_f=\dfrac{RT_f^2}{1000L_f} \)

      \(T_f\) must be in kelvin.

      \(L_f\) must be in \(\mathrm{J\,g^{-1}}\).

      Trap: keep the \(1000\) factor when latent heat is per gram.

    12. Osmotic pressure

      \( \pi=iCRT \)

      For mixed solutes:

      \( \pi=(\sum iC)RT \)

      Trap: apply each solute's own \(i\) first, then add \(iC\) values.

    13. van't Hoff factor and dissociation

      \( i=1+\alpha(n-1) \)

      For \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), \(n=2\), so \(i=1+\alpha\).

      For \(94\%\) dissociation of \(\mathrm{NaCl}\), \(i=1.94\).

      Trap: do not use full dissociation \(i=2\) when degree of dissociation is given.

    14. Highest colligative property at equal concentration

      Colligative effect \( \propto i \times \text{concentration} \).

      Typical \(i\) values:

      \( \mathrm{urea}=1,\ \mathrm{AgNO_3}=2,\ \mathrm{BaCl_2}=3,\ (\mathrm{NH_4})_3\mathrm{PO_4}=4 \)

      Trap: at equal molarity/molality, compare number of particles, not molar mass.

    15. Osmotic pressure on dilution and temperature change

      For non-electrolyte: \( \pi=CRT \)

      \( \dfrac{C_1}{C_2}=\dfrac{\pi_1T_2}{\pi_2T_1} \)

      Trap: include temperature change; do not use osmotic pressure ratio alone.

    16. Ideal solution conditions

      Ideal solution obeys Raoult's law at all concentrations.

      \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}}=0 \)

      \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}}=0 \)

      Interactions \(A-A\), \(B-B\), and \(A-B\) are similar.

      Trap: chemical reaction between components means the solution is not ideal.

    17. Non-ideal solution clue

      Different unlike interactions cause deviation from Raoult's law.

      \(\mathrm{CH_3OH+CH_3COOH}\) is treated as non-ideal in the PYQ pattern.

      Trap: if the question asks for the "not correct" statement, check whether it falsely calls a real mixture ideal.

    18. Molarity temperature dependence

      \( M=\dfrac{n}{V} \)

      Moles of solute remain constant on heating, but solution volume can change.

      Molarity is temperature-dependent; molality is not temperature-dependent in the same way.

      Trap: if volume increases on heating, molarity decreases.

    19. Carbonate-bicarbonate acid requirement

      \( \mathrm{Na_2CO_3+2HCl\rightarrow2NaCl+H_2O+CO_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{NaHCO_3+HCl\rightarrow NaCl+H_2O+CO_2} \)

      \(\mathrm{Na_2CO_3}\) needs \(2\) mol \(\mathrm{HCl}\) per mole; \(\mathrm{NaHCO_3}\) needs \(1\) mol \(\mathrm{HCl}\) per mole.

      Trap: equal moles of carbonate and bicarbonate require \(3n\) moles of \(\mathrm{HCl}\).


    1. Nernst equation for cell potential

      \( E_{\mathrm{cell}}=E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}-\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log Q \)

      \( E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=E^\circ_{\mathrm{cathode}}-E^\circ_{\mathrm{anode}} \)

      \( \Delta G=-nFE \)

      Trap: balance electrons first to get \(n\), and omit pure solids/liquids from \(Q\).

    2. Hydrogen electrode and pH from Nernst equation

      For hydrogen electrode at \(298\,\mathrm{K}\): \( E=-0.0591\,\mathrm{pH} \).

      \( \mathrm{pH}=-\log[H^+] \)

      For \( \mathrm{Zn(s)+2H^+\rightarrow Zn^{2+}+H_2} \):

      \( Q=\dfrac{[\mathrm{Zn^{2+}}]P_{\mathrm{H_2}}}{[H^+]^2} \)

      Trap: do not forget the square on \([H^+]\).

    3. Electrode potential and concentration effect

      For \( \mathrm{Fe^{3+}+e^-\rightarrow Fe^{2+}} \):

      \( E=E^\circ-0.0591\log\dfrac{[\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}]}{[\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}]} \)

      For \( \mathrm{Cl_2+2e^-\rightarrow2Cl^-} \): increasing \([\mathrm{Cl^-}]\) decreases reduction potential.

      Trap: product-ion concentration appears in \(Q\) and lowers \(E\).

    4. Standard cell potential and equilibrium constant

      \( E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=\dfrac{2.303RT}{nF}\log K \)

      At \(298\,\mathrm{K}\): \( E^\circ_{\mathrm{cell}}=\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log K \)

      Trap: determine \(n\) from the balanced redox equation, not from one half-cell alone.

    5. Latimer-style potential calculation

      Electrode potentials are not directly additive.

      Use \( \Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ \).

      Combine \(nE^\circ\) values, not \(E^\circ\) values alone.

      Example pattern: \(5E^\circ_{\mathrm{overall}}=3E^\circ_1+2E^\circ_2\).

      Trap: simple subtraction of potentials is wrong when electron numbers differ.

    6. Faraday's laws of electrolysis

      \( Q=It \)

      Faradays passed \(=\dfrac{Q}{F}\), where \(F\approx96500\,\mathrm{C\,mol^{-1}}\).

      \( \text{mass deposited}=\dfrac{Q}{F}\times\text{equivalent mass} \)

      One gram-equivalent needs \(1F\) of charge.

      Trap: convert time to seconds before using \(Q=It\).

    7. Electrochemical equivalent

      \( Z=\dfrac{m}{Q}=\dfrac{m}{It} \)

      \(Z\) is mass deposited per coulomb.

      Trap: electrochemical equivalent is not the same as equivalent weight; equivalent weight is mass deposited per faraday.

    8. Electrolysis products with inert electrodes

      Aqueous \(\mathrm{CuSO_4}\) with Pt electrodes:

      \( \mathrm{Cu^{2+}+2e^-\rightarrow Cu} \) at cathode.

      \( \mathrm{2H_2O\rightarrow O_2+4H^++4e^-} \) at anode.

      Molten \(\mathrm{NaCl}\): \(\mathrm{Na}\) at cathode and \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\) at anode.

      Trap: oxygen forms at the anode, not cathode, in aqueous sulfate electrolysis.

    9. Electrolysis gas volume at STP

      \( n(e^-)=\dfrac{Q}{F} \)

      \( \mathrm{2Cl^-\rightarrow Cl_2+2e^-} \), so \(1\) mol \(\mathrm{Cl_2}\) needs \(2F\).

      \( \mathrm{2H_2O\rightarrow O_2+4H^++4e^-} \), so \(1\) mol \(\mathrm{O_2}\) needs \(4F\).

      At STP, gas volume \(=n\times22.4\,\mathrm{L}\).

    10. Castner-Kellner process electrode roles

      Brine is electrolysed to manufacture \(\mathrm{NaOH}\).

      Carbon/graphite is anode; mercury is cathode.

      \( \mathrm{2Cl^-\rightarrow Cl_2+2e^-} \) at anode.

      Mercury cathode forms sodium amalgam.

      Trap: mercury is cathode, not anode.

    11. Kohlrausch's law and limiting molar conductivity

      \( \Lambda_m^\circ=\sum \nu_i\lambda_i^\circ \)

      For \(\mathrm{CaCl_2}\): \( \Lambda_m^\circ=\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}}+2\lambda^\circ_{\mathrm{Cl^-}} \).

      Trap: multiply ionic conductance by ion coefficient; chloride contribution is doubled in \(\mathrm{CaCl_2}\).

    12. Molar conductivity and conductivity

      In common \(\mathrm{S\,cm^2\,mol^{-1}}\) form:

      \( \Lambda_m=\dfrac{\kappa\times1000}{C} \)

      \( \alpha=\dfrac{\Lambda_m}{\Lambda_m^\circ} \)

      Trap: distinguish \(\Lambda_m\) from \(\kappa\); conductivity is in \(\mathrm{S\,cm^{-1}}\).

    13. Weak electrolyte: degree of dissociation and Ostwald relation

      \( \alpha=\dfrac{\Lambda_m}{\Lambda_m^\circ} \)

      \( K_a=\dfrac{C\alpha^2}{1-\alpha} \)

      For small \(\alpha\): \( \alpha\approx\sqrt{\dfrac{K_a}{C}} \).

      Trap: weak electrolytes do not have \(\Lambda_m=\Lambda_m^\circ\) at finite concentration.

    14. Order and rate law from initial-rate data

      \( \mathrm{Rate}=k[A]^x[B]^y \)

      Change one reactant concentration while keeping the other constant to find its order.

      Overall order \(=x+y\).

      Trap: reaction order is experimental; do not copy stoichiometric coefficients blindly.

    15. Units of rate constant

      For order \(n\): \( [k]=(\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}})^{1-n}\mathrm{s^{-1}} \).

      First order: \( \mathrm{s^{-1}} \).

      Second order: \( \mathrm{L\,mol^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \).

      Trap: unit \(\mathrm{s^{-1}}\) strongly indicates first order.

    16. Zero-order integrated rate law

      \( [A]_t=[A]_0-kt \)

      \( t_{1/2}=\dfrac{[A]_0}{2k} \)

      Plot of \([A]\) vs \(t\) is straight line with slope \(-k\).

      Trap: zero-order half-life depends on initial concentration.

    17. First-order integrated rate law

      \( k=\dfrac{2.303}{t}\log\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]} \)

      \( t_{1/2}=\dfrac{0.693}{k} \)

      \( \ln[A]=\ln[A]_0-kt \)

      Trap: \([A]\) vs \(t\) is not linear for first order; \(\ln[A]\) vs \(t\) is linear.

    18. First-order graph traps

      Plot of \(\ln[A]\) vs \(t\): slope \(=-k\), intercept \(=\ln[A]_0\).

      Plot of \(\log\dfrac{a}{a-x}\) vs \(t\): slope \(=\dfrac{k}{2.303}\).

      Trap: if slope is given for common-log plot, multiply by \(2.303\) to get \(k\).

    19. First-order gas-phase pressure method

      For \(\mathrm{A(g)\rightarrow B(g)+C(g)}\), if pressure increase is \(x\):

      \( p_t=p_0+x \)

      \( p_A=p_0-x \)

      \( k=\dfrac{2.303}{t}\log\dfrac{p_0}{p_A} \)

      Trap: do not use total pressure \(p_t\) directly as remaining reactant pressure.

    20. Completion percentage in first-order reaction

      \( t=\dfrac{2.303}{k}\log\dfrac{a}{a-x} \)

      For \(90\%\) completion, \(10\%\) remains, so \(\dfrac{a}{a-x}=10\).

      Therefore \( t=\dfrac{2.303}{k} \).

      Trap: denominator is amount remaining, not amount decomposed.

    21. Half-life and order identification

      First order: \(t_{1/2}\) is independent of initial concentration.

      For \(n\)-th order: \( t_{1/2}\propto\dfrac{1}{a^{\,n-1}} \).

      If concentration decreases \(10\) times and half-life increases \(10\) times, order \(=2\).

      Trap: changing half-life rules out first order.

    22. Stoichiometric rate relation

      For \( \mathrm{N_2O_5\rightarrow2NO_2+\dfrac{1}{2}O_2} \):

      \( -\dfrac{d[\mathrm{N_2O_5}]}{dt}=\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{NO_2}]}{dt}=2\dfrac{d[\mathrm{O_2}]}{dt} \)

      Trap: use reciprocal of stoichiometric coefficients in rate expression.

    23. Mechanism-derived rate law

      Write rate from the slow step first.

      If slow step gives \(R=k[\mathrm{N_2O_2}][\mathrm{H_2}]\), and fast pre-equilibrium gives \([\mathrm{N_2O_2}]\propto[\mathrm{NO}]^2\), then:

      \( R=k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{H_2}] \)

      Trap: do not write rate law directly from overall balanced equation.

    24. Arrhenius equation and activation energy

      \( \ln k=\ln A-\dfrac{E_a}{RT} \)

      For \(\ln k\) vs \(1/T\): slope \(=-\dfrac{E_a}{R}\).

      \( \log k=\log A-\dfrac{E_a}{2.303RT} \)

      Trap: use \(2.303\) only for common-log form, not for \(\ln k\).

    25. Two-temperature Arrhenius equation

      \( \log\dfrac{k_2}{k_1}=\dfrac{E_a}{2.303R}\left(\dfrac{1}{T_1}-\dfrac{1}{T_2}\right) \)

      If rate doubles for a \(10^\circ\mathrm{C}\) rise, use \(k_2/k_1=2\).

      Trap: always use Kelvin temperatures.

    26. Catalyst and activation energy

      A catalyst increases \(k\) by lowering \(E_a\).

      \( \ln\dfrac{k_1}{k_2}=\dfrac{\Delta E_a}{RT} \)

      \( E_a=E_t-E_i \)

      Trap: threshold energy is not activation energy; activation energy is the extra energy needed to reach threshold energy.

    27. Standard zero-order example

      \( \mathrm{H_2+Cl_2\xrightarrow{h\nu}2HCl} \)

      Photochemical formation of \(\mathrm{HCl}\) is treated as zero order in basic kinetics MCQs.

      Trap: \(h\nu\) is the clue; do not decide order from stoichiometric coefficients alone.

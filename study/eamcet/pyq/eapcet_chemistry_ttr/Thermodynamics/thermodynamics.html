Thermodynamics

    Exam map for thermodynamics
  • The repeated PYQ buckets are: Hess law, \(\Delta_fH^\circ\), combustion/formation enthalpy, reversible and irreversible work, \(\Delta G\)–\(K\) relation, spontaneity from \(\Delta H,\Delta S\), \(q_p=nC_p\Delta T\), and intensive/extensive properties.
  • Before solving, first tag the question: state function / Hess law / work / heat capacity / spontaneity / equilibrium / property type.
  • Most questions collapse to one standard formula once the process type is identified correctly.

    State functions and process quantities
  • \(U,\ H,\ S,\ G\) are state functions: they depend only on initial and final states.
  • \(q\) and \(w\) are path functions: they depend on how the process occurs.
  • On a \(P\)-\(V\) diagram, work equals the area under the curve, so different paths can give different work for the same end states.
  • Intensive properties do not depend on amount of matter; extensive properties do.

    First law and sign convention
  • First law: \(\Delta U=q+w\).
  • Use chemistry sign convention: work done by the system is negative, work done on the system is positive.
  • Expansion usually gives \(w<0\); compression usually gives \(w>0\).
  • At constant pressure, \(q_p=\Delta H\).

    Work formulas: solve-fast map
  • Reversible isothermal ideal-gas work: \(w=-nRT\ln\!\left(\dfrac{V_2}{V_1}\right)=-nRT\ln\!\left(\dfrac{P_1}{P_2}\right)\).
  • Irreversible work against constant external pressure: \(w=-P_{ext}\Delta V\).
  • Open-vessel gas-generation questions often reduce to \(w=-\Delta n_gRT\).
  • If the path is shown on a \(P\)-\(V\) graph, compare areas directly before doing algebra.

    Enthalpy, internal energy, and gas-mole relation
  • \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\) for reactions involving ideal gases.
  • If no gas mole change occurs, especially in condensed-phase changes, \(\Delta H\approx \Delta U\).
  • For one mole at constant pressure, \(q_p=nC_p\Delta T=C_p\Delta T\).
  • At constant pressure, \(q\) and \(\Delta H\) are numerically equal.

    Hess law and formation/combustion shortcuts
  • Hess law: if equations add, their enthalpy changes also add.
  • \(\Delta_rH^\circ=\sum \nu \Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu \Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants})\).
  • For combustion questions, write the combustion equation first, then substitute tabulated \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) or \(\Delta_cH^\circ\).
  • If a reaction must be reversed, its \(\Delta H\) changes sign.

    Gibbs free energy and spontaneity
  • \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\).
  • Spontaneous if \(\Delta G<0\), non-spontaneous if \(\Delta G>0\), equilibrium if \(\Delta G=0\).
  • Temperature where direction changes is found from \(\Delta G=0 \Rightarrow T=\dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}\), with units made consistent.
  • Watch the sign of \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta S\): both negative often means spontaneous only at low temperature.

    \(\Delta G^\circ\) and equilibrium constant
  • \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K\).
  • If \(\Delta G^\circ<0\), then \(K>1\).
  • For \(\log_{10}K\), first find \(\ln K\), then divide by \(2.303\).
  • Always convert \(\Delta G^\circ\) to joules before using \(R=8.314\ \mathrm{J\,K^{-1}\,mol^{-1}}\).

    Bond enthalpy and reaction enthalpy
  • Bond-enthalpy method: \(\Delta H=\sum(\text{bonds broken})-\sum(\text{bonds formed})\).
  • This method is best for gas-phase molecular reactions when bond energies are given directly.
  • Do not mix bond enthalpy with standard enthalpy of formation; they are different data types.

    Common traps
  • Do not use reversible-work formula for compression/expansion against constant external pressure.
  • Do not forget sign reversal when reversing a Hess-law equation.
  • Do not use \(\Delta H=\Delta U\) when gas moles change significantly.
  • Do not forget unit conversion between kJ and J or between \(\ln\) and \(\log_{10}\).
  • Do not confuse state functions with path functions in theory questions.

    Basic thermodynamic terms
  • A thermodynamic system is the part of the universe chosen for study; the rest is the surroundings.
  • State functions depend only on the state of the system, not on the path used to reach that state.
  • Internal energy \((U)\), enthalpy \((H)\), entropy \((S)\), and Gibbs free energy \((G)\) are state functions.
  • Heat \((q)\) and work \((w)\) are path functions.

    Types of systems and processes
  • Open systems can exchange both matter and energy with surroundings.
  • Closed systems exchange energy but not matter.
  • Isolated systems exchange neither matter nor energy.
  • Processes may be isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric, isochoric, reversible, or irreversible.
  • Reversible processes proceed through a continuous series of equilibrium states and give maximum work in expansion.

    First law of thermodynamics
  • First law expresses conservation of energy: \(\Delta U=q+w\).
  • In chemistry sign convention, \(q>0\) when heat is absorbed by the system, and \(w>0\) when work is done on the system.
  • Expansion work is negative for the system; compression work is positive.
  • This law is the foundation for most work and heat calculations in thermodynamics. :contentReference[oaicite:31]{index=31}

    Work done in expansion or compression
  • For reversible isothermal expansion of an ideal gas: \[ w=-nRT\ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)=-nRT\ln\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right) \]
  • For expansion/compression against constant external pressure: \[ w=-P_{ext}\Delta V \]
  • On a \(P\)-\(V\) graph, work equals the area under the path curve.

    Enthalpy and relation with internal energy
  • Enthalpy is defined as \(H=U+PV\).
  • At constant pressure, \(q_p=\Delta H\).
  • For reactions involving ideal gases: \[ \Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT \]
  • If there is no gaseous mole change, \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta U\) become equal or nearly equal in exam calculations. :contentReference[oaicite:32]{index=32}

    Heat capacities
  • Heat capacity is the heat required to raise the temperature of a system by \(1\ \mathrm{K}\).
  • Molar heat capacities at constant volume and pressure are \(C_V\) and \(C_P\).
  • For an ideal gas, \(C_P-C_V=R\).
  • At constant pressure: \[ q_p=nC_P\Delta T \]

    Hess law and thermochemical equations
  • Hess law states that the enthalpy change of a reaction depends only on initial and final states, not on the path.
  • This allows unknown \(\Delta H\) values to be found by adding, subtracting, or reversing known equations.
  • Reversing an equation changes the sign of \(\Delta H\); multiplying an equation multiplies \(\Delta H\) by the same factor.
  • Many EAPCET thermodynamics PYQs are direct Hess-law applications. :contentReference[oaicite:33]{index=33}

    Standard enthalpies of formation and combustion
  • Standard enthalpy of formation \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) is the enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
  • Standard enthalpy of combustion \(\Delta_cH^\circ\) is the enthalpy change when one mole of a substance is completely burned in oxygen under standard conditions.
  • Reaction enthalpy can be calculated from formation enthalpies using: \[ \Delta_rH^\circ=\sum \nu \Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu \Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants}) \]

    Entropy and spontaneity
  • Entropy measures disorder or randomness of a system.
  • Processes like melting and vaporisation usually increase entropy.
  • A process can be spontaneous only if the total entropy of the universe increases.
  • For practical chemistry questions, spontaneity is usually handled through Gibbs free energy rather than entropy of universe directly.

    Gibbs free energy and equilibrium
  • At constant temperature and pressure: \[ \Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S \]
  • \(\Delta G<0\) means spontaneous, \(\Delta G>0\) means non-spontaneous, and \(\Delta G=0\) means equilibrium.
  • The temperature at which a reaction changes direction is often obtained by setting \(\Delta G=0\).
  • Standard free energy and equilibrium constant are related by: \[ \Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K \]

    Intensive and extensive properties
  • Intensive properties do not depend on the amount of matter, for example temperature, density, and molar volume.
  • Extensive properties depend on amount of matter, for example mass, volume, enthalpy, and internal energy.
  • This distinction is frequently tested as a conceptual MCQ. :contentReference[oaicite:34]{index=34}

    Bond enthalpy method
  • Reaction enthalpy can also be estimated from average bond enthalpies: \[ \Delta H=\sum(\text{bond enthalpy of bonds broken})-\sum(\text{bond enthalpy of bonds formed}) \]
  • This is especially useful when the question directly gives bond enthalpies instead of formation enthalpies. :contentReference[oaicite:35]{index=35}

    How to solve thermodynamics quickly in exams
  • If a reaction enthalpy is asked from tabulated \(\Delta_fH^\circ\) or \(\Delta_cH^\circ\), think Hess law first.
  • If a gas expands/compresses, identify whether it is reversible or against constant external pressure before choosing the work formula.
  • If spontaneity or direction is asked, move immediately to \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\).
  • If \(K\) is asked, connect it to \(\Delta G^\circ\).
  • If the question is conceptual, first classify quantities as state/path or intensive/extensive.

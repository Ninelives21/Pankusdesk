ATOMIC STRUCTURE, PERIODIC CLASSIFICATION, CHEMICAL BONDING

    1. Bohr radius of hydrogen-like species

      \( \mathrm{r_n=\dfrac{n^2a_0}{Z}} \)

      For hydrogen atom, \(Z=1\), so \( \mathrm{r_n=n^2a_0} \).

      For radius comparison, use \( \mathrm{\dfrac{r_1}{r_2}=\dfrac{n_1^2/Z_1}{n_2^2/Z_2}} \).

      Trap: \(\mathrm{He^+}\), \(\mathrm{Li^{2+}}\), \(\mathrm{Be^{3+}}\) are hydrogen-like but \(Z=2,3,4\).

    2. Bohr energy of hydrogen-like species

      \( \mathrm{E_n=-13.6\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ eV} \)

      \( \mathrm{E_n=-2.18\times10^{-18}\dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ J} \)

      Equal energy condition: \( \mathrm{\dfrac{Z_1}{n_1}=\dfrac{Z_2}{n_2}} \).

      Trap: bound-state energy is negative; excitation energy is taken as positive absorbed energy.

    3. Hydrogen spectral lines and series

      \( \mathrm{\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=RZ^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_f^2}-\dfrac{1}{n_i^2}\right)} \)

      Lyman series: \(n_f=1\).

      Balmer series: \(n_f=2\).

      Paschen series: \(n_f=3\).

      Trap: series is decided by the final orbit, not the starting orbit.

    4. Number of spectral lines

      \( \mathrm{N=\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}} \)

      Use \(n\) as the number of involved energy levels, not always the initial shell number.

      Example: transition from \(n=6\) to \(n=2\) involves levels \(6,5,4,3,2\), so \(n=5\) and \(N=10\).

    5. Photoelectric effect formulae

      \( \mathrm{K_{max}=h\nu-\phi=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}-\phi} \)

      \( \mathrm{K_{max}=\dfrac{1}{2}mv^2} \)

      \( \mathrm{h\nu_0=\phi} \)

      \( \mathrm{E(eV)=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda(nm)}} \)

      Trap: subtract work function before calculating velocity; no emission if photon energy is less than work function.

    6. de Broglie equation

      \( \mathrm{\lambda=\dfrac{h}{p}=\dfrac{h}{mv}} \)

      If wavelength is compared with radiation wavelength, use \( \mathrm{\lambda=\dfrac{c}{\nu}} \).

      Therefore, \( \mathrm{\dfrac{h}{mv}=\dfrac{c}{\nu}\Rightarrow v=\dfrac{h\nu}{mc}} \).

      Trap: convert nm to m before substitution.

    7. Heisenberg uncertainty principle

      \( \mathrm{\Delta x\Delta p\ge\dfrac{h}{4\pi}} \)

      \( \mathrm{\Delta p=m\Delta v} \)

      Percentage uncertainty in velocity: \( \mathrm{\dfrac{\Delta v}{v}\times100} \).

      Trap: convert mass to kg and keep SI units consistent.

    8. Quantum numbers and allowed values

      \( \mathrm{l=0,1,2,\ldots,n-1} \)

      \( \mathrm{m_l=-l,\ldots,0,\ldots,+l} \)

      \( \mathrm{m_s=+\dfrac{1}{2}\ or\ -\dfrac{1}{2}} \)

      \( \mathrm{l=0\rightarrow s,\ l=1\rightarrow p,\ l=2\rightarrow d,\ l=3\rightarrow f} \)

      Trap: check \(l\) first; if \(l\ge n\), the whole set is impossible.

    9. Number of orbitals, electron capacity and nodes

      \( \mathrm{number\ of\ orbitals\ in\ shell=n^2} \)

      \( \mathrm{maximum\ electrons\ in\ shell=2n^2} \)

      \( \mathrm{number\ of\ orbitals\ in\ subshell=2l+1} \)

      \( \mathrm{total\ nodes=n-1} \)

      \( \mathrm{angular\ nodes=l} \)

      \( \mathrm{radial\ nodes=n-l-1} \)

      Trap: electrons with same spin in a shell can be at most one per orbital, so maximum same-spin electrons \(=n^2\).

    10. Orbital angular momentum and Bohr angular momentum

      \( \mathrm{L=\sqrt{l(l+1)}\hbar} \)

      For \(d\)-orbital, \(l=2\), so \( \mathrm{L=\sqrt6\hbar} \).

      \( \mathrm{mvr=\dfrac{nh}{2\pi}} \)

      For first Bohr orbit, angular momentum \( \mathrm{=\dfrac{h}{2\pi}} \).

    11. Degenerate orbitals and multi-electron energy order

      In hydrogen atom, energy depends only on \(n\).

      Therefore \(2s\) and \(2p\) are degenerate in hydrogen.

      In multi-electron atoms, energy depends on both \(n\) and \(l\).

      Trap: do not apply hydrogen-like degeneracy to helium or multi-electron atoms.

    12. Electronic configuration exceptions and counting electrons

      \( \mathrm{Cr=[Ar]\,3d^5\,4s^1} \)

      \( \mathrm{Cu=[Ar]\,3d^{10}\,4s^1} \)

      Half-filled and fully-filled subshells have extra stability.

      For transition-metal ions, remove \(4s\) electrons before \(3d\) electrons.

      Trap: when asked for total \(s,p,d\) electrons, count all occupied subshells of that type, not only the valence shell.

    13. Photon energy and power relation

      \( \mathrm{E=h\nu=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}=hc\bar{\nu}} \)

      \( \mathrm{P=N\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}} \)

      \(N\) is the number of photons emitted per second.

      Trap: energy is directly proportional to frequency and wave number, but inversely proportional to wavelength.

    14. Bohr electron speed in first orbit

      \( \mathrm{v_1\approx2.18\times10^6\ m\,s^{-1}} \)

      \( \mathrm{\dfrac{c}{v_1}\approx\dfrac{3\times10^8}{2.18\times10^6}\approx137} \)

      Trap: \(c:v\) for first Bohr orbit of hydrogen is about \(137:1\).

    15. Isoelectronic species and common stability traps

      Isoelectronic species have the same total number of electrons.

      Example: \(\mathrm{O^{2-},F^-,Na^+,Mg^{2+}}\) each have \(10\) electrons.

      Nitrogen has stable half-filled \(2p^3\), so its first ionisation enthalpy is greater than oxygen.

      Trap: oxygen has paired-electron repulsion in \(2p^4\), so removing one electron is easier than from nitrogen.


    1. Atomic radius trend

      \( \mathrm{atomic\ radius:\ decreases\ across\ a\ period,\ increases\ down\ a\ group} \)

      Across a period, effective nuclear charge increases, so size decreases.

      Down a group, number of shells increases, so size increases.

      Trap: do not arrange purely by atomic mass; split by period/group first.

    2. Isoelectronic ionic radius rule

      \( \mathrm{same\ number\ of\ electrons:\ higher\ nuclear\ charge\Rightarrow smaller\ radius} \)

      \( \mathrm{Mg^{2+} < Na^+ < F^- < O^{2-}} \)

      \( \mathrm{Al^{3+} < Na^+ < F^- < N^{3-}} \)

      Trap: isoelectronic does not mean equal size; compare nuclear charge.

    3. Shell effect before charge effect

      \( \mathrm{K^+ > Na^+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}} \)

      First compare number of shells.

      Then apply isoelectronic nuclear-charge logic within the same shell.

      Trap: a larger positive charge does not always mean smaller if the ion has an extra shell.

    4. Group 13 radius exception: aluminium and gallium

      \( \mathrm{Tl > In > Al > Ga} \)

      \(\mathrm{Ga}\) is smaller than \(\mathrm{Al}\) due to poor shielding by \(3d\)-electrons.

      Trap: do not blindly apply simple down-group increase in Group 13.

    5. Lanthanoid contraction radius pairs

      \( \mathrm{Zr \approx Hf} \)

      \( \mathrm{Mo \approx W} \)

      Poor shielding by \(4f\)-electrons makes \(5d\) elements nearly equal in size to corresponding \(4d\) elements.

      Trap: ordinary down-group increase is strongly reduced after lanthanoids.

    6. Electron gain enthalpy exceptions

      \( \mathrm{Cl > F > S > O} \)

      \( \mathrm{S > Se > O} \)

      More negative electron gain enthalpy means greater electron-gain tendency.

      \(\mathrm{Cl}\) is more favourable than \(\mathrm{F}\), and \(\mathrm{S}\) is more favourable than \(\mathrm{O}\), due to compact \(2p\)-orbital repulsion in \(\mathrm{F}\) and \(\mathrm{O}\).

    7. Positive electron gain enthalpy of noble gases

      \( \mathrm{Kr,\ Xe,\ Rn:\ positive\ electron\ gain\ enthalpy} \)

      Noble gases have filled valence shells.

      Adding an electron requires entry into a higher energy shell.

      Trap: positive electron gain enthalpy means electron addition is unfavourable.

    8. First ionisation enthalpy exceptions

      \( \mathrm{Li < B < Be < N} \)

      \( \mathrm{IE_1(O) < IE_1(N)} \)

      \(\mathrm{Be}\) is high due to stable \(2s^2\).

      \(\mathrm{N}\) is high due to stable half-filled \(2p^3\).

      \(\mathrm{O}\) is lower than \(\mathrm{N}\) due to paired-electron repulsion in \(2p^4\).

    9. Second ionisation enthalpy trap

      \( \mathrm{IE_2:\ write\ X^+\ configuration\ first} \)

      For \(\mathrm{C,N,O,F}\): \( \mathrm{O > F > N > C} \).

      \(\mathrm{O^+}\) has stable half-filled \(2p^3\), so removing another electron is hardest.

      Trap: do not compare neutral atoms directly for second ionisation enthalpy.

    10. Successive ionisation enthalpy jump

      \( \mathrm{large\ jump\ after\ nth\ IE\Rightarrow n\ valence\ electrons} \)

      Big jump after \(3^\text{rd}\) ionisation enthalpy means \(3\) valence electrons.

      Therefore the element belongs to Group 13.

      Trap: focus on the jump position, not only the absolute values.

    11. Metallic character trend

      \( \mathrm{metallic\ character:\ decreases\ across\ a\ period,\ increases\ down\ a\ group} \)

      Metallic character increases with larger size, lower ionisation enthalpy, and easier electron loss.

      Example order: \( \mathrm{Sn < Cd < Zr < Sr} \).

      Trap: highly negative electron gain enthalpy indicates non-metallic behaviour, not metallic reactivity.

    12. Acidic and basic nature of oxides

      \( \mathrm{metal\ oxides\rightarrow basic} \)

      \( \mathrm{non\text{-}metal\ oxides\rightarrow acidic} \)

      \( \mathrm{BeO,\ Al_2O_3,\ PbO_2\rightarrow amphoteric} \)

      \( \mathrm{CO\rightarrow neutral} \)

      Example acidity order: \( \mathrm{Al_2O_3 < SiO_2 < P_2O_3 < SO_2} \).

    13. Acidic oxides counting trap

      \( \mathrm{B_2O_3,\ SiO_2,\ CO_2\rightarrow acidic} \)

      \( \mathrm{Al_2O_3,\ PbO_2\rightarrow amphoteric} \)

      \( \mathrm{CO\rightarrow neutral} \)

      Trap: do not count amphoteric oxides or neutral oxides as purely acidic.

    14. Block classification rule

      \( \mathrm{s\text{-}block:\ differentiating\ electron\ enters\ s} \)

      \( \mathrm{p\text{-}block:\ differentiating\ electron\ enters\ p} \)

      \( \mathrm{d\text{-}block:\ differentiating\ electron\ enters\ (n-1)d} \)

      \( \mathrm{f\text{-}block:\ differentiating\ electron\ enters\ (n-2)f} \)

      Examples: \(\mathrm{Ba}\rightarrow s\), \(\mathrm{Se}\rightarrow p\), \(\mathrm{Cd}\rightarrow d\), \(\mathrm{Eu}\rightarrow f\).

    15. d-block period and group from configuration

      \( \mathrm{group\ number=(n-1)d\ electrons+ns\ electrons} \)

      Example: \(\mathrm{Pt=[Xe]\,4f^{14}5d^9 6s^1}\).

      Period \(=6\), group \(=10\).

      Trap: for \(d\)-block, group number is not just the outer \(s\)-electron count.

    16. Important group-family sets

      \( \mathrm{Mn,\ Tc,\ Re\rightarrow Group\ 7} \)

      \( \mathrm{Zn,\ Cd,\ Hg\rightarrow Group\ 12} \)

      \( \mathrm{Ti,\ Zr,\ Hf\rightarrow Group\ 4} \)

      \( \mathrm{Ga,\ In,\ Tl\rightarrow Group\ 13} \)

      Trap: transition-metal family numbers are often interchanged in options.

    17. Oxidation-state and diagonal-boundary clue

      If consecutive atomic numbers are \(X=a\), \(Y=a+1\), \(Z=a+2\), and \(Z\) is an alkali metal, then \(Y\) is a noble gas and \(X\) is a halogen.

      \( \mathrm{alkali\ metal+halogen\rightarrow ionic\ compound} \)

      Trap: recognise the periodic-table boundary between noble gas and alkali metal.

    18. Useful one-line factual anchors

      \( \mathrm{Fr:\ most\ electropositive} \)

      \( \mathrm{Os:\ densest\ element\ in\ standard\ exam\ convention} \)

      \( \mathrm{Ds:\ Z=110} \)

      \( \mathrm{Bh:\ Z=107} \)

      Trap: do not confuse bohrium \((\mathrm{Bh})\) with darmstadtium \((\mathrm{Ds})\).

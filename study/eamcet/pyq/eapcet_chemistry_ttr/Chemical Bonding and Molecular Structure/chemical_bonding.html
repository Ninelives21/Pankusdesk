Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

    Exam map for this chapter
  • Most PYQs fall into a few buckets: VSEPR shape, hybridisation, lone-pair count, bond angle, dipole moment, bond order, magnetism, formal charge, Fajans’ rule, Lewis acid/base, and hydrogen bonding.
  • Before solving, first tag the question: shape / hybridisation / lone pair / MOT / polarity / H-bond / ionic-vs-covalent / formal charge.
  • Many questions are one-step if electron domains are counted correctly.

    Lewis structure and electron-pair counting
  • First draw the skeletal structure, count total valence electrons, then place bond pairs and lone pairs.
  • For central-atom lone pairs, use: steric number \(=\) \(\sigma\)-bonds + lone pairs.
  • Ozone-type counting questions usually reduce to: count total \(\sigma\)-bonds, \(\pi\)-bonds, and lone pairs carefully from one resonance picture.
  • Formal charge shortcut: \(\text{FC} = \text{valence e}^- - \text{nonbonding e}^- - \frac{1}{2}(\text{bonding e}^-)\).

    VSEPR: shape from steric number
  • 2 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp\) \(\rightarrow\) linear.
  • 3 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp^2\) \(\rightarrow\) trigonal planar or bent.
  • 4 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp^3\) \(\rightarrow\) tetrahedral / trigonal pyramidal / bent.
  • 5 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp^3d\) \(\rightarrow\) trigonal bipyramidal / see-saw / T-shaped / linear.
  • 6 electron domains \(\rightarrow sp^3d^2\) \(\rightarrow\) octahedral / square pyramidal / square planar.

    Must-know molecule patterns
  • \(SO_2\), \(O_3\), \(SnCl_2\): bent.
  • \(NH_3\): trigonal pyramidal; \(H_2O\): bent.
  • \(SF_4\): see-saw; \(ClF_3\): T-shaped; \(BrF_5\): square pyramidal; \(XeF_4\): square planar.
  • \(I_3^-\): \(sp^3d\) but linear molecular shape.
  • \(BF_3\): trigonal planar; \(BeF_2/HgCl_2/CO_2\): linear.

    Lone-pair rules that repeatedly matter
  • More lone pairs on the central atom usually compress bond angles.
  • Typical counts: \(ClF_3=2,\ NF_3=1,\ SF_4=1,\ XeF_4=2\).
  • Lone-pair count questions are often disguised as hybridisation or shape questions.
  • If the question compares same geometry but different hybridisation, count electron domains first, not just visible shape.

    Bond angle and dipole moment shortcuts
  • For bond angles, electron-pair repulsion order is \(LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP\).
  • Useful patterns from PYQs: \(H_2O < NH_3 < O_3 < SO_2\) and \(BeCl_2 > BF_3 > SiCl_4 > SF_6\).
  • Dipole moment depends on bond polarity and vector addition, not electronegativity alone.
  • Symmetric molecules like \(BF_3,\ CO_2,\ CS_2,\ CCl_4,\ XeF_4\) often have zero net dipole moment.
  • \(NF_3\) has smaller dipole moment than \(NH_3\) because bond moments partially oppose lone-pair direction.

    MOT: fastest bond-order toolkit
  • Bond order \(=\dfrac{N_b-N_a}{2}\).
  • Higher bond order \(\rightarrow\) shorter bond length and greater bond strength.
  • More unpaired electrons \(\rightarrow\) paramagnetic; no unpaired electrons \(\rightarrow\) diamagnetic.
  • For oxygen family: removing antibonding electrons raises bond order; adding them lowers bond order.
  • This is why \(O_2^{2+}\) has shorter bond than \(O_2\), and \(O_2^{2-}\) has lower bond order.

    MOT memory anchors
  • \(C_2\) is a high-yield exception in school-level PYQs: treated as diamagnetic and often discussed as having only \(\pi\)-bond contribution between atoms.
  • \(He_2\) and \(Ne_2\) do not exist because bond order becomes zero.
  • Existence / non-existence questions are just bond-order questions in disguise.
  • If bond-order difference is asked among oxygen ions, compute each quickly and compare.

    Bond length, bond type, and covalent character
  • Higher bond order \(\rightarrow\) shorter bond length: \(CO < CO_2 < CO_3^{2-}\).
  • Fajans’ rule: covalent character increases with small, highly charged cations and large, polarizable anions.
  • So \(LiF\) is more covalent than \(KF\), and \(CuCl\) is more covalent than \(NaCl\).
  • Alkali metal + halogen questions usually point to ionic bonding.

    Lewis acids and hydrogen bonding
  • Lewis acid = electron-pair acceptor; Lewis base = electron-pair donor.
  • \(AlCl_3,\ SnCl_4,\ CO_2,\ Ag^+\) are standard Lewis-acid-type species.
  • Intermolecular hydrogen bonding raises boiling and melting points strongly.
  • \(H_2O\) has an extended H-bond network, giving very high boiling point.
  • Intramolecular H-bonding can lower melting/boiling tendencies by reducing intermolecular association.

    Question-to-formula / rule map
  • Shape asked \(\rightarrow\) count electron domains \(\rightarrow\) assign hybridisation \(\rightarrow\) remove lone-pair directions to get molecular shape.
  • Bond angle order asked \(\rightarrow\) compare lone pairs, hybridisation, and ideal geometry first.
  • Dipole moment asked \(\rightarrow\) check symmetry before doing anything else.
  • Bond order / magnetism / existence asked \(\rightarrow\) switch immediately to MOT.
  • Formal charge asked \(\rightarrow\) use \(V-N-\frac{B}{2}\) directly.

    Common traps
  • Same shape does not always mean same hybridisation.
  • Zero dipole moment needs symmetry of bond moments, not just “polar bonds present.”
  • Do not confuse electron-pair geometry with molecular shape.
  • In MOT questions, do not rely on Lewis structures for magnetism.
  • Hydrogen bonding questions often hinge on intra- versus intermolecular bonding, not just “H is present.”

    Kossel–Lewis approach, octet rule, and Lewis structures
  • Kossel emphasized electron transfer for ionic bonding, while Lewis described bonding through electron-pair sharing.
  • The octet rule says atoms tend to achieve noble-gas-like valence-shell stability, usually 8 electrons.
  • Main limitations: incomplete octet (\(BF_3\), \(BeCl_2\)), odd-electron molecules, and expanded octet species (\(SF_6\), \(PCl_5\)).
  • Lewis structures are the base for counting bond pairs, lone pairs, formal charge, and resonance contributors.

    Formal charge, resonance, and bond order basics
  • Formal charge helps choose the most reasonable Lewis structure.
  • \(\text{Formal charge} = \text{valence electrons} - \text{nonbonding electrons} - \frac{1}{2}\text{(bonding electrons)}\).
  • In resonance structures, the actual molecule is a resonance hybrid with delocalized electron density.
  • Resonance often gives intermediate bond length and bond order, as in \(CO_3^{2-}\) and \(O_3\).
  • Average bond order explains why \(CO_3^{2-}\) bonds are longer than \(CO\) but shorter than a pure single bond expectation.

    Ionic bond, lattice enthalpy, and Fajans’ rule
  • Ionic bonding is favored by low ionisation enthalpy, high electron gain enthalpy, and high lattice enthalpy.
  • Lattice enthalpy reflects the strength of electrostatic attraction in an ionic crystal.
  • Ionic compounds usually have high melting points, conduct when molten, and are hard but brittle.
  • Fajans’ rule: covalent character increases with small/highly charged cations and large/polarizable anions.
  • This is why \(LiF\) shows more covalent character than \(KF\), and \(CuCl\) more than \(NaCl\).

    Bond parameters: length, angle, enthalpy, order, polarity, dipole moment
  • Bond length decreases as bond order increases.
  • Bond enthalpy is the energy required to break one mole of bonds in the gaseous state.
  • Bond angle depends on electron-pair repulsions and hybridisation.
  • Bond polarity arises from electronegativity difference; dipole moment depends on both bond polarity and geometry.
  • Symmetric molecules may contain polar bonds but still have zero net dipole moment, such as \(CO_2,\ BF_3,\ XeF_4,\ CCl_4\).

    VSEPR theory and molecular shapes
  • Electron pairs around a central atom arrange themselves to minimize repulsion.
  • Molecular shape is determined after ignoring lone-pair positions and focusing on atom positions.
  • Repulsion order: \(LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP\).
  • This is why lone pairs reduce bond angles in \(NH_3\) and even more in \(H_2O\).

    Hybridisation and standard shape memory map
  • \(sp \rightarrow\) linear: \(BeCl_2,\ CO_2,\ HgCl_2,\ I_3^-\) (molecular shape linear).
  • \(sp^2 \rightarrow\) trigonal planar or bent: \(BF_3,\ SO_3,\ SO_2,\ O_3,\ SnCl_2\).
  • \(sp^3 \rightarrow\) tetrahedral / pyramidal / bent: \(CH_4,\ NH_3,\ H_2O\).
  • \(sp^3d \rightarrow\) trigonal bipyramidal / see-saw / T-shaped / linear: \(PCl_5,\ SF_4,\ ClF_3,\ XeF_2\).
  • \(sp^3d^2 \rightarrow\) octahedral / square pyramidal / square planar: \(SF_6,\ BrF_5,\ XeF_4\).

    Valence bond theory and orbital overlap
  • A covalent bond forms when half-filled atomic orbitals overlap and paired electrons are shared.
  • \(\sigma\)-bonds form by head-on overlap and are stronger than \(\pi\)-bonds.
  • \(\pi\)-bonds form by sidewise overlap and restrict free rotation around the bond.
  • Single bond = one \(\sigma\); double bond = one \(\sigma\) + one \(\pi\); triple bond = one \(\sigma\) + two \(\pi\).

    Coordinate bond and Lewis acid–base idea
  • A coordinate bond is formed when both shared electrons come from one atom.
  • Lewis acid accepts an electron pair; Lewis base donates an electron pair.
  • Examples of Lewis acids in PYQ style: \(AlCl_3,\ SnCl_4,\ CO_2,\ Ag^+\).
  • Examples of Lewis bases: \(NH_3,\ H_2O,\ Cl^-,\ F^-\).

    Molecular orbital theory: core rules
  • MOs form by linear combination of atomic orbitals with proper symmetry and comparable energy.
  • Bonding MOs are lower in energy; antibonding MOs are higher in energy.
  • \(\text{Bond order} = \dfrac{N_b - N_a}{2}\).
  • Bond order \(>0\) suggests existence; bond order \(=0\) suggests non-existence.
  • Unpaired electrons in MOs cause paramagnetism.

    Hydrogen bonding and its consequences
  • Hydrogen bonding occurs when H bonded to highly electronegative atoms like F, O, or N interacts with a lone pair on another electronegative atom.
  • Intermolecular hydrogen bonding raises boiling point, viscosity, and often melting point.
  • Intramolecular hydrogen bonding can reduce intermolecular association and may lower boiling/melting behavior relative to para analogues.
  • This explains the abnormal boiling point of HF and the ortho/para hydroxybenzaldehyde comparison.

    How to solve chapter questions quickly
  • If shape or bond angle is asked, count electron domains first.
  • If bond order, existence, or magnetism is asked, go straight to MOT.
  • If zero dipole is asked, test symmetry before anything else.
  • If formal charge is asked, apply the formula directly on the marked atoms.
  • If boiling/melting anomaly is asked, check for intermolecular versus intramolecular hydrogen bonding.

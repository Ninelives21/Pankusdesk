ATOMIC STRUCTURE

  • In Atomic Structure, PYQs repeatedly test:
    • Bohr model and energy of electron
    • de Broglie wavelength
    • Heisenberg uncertainty principle
    • quantum numbers and orbital identification
    • number of orbitals and electron capacity
    • electromagnetic radiation relations
  • Must-remember radiation formulas:
    • \(c = \nu \lambda\)
    • \(E = h \nu = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)
    • \(\tilde{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda}\)
  • Bohr-model formulas to memorise:
    • \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
    • \(E_n = - \dfrac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2}\,\text{eV}\)
    • \(\Delta E = 13.6 Z^2 \left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\,\text{eV}\)
  • de Broglie relation:
    • \(\lambda = \dfrac{h}{mv}\)
    • for moving charged particles, questions often reduce to “more momentum \(\rightarrow\) smaller wavelength”
  • Heisenberg uncertainty principle:
    • \(\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\)
    • position and momentum cannot both be known exactly at the same time
  • Quantum-number shortcuts:
    • \(n = 1,2,3,\dots\)
    • \(l = 0\) to \(n-1\)
    • \(m_l = -l\) to \(+l\)
    • \(m_s = \pm \dfrac{1}{2}\)
  • Very high-value memory rules:
    • number of orbitals in a subshell \(= 2l + 1\)
    • maximum electrons in a subshell \(= 2(2l+1)\)
    • maximum electrons in shell \(n = 2n^2\)
  • Last-minute checklist:
    • learn \(c = \nu \lambda\) and \(E = h\nu\)
    • learn Bohr energy formula and sign of energy
    • learn all four quantum numbers with allowed values
    • do not confuse orbit, orbital, shell and subshell
    • remember: 1s has 1 orbital, p has 3, d has 5, f has 7

  1. Electromagnetic radiation and basic relations
    • Light behaves as electromagnetic radiation and travels in the form of waves.
    • Important terms:
      • wavelength \((\lambda)\): distance between two successive crests
      • frequency \((\nu)\): number of waves passing a point in one second
      • velocity \((c)\): speed of light in vacuum
      • wavenumber \((\tilde{\nu})\): number of waves per unit length
    • Main formula:
      • \(c = \nu \lambda\)
      • \(E = h \nu\)
      • \(E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)
    • So:
      • higher frequency \(\rightarrow\) higher energy
      • larger wavelength \(\rightarrow\) lower energy
  2. From classical ideas to Bohr model
    • Classical physics could not explain:
      • black-body radiation
      • photoelectric effect
      • line spectrum of hydrogen
      • stability of atom
    • Bohr proposed that:
      • electrons move only in certain allowed circular orbits
      • these allowed orbits have fixed energy
      • electron does not radiate energy while staying in an allowed orbit
      • radiation is emitted or absorbed only when electron jumps from one orbit to another
    • Bohr energy formula for hydrogen-like species:
      • \(E_n = - \dfrac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2}\,\text{eV}\)
    • Radius formula:
      • \(r_n = \dfrac{n^2 a_0}{Z}\)
    • Transition energy:
      • \(\Delta E = h\nu = 13.6 Z^2\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\,\text{eV}\)
    • Important meaning:
      • more negative energy \(\rightarrow\) electron is more tightly bound
      • as \(n\) increases, energy becomes less negative
      • at \(n = \infty\), energy becomes zero
  3. Dual nature of matter and de Broglie concept
    • Light shows wave nature and particle nature.
    • de Broglie extended this idea to moving particles and said that matter also has wave character.
    • de Broglie equation:
      • \(\lambda = \dfrac{h}{mv}\)
    • This means:
      • heavy particle \(\rightarrow\) very small wavelength
      • fast particle \(\rightarrow\) smaller wavelength
      • small particle like electron \(\rightarrow\) observable wave nature
    • For exam questions:
      • greater momentum \((mv)\) means smaller \(\lambda\)
      • electron has much larger de Broglie wavelength than a cricket ball
  4. Heisenberg uncertainty principle
    • It is impossible to determine simultaneously the exact position and exact momentum of a microscopic particle.
    • Mathematically:
      • \(\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \dfrac{h}{4\pi}\)
    • Meaning:
      • if position is known very accurately, momentum becomes uncertain
      • if momentum is known very accurately, position becomes uncertain
    • This idea is important because it shows why electrons cannot be described as tiny planets moving in exact fixed paths.
    • That is why the modern view uses orbitals, not fixed circular orbits.
  5. Quantum numbers and orbital description
    • Each electron in an atom is described using four quantum numbers.
    • Principal quantum number \((n)\)
      • tells the main shell
      • \(n = 1,2,3,\dots\)
      • larger \(n\) means larger size and higher energy
    • Azimuthal quantum number \((l)\)
      • tells the subshell and shape of orbital
      • for a given \(n\), \(l = 0\) to \(n-1\)
      • \(l=0 \rightarrow s\), \(l=1 \rightarrow p\), \(l=2 \rightarrow d\), \(l=3 \rightarrow f\)
    • Magnetic quantum number \((m_l)\)
      • tells the orientation of orbital
      • values range from \(-l\) to \(+l\)
    • Spin quantum number \((m_s)\)
      • tells the spin of electron
      • \(+\dfrac{1}{2}\) or \(-\dfrac{1}{2}\)
    • Useful counting rules:
      • number of orbitals in a subshell \(= 2l+1\)
      • maximum electrons in a subshell \(= 2(2l+1)\)
      • maximum electrons in shell \(= 2n^2\)
    • Examples:
      • s subshell \(\rightarrow 1\) orbital \(\rightarrow 2\) electrons
      • p subshell \(\rightarrow 3\) orbitals \(\rightarrow 6\) electrons
      • d subshell \(\rightarrow 5\) orbitals \(\rightarrow 10\) electrons
      • f subshell \(\rightarrow 7\) orbitals \(\rightarrow 14\) electrons
  6. Orbitals, probability and modern picture of atom
    • An orbital is the region around the nucleus where the probability of finding an electron is maximum.
    • It is not a fixed circular path.
    • Orbitals differ in:
      • shape
      • size
      • orientation
    • Basic shapes:
      • s orbital: spherical
      • p orbital: dumb-bell shaped
      • d and f orbitals: more complex shapes
    • Important contrast:
      • orbit: fixed path in Bohr model
      • orbital: probability region in quantum-mechanical model

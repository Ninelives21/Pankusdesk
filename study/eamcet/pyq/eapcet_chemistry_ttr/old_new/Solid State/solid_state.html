Solid State

    Exam map / pattern map
  • Solid State PYQs here are heavily clustered around four zones: unit-cell density, void counting, site-sharing formula, and defects.
  • If the question gives \( \rho, a, M \), immediately think \( \rho=\dfrac{ZM}{N_Aa^3} \).
  • If oxygen or anions form ccp/fcc lattice, immediately set host particles \(=N\), octahedral voids \(=N\), tetrahedral voids \(=2N\).
  • If atoms are placed at corners / edges / faces / body centre, do site-sharing first, then reduce ratio to formula.
  • If the question says fcc radius-edge relation, use face diagonal; if bcc, use body diagonal.
  • If Schottky / Frenkel / F-centre appears, this is a direct-definition plus consequence question, not a long derivation question.

    Core formula and geometry rules
  • Density formula: \( \rho=\dfrac{ZM}{N_Aa^3} \).
  • Rearrange as needed: \(M=\dfrac{\rho N_Aa^3}{Z}\), \(Z=\dfrac{\rho a^3N_A}{M}\), \(a^3=\dfrac{ZM}{\rho N_A}\).
  • sc: \(a=2r\), \(Z=1\).
  • bcc: \( \sqrt{3}a=4r \), \(Z=2\), coordination number \(=8\).
  • fcc/ccp: \( \sqrt{2}a=4r \), \(Z=4\), coordination number \(=12\).
  • Same substance in two cubic forms: density ratio \( \dfrac{\rho_1}{\rho_2}=\dfrac{Z_1/a_1^3}{Z_2/a_2^3} \); \(M\) and \(N_A\) cancel.

    Void counting and occupancy shortcuts
  • For \(N\) close-packed host atoms: octahedral voids \(=N\), tetrahedral voids \(=2N\).
  • If host is ccp oxygen, take \(O=4\) per unit cell first; then octahedral voids \(=4\), tetrahedral voids \(=8\).
  • \(\frac{1}{8}\) of tetrahedral voids in ccp means \( \frac18 \times 8 = 1 \) occupant per unit cell.
  • \(50\%\) of octahedral voids in ccp means \( \frac12 \times 4 = 2 \) occupants per unit cell.
  • \(50\%\) of tetrahedral voids in ccp means \( \frac12 \times 8 = 4 \) occupants per unit cell, or \(N\) if you started with host \(=N\).
  • If asked occupancy percent, use \( \dfrac{\text{occupied voids}}{\text{total voids of that type}}\times 100 \).

    Crystal-system quick ID
  • If the question gives unit-cell edge relations and angles, think direct crystal-system identification.
  • Hexagonal system: \(a=b\neq c\), \( \alpha=\beta=90^\circ \), \( \gamma=120^\circ \).
  • Tetragonal system: \(a=b\neq c\), \( \alpha=\beta=\gamma=90^\circ \); do not confuse it with hexagonal.
  • If one angle becomes \(120^\circ\), that is the fastest trigger for hexagonal.

    Formula from site sharing
  • Corner atom contributes \( \dfrac18 \), edge-centre \( \dfrac14 \), face-centre \( \dfrac12 \), body-centre \(1\).
  • For site-count formula questions, do not name the compound first; count contributions first.
  • 8 occupied corners \( \Rightarrow 8\times \dfrac18 =1\).
  • 12 occupied edges \( \Rightarrow 12\times \dfrac14 =3\).
  • 1 body-centre \( \Rightarrow 1\).
  • Missing corners means reduce the number of occupied corners before multiplying by \( \dfrac18 \).
  • After counting, convert fractional ratio to whole-number empirical formula by multiplying through.

    Defects and non-stoichiometry
  • Schottky defect: equal number of cations and anions missing from lattice points.
  • Schottky defect keeps electrical neutrality but lowers density because mass decreases.
  • Schottky defect is common when cation and anion sizes are nearly comparable.
  • Frenkel defect is a dislocation defect: a smaller ion leaves its site and occupies an interstitial position.
  • F-centre: electron trapped in an anion vacancy; this causes colour in crystals.
  • For density with defect %, do not invent a new formula; just reduce effective \(Z\) or effective mass in the usual density formula.
  • For non-stoichiometric oxides like \(M_{0.96}O\), use charge balance first, then split fractions into \(M^{2+}\) and \(M^{3+}\).

    Fast question-to-formula mapping
  • If the question asks molar mass from density and edge length, immediately take known \(Z\) from lattice type.
  • If the question asks number of atoms per unit cell, rearrange density formula for \(Z\).
  • If the question asks radius from fcc edge length, use \( r=\dfrac{a\sqrt2}{4} \).
  • If the question asks edge length from bcc radius, use \( a=\dfrac{4r}{\sqrt3} \).
  • If the question asks nearest neighbours in bcc, answer \(8\) immediately.
  • If the question asks formula from ccp anions plus occupied void fractions, start with host \(=N\) or host \(=4\), not with guessed formula.
  • If the question asks vacant octahedral fraction, first find occupied octahedral fraction from compound stoichiometry, then subtract from total octahedral voids.

    Common traps
  • Do not mix up ccp/fcc host atoms with the number of voids.
  • Do not forget unit conversion: \(1\ \text{Å}=10^{-8}\ \text{cm}\), \(1\ \text{pm}=10^{-10}\ \text{cm}\).
  • Do not use \( \sqrt{2}a=4r \) for bcc or \( \sqrt{3}a=4r \) for fcc.
  • Do not say Schottky defect increases density; vacancy defects reduce mass, so density falls.
  • Do not count full corner or edge atoms; always apply sharing fraction first.
  • Do not force every listed anchor into Solid State: some source-list entries drift on the live pages.

    Crystal systems and unit cells
  • Solid state begins with the distinction between crystalline and amorphous solids. Crystalline solids show long-range order; amorphous solids do not.
  • A lattice is the regular three-dimensional arrangement of lattice points; a basis attached to each lattice point gives the crystal structure.
  • A unit cell is the smallest repeating part of the crystal that reproduces the whole lattice on repetition.
  • Primitive unit cell contains lattice points only at corners; centred unit cells have extra lattice points at body centre or face centres.
  • For crystal-system identification, memorize edge/angle patterns:
    • Cubic: \(a=b=c\), \( \alpha=\beta=\gamma=90^\circ \)
    • Tetragonal: \(a=b\neq c\), \( \alpha=\beta=\gamma=90^\circ \)
    • Orthorhombic: \(a\neq b\neq c\), \( \alpha=\beta=\gamma=90^\circ \)
  • PYQs here repeatedly use direct crystal-system recognition, especially tetragonal identification from \(a=b\neq c\).

    Counting atoms and site contributions
  • Standard effective contributions in one unit cell:
    • corner atom \(=\dfrac18\)
    • edge-centred atom \(=\dfrac14\)
    • face-centred atom \(=\dfrac12\)
    • body-centred atom \(=1\)
  • From these:
    • simple cubic has \(1\) atom per cell
    • bcc has \(2\) atoms per cell
    • fcc has \(4\) atoms per cell
  • This counting is the base for formula derivation questions such as \(NaWO_3\), \(X_8Y_7\), and \(X_5Y_8\).
  • Whenever a corner or edge is vacant, reduce the number of occupied positions before applying the contribution factor.

    Close packing and voids
  • Close packing in 3D gives two standard arrangements: hcp and ccp/fcc.
  • For \(N\) close-packed particles:
    • octahedral voids \(=N\)
    • tetrahedral voids \(=2N\)
  • In ccp/fcc with \(4\) host atoms per unit cell:
    • octahedral voids \(=4\)
    • tetrahedral voids \(=8\)
  • Questions may ask either:
    • formula from occupied void fractions
    • percent occupancy of tetrahedral or octahedral voids
    • fraction of vacant octahedral voids
  • Use a host-first method: count host particles first, then total voids, then occupied voids, then final ratio.

    Radius-edge relations and coordination
  • In simple cubic, atoms touch along the edge, so \(a=2r\).
  • In bcc, atoms touch along the body diagonal, so \( \sqrt{3}a=4r \).
  • In fcc, atoms touch along the face diagonal, so \( \sqrt{2}a=4r \).
  • Coordination number:
    • sc \(=6\)
    • bcc \(=8\)
    • fcc \(=12\)
  • These relations are repeatedly tested in direct one-step MCQs and in longer density questions.

    Density of unit cell
  • The core formula is:
    • \( \rho=\dfrac{ZM}{N_Aa^3} \)
  • Here:
    • \(Z\) = number of atoms or formula units per cell
    • \(M\) = molar mass
    • \(a\) = edge length
    • \(N_A\) = Avogadro constant
  • Typical PYQ variants:
    • find \(M\) from \( \rho \) and \(a\)
    • find \(Z\) from \( \rho, a, M \)
    • find \(a\) from \( \rho, M, Z \)
    • compare densities of same metal in different cubic forms
  • Be very careful with unit conversion:
    • \(1\ \text{Å}=10^{-8}\ \text{cm}\)
    • \(1\ \text{pm}=10^{-10}\ \text{cm}\)

    Defects in solids
  • Stoichiometric defects keep the overall stoichiometric ratio unchanged.
  • Schottky defect:
    • equal number of cations and anions missing
    • electrical neutrality maintained
    • mass decreases, so density decreases
    • common when ionic sizes are comparable
  • Frenkel defect:
    • usually a smaller cation leaves lattice site and enters an interstitial site
    • it is a dislocation defect
    • density remains nearly unchanged because ions are not lost from crystal
  • F-centre:
    • electron trapped in anion vacancy
    • responsible for colour in some crystals
  • In defect-corrected density questions, the same density formula remains valid; only effective particle count or mass changes.

    Non-stoichiometric solids
  • Some solids deviate slightly from simple whole-number formulas because of vacancies or variable oxidation states.
  • Metal-deficient oxides such as \(M_{0.96}O\) are solved by charge balance:
    • oxygen contributes \(-2\)
    • total positive charge from metals must be \(+2\)
    • let part of metal be \(M^{3+}\) and remainder \(M^{2+}\), then solve fraction-wise
  • These questions test oxidation-state accounting more than geometry.

    Packing efficiency, electrical and magnetic overview
  • The syllabus also includes packing efficiency in sc, bcc, hcp, and ccp/fcc lattices, though this specific PYQ set focuses more on density and geometry than direct efficiency calculation.
  • Electrical properties are explained through band theory: conductors, semiconductors, and insulators differ by band gap and electron mobility.
  • Magnetic properties usually require direct recall of diamagnetic, paramagnetic, ferromagnetic, antiferromagnetic, and ferrimagnetic behaviour.
  • For this Solid State page, treat packing, defects, unit-cell arithmetic, and void logic as the highest-return core, because that is where the recurring PYQs are concentrated.

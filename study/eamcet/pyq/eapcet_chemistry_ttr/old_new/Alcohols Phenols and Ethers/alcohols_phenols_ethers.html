Alcohols, Phenols and Ethers

    Acidity and order map
  • Phenol is more acidic than alcohol because phenoxide ion is resonance-stabilised, while alkoxide ion is not.
  • Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity of phenol; electron-donating groups decrease it.
  • For simple alcohols, acidity decreases with \(+I\) effect of alkyl groups: methyl alcohol \(>\) primary \(>\) secondary \(>\) tertiary.
  • If the question compares phenol with alcohols or water, phenol usually comes out more acidic than alcohols.

    Reactions of alcohols
  • Dehydration of alcohol gives alkene; usually the more substituted alkene is major under acidic conditions.
  • Oxidation pattern: \(1^\circ\) alcohol \(\rightarrow\) aldehyde \(\rightarrow\) acid, \(2^\circ\) alcohol \(\rightarrow\) ketone, \(3^\circ\) alcohol resists mild oxidation.
  • Lucas test / substitution tendency usually follows: \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ\).
  • Alcohol \(\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Na}}\) alkoxide + hydrogen is a direct acidic-hydrogen clue.

    Phenol reaction map
  • Phenol undergoes electrophilic substitution more easily than benzene because \(-OH\) activates the ring.
  • Phenol with bromine water gives \(2,4,6\)-tribromophenol.
  • Phenol with dilute \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) gives o- and p-nitrophenols; with concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) gives picric acid.
  • Kolbe reaction: sodium phenoxide + \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) \(\rightarrow\) salicylic acid after acidification.
  • Reimer–Tiemann reaction: phenol + \(\mathrm{CHCl_3/NaOH}\) \(\rightarrow\) salicylaldehyde mainly.

    Ether map
  • Ethers are less reactive than alcohols because they do not have the \(\mathrm{O-H}\) bond.
  • Williamson ether synthesis: alkoxide + primary alkyl halide \(\rightarrow\) ether.
  • Use primary alkyl halide in Williamson synthesis to avoid elimination.
  • Aryl halides do not normally undergo Williamson substitution at the aryl carbon.
  • Ethers are cleaved by excess \(\mathrm{HI}\) or \(\mathrm{HBr}\), especially at the less hindered alkyl side unless one side is tertiary.

    Distinguishing tests
  • Phenol gives violet / blue / green colour with neutral \(\mathrm{FeCl_3}\).
  • Alcohols generally do not give the ferric chloride colour test.
  • Iodoform test is given by ethanol and alcohols containing \(\mathrm{CH_3-CH(OH)-}\) group.
  • Anisole does not behave like phenol in acidity-based tests because the oxygen is part of an ether linkage, not \(\mathrm{O-H}\).

    Fast exam triggers
  • Need salicylic acid \(\Rightarrow\) Kolbe reaction.
  • Need salicylaldehyde \(\Rightarrow\) Reimer–Tiemann reaction.
  • Need tribromo product from phenol \(\Rightarrow\) bromine water.
  • Need ether from alkoxide \(\Rightarrow\) Williamson synthesis.
  • Need oxidation product of alcohol \(\Rightarrow\) first identify whether alcohol is \(1^\circ\), \(2^\circ\), or \(3^\circ\).

  1. Alcohols: classification and structure
    • Alcohols contain the \(\mathrm{-OH}\) group attached to an \(sp^3\)-hybridised carbon.
    • They are classified as primary, secondary, and tertiary depending on how many carbon groups are attached to the carbon bearing \(\mathrm{-OH}\).
    • The physical properties of alcohols are strongly influenced by intermolecular hydrogen bonding.
    • Because of hydrogen bonding, alcohols generally have much higher boiling points than corresponding hydrocarbons and ethers of similar molar mass.

  2. Acidity and reactions of alcohols
    • Alcohols are weakly acidic and react with active metals like sodium to give alkoxides and hydrogen gas.
    • Acidity decreases as the \(+I\) effect of alkyl groups increases.
    • Primary alcohols on oxidation give aldehydes and then acids; secondary alcohols give ketones; tertiary alcohols resist mild oxidation.
    • Dehydration of alcohols under acidic conditions generally gives the more substituted alkene.
    • Lucas-type reactivity toward substitution usually follows \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ\).

  3. Phenols: structure and acidity
    • Phenols have the \(\mathrm{-OH}\) group directly attached to an aromatic ring.
    • Phenol is more acidic than alcohol because the phenoxide ion is resonance-stabilised.
    • Electron-withdrawing groups increase the acidity of phenol, while electron-donating groups decrease it.
    • Phenol is not acidic enough to liberate \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) from sodium bicarbonate, but it does react with sodium hydroxide.

  4. Important reactions of phenol
    • Phenol is strongly activating and ortho/para-directing in electrophilic substitution reactions.
    • With bromine water, phenol gives \(2,4,6\)-tribromophenol.
    • With dilute nitric acid, phenol gives ortho- and para-nitrophenols; with concentrated nitric acid, it gives picric acid.
    • Kolbe reaction converts sodium phenoxide into salicylic acid after reaction with \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) and acidification.
    • Reimer–Tiemann reaction converts phenol into salicylaldehyde using \(\mathrm{CHCl_3/NaOH}\).

  5. Ethers: structure and preparation
    • Ethers contain an oxygen atom bonded to two alkyl and/or aryl groups.
    • They are less reactive than alcohols because they do not contain an \(\mathrm{O-H}\) bond.
    • Williamson ether synthesis is the standard laboratory method for preparing ethers.
    • In Williamson synthesis, an alkoxide ion attacks a primary alkyl halide by \(S_N2\) mechanism.
    • Primary halides are preferred because secondary and tertiary halides tend to undergo elimination.

  6. Cleavage and properties of ethers
    • Ethers undergo cleavage with excess \(\mathrm{HI}\) or \(\mathrm{HBr}\).
    • If one side is tertiary, cleavage tends to give the tertiary halide by \(S_N1\)-type pathway.
    • For simple unsymmetrical primary ethers, cleavage generally occurs at the less hindered alkyl side by \(S_N2\)-type attack.
    • Anisole behaves differently from phenol because it is an ether and does not have acidic \(\mathrm{O-H}\).

  7. Distinguishing features and common tests
    • Phenol gives a characteristic colour reaction with neutral ferric chloride.
    • Alcohols generally do not give the ferric chloride test of phenol.
    • Iodoform test is shown by ethanol and secondary alcohols containing the \(\mathrm{CH_3-CH(OH)-}\) group.
    • This makes ethanol a very common exception among primary alcohols in qualitative testing.

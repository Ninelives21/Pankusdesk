Hydrocarbons

    Core map: aromaticity, hyperconjugation, carbocation stability
  • Hyperconjugation = delocalisation of \(\sigma\)(C–H) electrons of an alkyl group into an adjacent \(\pi\)-system or vacant \(p\)-orbital.
  • If the question says “delocalisation of benzylic or alkyl C–H \(\sigma\)-electrons”, think hyperconjugation immediately.
  • Carbocation stability: benzylic / allylic \(>\) \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ >\) methyl \(>\) vinylic.
  • Vinylic carbocation is the classic “least stable” trap.
  • Aromatic species must be cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, and obey \((4n+2)\pi\) electrons.
  • If aromaticity is broken and not restored, the species is non-aromatic.

    Alkanes and alkynes
  • Boiling point of isomeric alkanes decreases with branching: straight chain \(>\) branched chain.
  • Only terminal alkynes \(\mathrm{R-C{\equiv}C-H}\) have acidic hydrogen.
  • Internal alkynes do not respond as acidic-hydrogen alkynes.
  • For alkyne counting, first list all structural isomers, then separate terminal from internal.
  • Questions asking “1-alkyne isomers” are narrower than “all alkynes” and usually much fewer.

    Reactions of alkenes and alkynes
  • Ozonolysis cleaves \(\mathrm{C=C}\) into carbonyl fragments; reconstruct the original alkene by joining the two carbonyl carbons with a double bond.
  • Terminal \(\mathrm{=CH_2}\) carbon often gives \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) under strong oxidative cleavage conditions.
  • Hydration of alkynes under \(\mathrm{Hg^{2+}/H^+}\) gives enol first, then keto form by tautomerisation.
  • Elimination from alcohol / alkyl halide usually favours the more substituted alkene under ordinary acidic conditions.
  • Many hydrocarbon PYQs are “rebuild-the-starting-alkene” questions, not direct memory questions.

    Benzene and electrophilic substitution
  • Benzene undergoes electrophilic substitution, not simple addition, because aromaticity is preserved.
  • Friedel–Crafts alkylation: alkyl halide + anhydrous \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\).
  • Friedel–Crafts acylation: acyl halide + anhydrous \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\).
  • Benzene \(\rightarrow\) toluene suggests \(\mathrm{CH_3Cl/AlCl_3}\).
  • Benzene \(\rightarrow\) acetophenone suggests \(\mathrm{CH_3COCl/AlCl_3}\).

    Practical shortcut map
  • Product-based hydrocarbon questions: work backward from the product.
  • Boiling-point order of alkanes: first compare branching, then carbon count.
  • Aromaticity questions: check conjugation before counting \(\pi\)-electrons.
  • Terminal alkyne question: look for hydrogen directly on the triple-bond carbon.
  • If a benzene question contains \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\), expect electrophilic substitution logic.

  1. Classification of hydrocarbons
    • Hydrocarbons are compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen.
    • They are broadly classified as aliphatic and aromatic.
    • Aliphatic hydrocarbons may be saturated (alkanes) or unsaturated (alkenes, alkynes).
    • Aromatic hydrocarbons contain benzene-like aromatic systems satisfying aromaticity conditions.

  2. Alkanes
    • Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with only single bonds.
    • They usually undergo substitution reactions rather than addition reactions.
    • Boiling point increases with molecular mass, but among isomers decreases with branching because compact molecules have lower surface area.
    • This is why straight-chain hexane has a higher boiling point than its more branched isomers.

  3. Alkenes
    • Alkenes contain a carbon–carbon double bond and commonly undergo electrophilic addition reactions.
    • Ozonolysis cleaves the double bond to give aldehydes and/or ketones.
    • By examining ozonolysis products, the original alkene can often be reconstructed directly.
    • Strong oxidation of alkenes can also cleave the double bond and convert terminal carbon to carbon dioxide under vigorous conditions.

  4. Alkynes
    • Alkynes contain a carbon–carbon triple bond.
    • Terminal alkynes have acidic hydrogen because the carbon bonded to hydrogen is \(sp\)-hybridised and more electronegative than \(sp^2\) or \(sp^3\) carbon.
    • Internal alkynes do not show this acidic-hydrogen behaviour.
    • Hydration of alkynes under mercuric-ion catalysis gives enol first, then tautomerises to a carbonyl compound.

  5. Aromatic hydrocarbons and aromaticity
    • Aromatic compounds must be cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, and must contain \((4n+2)\pi\) electrons.
    • Benzene is the standard aromatic hydrocarbon because it has \(6\pi\) electrons and a planar conjugated ring.
    • Species that fail planarity or continuous conjugation, or do not satisfy Hückel’s rule, are not aromatic.
    • Many objective questions are solved fastest by checking conjugation first and electron count second.

  6. Hyperconjugation and stability ideas used in hydrocarbons
    • Hyperconjugation involves delocalisation of \(\sigma\)(C–H) electrons adjacent to a \(\pi\)-bond, carbocation centre, or aromatic ring.
    • It stabilises substituted alkenes and carbocations.
    • Resonance-stabilised benzylic and allylic carbocations are generally more stable than ordinary alkyl carbocations.
    • Vinylic carbocations are very unstable because positive charge is located on a double-bond carbon.

  7. Electrophilic substitution in benzene
    • Benzene generally reacts by electrophilic substitution because the ring regains aromaticity after substitution.
    • Nitration, sulphonation, halogenation, Friedel–Crafts alkylation and Friedel–Crafts acylation are standard examples.
    • \(\mathrm{CH_3Cl/AlCl_3}\) introduces a methyl group; \(\mathrm{CH_3COCl/AlCl_3}\) introduces an acetyl group.
    • When a benzene reaction question includes anhydrous \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\), Friedel–Crafts logic is usually the first

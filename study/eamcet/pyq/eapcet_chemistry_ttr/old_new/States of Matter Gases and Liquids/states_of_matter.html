States of Matter: Gases and Liquids

    Exam map for this chapter
  • The repeated PYQ buckets are: ideal gas law, Dalton’s law / gas mixing, density–molar mass–mole fraction, Graham’s law diffusion, speed relations \((v_{rms}, v_{mp})\), compressibility factor \(Z\), and one direct viscosity formula.
  • Before solving, tag the question first: gas-law state change / mixing-partial pressure / density-composition / diffusion / molecular speed / real-gas deviation / liquid property.
  • Most questions become one-line substitutions once the right formula is chosen.

    Ideal gas equation: the main engine
  • Use \(PV=nRT\) or \(PV=\dfrac{w}{M}RT\) whenever pressure, volume, temperature, mass, or molar mass appear together.
  • At fixed \(n\): \(\dfrac{P_1V_1}{T_1}=\dfrac{P_2V_2}{T_2}\).
  • At same \(T\) and \(V\), pressure is directly proportional to moles: \(P \propto n\).
  • At same \(P\) and \(V\), moles are inversely proportional to temperature for an open vessel heating / expulsion type question.

    Gas mixing and Dalton’s law
  • Dalton’s law: \(P_{total}=p_1+p_2+\cdots\).
  • Partial pressure relation: \(p_i=x_iP_{total}\).
  • After ideal gases mix at constant temperature, each gas occupies the total volume, but its moles stay unchanged.
  • Fast route for connected-vessel problems: first find \(n_i=\dfrac{P_iV_i}{RT}\), then compute new \(p_i=\dfrac{n_iRT}{V_{total}}\).
  • If one component’s partial pressure is given, total pressure often follows immediately from its mole fraction.

    Density, average molar mass, and mole fraction
  • For an ideal gas or gas mixture: \(d=\dfrac{PM}{RT}\).
  • If density is given, first find the average molar mass \(M=\dfrac{dRT}{P}\).
  • For a gas mixture, average molar mass is the weighted mean of component molar masses.
  • Use mole fraction for composition questions; use mass percent only at the last step if asked.
  • Air-type problems are direct weighted-average questions: \(\bar M = \sum x_i M_i\).

    Graham’s law: diffusion / effusion shortcuts
  • Basic rule: \(r \propto \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\).
  • For equal volumes diffusing, rate ratio becomes inverse time ratio.
  • If pressure is also involved, use the PYQ-style proportionality \(r \propto \dfrac{P}{\sqrt{M}}\) only when the question explicitly compares rates under different pressures the way the source page does.
  • Fast memory: if one gas diffuses \(\sqrt{k}\) times faster, the other has \(k\) times the molar mass.

    Speed relations from kinetic theory
  • \(v_{rms}=\sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}}\), \(v_{mp}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2RT}{M}}\).
  • So \(v_{rms}\propto \sqrt{\dfrac{T}{M}}\).
  • For same gas, higher temperature means higher speed; for same temperature, lighter gas means higher speed.
  • Average kinetic energy per mole is \(\dfrac{1}{2}Mv_{rms}^2=\dfrac{3}{2}RT\).
  • If a question equates two different speed types, write both formulas first before substituting.

    Real gases and compressibility factor
  • \(Z=\dfrac{PV}{RT}\) for one mole.
  • \(Z=1\) for ideal gas.
  • \(Z<1\) means attractions dominate; \(Z>1\) means repulsions / excluded volume dominate.
  • At high pressure, PYQs repeatedly use \(Z=1+\dfrac{Pb}{RT}\).
  • Larger van der Waals constant \(a\) means stronger attractions and hence deeper negative deviation from ideality.

    Liquid-state crux: viscosity
  • Newton’s law of viscosity: \(F=\eta A\dfrac{du}{dz}\).
  • Force is directly proportional to viscosity, contact area, and velocity gradient.
  • This chapter’s liquid questions are usually direct formula recognition rather than long derivations.

    Question-to-formula map
  • Mixing / connected vessels \(\rightarrow\) \(n=\dfrac{PV}{RT}\) + Dalton’s law.
  • Density / composition \(\rightarrow\) \(d=\dfrac{PM}{RT}\).
  • Diffusion time / rate \(\rightarrow\) Graham’s law.
  • RMS / most probable speed \(\rightarrow\) kinetic-theory speed formulas.
  • Deviation from ideality \(\rightarrow\) compressibility factor \(Z\).
  • Liquid layer force \(\rightarrow\) viscosity formula.

    Common traps
  • Do not mix molecule-level formulas with mole-level formulas.
  • In gas-mixing questions, moles do not change on mixing; only volume available to each gas changes.
  • In density questions, always solve for average molar mass before jumping to composition.
  • Do not confuse \(v_{rms}\) with \(v_{mp}\); their constants are different.
  • For real gases, do not interpret \(Z<1\) and \(Z>1\) backward.

    Intermolecular forces and thermal energy
  • States of matter depend on the competition between intermolecular forces and thermal energy.
  • When thermal energy dominates strongly, particles behave more gas-like; when intermolecular attraction becomes important, liquid-like behavior appears.
  • This contrast is the base reason why gases are highly compressible but liquids are not.

    Gas laws and ideal gas equation
  • Boyle’s law: at constant temperature, \(PV=\text{constant}\).
  • Charles’ law: at constant pressure, \(V \propto T\).
  • Gay-Lussac’s law: at constant volume, \(P \propto T\).
  • Avogadro’s law: at constant \(T\) and \(P\), \(V \propto n\).
  • Combined form: \(PV=nRT\).
  • The ideal gas equation is the backbone for almost every calculation in this chapter.

    Dalton’s law and gaseous mixtures
  • For a mixture of non-reacting ideal gases, total pressure is the sum of partial pressures.
  • \(P_{total}=p_1+p_2+\cdots\)
  • \(p_i=x_iP_{total}\), where \(x_i\) is mole fraction of component \(i\).
  • This directly links composition, mole fraction, and partial pressure in connected-vessel and mixture problems.

    Graham’s law of diffusion
  • Graham’s law states that the rate of diffusion or effusion is inversely proportional to the square root of molar mass.
  • \(r \propto \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\)
  • Lighter gases diffuse faster; heavier gases diffuse slower.
  • For equal volumes, time taken is inversely related to the rate.

    Kinetic molecular theory and molecular speeds
  • Kinetic theory assumes gas molecules are in continuous random motion and undergo elastic collisions.
  • Average kinetic energy depends only on temperature.
  • \(\dfrac{1}{2}Mv_{rms}^2=\dfrac{3}{2}RT\)
  • Main speed formulas:
    • \(v_{rms}=\sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}}\)
    • \(v_{mp}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2RT}{M}}\)
  • So \(v_{rms} > v_{avg} > v_{mp}\).

    Density and average molar mass of gases
  • For an ideal gas, density is related to molar mass by \(d=\dfrac{PM}{RT}\).
  • This is especially useful for finding the molar mass of a gas or the average molar mass of a mixture.
  • Once average molar mass is found, composition can be obtained using mole fractions or mass percentages.

    Real gases and compressibility factor
  • Real gases deviate from ideal behavior because molecules have finite size and intermolecular attractions.
  • The compressibility factor is \(Z=\dfrac{PV}{RT}\) for one mole.
  • \(Z=1\) for ideal gas, \(Z<1\) when attractions dominate, and \(Z>1\) when repulsions / excluded volume dominate.
  • At high pressure, excluded-volume effects become important and \(Z\) becomes greater than 1.

    van der Waals picture of real gases
  • The van der Waals equation introduces two corrections:
    • \(a\) for intermolecular attraction
    • \(b\) for finite molecular volume
  • Larger \(a\) means stronger attractions.
  • Larger \(b\) means larger excluded volume.
  • At high pressure, PYQs often reduce the behavior to \(Z=1+\dfrac{Pb}{RT}\).

    Liquefaction of gases
  • Liquefaction becomes easier when temperature is lowered and pressure is increased.
  • Real-gas behavior and intermolecular attractions explain why sufficiently cooled and compressed gases can condense.
  • Conceptually, stronger intermolecular attractions favor liquefaction.

    Liquid state: vapour pressure, viscosity, and surface tension
  • Vapour pressure is the pressure exerted by vapour in equilibrium with its liquid.
  • Viscosity is the resistance offered by a liquid to flow.
  • Newton’s law of viscosity: \(F=\eta A\dfrac{du}{dz}\).
  • Surface tension arises because molecules at the surface experience net inward attraction.
  • In this syllabus, viscosity and surface tension are treated qualitatively, while the PYQs may still test the standard viscosity formula directly.

    How to solve this chapter quickly
  • If the question mentions mixture or connected vessels, think Dalton’s law plus \(PV=nRT\).
  • If density appears, think \(d=\dfrac{PM}{RT}\) first.
  • If diffusion or time appears, switch immediately to Graham’s law.
  • If RMS / most probable / kinetic energy appears, use kinetic-theory speed formulas.
  • If the question asks about ideal vs real behavior, move to \(Z\), \(a\), and \(b\).

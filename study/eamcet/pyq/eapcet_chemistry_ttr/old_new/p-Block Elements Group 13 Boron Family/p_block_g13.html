p-Block Elements – Group 13 (Boron Family)

    Group 13 trend map
  • Elements: \(B, Al, Ga, In, Tl\).
  • Valence shell configuration: \(ns^2 np^1\).
  • Common oxidation states: \(+3\), and \(+1\) becomes more stable down the group due to inert pair effect.
  • Standard trend question: stability of \(+1\) oxidation state increases as \(Tl > In > Ga > Al > B\).
  • Boron is strongly anomalous because of very small size, high ionization enthalpy, and absence of \(d\)-orbitals.

    Atomic size and shielding exception
  • Down the group, size generally increases, but Ga is smaller than expected.
  • Poor shielding by \(3d\)-electrons causes contraction in gallium.
  • Important memory order from live PYQ: \(Tl > In > Al > Ga\).
  • Also remember the comparison: \(Ga < Al < In\).

    Boron: anomalous behavior
  • Boron is a non-metal with strong covalent bonding and high melting point.
  • It usually shows covalency 3, but maximum covalency 4 is possible in species such as \(\mathrm{BF_4^-}\).
  • Boron does not form \(B^{3+}\) easily; its compounds are mainly covalent.
  • Boron compounds often act as Lewis acids because boron is electron-deficient.

    Diborane and boron compounds
  • \(\mathrm{B_2H_6}\) is a classic electron-deficient hydride, not electron-precise.
  • It contains three-center two-electron \((3c\text{-}2e)\) bonds, called banana bonds.
  • Diborane can be prepared by oxidation of sodium borohydride with iodine:
  • \(\mathrm{2NaBH_4 + I_2 \rightarrow B_2H_6 + 2NaI + H_2}\)
  • Hydrolysis of diborane gives boric acid.

    Boric acid and borax
  • Boric acid, \(\mathrm{H_3BO_3}\), is monobasic, not tribasic.
  • It acts as a Lewis acid by accepting \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) from water.
  • Key equation: \(\mathrm{B(OH)_3 + 2H_2O \rightleftharpoons [B(OH)_4]^- + H_3O^+}\)
  • In borax bead test, cobalt gives a blue bead due to cobalt metaborate.

    Aluminium: important direct facts
  • Aluminium is passive to concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) because of protective oxide layer.
  • Aluminium is amphoteric and reacts with aqueous \(\mathrm{NaOH}\) to liberate hydrogen gas.
  • \(\mathrm{2Al + 2NaOH + 6H_2O \rightarrow 2Na[Al(OH)_4] + 3H_2}\)
  • Hydrolysis of trihalides increases coordination and gives tetrahedral species in the asked PYQ pattern.

  1. General introduction and electronic configuration
    • Group 13 elements are \(B, Al, Ga, In,\) and \(Tl\).
    • Their general valence shell configuration is \(ns^2 np^1\).
    • They commonly show oxidation state \(+3\), while \(+1\) becomes increasingly important down the group.
    • The gradual stabilization of \(+1\) state down the group is due to inert pair effect.
  2. Atomic radii, shielding, and periodic exceptions
    • Atomic radius generally increases down the group because new shells are added.
    • However, gallium shows contraction because of poor shielding by inner \(d\)-electrons.
    • This leads to the well-known exception \(Ga < Al\).
    • A high-yield order is: \(\mathrm{Tl > In > Al > Ga}\).
    • Another useful increasing pattern is: \(\mathrm{Ga < Al < In < Tl}\) with the \(Ga/Al\) reversal remembered clearly.
  3. Oxidation states and inert pair effect
    • The \(+3\) oxidation state is common throughout the group, but its stability decreases down the group.
    • The \(+1\) oxidation state becomes more stable from \(Ga\) to \(In\) to \(Tl\).
    • This trend is explained by the inert pair effect, where the \(ns^2\) electrons are less willing to participate in bonding.
    • Thus, \(Tl(I)\) compounds are especially stable compared with \(Tl(III)\) compounds.
  4. Anomalous properties of boron
    • Boron is anomalous because of its very small size, high ionization enthalpy, and absence of vacant \(d\)-orbitals.
    • It is a non-metal, unlike the other members which are metals.
    • Its compounds are mainly covalent.
    • Boron has very high melting and boiling points because of its strong covalent network nature.
    • It usually forms compounds with covalency \(3\), but can show maximum covalency \(4\) in species like \(\mathrm{BF_4^-}\).
  5. Trihalides and hydrolysis
    • Group 13 forms trihalides of the type \(\mathrm{MX_3}\).
    • \(\mathrm{TlI_3}\) is not stable because thallium prefers the \(+1\) oxidation state.
    • Many trihalides are electron-deficient and act as Lewis acids.
    • On hydrolysis, the coordination number around the central atom increases and tetrahedral species can form.
  6. Borax and borax bead test
    • Borax is an important boron compound frequently tested for composition and uses.
    • In borax bead test, borax forms a glassy bead on heating.
    • Transition-metal salts give characteristic colours with this bead.
    • Cobalt gives a blue bead due to cobalt metaborate formation.
  7. Orthoboric acid
    • Orthoboric acid is \(\mathrm{H_3BO_3}\).
    • It is not tribasic; it is monobasic.
    • It acts as a Lewis acid by accepting \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) from water instead of donating protons directly.
    • Important equilibrium: \(\mathrm{B(OH)_3 + 2H_2O \rightleftharpoons [B(OH)_4]^- + H_3O^+}\)
  8. Diborane
    • Diborane is \(\mathrm{B_2H_6}\).
    • It is an electron-deficient hydride, not an electron-precise hydride.
    • It contains three-center two-electron \((3c\text{-}2e)\) bonds, often called banana bonds.
    • Diborane can be prepared by oxidation of sodium borohydride with iodine:
    • \(\mathrm{2NaBH_4 + I_2 \rightarrow B_2H_6 + 2NaI + H_2}\)
    • Hydrolysis of diborane gives boric acid.
  9. Uses of boron, aluminium, and their compounds
    • Boron and boron compounds are used in borosilicate glass, detergents, and laboratory reagents.
    • Aluminium is light, corrosion-resistant, and widely used in structural materials and utensils.
    • Aluminium becomes passive in concentrated \(\mathrm{HNO_3}\) because of a protective oxide film.
    • Aluminium is amphoteric and reacts with aqueous \(\mathrm{NaOH}\) to liberate hydrogen gas: \(\mathrm{2Al + 2NaOH + 6H_2O \rightarrow 2Na[Al(OH)_4] + 3H_2}\)
  10. Quick exam-oriented takeaways
    • Watch for three repeated Group 13 patterns: inert pair effect, boron anomaly, and the \(Ga/Al\) radius exception.
    • If diborane appears, check for electron deficiency and banana bonds.
    • If boric acid appears, remember “monobasic Lewis acid”, not “tribasic acid”.
    • If aluminium with \(\mathrm{NaOH}\) appears, think “aluminate + hydrogen gas”.

STATES OF MATTER, STOICHIOMETRY, THERMODYNAMICS

    1. Ideal gas equation and comparison form

      \( pV=nRT \)

      \( n=\dfrac{pV}{RT} \)

      For the same gas, \( w\propto\dfrac{pV}{T} \).

      For fixed \(T\) and \(V\), \( p\propto\dfrac{w}{M} \).

      Trap: compare moles using \(pV/RT\), not just pressure or volume separately.

    2. Combined gas law

      \( \dfrac{p_1V_1}{T_1}=\dfrac{p_2V_2}{T_2} \)

      Use this only for a fixed amount of gas.

      Trap: always convert temperature to Kelvin before substitution.

    3. Open vessel heating rule

      \( pV=nRT \)

      At constant \(p\) and \(V\), \( n\propto\dfrac{1}{T} \).

      \( \dfrac{n_2}{n_1}=\dfrac{T_1}{T_2} \)

      Trap: in an open vessel, gas escapes; moles are not constant.

    4. Dalton's law for gas mixtures

      \( p_{\text{total}}=p_1+p_2+p_3+\cdots \)

      \( p_i=x_i p_{\text{total}} \)

      \( x_i=\dfrac{n_i}{n_{\text{total}}} \)

      Trap: partial pressures must add up to total pressure.

    5. Mixing gases in connected vessels

      \( p_i=\dfrac{n_iRT}{V_{\text{total}}} \)

      Fast form at same temperature: \( p_i=\dfrac{P_iV_i}{V_{\text{total}}} \).

      After opening the stopcock, each gas occupies the total volume.

      Trap: do not keep each gas in its original vessel volume after mixing.

    6. Mole fraction from initial gas data

      \( x_i=\dfrac{n_i}{n_{\text{total}}}=\dfrac{p_iV_i}{\sum pV} \)

      This shortcut works when gases are initially at the same temperature.

      Trap: for mole fraction, compare moles first; do not use final total volume blindly.

    7. Average molar mass of gas mixture

      \( M_{\text{avg}}=x_1M_1+x_2M_2+\cdots \)

      From density: \( M=\dfrac{dRT}{p} \).

      For mass percent: \( \%A=\dfrac{x_AM_A}{\sum x_iM_i}\times100 \).

      Trap: mole percent and mass percent are not the same unless molar masses are equal.

    8. Graham's law of diffusion

      \( r\propto\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{M}} \)

      \( \dfrac{r_1}{r_2}=\sqrt{\dfrac{M_2}{M_1}} \)

      For equal diffused volume: \( \dfrac{t_1}{t_2}=\sqrt{\dfrac{M_1}{M_2}} \).

      Trap: rate is inversely proportional to time for the same diffused volume.

    9. Diffusion when pressure also changes

      \( r\propto\dfrac{p}{\sqrt{M}} \)

      \( \dfrac{r_1}{r_2}=\dfrac{p_1}{p_2}\sqrt{\dfrac{M_2}{M_1}} \)

      Trap: if pressures are different, do not use only molar mass unless the answer key explicitly ignores pressure.

    10. RMS, average and most probable speeds

      \( v_{\mathrm{rms}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}} \)

      \( v_{\mathrm{mp}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2RT}{M}} \)

      \( v_{\mathrm{avg}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{8RT}{\pi M}} \)

      \( v_{\mathrm{rms}}\propto\sqrt{\dfrac{T}{M}} \)

      Trap: square the speed ratio before comparing \(T/M\).

    11. Kinetic energy of gas molecules

      \( E=\dfrac{1}{2}Mv_{\mathrm{rms}}^2 \)

      For one mole: \( E=\dfrac{3}{2}RT \).

      Average kinetic energy per molecule: \( \dfrac{3}{2}kT \).

      Trap: use \(M\) in \(\mathrm{kg\,mol^{-1}}\), not \(\mathrm{g\,mol^{-1}}\), when calculating joules per mole.

    12. Gas-law graph slopes

      For \(V\) vs \(T\) at constant pressure: \( V=\dfrac{nR}{p}T \).

      Slope \(=\dfrac{nR}{p}\), so slope \( \propto\dfrac{1}{p} \).

      For \(V\) vs \(n\) at constant \(T,p\): slope \(=\dfrac{RT}{p}\).

      Trap: steeper \(V-T\) line means lower pressure.

    13. Compressibility factor

      \( Z=\dfrac{pV}{nRT} \)

      Ideal gas: \( Z=1 \).

      At moderate pressure, attractions dominate and \( Z\lt1 \).

      At high pressure, molecular volume/repulsions dominate and \( Z\gt1 \).

      Trap: stronger attraction gives a deeper dip below \(1\).

    14. van der Waals equation high-pressure shortcut

      \( \left(p+\dfrac{a}{V^2}\right)(V-b)=RT \)

      At very high pressure, attraction term is neglected: \( p(V-b)=RT \).

      \( V=\dfrac{RT}{p}+b \)

      \( Z=1+\dfrac{pb}{RT} \), so \( Z\gt1 \).

    15. van der Waals constant \(a\)

      \( \text{larger }a\Rightarrow\text{ stronger intermolecular attraction} \)

      \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has large \(a\) because of strong intermolecular attraction/hydrogen bonding.

      \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) also shows stronger attraction than \(\mathrm{N_2}\).

      Trap: lower \(Z\) at moderate pressure usually indicates stronger attractions.

    16. Gaseous hydrocarbon combustion by volume ratio

      CxHy + (x + y/4)O2 → xCO2

      At same \(T,p\), gaseous volumes are proportional to mole ratios.

      If hydrocarbon : \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) : \(\mathrm{H_2O}=1:x:\dfrac{y}{2}\), identify \(x\) and \(y\).

      Trap: use vapour volume ratios only when all species are treated as gases.

    17. Newton's law of viscosity

      \( F=\eta A\dfrac{du}{dz} \)

      Force is proportional to area of contact and velocity gradient.

      \(\eta\) is the coefficient of viscosity.

    18. Kinetic molecular theory standard traps

      Gas molecules are treated as point particles with negligible volume in ideal gas theory.

      Collisions are perfectly elastic.

      Pressure is due to collisions with container walls.

      Trap: molecules do not all have the same speed at a given instant.

    1. Hess's law and thermochemical equations

      \( \Delta H_{\text{target}}=\sum \Delta H_{\text{steps}} \)

      Reverse an equation \(\Rightarrow\) change sign of \(\Delta H\).

      Multiply an equation \(\Rightarrow\) multiply \(\Delta H\).

      Add equations \(\Rightarrow\) add enthalpies.

      Trap: cancel species first, then add enthalpies; extra given equations may be unnecessary.

    2. Reaction enthalpy from enthalpy of formation

      \( \Delta_rH^\circ=\sum \nu\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \nu\Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants}) \)

      \( \Delta_fH^\circ=0 \) for an element in its standard state.

      Example: \(\Delta_fH^\circ(\mathrm{O_2})=0\).

      Trap: multiply by stoichiometric coefficients; formation enthalpy is for \(1\) mole of compound.

    3. Combustion enthalpy from formation enthalpies

      \( \Delta_cH^\circ=\sum \Delta_fH^\circ(\text{products})-\sum \Delta_fH^\circ(\text{reactants}) \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{CO+\dfrac{1}{2}O_2\rightarrow CO_2} \)

      \( \Delta_cH^\circ(\mathrm{CO})=\Delta_fH^\circ(\mathrm{CO_2})-\Delta_fH^\circ(\mathrm{CO}) \)

      Trap: combustion enthalpy is usually negative; \(\mathrm{O_2}\) contributes zero.

    4. Bond enthalpy formula

      \( \Delta H=\sum E(\text{bonds broken})-\sum E(\text{bonds formed}) \)

      Bonds broken absorb energy; bonds formed release energy.

      Trap: count every bond using the balanced reaction; \(\dfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{O_2}\) means half an \(\mathrm{O{=}O}\) bond contribution.

    5. Reversible isothermal work of ideal gas

      \( w=-nRT\ln\left(\dfrac{V_2}{V_1}\right) \)

      \( w=-nRT\ln\left(\dfrac{p_1}{p_2}\right) \)

      Expansion gives negative work; compression gives positive work.

      Trap: use natural log, not \(\log_{10}\); convert J to kJ only if needed.

    6. Irreversible work against constant external pressure

      \( w=-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V \)

      \( \Delta V=V_2-V_1 \)

      For compression, \(\Delta V<0\), so \(w>0\).

      Trap: if constant external pressure is given, do not use the reversible logarithmic formula.

    7. Expansion work from gaseous mole change

      \( w=-P\Delta V \)

      For ideal gas formation at constant pressure: \( w=-\Delta n_gRT \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{2Na+2H_2O\rightarrow2NaOH+H_2} \)

      Trap: first balance the reaction and find \(\Delta n_g\); only gaseous moles count.

    8. Work as area under \(P\)-\(V\) curve

      \( W=\int P\,dV \)

      Work depends on path, so it is not a state function.

      \(\Delta U\) depends only on initial and final states.

      Trap: higher \(P\)-\(V\) path gives larger area and greater work magnitude.

    9. Constant-pressure heat and enthalpy

      \( q_p=nC_p\Delta T \)

      At constant pressure: \( \Delta H=q_p \)

      For \(1\) mole and \(\Delta T=1\,\mathrm{K}\), \(q_p=C_p\).

      Trap: do not confuse \(C_p\) with \(C_v\).

    10. Relation between enthalpy and internal energy

      \( \Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT \)

      If no gaseous mole change occurs, \(\Delta n_g=0\), so \(\Delta H=\Delta U\).

      Trap: do not add \(RT\) correction when the reaction has no gaseous species or no gaseous mole change.

    11. Gibbs energy and spontaneity

      \( \Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S \)

      Spontaneous forward reaction: \(\Delta G<0\).

      Non-spontaneous forward reaction: \(\Delta G>0\).

      Equilibrium: \(\Delta G=0\).

      Trap: convert \(\Delta H\) and \(T\Delta S\) into the same units before subtracting.

    12. Temperature dependence of spontaneity

      At the changeover point: \( \Delta G=0 \).

      \( T=\dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S} \)

      If \(\Delta H<0\) and \(\Delta S<0\), the forward reaction is favoured at low temperature.

      If \(\Delta H>0\) and \(\Delta S>0\), the forward reaction is favoured at high temperature.

      Trap: convert \(\Delta H\) from kJ to J if \(\Delta S\) is in \(\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}\).

    13. Standard Gibbs energy and equilibrium constant

      \( \Delta_rG^\circ=-RT\ln K \)

      \( \Delta_rG^\circ=-2.303RT\log_{10}K \)

      \( \log_{10}K=\dfrac{-\Delta_rG^\circ}{2.303RT} \)

      Trap: negative \(\Delta_rG^\circ\) gives positive \(\log K\), so products are strongly favoured.

    14. Calculating \(\Delta G\) from \(\Delta U\), \(\Delta n_g\) and \(\Delta S\)

      \( \Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT \)

      \( \Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S \)

      First find \(\Delta n_g\), then calculate \(\Delta H\), then calculate \(\Delta G\).

      Trap: keep all energy units consistent, such as all in cal or all in J.

    15. Reversible and irreversible processes

      Phase change at transition point is reversible.

      Vaporisation at boiling point and melting at melting point are reversible ideal cases.

      Free expansion into vacuum and neutralisation are irreversible.

      Trap: slow process is not automatically reversible; the system must pass through equilibrium states.

    16. Intensive and extensive properties

      Intensive: temperature, density, molar volume.

      Extensive: mass, volume, internal energy, enthalpy.

      Trap: molar volume is intensive even though volume itself is extensive.

    17. Standard enthalpy of formation of carbon allotropes

      \( \Delta_fH^\circ=0 \) for the most stable standard state of an element.

      For carbon, graphite is the standard state.

      \( \Delta_fH^\circ \) order: fullerene \(>\) diamond \(>\) graphite.

      Trap: diamond is familiar but graphite is the standard and most stable form.

    18. Adiabatic expansion relation

      \( PV^\gamma=\text{constant} \)

      For small changes: \( \dfrac{\Delta P}{P}\approx-\gamma\dfrac{\Delta V}{V} \)

      During expansion, pressure decreases.

      Trap: if volume increases by \(5\%\) and \(\gamma=1.4\), pressure decreases by about \(7\%\).

    19. Heterogeneous equilibrium and catalyst trap

      Pure solids are omitted from equilibrium expressions.

      For \( \mathrm{A(s)\rightleftharpoons B(s)+C(g)} \), \(K_p=p_C\).

      A catalyst changes rate, not \(\Delta H\), \(\Delta G^\circ\), or \(K\).

      Trap: catalyst affects how fast equilibrium is reached, not the equilibrium position.

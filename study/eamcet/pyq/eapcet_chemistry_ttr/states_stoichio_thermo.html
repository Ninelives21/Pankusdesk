STATES OF MATTER, STOICHIOMETRY, THERMODYNAMICS

    1. Ideal gas equation and comparison form

      \( pV=nRT \)

      \( n=\dfrac{pV}{RT} \)

      For the same gas, \( w\propto\dfrac{pV}{T} \).

      For fixed \(T\) and \(V\), \( p\propto\dfrac{w}{M} \).

      Trap: compare moles using \(pV/RT\), not just pressure or volume separately.

    2. Combined gas law

      \( \dfrac{p_1V_1}{T_1}=\dfrac{p_2V_2}{T_2} \)

      Use this only for a fixed amount of gas.

      Trap: always convert temperature to Kelvin before substitution.

    3. Open vessel heating rule

      \( pV=nRT \)

      At constant \(p\) and \(V\), \( n\propto\dfrac{1}{T} \).

      \( \dfrac{n_2}{n_1}=\dfrac{T_1}{T_2} \)

      Trap: in an open vessel, gas escapes; moles are not constant.

    4. Dalton's law for gas mixtures

      \( p_{\text{total}}=p_1+p_2+p_3+\cdots \)

      \( p_i=x_i p_{\text{total}} \)

      \( x_i=\dfrac{n_i}{n_{\text{total}}} \)

      Trap: partial pressures must add up to total pressure.

    5. Mixing gases in connected vessels

      \( p_i=\dfrac{n_iRT}{V_{\text{total}}} \)

      Fast form at same temperature: \( p_i=\dfrac{P_iV_i}{V_{\text{total}}} \).

      After opening the stopcock, each gas occupies the total volume.

      Trap: do not keep each gas in its original vessel volume after mixing.

    6. Mole fraction from initial gas data

      \( x_i=\dfrac{n_i}{n_{\text{total}}}=\dfrac{p_iV_i}{\sum pV} \)

      This shortcut works when gases are initially at the same temperature.

      Trap: for mole fraction, compare moles first; do not use final total volume blindly.

    7. Average molar mass of gas mixture

      \( M_{\text{avg}}=x_1M_1+x_2M_2+\cdots \)

      From density: \( M=\dfrac{dRT}{p} \).

      For mass percent: \( \%A=\dfrac{x_AM_A}{\sum x_iM_i}\times100 \).

      Trap: mole percent and mass percent are not the same unless molar masses are equal.

    8. Graham's law of diffusion

      \( r\propto\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{M}} \)

      \( \dfrac{r_1}{r_2}=\sqrt{\dfrac{M_2}{M_1}} \)

      For equal diffused volume: \( \dfrac{t_1}{t_2}=\sqrt{\dfrac{M_1}{M_2}} \).

      Trap: rate is inversely proportional to time for the same diffused volume.

    9. Diffusion when pressure also changes

      \( r\propto\dfrac{p}{\sqrt{M}} \)

      \( \dfrac{r_1}{r_2}=\dfrac{p_1}{p_2}\sqrt{\dfrac{M_2}{M_1}} \)

      Trap: if pressures are different, do not use only molar mass unless the answer key explicitly ignores pressure.

    10. RMS, average and most probable speeds

      \( v_{\mathrm{rms}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}} \)

      \( v_{\mathrm{mp}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2RT}{M}} \)

      \( v_{\mathrm{avg}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{8RT}{\pi M}} \)

      \( v_{\mathrm{rms}}\propto\sqrt{\dfrac{T}{M}} \)

      Trap: square the speed ratio before comparing \(T/M\).

    11. Kinetic energy of gas molecules

      \( E=\dfrac{1}{2}Mv_{\mathrm{rms}}^2 \)

      For one mole: \( E=\dfrac{3}{2}RT \).

      Average kinetic energy per molecule: \( \dfrac{3}{2}kT \).

      Trap: use \(M\) in \(\mathrm{kg\,mol^{-1}}\), not \(\mathrm{g\,mol^{-1}}\), when calculating joules per mole.

    12. Gas-law graph slopes

      For \(V\) vs \(T\) at constant pressure: \( V=\dfrac{nR}{p}T \).

      Slope \(=\dfrac{nR}{p}\), so slope \( \propto\dfrac{1}{p} \).

      For \(V\) vs \(n\) at constant \(T,p\): slope \(=\dfrac{RT}{p}\).

      Trap: steeper \(V-T\) line means lower pressure.

    13. Compressibility factor

      \( Z=\dfrac{pV}{nRT} \)

      Ideal gas: \( Z=1 \).

      At moderate pressure, attractions dominate and \( Z\lt1 \).

      At high pressure, molecular volume/repulsions dominate and \( Z\gt1 \).

      Trap: stronger attraction gives a deeper dip below \(1\).

    14. van der Waals equation high-pressure shortcut

      \( \left(p+\dfrac{a}{V^2}\right)(V-b)=RT \)

      At very high pressure, attraction term is neglected: \( p(V-b)=RT \).

      \( V=\dfrac{RT}{p}+b \)

      \( Z=1+\dfrac{pb}{RT} \), so \( Z\gt1 \).

    15. van der Waals constant \(a\)

      \( \text{larger }a\Rightarrow\text{ stronger intermolecular attraction} \)

      \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) has large \(a\) because of strong intermolecular attraction/hydrogen bonding.

      \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) also shows stronger attraction than \(\mathrm{N_2}\).

      Trap: lower \(Z\) at moderate pressure usually indicates stronger attractions.

    16. Gaseous hydrocarbon combustion by volume ratio

      CxHy + (x + y/4)O2 → xCO2

      At same \(T,p\), gaseous volumes are proportional to mole ratios.

      If hydrocarbon : \(\mathrm{CO_2}\) : \(\mathrm{H_2O}=1:x:\dfrac{y}{2}\), identify \(x\) and \(y\).

      Trap: use vapour volume ratios only when all species are treated as gases.

    17. Newton's law of viscosity

      \( F=\eta A\dfrac{du}{dz} \)

      Force is proportional to area of contact and velocity gradient.

      \(\eta\) is the coefficient of viscosity.

    18. Kinetic molecular theory standard traps

      Gas molecules are treated as point particles with negligible volume in ideal gas theory.

      Collisions are perfectly elastic.

      Pressure is due to collisions with container walls.

      Trap: molecules do not all have the same speed at a given instant.

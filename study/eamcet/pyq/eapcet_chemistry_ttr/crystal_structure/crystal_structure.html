CRYSTAL STRUCTURE

  • Voids / Holes in crystal packing
    1. When atoms or ions are packed closely, some empty spaces remain between them. These empty spaces are called voids or holes.
    2. These holes are important because in ionic solids, usually one type of ion forms the main packing and the other type of ion occupies these holes.
  • Tetrahedral hole
    1. A tetrahedral hole is an empty space surrounded by 4 atoms.
    2. Easy picture:
      1. 3 atoms in one layer
      2. 1 atom above them
    3. The gap between them is a tetrahedral hole.
    4. It is called tetrahedral because the surrounding atoms form a tetrahedron.
  • Octahedral hole
    1. An octahedral hole is an empty space surrounded by 6 atoms.
    2. Easy picture:
      1. 3 atoms in one layer
      2. 3 atoms in another layer
    3. The gap between them is an octahedral hole.
    4. It is called octahedral because the surrounding atoms form an octahedron.
    5. It can be visualized as two square pyramids connected at their square bases.
  • Important rule for CCP/FCC packing
    1. If \(n\) atoms form ccp/fcc packing, then:
      1. \(\text{Tetrahedral holes} = 2n\)
      2. \(\text{Octahedral holes} = n\)
    2. So if packed atoms \(= 4\), then:
      1. tetrahedral holes \(= 8\)
      2. octahedral holes \(= 4\)
  • NaCl structure
    1. In NaCl:
      1. \(\text{Cl}^-\) ions form FCC/CCP lattice
      2. \(\text{Na}^+\) ions occupy all octahedral holes
    2. Coordination:
      1. each \(\text{Na}^+\) is surrounded by 6 \(\text{Cl}^-\)
      2. each \(\text{Cl}^-\) is surrounded by 6 \(\text{Na}^+\)
    3. So coordination number is \(6:6\).
    4. Correct counting in one unit cell:
      1. For \(\text{Cl}^-\) ions:
        1. 8 corner \(\text{Cl}^-\) \(\rightarrow 8 \times \frac{1}{8} = 1\)
        2. 6 face-center \(\text{Cl}^-\) \(\rightarrow 6 \times \frac{1}{2} = 3\)
        3. Total \(\text{Cl}^- = 1 + 3 = 4\)
      2. For \(\text{Na}^+\) ions:
        1. 12 edge-center \(\text{Na}^+\) \(\rightarrow 12 \times \frac{1}{4} = 3\)
        2. 1 body-center \(\text{Na}^+\) \(\rightarrow 1\)
        3. Total \(\text{Na}^+ = 3 + 1 = 4\)
      3. So \(\text{Na}^+ : \text{Cl}^- = 4:4 = 1:1\).
      4. That is why the formula is NaCl.
    5. Important note:
      1. In diagrams, it may look like there are unequal numbers of ions, but that is because the drawing is only a representation.
      2. Ions must be counted by fractional contribution, not by direct visual counting.
      3. Useful fractions:
        1. corner \(= \frac{1}{8}\)
        2. face center \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
        3. edge center \(= \frac{1}{4}\)
        4. body center / fully inside \(= 1\)

  • Last-minute memory points for unit cell numericals
    1. For density-based crystal questions, remember:
      1. \(\rho = \dfrac{Z \times M}{N_A \times a^3}\)
      2. \(M = \dfrac{\rho \times N_A \times a^3}{Z}\)
    2. Meaning of symbols:
      1. \(\rho\) = density
      2. \(M\) = molar mass
      3. \(N_A\) = Avogadro number
      4. \(a\) = edge length of unit cell
      5. \(Z\) = number of particles per unit cell
    3. Important \(Z\) values:
      1. simple cubic (sc) \(\rightarrow Z = 1\)
      2. body-centered cubic (bcc) \(\rightarrow Z = 2\)
      3. face-centered cubic (fcc/ccp) \(\rightarrow Z = 4\)
    4. How these values come:
      1. sc: \(8\) corners \(\times \frac{1}{8} = 1\)
      2. bcc: \(8\) corners \(\times \frac{1}{8} = 1\), plus \(1\) body-center atom \(= 2\)
      3. fcc: \(8\) corners \(\times \frac{1}{8} = 1\), plus \(6\) face-center atoms \(\times \frac{1}{2} = 3\), so total \(= 4\)
    5. Unit conversion to remember:
      1. \(1 \, \text{pm} = 10^{-12} \, \text{m}\)
      2. \(1 \, \text{m} = 100 \, \text{cm}\)
      3. So convert edge length into cm before using the formula.
    6. Very common step:
      1. First convert \(a\) into cm
      2. Then calculate \(a^3\)
      3. Then substitute in \(M = \dfrac{\rho \times N_A \times a^3}{Z}\)
  • Radius relations in cubic lattices
    1. In simple cubic (sc), atoms touch along the edge.
      1. \(a = 2r\)
      2. \(r = \dfrac{a}{2}\)
    2. In bcc, atoms touch along the body diagonal.
      1. \(\sqrt{3}a = 4r\)
      2. \(r = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}a}{4}\)
    3. In fcc, atoms touch along the face diagonal.
      1. \(\sqrt{2}a = 4r\)
      2. \(r = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}a}{4}\)
    4. Quick memory:
      1. sc \(\rightarrow\) edge
      2. bcc \(\rightarrow\) body diagonal
      3. fcc \(\rightarrow\) face diagonal

  • Schottky defect
    1. Schottky defect is due to missing of equal number of cations and anions from their lattice points.
    2. Because equal numbers are missing, the crystal remains electrically neutral.
    3. It is commonly shown by ionic compounds in which cation and anion are of almost the same size.
    4. Since ions are missing, the mass of the crystal decreases while the volume remains almost the same.
    5. So the density decreases.
    6. Important memory:
      1. equal number of ions missing
      2. neutrality maintained
      3. density decreases
  • Frenkel defect
    1. In Frenkel defect, an ion leaves its normal lattice site and occupies an interstitial site.
    2. It usually involves a smaller cation, because the smaller ion can fit into the interstitial space.
    3. One vacancy is created at the original site and one interstitial ion is created at a new site.
    4. The crystal remains electrically neutral.
    5. Since no ion is lost from the crystal, the mass remains almost the same.
    6. So the density remains nearly unchanged.
    7. It is also called a dislocation defect.
    8. Important memory:
      1. ion shifts, not lost
      2. vacancy + interstitial pair formed
      3. density almost unchanged
  • Schottky defect vs Frenkel defect
    1. Schottky defect:
      1. equal number of cations and anions are missing
      2. density decreases
      3. common when ions are of similar size
    2. Frenkel defect:
      1. usually a smaller cation leaves its site and occupies interstitial position
      2. density remains nearly same
      3. common when there is large size difference between ions

  • Question 141 - 2024, May 09 Shift 1

    \(AB_2O_4\)
    1. Given:
      1. oxygen forms ccp
      2. A occupies \(\frac{1}{8}\) of tetrahedral holes
      3. find fraction of vacant octahedral holes
  • Step-by-step idea
    1. The formula is \(AB_2O_4\).
    2. This means the ratio is:
      1. \(A : B : O = 1 : 2 : 4\)
    3. So take:
      1. \(A = 1\)
      2. \(B = 2\)
      3. \(O = 4\)
  • Since oxygen forms CCP
    1. If oxygen atoms \(= 4\), then:
      1. \(\text{Tetrahedral holes} = 8\)
      2. \(\text{Octahedral holes} = 4\)
    2. So now we have:
      1. 4 oxygen atoms
      2. 8 tetrahedral holes
      3. 4 octahedral holes
  • A occupies \(\frac{1}{8}\) of tetrahedral holes
    1. Total tetrahedral holes \(= 8\).
    2. So number of A atoms:
      1. \(\frac{1}{8} \times 8 = 1\)
    3. This matches the formula.
  • Now find B occupancy
    1. From the formula \(AB_2O_4\), when \(A = 1\) and \(O = 4\), then:
      1. \(B = 2\)
    2. Total octahedral holes \(= 4\).
    3. B atoms occupy 2 of them.
    4. So occupied octahedral fraction:
      1. \(\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\)
    5. Therefore vacant octahedral fraction:
      1. \(1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\)
    6. Final answer:
      1. \(\boxed{\frac{1}{2}}\)
  • Super-simple way to think of this question
    1. With 4 oxygen atoms:
      1. tetrahedral holes \(= 8\)
      2. octahedral holes \(= 4\)
    2. A fills \(\frac{1}{8}\) of 8 tetrahedral holes:
      1. so \(A = 1\)
    3. Formula says if \(A = 1\), then \(B = 2\).
    4. So B fills 2 out of 4 octahedral holes.
    5. Hence:
      1. occupied octahedral holes \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
      2. vacant octahedral holes \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
  • Final memory points
    1. Tetrahedral hole \(\rightarrow\) surrounded by 4 atoms
    2. Octahedral hole \(\rightarrow\) surrounded by 6 atoms
    3. In ccp/fcc, if atoms \(= n\):
      1. tetrahedral holes \(= 2n\)
      2. octahedral holes \(= n\)
    4. In NaCl:
      1. \(\text{Cl}^-\) forms fcc
      2. \(\text{Na}^+\) occupies all octahedral holes
      3. coordination number \(= 6:6\)
    5. In \(AB_2O_4\) with oxygen in ccp:
      1. 4 O gives 8 tetrahedral holes and 4 octahedral holes
      2. A occupies 1 tetrahedral hole
      3. B occupies 2 octahedral holes
      4. vacant octahedral holes \(= \frac{1}{2}\)

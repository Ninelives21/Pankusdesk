Surface Chemistry

    Exam map / pattern map
  • Surface Chemistry PYQs here cluster mainly into four buckets: adsorption, colloids, catalysis, and direct catalyst-memory facts.
  • If you see \( \log \frac{x}{m} \) vs \( \log p \), immediately switch to Freundlich log form.
  • If you see sol coagulation, immediately identify sol charge first, then apply Hardy–Schulze logic.
  • If you see Tyndall / Brownian / zeta / electrophoresis, think “property of colloids” rather than reaction chemistry.
  • If you see Fe / Mo / CO or Haber / Deacon / zeolite, treat it as fast catalyst-memory scoring.

    Adsorption: core formula and fast rules
  • Freundlich isotherm: \( \frac{x}{m} = kp^{1/n} \), with \( n>1 \).
  • Log form: \( \log \frac{x}{m} = \log k + \frac{1}{n}\log p \).
  • On a \( \log \frac{x}{m} \) vs \( \log p \) graph, slope \(=\frac{1}{n}\), intercept \(=\log k\).
  • If slope is \(0.5\), then \( \frac{x}{m}\propto p^{1/2} \).
  • If intercept is \(1\), then \(k=10\).
  • Gas adsorption generally increases with pressure and decreases with temperature for physisorption.
  • Physical adsorption is favored by high surface area, low temperature, high pressure.
  • Fast trap: “adsorption is more at high temperature” is usually false for physisorption questions.

    Colloids: recognition, classification, and property triggers
  • Tyndall effect = scattering of light by colloidal particles; use it to distinguish colloid from true solution.
  • Brownian motion = zig-zag random motion; it helps stabilize sols against settling.
  • Higher viscosity slows Brownian motion, so “very high viscosity makes it faster” is false.
  • Zeta potential is tied to charge behavior and motion of colloidal particles.
  • Remember standard matches: sol–paint, foam–whipped cream, gel–butter, aerosol–cloud.
  • If the example is gas dispersed in liquid/solid, check foam; if liquid/solid dispersed in gas, think aerosol.

    Charge / coagulation / counter-ion shortcut
  • First identify whether the sol is positively or negatively charged.
  • \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\) sol is negatively charged, so cations cause coagulation.
  • Hardy–Schulze rule: greater the valency of oppositely charged ion, stronger the coagulating power.
  • So for a negative sol, \( \mathrm{Al^{3+}} \gt \mathrm{Ca^{2+}} \gt \mathrm{NH_4^+} \) in coagulating power.
  • Fast mapping: same-sign ions do not coagulate effectively; look only at counter-ions.

    Catalysis / promoter / poison memory map
  • Haber process trio: catalyst \(=\mathrm{Fe}\), promoter \(=\mathrm{Mo}\), poison \(=\mathrm{CO}\).
  • Deacon process catalyst: \( \mathrm{CuCl_2} \).
  • Cracking catalyst: zeolites.
  • Catalyst poison blocks active surface sites, so reactants cannot adsorb properly.
  • Shape-selective catalysis is linked with zeolites.
  • If the question asks industrial catalyst matching, treat it as instant-recall scoring, not derivation.

    Enzyme and catalyst one-line reminders
  • Malt contains diastase.
  • Diastase converts starch into maltose.
  • For \(CO + H_2 \to HCHO\), copper is the standard catalyst asked here.
  • For \(CO + 3H_2 \to CH_4 + H_2O\), nickel is the catalyst asked here.

    Common traps / mistakes
  • Do not confuse adsorption with absorption; surface vs bulk is the first check.
  • Do not forget that in Freundlich questions, graph slope is \(1/n\), not \(n\).
  • Do not pick coagulating agent by formula size; pick by opposite charge and valency.
  • Do not treat Tyndall effect as a property of true solutions.
  • Do not ignore viscosity while judging Brownian motion speed.
  • For catalyst-memory questions, poison is the blocker, promoter is the helper.

    Adsorption and absorption
  • Adsorption is accumulation of particles only on the surface; absorption is entry into the bulk.
  • Adsorption is a surface phenomenon, so larger surface area gives stronger effect.
  • Main driving cause in physisorption is weak van der Waals attraction.
  • Chemisorption involves stronger bond formation between adsorbate and surface.
  • Physisorption is usually favored by low temperature and high pressure for gases.
  • Chemisorption is usually more specific and may require activation energy.

    Adsorption isotherms and how to use them
  • Freundlich isotherm: \( \frac{x}{m} = kp^{1/n} \), valid over a limited pressure range.
  • Log form: \( \log \frac{x}{m} = \log k + \frac{1}{n}\log p \).
  • Slope of log-plot gives \(1/n\); intercept gives \(\log k\).
  • If the graph question gives slope only, convert it immediately into pressure exponent.
  • If the graph gives intercept, first recover \(k\), then substitute pressure.
  • Typical exam uses: identify wrong statement, read graph slope, compute \(x/m\), infer pressure dependence.

    Catalysis
  • A catalyst changes reaction rate without permanent consumption in the overall reaction.
  • In heterogeneous catalysis, adsorption of reactants on the catalyst surface is the central idea.
  • Adsorption weakens old bonds, brings reactants together, lowers effective activation barrier, then products desorb.
  • A promoter increases catalyst efficiency but is not itself the main catalyst.
  • A poison reduces catalytic activity by occupying active sites strongly.
  • Industrial-memory facts repeatedly asked: Haber–Fe, Deacon–\(CuCl_2\), cracking–zeolites.
  • Shape-selective catalysis is associated with zeolites because pore size controls which molecules react.

    Enzyme catalysis
  • Enzymes are highly selective biological catalysts.
  • They usually work under mild conditions of temperature and pH.
  • Substrate fits into the active site, forming an enzyme–substrate complex.
  • This lowers activation energy and gives specific product formation.
  • Important direct fact from these PYQs: malt contains diastase, and diastase converts starch into maltose.

    Colloids: classification
  • Colloids have dispersed particles intermediate between true solutions and suspensions.
  • By physical states, common classes include sol, gel, foam, emulsion, and aerosol.
  • Fast examples: paint is a sol, whipped cream is a foam, butter is a gel/emulsion-type everyday colloid example, cloud is an aerosol.
  • By interaction with medium, colloids are lyophilic or lyophobic.
  • Lyophobic sols are less stable and are more easily coagulated by electrolytes.

    Properties of colloidal solutions
  • Tyndall effect is scattering of light by colloidal particles; true solutions do not show it appreciably.
  • Brownian movement is continuous random motion due to bombardment by medium molecules.
  • Brownian movement helps keep particles suspended and thus stabilizes sols.
  • It becomes less pronounced when viscosity is high.
  • Colloidal particles often carry charge; this contributes to stability by mutual repulsion.
  • Electrophoresis is motion of colloidal particles under electric field.
  • Zeta potential is the practical charge-related measure often used while discussing colloidal stability.

    Coagulation and Hardy–Schulze rule
  • Coagulation means destruction of colloidal stability so particles aggregate and settle.
  • Electrolytes coagulate lyophobic sols by neutralizing their charge.
  • The effective ion is the ion with charge opposite to the sol particles.
  • Hardy–Schulze rule: coagulating power increases sharply with valency of the counter-ion.
  • Thus a trivalent counter-ion is far more effective than a divalent or monovalent one.
  • For negatively charged \(\mathrm{Sb_2S_3}\) sol, \( \mathrm{Al^{3+}} \) salts are especially effective.

    Preparation, purification, and related ideas
  • Colloids can be prepared by dispersion methods or condensation methods.
  • Purification methods include dialysis, electrodialysis, and ultrafiltration.
  • Peptization converts a precipitate into colloidal state using a suitable electrolyte.
  • These ideas appear less often than adsorption and colloid-property questions, but remain part of the syllabus.

    Emulsions and applications
  • Emulsions are liquid-in-liquid colloids with one immiscible liquid dispersed in another.
  • They are stabilized by emulsifying agents such as soaps and detergents.
  • Cleansing action of soap is due to micelle formation.
  • Applications of colloids and adsorption include purification, catalysis, medicine, food systems, and pollution control.

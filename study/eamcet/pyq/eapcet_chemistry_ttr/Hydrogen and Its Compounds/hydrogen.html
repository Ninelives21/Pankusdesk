Hydrogen and Its Compounds

    Hydrides: exam-classification map
  • Ionic / saline hydrides are formed mainly by alkali metals and heavier alkaline earth metals; they contain \(H^-\).
  • Covalent hydrides may be electron-deficient, electron-precise, or electron-rich.
  • Electron-rich hydrides have lone pairs on the central atom; common examples: \(HCl,\ H_2S,\ NH_3,\ H_2O\).
  • Electron-deficient hydrides have fewer electrons than needed for normal two-centre two-electron bonds; example: \(B_2H_6\).
  • Polymeric hydrides repeatedly asked: \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\).
  • \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\) also show significant covalent character.

    Ionic hydrides: one-step recall
  • Molten ionic hydrides contain \(H^-\), so on electrolysis oxidation occurs at anode.
  • Anode process: \(2H^- \rightarrow H_2 + 2e^-\).
  • Fast trap: hydrogen is liberated at the anode, not at the cathode.
  • Stability trend of alkali metal hydrides increases down the group: \(LiH < NaH < KH < RbH < CsH\).

    Water and heavy water: direct-fact cluster
  • Heavy water is \(D_2O\).
  • Freezing point of \(D_2O\) is about \(3.8^\circ C\).
  • Temperature of maximum density: \(H_2O \approx 4^\circ C\), \(D_2O \approx 11.2^\circ C\).
  • So \((x-y)\) for maximum-density temperatures of \(D_2O\) and \(H_2O\) is nearly \(7\ \mathrm{K}\).
  • Reaction memory: \(CaC_2 + 2D_2O \rightarrow C_2D_2 + Ca(OD)_2\).

    Hardness of water: methods and mappings
  • Temporary hardness due to bicarbonates can be removed by boiling.
  • \(Mg(HCO_3)_2\) on boiling gives \(Mg(OH)_2\) precipitate.
  • \(Ca(HCO_3)_2\) on boiling gives \(CaCO_3\) precipitate.
  • Clark’s method uses \(Ca(OH)_2\) to remove bicarbonate hardness.
  • Calgon method complexes \(Ca^{2+}\) / \(Mg^{2+}\) ions and keeps them in solution.
  • Ion exchange / zeolite methods replace hardness-causing ions by \(Na^+\) or \(H^+\).
  • Deionised water needs both cationic and anionic exchangers, one after the other.

    \(H_2O_2\): structure, storage, and numerical shortcuts
  • \(H_2O_2\) is non-planar.
  • Dihedral angle is about \(111.5^\circ\) in gaseous phase and \(90.2^\circ\) in solid phase.
  • Store \(H_2O_2\) in a wax-lined container and keep it in the dark.
  • It decomposes in light and in presence of alkali, rough surfaces, or metal impurities.
  • \(2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2\).
  • \(x\)-volume means \(1\) volume of solution liberates \(x\) volumes of \(O_2\) at STP/NTP.
  • Useful shortcut: \((w/v)\% \approx \dfrac{x}{3.3}\).
  • So \(20\)-volume \(H_2O_2 \approx 6.06\%\ (w/v)\).
  • For \(H_2O_2\), normality \(N \approx \dfrac{\text{strength in g L}^{-1}}{17}\).
  • Hence \(20\)-volume \(H_2O_2\) has normality about \(3.57\ N\).

  1. Position of hydrogen and isotopes
    • Hydrogen shows similarities with both alkali metals and halogens, so its position in the periodic table is unique.
    • Main isotopes are protium \((^1H)\), deuterium \((^2H\ or\ D)\), and tritium \((^3H\ or\ T)\).
    • Heavy water is \(D_2O\), in which ordinary hydrogen is replaced by deuterium.
  2. Hydrides: classification and properties
    • Hydrides are compounds of hydrogen with other elements.
    • Ionic / saline hydrides are formed by highly electropositive metals and contain \(H^-\); examples: \(LiH,\ NaH,\ CaH_2\).
    • Covalent hydrides are formed mainly by p-block elements.
    • Electron-deficient hydrides have fewer electrons than required for normal bonding; classic example: \(B_2H_6\).
    • Electron-precise hydrides have exact electron count for normal covalent bonding; examples: \(CH_4,\ SiH_4\).
    • Electron-rich hydrides have lone pairs on the central atom; examples: \(NH_3,\ H_2O,\ HCl,\ H_2S\).
    • Some hydrides are polymeric in solid state; repeatedly asked examples: \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\).
    • \(BeH_2\) and \(MgH_2\) also show appreciable covalent character because of the polarising power of the small cation.
  3. Ionic hydrides and electrolysis
    • In molten ionic hydrides, free ions are present, including \(H^-\).
    • At the anode, hydride ion is oxidised: \(2H^- \rightarrow H_2 + 2e^-\).
    • Hence hydrogen gas is liberated at the anode during electrolysis of molten ionic hydrides.
    • These hydrides are powerful reducing agents and react vigorously with water to liberate hydrogen.
  4. Water, heavy water, and isotope effects
    • Ordinary water and heavy water differ in physical properties because deuterium is heavier than protium.
    • Freezing point of heavy water is about \(3.8^\circ C\), higher than ordinary water.
    • Temperature of maximum density of water is about \(4^\circ C\), whereas for heavy water it is about \(11.2^\circ C\).
    • Differences in temperature are numerically the same in \(^\circ C\) and K.
    • Heavy water is often used to introduce deuterium into products, for example: \(CaC_2 + 2D_2O \rightarrow C_2D_2 + Ca(OD)_2\).
  5. Hard and soft water
    • Hardness of water is mainly due to dissolved calcium and magnesium salts.
    • Temporary hardness is caused by bicarbonates such as \(Ca(HCO_3)_2\) and \(Mg(HCO_3)_2\).
    • On boiling, \(Ca(HCO_3)_2\) gives \(CaCO_3\) precipitate and \(Mg(HCO_3)_2\) gives \(Mg(OH)_2\) precipitate.
    • Clark’s method removes temporary hardness by adding calculated lime, \(Ca(OH)_2\).
    • Calgon method uses sodium hexametaphosphate to complex hardness-causing ions.
    • Zeolite / ion-exchange methods replace \(Ca^{2+}\) and \(Mg^{2+}\) by non-hardness ions.
    • Deionised water is obtained by passing water through both cationic and anionic exchangers successively.
  6. Hydrogen peroxide: structure and decomposition
    • \(H_2O_2\) has a non-planar, open-book type structure.
    • The dihedral angle is about \(111.5^\circ\) in gaseous phase and about \(90.2^\circ\) in solid phase.
    • It decomposes as: \(2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2\).
    • Its decomposition is accelerated by light, alkali, rough surfaces, and traces of transition metals.
    • Therefore it is stored in wax-lined or stabilised containers and kept in the dark.
  7. Volume strength, percentage strength, and normality of \(H_2O_2\)
    • Volume strength tells how many volumes of \(O_2\) are liberated by one volume of solution at STP/NTP.
    • If 1 mL solution gives 20 mL oxygen, it is a 20-volume solution.
    • From \(2H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O + O_2\), 68 g \(H_2O_2\) liberate 22.4 L oxygen.
    • This relation is used to convert volume strength into grams per litre, \((w/v)\%\), molarity, or normality.
    • For quick exam work, 20-volume \(H_2O_2\) corresponds to about \(6.06\%\ (w/v)\).
    • The equivalent weight of \(H_2O_2\) in this redox use is 17, so 20-volume solution has normality about \(3.57\ N\).

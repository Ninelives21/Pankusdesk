s-Block Elements (Alkali and Alkaline Earth Metals)

    Group 1: alkali metals
  • Electronic configuration: \(ns^1\); they form \(M^+\) ions very easily.
  • Atomic and ionic radii increase down the group.
  • Ionization enthalpy decreases down the group, so reactivity increases down the group.
  • Reducing character generally increases down the group, but lithium shows anomalies because of very high hydration enthalpy.
  • Alkali metals are soft, low-density, highly electropositive metals.
  • They react vigorously with water to form hydroxides and hydrogen.
  • Their oxides change down the group: \(Li_2O\), \(Na_2O_2\), \(KO_2\) pattern is very important.

    Lithium anomaly and diagonal relationship
  • Lithium differs from other alkali metals because of small size, high polarising power, and high charge density.
  • Lithium resembles magnesium diagonally in several properties.
  • \(Li_2CO_3\), \(LiOH\), and \(LiNO_3\) are less stable to heat than the corresponding sodium and potassium compounds.
  • Lithium forms normal oxide mainly, whereas heavier alkali metals more readily form peroxides or superoxides.
  • Lithium salts often show more covalent character than those of other alkali metals.

    Important sodium compounds
  • \(NaCl\): common salt, major raw material and biologically important electrolyte.
  • \(NaOH\): caustic soda, strong base, prepared by electrolysis of brine.
  • \(Na_2CO_3 \cdot 10H_2O\): washing soda.
  • \(NaHCO_3\): baking soda; on heating gives \(Na_2CO_3\), \(CO_2\), and \(H_2O\).
  • Questions often test uses, formulas, and thermal decomposition products.

    Group 2: alkaline earth metals
  • Electronic configuration: \(ns^2\); they usually form \(M^{2+}\) ions.
  • They are harder, denser, and less reactive than alkali metals.
  • Ionization enthalpy is higher than Group 1, so they are less reactive than alkali metals.
  • Basicity of hydroxides increases down the group: \(Be(OH)_2\) is least basic, \(Ba(OH)_2\) is most basic.
  • Solubility of hydroxides generally increases down the group.
  • Solubility of sulphates generally decreases down the group.

    Beryllium anomaly and diagonal relationship
  • Beryllium is anomalous because of small size and high polarising power.
  • It shows diagonal relationship with aluminium.
  • \(BeO\) and \(Be(OH)_2\) are amphoteric.
  • Beryllium halides are more covalent and often polymeric.
  • Beryllium does not react readily with water because of protective oxide film.

    General compound trends to remember
  • Carbonates and nitrates of Group 1 are generally stable; lithium is the main exception.
  • Carbonates of Group 2 decompose on heating to oxides and \(CO_2\).
  • Nitrates of Group 2 decompose on heating to oxides, \(NO_2\), and \(O_2\).
  • Hydroxides of Group 1 are strongly basic and highly soluble.
  • Hydroxides of Group 2 become more soluble and more basic down the group.

    Important calcium compounds
  • \(CaO\): quick lime.
  • \(Ca(OH)_2\): slaked lime.
  • \(CaCO_3\): limestone / marble / chalk.
  • Plaster of Paris: \(CaSO_4 \cdot \frac{1}{2}H_2O\).
  • Gypsum: \(CaSO_4 \cdot 2H_2O\).
  • Cement chemistry and setting are standard memory areas.

  1. Introduction to s-block elements
    • The s-block consists mainly of Group 1 and Group 2 elements.
    • Valence electrons enter the \(s\)-orbital, so their outer configurations are \(ns^1\) and \(ns^2\).
    • Because of low ionization enthalpy, these elements are highly reactive and strongly electropositive.
  2. Alkali metals: general trends
    • Group 1 elements have electronic configuration \(ns^1\).
    • They form unipositive ions, \(M^+\), very easily.
    • Atomic size, ionic size, metallic character, and reactivity increase down the group.
    • Ionization enthalpy decreases down the group.
    • They are soft metals with low melting points and low densities.
    • They are strong reducing agents and react vigorously with water.
  3. Chemical properties of alkali metals
    • With oxygen, lithium mainly forms oxide, sodium commonly forms peroxide, and potassium/rubidium/cesium tend to form superoxides.
    • With water: \(2M + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2MOH + H_2\uparrow\)
    • The hydroxides of alkali metals are strong bases and generally very soluble.
    • Halides of alkali metals are mostly ionic and highly stable.
    • Carbonates and bicarbonates of sodium are especially important industrially.
  4. Anomalous properties of lithium
    • Lithium differs from the rest of Group 1 because of its small size and high polarising power.
    • It is harder and less reactive than sodium and potassium.
    • Lithium forms only normal oxide, \(Li_2O\), under ordinary conditions.
    • \(Li_2CO_3\), \(LiOH\), and \(LiNO_3\) are less thermally stable than the corresponding sodium and potassium compounds.
    • Many lithium compounds show more covalent character.
  5. Diagonal relationship: lithium and magnesium
    • Lithium shows similarities with magnesium because of diagonal relationship.
    • Both form nitrides directly on heating with nitrogen.
    • Both carbonates and nitrates decompose on heating more readily than the corresponding compounds of other members in their groups.
    • Both show greater tendency toward covalency than expected.
  6. Important compounds of sodium
    • \(NaCl\): common salt, used in food, industry, and as raw material for \(NaOH\), \(HCl\), \(Cl_2\), and \(Na_2CO_3\).
    • \(NaOH\): caustic soda, obtained by electrolysis of brine; used in soap, paper, and textile industries.
    • \(Na_2CO_3 \cdot 10H_2O\): washing soda; used in cleaning, softening hard water, and glass manufacture.
    • \(NaHCO_3\): baking soda; on heating: \(2NaHCO_3 \rightarrow Na_2CO_3 + CO_2 + H_2O\)
  7. Biological importance of sodium and potassium
    • \(Na^+\) and \(K^+\) are essential electrolytes in living systems.
    • \(Na^+\) is important in extracellular fluids and nerve impulse transmission.
    • \(K^+\) is important in intracellular fluids and muscle/nerve function.
    • Potassium deficiency affects plant growth and chlorophyll balance.
  8. Alkaline earth metals: general trends
    • Group 2 elements have configuration \(ns^2\).
    • They form \(M^{2+}\) ions.
    • They are harder, denser, and less reactive than alkali metals.
    • Basicity of oxides and hydroxides increases down the group.
    • Solubility of hydroxides increases down the group.
    • Solubility of sulphates generally decreases down the group.
  9. Anomalous behaviour of beryllium
    • Beryllium is anomalous because of very small size and high charge density.
    • Its compounds are more covalent than those of the rest of Group 2.
    • \(BeO\) and \(Be(OH)_2\) are amphoteric.
    • Beryllium halides can be polymeric.
    • Beryllium shows diagonal relationship with aluminium.
  10. General characteristics of Group 2 compounds
    • Carbonates of Group 2 decompose on heating: \(MCO_3 \rightarrow MO + CO_2\)
    • Nitrates of Group 2 decompose on heating: \(2M(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow 2MO + 4NO_2 + O_2\)
    • Hydroxides become more basic and more soluble down the group.
    • Halides of Group 2 are generally ionic, except beryllium halides which are more covalent.
  11. Important calcium compounds
    • \(CaO\): quick lime, obtained by heating limestone.
    • \(Ca(OH)_2\): slaked lime, formed by adding water to \(CaO\).
    • \(CaCO_3\): limestone / marble / chalk; important raw material.
    • Plaster of Paris: \(CaSO_4 \cdot \frac{1}{2}H_2O\), prepared by heating gypsum.
    • Gypsum: \(CaSO_4 \cdot 2H_2O\).
    • Cement contains calcium silicates and aluminates and hardens by hydration.
  12. Biological importance of calcium and magnesium
    • Calcium is essential for bones, teeth, and blood clotting.
    • Magnesium is present in chlorophyll and is important for plant metabolism.
    • Both ions are biologically essential and frequently tested as direct factual points.

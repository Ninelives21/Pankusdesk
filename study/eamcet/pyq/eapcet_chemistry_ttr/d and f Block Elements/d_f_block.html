d and f Block Elements

    3d-series oxidation-state and \(E^\circ\) map
  • \(\mathrm{Ti^{4+}}\) is more stable than \(\mathrm{Ti^{3+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Ti^{2+}}\).
  • In the 3d series from \(Z=22\) to \(29\), copper is the standard exception with positive \((M^{2+}/M)\) reduction potential.
  • \(\mathrm{Sc}\) does not commonly show \(+1\) oxidation state; its characteristic state is \(+3\).
  • Direct PYQ memory line: “Ti(IV) stable, Cu positive \(E^\circ\), Sc no \(+1\).”

    Colour of aquated ions
  • \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) (aq) \(\rightarrow\) pale green.
  • \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) (aq) \(\rightarrow\) blue.
  • \(\mathrm{V^{3+}}\) (aq) \(\rightarrow\) green.
  • \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) (aq) is yellow / yellow-brown, not pink.
  • If the question asks for an incorrectly matched ion-colour pair, \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\)-pink is the classic trap.

    Lanthanoid contraction map
  • From La to Lu, ionic size decreases because \(4f\) electrons shield poorly.
  • As a result, covalent character of lanthanoid hydroxides increases from La to Lu.
  • Basicity of lanthanoid hydroxides decreases from La to Lu, so \(\mathrm{La(OH)_3}\) is more basic than \(\mathrm{Lu(OH)_3}\).
  • Chemical reactivity also decreases from La to Lu.
  • \(\mathrm{Zr}\) and \(\mathrm{Hf}\) have nearly the same radius due to lanthanoid contraction.
  • Separation of lanthanoids from one another is difficult, not easy.

    Lanthanoid ion facts
  • \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Tb^{4+}}\) act as oxidising agents because they tend to reduce to the more stable \(+3\) state.
  • \(\mathrm{Eu^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) act as reducing agents, not oxidising agents.
  • \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) is diamagnetic.
  • \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) is also diamagnetic because it has \(4f^0\) configuration.
  • \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}}\) has \(4f^7\); \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) has \(4f^{14}\).

    Ionic radius and \(f\)-electron shortcuts
  • Standard ionic-radius order: \[ \mathrm{Y^{3+} < Lu^{3+} < Eu^{3+} < La^{3+}} \]
  • Reason: \(Y^{3+}\) is smaller than \(La^{3+}\), and within lanthanoids radius decreases from La to Lu.
  • \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}}\) \(\rightarrow 4f^7\), \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) \(\rightarrow 4f^{14}\); sum \(=21\).

    Permanganate and dichromate fact map
  • On heating: \[ \mathrm{2KMnO_4 \rightarrow K_2MnO_4 + MnO_2 + O_2} \]
  • Among the manganese products, manganate has the higher oxidation state \((+6)\).
  • \(\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}}\) / manganate is green and paramagnetic.
  • Ammonium dichromate on heating gives: \[ \mathrm{(NH_4)_2Cr_2O_7 \rightarrow Cr_2O_3 + N_2 + 4H_2O} \]

    Common traps
  • Do not mix d/f-block questions with nearby coordination-compound or redox questions; several supplied anchors drift into those chapters.
  • Misch metal is not “95% iron and 5% lanthanoid”; that statement is false.
  • If a lanthanoid ion has \(f^0\) or \(f^{14}\), check diamagnetism immediately.
  • If a colour question involves \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\), avoid choosing pink.

  1. Position and electronic configuration
    • d-block elements are placed between the s- and p-blocks and involve filling of \((n-1)d\) orbitals.
    • f-block elements are inner transition elements involving filling of \((n-2)f\) orbitals.
    • Lanthanoids mainly correspond to filling of \(4f\) orbitals; actinoids mainly correspond to filling of \(5f\) orbitals.
    • The syllabus also groups coordination-compound content nearby, so exam papers sometimes place d/f-block and coordination questions close together. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

  2. General properties of transition elements
    • Transition elements show variable oxidation states because both \(ns\) and \((n-1)d\) electrons can participate in bonding.
    • They often form coloured ions due to \(d\)-\(d\) electronic transitions.
    • They show catalytic behaviour, complex formation, alloy formation, and interstitial compound formation.
    • Standard-potential trends and oxidation-state stability are repeatedly tested in direct statement questions. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

  3. Oxidation states and standard-potential trend
    • For titanium, \(+4\) is the most stable common oxidation state among \(+2\), \(+3\), and \(+4\).
    • Across the 3d series from Ti to Cu, most \((M^{2+}/M)\) reduction potentials are negative.
    • Copper is the familiar exception with positive \((M^{2+}/M)\) reduction potential.
    • Scandium generally shows only \(+3\) oxidation state in ordinary chemistry questions. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

  4. Colour of transition-metal ions
    • Aquated \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) is pale green.
    • Aquated \(\mathrm{Cu^{2+}}\) is blue.
    • Aquated \(\mathrm{V^{3+}}\) is green.
    • Aquated \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) is yellow to yellow-brown, so any “pink” match is incorrect.
    • Such colour questions are factual recall questions based on coloured \(d\)-electron species. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

  5. Lanthanoids and lanthanoid contraction
    • Lanthanoids usually show \(+3\) oxidation state most commonly.
    • Because \(4f\) electrons shield poorly, there is a steady decrease in size from La to Lu. This is lanthanoid contraction.
    • As a result, covalent character of lanthanoid hydroxides increases and their basicity decreases from La to Lu.
    • Chemical reactivity also decreases across the series.
    • \(\mathrm{Zr}\) and \(\mathrm{Hf}\) end up with nearly the same radius because of lanthanoid contraction.
    • Separation of lanthanoids is difficult because their sizes and chemical properties are very similar. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

  6. Special oxidation states and magnetism in lanthanoids
    • \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Tb^{4+}}\) behave as oxidising agents because they tend to return to \(+3\).
    • \(\mathrm{Eu^{2+}}\) and \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) behave as reducing agents because they tend to oxidise to \(+3\).
    • \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) is diamagnetic because it has no \(f\)-electrons.
    • \(\mathrm{Ce^{4+}}\) is also diamagnetic because it is \(4f^0\).
    • \(\mathrm{Gd^{3+}}\) has \(4f^7\), while \(\mathrm{Yb^{2+}}\) has \(4f^{14}\). :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

  7. Ionic radii in lanthanoids
    • Ionic radius decreases from \(\mathrm{La^{3+}}\) to \(\mathrm{Lu^{3+}}\).
    • A standard comparison is: \[ \mathrm{Y^{3+} < Lu^{3+} < Eu^{3+} < La^{3+}} \]
    • This combines normal group trend with lanthanoid contraction. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

  8. Important d-block compounds: permanganate and dichromate
    • The syllabus explicitly includes preparation and properties of potassium permanganate and potassium dichromate, and structures of related oxoanions. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}
    • On heating potassium permanganate: \[ \mathrm{2KMnO_4 \rightarrow K_2MnO_4 + MnO_2 + O_2} \]
    • Among the manganese-containing products, manganate \((\mathrm{MnO_4^{2-}})\) has the higher oxidation state \((+6)\).
    • Manganate is green and paramagnetic.
    • Ammonium dichromate thermally decomposes to \(\mathrm{Cr_2O_3}\), \(\mathrm{N_2}\), and water vapour: \[ \mathrm{(NH_4)_2Cr_2O_7 \rightarrow Cr_2O_3 + N_2 + 4H_2O} \]
    • This decomposition is a common direct-PYQ fact. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

p-Block Elements : Group 14 (Carbon Family)

    Oxidation-state pattern map
  • Group 14 valence configuration is \(ns^2 np^2\), so common oxidation states are \(+4\) and \(+2\).
  • \(+4\) is most stable for \(C\) and \(Si\); stability of \(+2\) increases down the group due to inert pair effect.
  • \(Sn^{2+}\) behaves as a reducing agent.
  • \(Pb^{4+}\) behaves as an oxidising agent.
  • \(Pb^{2+}\) is relatively stable, so statements calling it a strong reducing agent are usually wrong.
  • Fast order to remember: stability of \(+2\) state is \(Ge < Sn < Pb\).

    Oxide classification map
  • \(CO\) is a neutral oxide.
  • \(CO_2\) and \(SiO_2\) are acidic oxides.
  • \(SnO,\ SnO_2,\ PbO,\ PbO_2\) are standard amphoteric oxides.
  • If both monoxide and dioxide of an element are amphoteric, think first of \(Sn\) and \(Pb\).
  • In mixed-oxide counting questions, classify each oxide separately; do not answer only from chapter name.
  • Group 13 distractors like \(B_2O_3,\ Al_2O_3,\ Ga_2O_3,\ Tl_2O_3\) are often inserted to trap hasty counting.

    Carbon allotropes and structures
  • Graphite has a layered structure and each carbon is \(sp^2\)-hybridised.
  • Buckminster fullerene is also treated as \(sp^2\)-hybridised in exam-level questions.
  • Diamond has a rigid three-dimensional network of directional covalent bonds.
  • Graphite is the thermodynamically most stable allotrope under ordinary conditions, not fullerene.
  • The interlayer distance in graphite is about \(340\)–\(342\ \mathrm{pm}\); \(141.5\ \mathrm{pm}\) is a trap value.
  • Graphite works as a dry lubricant because layers can slide over one another.

    Trend and comparison map
  • Melting-point order among the giant covalent trio is \(C > Si > Ge\).
  • A common broader order used in PYQs is \(Si > Ge > Pb > Sn\), so \(Sn\) comes out lowest in that set.
  • Catenation is maximum for carbon and decreases down the group.
  • Lead is treated as least abundant in the earth’s crust among the common Group 14 members used in such PYQs.
  • A standard electronegativity question here uses the order \(C > Pb > Ge\).
  • When the paper asks for “correct order” in Group 14, always check whether it is about melting point, electronegativity, or oxidation-state stability; all three have appeared.

    Silicon compounds and silicates
  • \(\mathrm{CCl_4}\) does not hydrolyse easily, but \(\mathrm{SiCl_4}\) hydrolyses readily because silicon can expand coordination number.
  • Glass is a man-made silicate.
  • Silicones contain repeating linkage \(\mathrm{-Si(R)_2-O-}\).
  • Silicones are used for water-proofing, lubricants, sealants, and insulating materials.
  • Preparation pattern: organochlorosilane \(\rightarrow\) hydrolysis \(\rightarrow\) condensation/polymerisation \(\rightarrow\) silicone.
  • \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists, but \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) is the standard non-existent species in PYQs.

    Fast reaction recall
  • \(\mathrm{HCOOH \xrightarrow[\ 373\ K\ ]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} CO + H_2O}\)
  • In \(CO\), carbon is \(sp\)-hybridised and the oxide is neutral.
  • \(\mathrm{CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O + CO_2}\)
  • \(CO_2\) is colourless, odourless, and acidic in nature; “poisonous gas” is the wrong statement in the referenced PYQ.
  • \(\mathrm{2CH_3Cl + Si \xrightarrow[\ 573\ K\ ]{Cu} (CH_3)_2SiCl_2 \xrightarrow{H_2O} (CH_3)_2Si(OH)_2 \xrightarrow{polymerisation} [-Si(CH_3)_2-O-]_n}\)

  1. Position, electronic configuration, and general character
    • Group 14 elements are \(C,\ Si,\ Ge,\ Sn,\ Pb\).
    • Their valence-shell electronic configuration is \(ns^2 np^2\).
    • They belong to the \(p\)-block and commonly show covalency equal to 4.
    • On moving down the group, metallic character increases: carbon is a non-metal, silicon and germanium are metalloids, while tin and lead are metals.
    • Catenation is strongest for carbon because the \(C-C\) bond is very strong and carbon is very small.

  2. Oxidation states and inert pair effect
    • The main oxidation states are \(+4\) and \(+2\).
    • \(+4\) is favoured for lighter members, especially carbon and silicon.
    • Due to inert pair effect, the \(ns^2\) pair becomes less available for bonding down the group.
    • Hence stability of \(+2\) increases from \(Ge\) to \(Pb\), while stability of \(+4\) decreases.
    • \(Sn^{2+}\) is reducing because it tends to get oxidised to \(Sn^{4+}\).
    • \(Pb^{4+}\) is oxidising because it tends to get reduced to \(Pb^{2+}\).
    • This single idea explains many repeated PYQs about correct and incorrect oxidation-state statements.

  3. Trends in physical properties
    • Atomic size generally increases down the group, though some values show irregularity due to poor shielding by inner \(d\)- and \(f\)-electrons.
    • Ionisation enthalpy generally decreases down the group, but irregular changes may appear because of poor shielding and inert pair effect.
    • Electronegativity does not vary very smoothly in the heavier members, so direct order questions should be learned carefully.
    • Melting-point trends depend strongly on structure.
    • Carbon, silicon, and germanium have giant covalent structures, so they have high melting points.
    • Tin and lead are metallic, so their melting points are lower than those giant covalent members.
    • That is why \(C > Si > Ge\) is standard, and in the set \(Si,\ Ge,\ Sn,\ Pb\), tin often comes out lowest.

  4. Allotropes of carbon
    • Diamond, graphite, and fullerene are the most familiar allotropes in EAPCET questions.
    • Diamond has a three-dimensional tetrahedral network; each carbon is \(sp^3\)-hybridised.
    • Because of strong directional covalent bonds throughout the crystal, diamond is very hard and has high melting point.
    • Graphite consists of planar hexagonal layers; each carbon is \(sp^2\)-hybridised.
    • Weak van der Waals forces between layers allow sliding, so graphite acts as a dry lubricant.
    • Graphite conducts electricity because of delocalised electrons within each layer.
    • Buckminster fullerene \((C_{60})\) is a cage-like allotrope; each carbon is treated as \(sp^2\)-hybridised at this level.
    • Graphite is the thermodynamically most stable allotrope under ordinary conditions.

  5. Catenation and special behaviour of carbon
    • Catenation means self-linking tendency of an element to form chains or rings.
    • Carbon shows maximum catenation because of strong \(C-C\) bonds and small size.
    • The tendency decreases down the group: \(C > Si > Ge > Sn > Pb\).
    • Lead is treated as the member that practically does not show catenation in common objective questions.
    • This is why carbon dominates organic chemistry while heavier Group 14 elements form far fewer stable chains.

  6. Nature of oxides
    • \(CO\) is a neutral oxide.
    • \(CO_2\) and \(SiO_2\) are acidic oxides.
    • \(GeO_2\) shows amphoteric tendency, though the most frequently tested amphoteric pair is \(SnO/SnO_2\) and \(PbO/PbO_2\).
    • \(SnO,\ SnO_2,\ PbO,\ PbO_2\) are standard amphoteric oxides.
    • Objective questions often mix these with Group 13 oxides to test classification, so each oxide must be judged individually.
    • \(CO_2\) is acidic because it forms carbonic acid in water and reacts with bases.
    • \(SiO_2\) is also acidic and reacts with strong bases to form silicates.

  7. Halides and anomalous behaviour of carbon and silicon
    • Carbon forms \(\mathrm{CCl_4}\), but it does not hydrolyse easily.
    • Reason: carbon cannot expand its octet because vacant \(d\)-orbitals are absent in the second period.
    • Silicon forms \(\mathrm{SiCl_4}\), which hydrolyses readily.
    • Silicon can increase coordination number using available vacant orbitals and larger atomic size.
    • This carbon-versus-silicon difference is one of the most asked conceptual contrasts in the carbon family chapter.
    • Similarly, \([SiF_6]^{2-}\) exists, while \([SiCl_6]^{2-}\) is the standard non-existent species asked in MCQs.

  8. Silicates, glass, and silicones
    • Silicates are salts containing silicon–oxygen structural units.
    • Glass is treated as a man-made silicate.
    • Silicones are organosilicon polymers containing repeating unit \(\mathrm{-Si(R)_2-O-}\).
    • They are prepared from organochlorosilanes by hydrolysis followed by condensation/polymerisation.
    • A standard route is: \[ \mathrm{2CH_3Cl + Si \xrightarrow[\ 573\ K\ ]{Cu} (CH_3)_2SiCl_2} \] followed by hydrolysis and polymerisation.
    • Silicones are used for water-proofing fabrics, lubricants, electrical insulation, and sealants.
    • The water-proofing use repeatedly appears in assertion–reason format.

  9. Important reactions involving carbon oxides
    • Formic acid on heating with concentrated \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) at \(373\ \mathrm{K}\) gives carbon monoxide and water: \[ \mathrm{HCOOH \xrightarrow[\ 373\ K\ ]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} CO + H_2O} \]
    • In \(CO\), carbon is \(sp\)-hybridised.
    • \(CO\) is a neutral oxide and also a reducing agent in many metallurgical reactions.
    • Calcium carbonate with dilute HCl gives carbon dioxide: \[ \mathrm{CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O + CO_2\uparrow} \]
    • \(CO_2\) is colourless, odourless, and acidic in nature.

  10. Exam shortcuts from the PYQ pattern
    • If the question mentions \(Sn^{2+}\), \(Pb^{2+}\), and \(Pb^{4+}\), think inert pair effect first.
    • If the question asks about neutral, acidic, or amphoteric oxides, classify \(CO,\ CO_2,\ SiO_2,\ SnO,\ SnO_2,\ PbO,\ PbO_2\) immediately from memory.
    • If the question compares graphite, diamond, and fullerene, check hybridisation, stability, and structure separately.
    • If the question contains hydrolysis of \(\mathrm{CCl_4}\) versus silicon halides, recall the octet-expansion difference.
    • If the question mentions waterproofing fabrics or repeating unit \(\mathrm{-Si(R)_2-O-}\), it is pointing to silicones.
    • If the question asks for a Group 14 trend order, first identify the property before using any memorised sequence.

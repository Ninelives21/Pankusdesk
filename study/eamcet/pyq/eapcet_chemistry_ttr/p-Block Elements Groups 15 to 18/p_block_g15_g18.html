p-Block Elements – Groups 15 to 18

    Phosphorus oxoacid map
  • Basicity of a phosphorus oxoacid depends on the number of ionisable \(P-OH\) hydrogens, not total hydrogens.
  • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_2}\) is monobasic and a strong reducing agent because it has two \(P-H\) bonds.
  • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\) is dibasic and reducing because it has one \(P-H\) bond.
  • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\) is tribasic and non-reducing because it has no \(P-H\) bond.
  • Key disproportionation fact: \(\mathrm{4H_3PO_3 \rightarrow 3H_3PO_4 + PH_3}\).
  • Silver-nitrate reduction questions usually point to \(\mathrm{H_3PO_2}\) or \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\), never \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\).

    Phosphorus halides and hydrolysis
  • \(\mathrm{P_4 + 10SO_2Cl_2 \rightarrow 4PCl_5 + 10SO_2}\); complete hydrolysis of \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\) gives \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\).
  • White phosphorus with thionyl chloride gives \(\mathrm{PCl_3}\); hydrolysis gives \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\).
  • \(\mathrm{PCl_3}\) is pyramidal due to one lone pair on phosphorus.
  • \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\) exists, but \(\mathrm{NCl_5}\) does not because nitrogen cannot expand octet.
  • If an assertion says nitrogen fails to form \(NCl_5\) because of higher electronegativity alone, that reason is not the correct explanation.

    Group 16 hydride and oxide trends
  • Boiling point order of hydrides: \(\mathrm{H_2O > H_2Te > H_2Se > H_2S}\) because water shows hydrogen bonding.
  • Bond angle decreases down Group 16: \(\mathrm{H_2O > H_2S > H_2Se > H_2Te}\).
  • Acidic character of nitrogen oxides increases with oxidation state: \(\mathrm{N_2O < NO < N_2O_3 < N_2O_4 < N_2O_5}\).
  • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) is a strong reducing agent because sulphur in it is at \(+4\) and can still be oxidised to \(+6\).

    Chlorine and industrial processes
  • Deacon’s process: \(\mathrm{4HCl + O_2 \xrightarrow[\ 723\,K\ ]{CuCl_2} 2Cl_2 + 2H_2O}\).
  • Chlorine bleaches by oxidation.
  • Chlorine oxidises ferrous salts to ferric salts in acidic medium.
  • Objective trap: chlorine does not oxidise iodine to periodic acid in plain water under the standard statement framing.

    Noble gas compounds and uses
  • \(\mathrm{XeF_6}\) on partial hydrolysis gives \(\mathrm{XeOF_4}\), which is square pyramidal.
  • Helium is used as a diluent for oxygen in modern diving apparatus.
  • Argon is used to provide an inert atmosphere in high-temperature metallurgical processes.
  • Noble-gas use questions are usually direct factual memory checks.

    Thermal decomposition and direct-fact memory
  • \(\mathrm{(NH_4)_2Cr_2O_7}\) and metal azides liberate \(\mathrm{N_2}\) on decomposition; ordinary sodium nitrate does not.
  • \(\mathrm{NaNO_2 + HCl \rightarrow HNO_2 \rightarrow NO + NO_2 + H_2O}\) is a standard memory reaction.
  • When a question asks about \(P-O-P\) versus \(P-P\) linkage, pyro and many meta forms suggest \(P-O-P\), while \(\mathrm{H_4P_2O_6}\) is the classic \(P-P\) exception.

    Fast elimination cues
  • \(P-H\) bond present \(\Rightarrow\) reducing acid.
  • Number of \(P-OH\) bonds \(\Rightarrow\) basicity.
  • Higher oxidation state oxide \(\Rightarrow\) generally more acidic.
  • Hydrogen bonding exception \(\Rightarrow\) water breaks smooth boiling-point trend.
  • Nitrogen cannot exceed covalency 4 in these chloride-type questions.

  1. Group 15: electronic configuration and oxidation states
    • Group 15 elements have outer configuration \(ns^2np^3\).
    • Common oxidation states are \(-3\), \(+3\), and \(+5\).
    • Stability of \(+5\) decreases down the group, while \(+3\) becomes more stable due to inert pair effect.
    • Nitrogen differs from heavier congeners because of small size, high electronegativity, and absence of vacant \(d\)-orbitals.
    • That is why nitrogen forms \(N_2\) with a strong \(p\pi-p\pi\) bond, but does not form \(NCl_5\).

  2. Phosphorus chlorides and related reactions
    • \(\mathrm{PCl_3}\) is prepared by reaction of phosphorus with limited chlorine or chlorinating agents such as thionyl chloride in standard objective questions.
    • \(\mathrm{PCl_3}\) is pyramidal because phosphorus has one lone pair.
    • \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\) exists and has trigonal bipyramidal geometry in gas phase.
    • Complete hydrolysis of \(\mathrm{PCl_5}\) gives \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\).
    • Hydrolysis of \(\mathrm{PCl_3}\) gives \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\).

  3. Oxoacids of phosphorus
    • Basicity depends on the number of ionisable \(\mathrm{-OH}\) hydrogens.
    • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_2}\) is monobasic; structure contains one \(\mathrm{-OH}\) and two \(P-H\) bonds.
    • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\) is dibasic; structure contains two \(\mathrm{-OH}\) bonds and one \(P-H\) bond.
    • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\) is tribasic; it contains three \(\mathrm{-OH}\) groups and no \(P-H\) bond.
    • Reducing character of phosphorus oxoacids is due to \(P-H\) bonds.
    • So \(\mathrm{H_3PO_2}\) and \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\) are reducing acids, while \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\) is not.
    • \(\mathrm{H_3PO_3}\) disproportionates to \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\) and \(\mathrm{PH_3}\).

  4. Linkages in phosphorus acids
    • Pyrophosphoric and many metaphosphoric forms involve \(P-O-P\) linkage.
    • \(\mathrm{H_4P_2O_6}\) is the classic exception containing a \(P-P\) bond.
    • Such linkage-based questions are asked as direct memory checks, so the exceptional structure should be memorised explicitly.

  5. Group 16 hydrides and acidity-related trends
    • Hydrides of Group 16 are \(\mathrm{H_2O,\ H_2S,\ H_2Se,\ H_2Te}\).
    • Boiling point order is irregular because water shows strong hydrogen bonding.
    • The standard order is \(\mathrm{H_2O > H_2Te > H_2Se > H_2S}\).
    • Bond angle decreases down the group because central atom size increases and lone pair–bond pair repulsion effect decreases.
    • Acidic strength of hydrides increases down the group, but that is different from boiling-point trend and often used as a distractor.

  6. Oxides of nitrogen and sulphur
    • Acidic character of oxides generally increases with oxidation state of the central atom.
    • For nitrogen oxides, a useful order is \(\mathrm{N_2O < NO < N_2O_3 < N_2O_4 < N_2O_5}\).
    • \(\mathrm{SO_2}\) behaves as a reducing agent because sulphur is in intermediate oxidation state \(+4\).
    • \(\mathrm{NaNO_2}\) with acid forms unstable \(\mathrm{HNO_2}\), which decomposes to \(\mathrm{NO}\) and \(\mathrm{NO_2}\).

  7. Halogens and chlorine
    • Halogens show high reactivity because they need one electron to complete octet.
    • Chlorine is a strong oxidising agent and converts \(\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}\) to \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\) in acidic medium.
    • Its bleaching action is due to oxidation through nascent oxygen or equivalent oxidising species in aqueous medium.
    • Deacon’s process prepares chlorine from hydrochloric acid: \[ \mathrm{4HCl + O_2 \xrightarrow[\ 723\,K\ ]{CuCl_2} 2Cl_2 + 2H_2O} \]

  8. Noble gases: compounds and uses
    • Noble gases are chemically inert because of stable valence-shell configuration.
    • Xenon forms compounds such as \(\mathrm{XeF_2,\ XeF_4,\ XeF_6}\).
    • Partial hydrolysis of \(\mathrm{XeF_6}\) gives \(\mathrm{XeOF_4}\).
    • \(\mathrm{XeOF_4}\) has square pyramidal shape.
    • Helium is used with oxygen in deep-sea diving mixtures because it is inert and has low density.
    • Argon is used to provide inert atmosphere in welding and high-temperature metallurgical operations.

  9. Thermal decomposition facts
    • \(\mathrm{(NH_4)_2Cr_2O_7}\) decomposes to give \(\mathrm{N_2}\).
    • Azides such as \(\mathrm{Ba(N_3)_2}\) also liberate \(\mathrm{N_2}\) on heating.
    • Simple nitrates like sodium nitrate do not directly liberate dinitrogen in standard thermal decomposition questions.

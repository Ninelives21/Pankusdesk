NAMED REACTIONS, GOC, HYDROCARBONS, HALOALKANES/HALOARENES, C-H-O-COMPOUNDS, NITROGEN-COMPOUNDS

    1. Aldol condensation

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3CHO \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dil.\ NaOH}} CH_3CH(OH)CH_2CHO} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH(OH)CH_2CHO \xrightarrow{\Delta} CH_3CH{=}CHCHO + H_2O} \)

      Aldehydes/ketones with at least one \(\alpha\)-hydrogen give \(\beta\)-hydroxy carbonyl compound, then dehydrated product.

    2. Cannizzaro reaction

      \( \mathrm{2HCHO \xrightarrow{\mathrm{conc.\ NaOH}} CH_3OH + HCOONa} \)

      Aldehydes without \(\alpha\)-hydrogen undergo self oxidation-reduction to alcohol and carboxylate salt.

    3. Decarboxylation with soda lime

      \( \mathrm{RCOONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\mathrm{CaO}]{\Delta} RH + Na_2CO_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3COONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\mathrm{CaO}]{\Delta} CH_4 + Na_2CO_3} \)

      Sodium salt of carboxylic acid gives hydrocarbon with one carbon less.

    4. Williamson synthesis

      \( \mathrm{R-X + R'O^-Na^+ \rightarrow R-O-R' + NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3I + C_2H_5ONa \rightarrow CH_3OC_2H_5 + NaI} \)

      Alkyl halide with sodium alkoxide gives ether.

    5. Reimer-Tiemann reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH + CHCl_3 + 3NaOH \xrightarrow{345\,K} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4CHO + 3NaCl + 2H_2O} \)

      Phenol gives mainly salicylaldehyde.

    6. Kolbe-Schmitt reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5ONa + CO_2 \xrightarrow[\mathrm{4{-}7\,atm}]{136^\circ C} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COONa} \)

      \( \mathrm{o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COONa \xrightarrow{H^+} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COOH} \)

      Sodium phenoxide gives salicylic acid after acidification.

    7. Carbylamine reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NH_2 + CHCl_3 + 3KOH \xrightarrow[\Delta]{\mathrm{alc.}} C_6H_5NC + 3KCl + 3H_2O} \)

      Primary amine gives foul-smelling isocyanide.

    8. Diazotisation

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NH_2 + NaNO_2 + 2HCl \xrightarrow{0{-}5^\circ C} C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- + NaCl + 2H_2O} \)

      Aniline gives benzene diazonium chloride at \(0{-}5^\circ\mathrm{C}\).

    9. Sandmeyer reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Cu_2Cl_2/HCl}} C_6H_5Cl + N_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Cu_2Br_2/HBr}} C_6H_5Br + N_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{\mathrm{CuCN/KCN}} C_6H_5CN + N_2} \)

      Diazonium salt gives aryl chloride, bromide or cyanide.

    10. Wurtz-Fittig reaction

      \( \mathrm{Ar-X + R-X + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} Ar-R + 2NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5Cl + C_2H_5Cl + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} C_6H_5C_2H_5 + 2NaCl} \)

      Aryl halide and alkyl halide give alkyl arene.

    11. HVZ reaction

      \( \mathrm{RCH_2COOH \xrightarrow[\mathrm{then\ H_2O}]{X_2/\mathrm{red\ P}} RCHXCOOH} \)

      \( \mathrm{X=Cl\ or\ Br} \)

      Carboxylic acids with \(\alpha\)-hydrogen give \(\alpha\)-halo acids.

    12. Wurtz reaction

      \( \mathrm{2R-X + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} R-R + 2NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3I + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} C_2H_6 + 2NaI} \)

      Alkyl halides give alkanes with double the carbon skeleton.

    13. Friedel-Crafts alkylation

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + CH_3Cl \xrightarrow{\mathrm{anhy.\ AlCl_3}} C_6H_5CH_3 + HCl} \)

      Benzene gives methylbenzene/toluene.

    14. Friedel-Crafts acylation

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + CH_3COCl \xrightarrow{\mathrm{anhy.\ AlCl_3}} C_6H_5COCH_3 + HCl} \)

      Benzene gives acetophenone.

    15. Hydroboration-oxidation

      \( \mathrm{6CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + B_2H_6 \rightarrow 2(CH_3CH_2CH_2)_3B} \)

      \( \mathrm{2(CH_3CH_2CH_2)_3B \xrightarrow{\mathrm{H_2O_2/OH^-}} 6CH_3CH_2CH_2OH + 2B(OH)_3} \)

      Alkene gives anti-Markovnikov alcohol.

    16. Clemmensen reduction

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Zn{-}Hg/conc.\ HCl}} R_2CH_2} \)

      Aldehydes and ketones are reduced to methylene compounds.

    17. Wolff-Kishner reduction

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O + NH_2NH_2 \rightarrow R_2C{=}NNH_2 + H_2O} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}NNH_2 \xrightarrow[\Delta]{\mathrm{KOH/ethylene\ glycol}} R_2CH_2 + N_2} \)

      Carbonyl group is reduced to \(\mathrm{-CH_2-}\).

    18. Ozonolysis

      \( \mathrm{H_2C{=}CH_2 + O_3 \rightarrow ethylene\ ozonide} \)

      \( \mathrm{ethylene\ ozonide \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Zn/H_2O}} 2HCHO + H_2O_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH + O_3 \rightarrow acetylene\ ozonide \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Zn/H_2O}} OHC{-}CHO + H_2O_2} \)

      Unsaturated hydrocarbons give carbonyl compounds after ozonide hydrolysis.

    19. Cross aldol condensation

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CHO + CH_3CH_2CHO \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dil.\ NaOH}} aldol\ products \xrightarrow{\Delta} \alpha,\beta\text{-}unsaturated\ carbonyls} \)

      Two different carbonyl compounds give self-aldol and cross-aldol products if both have \(\alpha\)-hydrogen.

    20. Esterification

      \( \mathrm{RCOOH + R'OH \xrightleftharpoons[\ ]{H^+} RCOOR' + H_2O} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3COOH + C_2H_5OH \xrightleftharpoons[\ ]{H^+} CH_3COOC_2H_5 + H_2O} \)

      Carboxylic acid and alcohol give ester.

    21. Friedel-Crafts alkylation of anisole

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OCH_3 + CH_3Cl \xrightarrow{\mathrm{AlCl_3}} o\text{-}CH_3C_6H_4OCH_3 + p\text{-}CH_3C_6H_4OCH_3} \)

      Para product is major.

    22. Friedel-Crafts acylation of anisole

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OCH_3 + CH_3COCl \xrightarrow{\mathrm{AlCl_3}} o\text{-}CH_3COC_6H_4OCH_3 + p\text{-}CH_3COC_6H_4OCH_3} \)

      \(p\)-methoxyacetophenone is major.

    23. Gattermann reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Cu/HCl}} C_6H_5Cl + N_2 + CuCl} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Cu/HBr}} C_6H_5Br + N_2 + CuCl} \)

      Diazonium salt gives chloro- or bromobenzene.

    24. Coupling reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- + C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow{OH^-} p\text{-}HOC_6H_4N{=}NC_6H_5 + HCl} \)

      Diazonium salt couples with phenol to give \(p\)-hydroxyazobenzene dye.

    25. Hofmann bromamide reaction

      \( \mathrm{RCONH_2 + Br_2 + 4NaOH \xrightarrow{\Delta} RNH_2 + Na_2CO_3 + 2NaBr + 2H_2O} \)

      Amide gives primary amine with one carbon less.

    26. Balz-Schiemann reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- + HBF_4 \rightarrow C_6H_5N_2^+BF_4^- + HCl} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+BF_4^- \xrightarrow{\Delta} C_6H_5F + BF_3 + N_2} \)

      Diazonium fluoroborate gives fluorobenzene on heating.

    27. Crossed Wurtz reaction

      \( \mathrm{R-X + R'-X + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} R-R + R'-R' + R-R' + 2NaX} \)

      Two different alkyl halides give a mixture of alkanes.

    28. Fittig reaction

      \( \mathrm{2Ar-X + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} Ar-Ar + 2NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{2C_6H_5Cl + 2Na \xrightarrow{\mathrm{dry\ ether}} C_6H_5-C_6H_5 + 2NaCl} \)

      Aryl halides give biaryl compounds.

    29. Kolbe electrolysis

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3COOK + 2H_2O \xrightarrow{\mathrm{electrolysis}} C_2H_6 + 2CO_2 + 2KOH + H_2} \)

      Acetate salt gives ethane at anode.

    30. Sabatier-Senderens reaction

      \( \mathrm{H_2C{=}CH_2 + H_2 \xrightarrow[\mathrm{300^\circ C}]{\mathrm{Ni}} C_2H_6} \)

      Catalytic hydrogenation of alkene gives alkane.

    31. Markovnikov rule

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + HBr \rightarrow CH_3CHBrCH_3\ (major)} \)

      In normal HX addition, halide goes to the double-bond carbon having fewer hydrogens.

    32. Anti-Markovnikov rule / peroxide effect

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + HBr \xrightarrow{ROOR} CH_3CH_2CH_2Br\ (major)} \)

      In peroxide effect, bromine goes to the double-bond carbon having more hydrogens.

    33. Polymerisation of ethene

      \( \mathrm{n(CH_2{=}CH_2) \xrightarrow[\mathrm{high\ pressure,\ 100^\circ C}]{O_2} (-CH_2-CH_2-)_n} \)

      Ethene polymerises to polyethylene.


    1. Wurtz reaction for higher alkanes

      \( \mathrm{2R{-}X + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} R{-}R + 2NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3CH_2CH_2Br + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} CH_3(CH_2)_4CH_3 + 2NaBr} \)

      Used repeatedly for alkyl halide \(\rightarrow\) symmetrical alkane conversions.

    2. Aromatization of \(n\)-hexane to benzene

      \( \mathrm{CH_3(CH_2)_4CH_3 \xrightarrow[10{-}20\,atm]{Cr_2O_3,\ 773\,K} C_6H_6 + 4H_2} \)

      This is the usual bridge from aliphatic chain reactions to benzene reactions.

    3. Acetylene trimerisation to benzene

      \( \mathrm{3HC{\equiv}CH \xrightarrow[873\,K]{red\ hot\ Fe\ tube} C_6H_6} \)

      High-repeat chain: vicinal dibromide \(\rightarrow\) acetylene \(\rightarrow\) benzene.

    4. Double dehydrohalogenation to alkynes

      \( \mathrm{BrCH_2CH_2Br \xrightarrow{alc.\ KOH} CH_2{=}CHBr \xrightarrow{NaNH_2} HC{\equiv}CH} \)

      Vicinal or geminal dihalides give alkynes after two eliminations.

    5. Friedel-Crafts alkylation of benzene

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + CH_3Cl \xrightarrow{anhy.\ AlCl_3} C_6H_5CH_3 + HCl} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + C_2H_5Cl \xrightarrow{anhy.\ AlCl_3} C_6H_5C_2H_5 + HCl} \)

      Alkylation gives alkylbenzene; carbocation rearrangement can occur with larger alkyl halides.

    6. Friedel-Crafts acylation of benzene

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + CH_3COCl \xrightarrow{anhy.\ AlCl_3} C_6H_5COCH_3 + HCl} \)

      Acetyl chloride gives acetophenone; the electrophile is the acylium ion \(\mathrm{CH_3CO^+}\).

    7. Benzene chlorination versus addition of chlorine

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{AlCl_3} C_6H_5Cl + HCl} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + 3Cl_2 \xrightarrow[dark,\ cold]{dry\ AlCl_3} C_6H_6Cl_6} \)

      Ring substitution gives chlorobenzene; dark/cold excess chlorine gives hexachlorocyclohexane addition product.

    8. Benzylic halogenation versus ring halogenation

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 + Br_2 \xrightarrow{h\nu} C_6H_5CH_2Br + HBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{Fe,\ dark} o/p{-}chlorotoluene + HCl} \)

      Light favours side-chain radical halogenation; \(\mathrm{Fe}\) or \(\mathrm{AlCl_3}\) favours ring substitution.

    9. Wurtz-Fittig reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5Cl + CH_3Cl + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} C_6H_5CH_3 + 2NaCl} \)

      Aryl halide plus alkyl halide gives alkylbenzene in the standard sequence.

    10. Sandmeyer reaction to chlorobenzene

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{Cu_2Cl_2/HCl} C_6H_5Cl + N_2} \)

      Used in sequences where diazonium salt is converted to chlorobenzene before further reactions.

    11. Kolbe electrolysis of acetate

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3COO^- \xrightarrow{electrolysis} C_2H_6 + 2CO_2 + 2e^-} \)

      Sodium acetate gives ethane at the anode, not methane.

    12. Decarboxylation with soda lime

      \( \mathrm{CH_3COONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\Delta]{CaO} CH_4 + Na_2CO_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5COONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\Delta]{CaO} C_6H_6 + Na_2CO_3} \)

      Soda-lime decarboxylation removes one carbon from sodium carboxylate.

    13. Controlled oxidation of methane and ethane

      \( \mathrm{CH_4 + \dfrac{1}{2}O_2 \xrightarrow[high\ pressure]{Cu} CH_3OH} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_4 + O_2 \xrightarrow{Mo_2O_3} HCHO + H_2O} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_2H_6 + O_2 \xrightarrow[\Delta]{(CH_3COO)_2Mn} CH_3COOH + H_2O} \)

      Copper clue gives methanol; \(\mathrm{Mo_2O_3}\) clue gives methanal.

    14. Hydration of terminal alkynes

      \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH \xrightarrow[333\,K]{H_2O,\ Hg^{2+}/H^+} CH_3CHO} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3C{\equiv}CH \xrightarrow[333\,K]{H_2O,\ Hg^{2+}/H^+} CH_3COCH_3} \)

      Ethyne gives ethanal; propyne gives propanone after keto-enol tautomerism.

    15. Markovnikov and peroxide-effect addition of HBr

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + HBr \rightarrow CH_3CHBrCH_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + HBr \xrightarrow{ROOR} CH_3CH_2CH_2Br} \)

      Peroxide effect is anti-Markovnikov and is characteristic for \(\mathrm{HBr}\).

    16. Hydroboration-oxidation of propene

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow[2.\ H_2O_2/NaOH]{1.\ B_2H_6} CH_3CH_2CH_2OH} \)

      This gives anti-Markovnikov alcohol without rearrangement.

    17. Cold alkaline \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) hydroxylation

      \( \mathrm{CH_2{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow[273\,K]{cold,\ dilute,\ alkaline\ KMnO_4} HOCH_2CH_2OH} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow[273\,K]{cold,\ dilute,\ alkaline\ KMnO_4} CH_3CH(OH)CH_2OH} \)

      Cold dilute alkaline permanganate gives vicinal glycol, not cleavage.

    18. Hot acidic \(\mathrm{KMnO_4}\) oxidative cleavage

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CHCH_3 \xrightarrow[\Delta]{KMnO_4/H^+} 2CH_3COOH} \)

      \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_2C{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow[\Delta]{KMnO_4/H^+} (CH_3)_2CO + CO_2 + H_2O} \)

      Strong oxidation cleaves the double bond; aldehyde stage is not retained.

    19. Ozonolysis of alkenes

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CHCH_3 \xrightarrow[Zn/H_2O]{O_3} 2CH_3CHO} \)

      \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_2C{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow[Zn/H_2O]{O_3} (CH_3)_2CO + HCHO} \)

      Reductive ozonolysis gives aldehydes/ketones, not carboxylic acids.

    20. Reverse ozonolysis reconstruction

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CHO + CH_3CH_2CHO \Rightarrow CH_3CH{=}CHCH_2CH_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_2CO + HCHO \Rightarrow (CH_3)_2C{=}CH2} \)

      Join the two carbonyl carbons back with a double bond to identify the alkene.

    21. Etard reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 \xrightarrow[2.\ H_3O^+]{1.\ CrO_2Cl_2/CS_2} C_6H_5CHO} \)

      Etard reaction oxidises the benzylic methyl group of toluene to benzaldehyde, not benzoic acid.

    22. Side-chain oxidation of alkylbenzene

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 \xrightarrow[2.\ H_3O^+]{1.\ KMnO_4/KOH,\ \Delta} C_6H_5COOH} \)

      Hot alkaline permanganate fully oxidises benzylic side chain to benzoic acid.

    23. Cannizzaro reaction of benzaldehyde

      \( \mathrm{2C_6H_5CHO + NaOH(conc.) \rightarrow C_6H_5CH_2OH + C_6H_5COONa} \)

      Benzaldehyde has no \(\alpha\)-hydrogen, so it undergoes Cannizzaro reaction in concentrated alkali.

    24. Aldol condensation of acetone

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3COCH_3 \xrightarrow[\Delta]{Ba(OH)_2} CH_3COCH{=}C(CH_3)_2 + H_2O} \)

      Acetone self-condensation followed by dehydration gives mesityl oxide.

    25. Aldol condensation of aldehydes

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3CHO \xrightarrow[dil.]{NaOH} CH_3CH(OH)CH_2CHO \xrightarrow{\Delta} CH_3CH{=}CHCHO + H_2O} \)

      Aldol condensation gives an \(\alpha,\beta\)-unsaturated carbonyl compound after dehydration.

    26. Alcohol vapours over copper at \(573\,K\)

      \( \mathrm{RCH_2OH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} RCHO + H_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_2CHOH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} R_2C{=}O + H_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_3COH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} alkene + H_2O} \)

      Primary alcohols give aldehydes, secondary alcohols give ketones, and tertiary alcohols dehydrate.

    27. Clemmensen reduction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5COCH_3 \xrightarrow{Zn{-}Hg/conc.\ HCl} C_6H_5CH_2CH_3} \)

      Clemmensen reduction converts \(\mathrm{C=O}\) to \(\mathrm{CH_2}\) in acidic medium.

    28. Wolff-Kishner reduction

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O \xrightarrow[\Delta]{NH_2NH_2/KOH,\ glycol} R_2CH_2} \)

      Wolff-Kishner reduction also converts carbonyl to methylene, but under strongly basic conditions.

    29. Lassaigne test for nitrogen

      \( \mathrm{Na + C + N \rightarrow NaCN} \)

      \( \mathrm{6CN^- + Fe^{2+} \rightarrow [Fe(CN)_6]^{4-}} \)

      \( \mathrm{3[Fe(CN)_6]^{4-} + 4Fe^{3+} \rightarrow Fe_4[Fe(CN)_6]_3\downarrow} \)

      Prussian blue colour confirms nitrogen in sodium fusion extract.

    30. Kjeldahl method limitation

      Organic nitrogen \(\xrightarrow{conc.\ H_2SO_4}\) ammonium salt \(\rightarrow\) ammonia estimation.

      Suitable: amines such as aniline and benzylamine.

      Not suitable: nitro compounds, azo compounds, diazonium salts and pyridine-type ring nitrogen.

    31. High-ROI purification methods

      Steam distillation: aniline + water type pair.

      Vacuum distillation: high-boiling liquid that decomposes below normal boiling point.

      Fractional distillation: liquids with close boiling points.


    1. \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) reactivity of alkyl and benzylic halides

      \( \mathrm{R{-}X \rightarrow R^+ + X^-} \)

      \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) rate follows carbocation stability: benzylic/allylic and \(3^\circ\) halides react faster than \(2^\circ\) and \(1^\circ\) halides.

      Aryl and vinylic halides resist \(S_\mathrm{N}1\) because phenyl/vinylic carbocations are highly unstable.

    2. \(S_\mathrm{N}2\) reactivity and steric hindrance

      \( \mathrm{R{-}X + Nu^- \rightarrow R{-}Nu + X^-} \)

      Usual \(S_\mathrm{N}2\) order: methyl \(>\) \(1^\circ\) \(>\) \(2^\circ\) \(\gg\) \(3^\circ\).

      Neopentyl halides are primary but unusually slow because backside attack is blocked by \(\beta\)-branching.

    3. Alcohol to alkyl chloride using Lucas reagent

      \( \mathrm{R{-}OH + HCl \xrightarrow{ZnCl_2} R{-}Cl + H_2O} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2OH + HCl \xrightarrow{ZnCl_2} CH_3CH_2Cl + H_2O} \)

      \(\mathrm{ZnCl_2/HCl}\) converts alcohols to chlorides and is also used in Lucas-test logic.

    4. Swarts reaction

      \( \mathrm{R{-}Cl/R{-}Br \xrightarrow{AgF,\ Hg_2F_2,\ SbF_3,\ or\ CoF_2} R{-}F} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2Br \xrightarrow{CoF_2} CH_3CH_2F} \)

      Swarts reaction is the standard halogen-exchange method for alkyl fluorides.

    5. Finkelstein reaction

      \( \mathrm{R{-}Cl/R{-}Br + NaI \xrightarrow{dry\ acetone} R{-}I + NaCl/NaBr\downarrow} \)

      Dry acetone helps the reaction because \(\mathrm{NaCl}\) or \(\mathrm{NaBr}\) precipitates.

    6. Alkyl halide to Grignard reagent and deuterium quench

      \( \mathrm{R{-}Br + Mg \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} R{-}MgBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}MgBr + D_2O \rightarrow R{-}D + MgBrOD} \)

      \(\mathrm{D_2O}\) replaces the \(\mathrm{MgBr}\) position by deuterium, not by \(\mathrm{OD}\).

    7. Aryl halide to acid and then decarboxylation

      \( \mathrm{ArBr \xrightarrow{Mg/dry\ ether} ArMgBr \xrightarrow{CO_2/H_3O^+} ArCOOH} \)

      \( \mathrm{ArCOONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\Delta]{CaO} ArH + Na_2CO_3} \)

      This sequence replaces aryl halogen by hydrogen after carboxylation and soda-lime decarboxylation.

    8. Alkyl halide to nitrile and one-carbon chain extension

      \( \mathrm{R{-}Br + KCN \rightarrow R{-}CN + KBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CN \xrightarrow[\,\Delta\,]{aq.\ acid} R{-}COOH} \)

      \(\mathrm{KCN}\) gives nitrile and adds one carbon to the chain before hydrolysis.

    9. \(\mathrm{KCN}\) versus \(\mathrm{AgCN}\)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}X + KCN \rightarrow R{-}CN + KX} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}X + AgCN \rightarrow R{-}NC + AgX} \)

      \(\mathrm{KCN}\) mainly gives nitrile; \(\mathrm{AgCN}\) mainly gives isocyanide.

      \(\mathrm{R{-}CN}\) hydrolysis gives carboxylic acid; \(\mathrm{R{-}NC}\) hydrolysis gives amine/formic acid type products.

    10. Dehydrohalogenation with alcoholic \(\mathrm{KOH}\)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CH_2{-}CH_2{-}X \xrightarrow[\,\Delta\,]{alc.\ KOH} R{-}CH{=}CH_2 + KX + H_2O} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_3CBr \xrightarrow[\,\Delta\,]{alc.\ KOH} (CH_3)_2C{=}CH_2} \)

      Alcoholic \(\mathrm{KOH}\) favours elimination, while aqueous \(\mathrm{KOH}\) favours substitution.

    11. Elimination followed by normal HBr addition

      \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_3CBr \xrightarrow[\,\Delta\,]{alc.\ KOH} (CH_3)_2C{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow{HBr} (CH_3)_3CBr} \)

      In absence of peroxide, \(\mathrm{HBr}\) adds by Markovnikov rule.

    12. Anti-Markovnikov HBr addition followed by halide chemistry

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + HBr \xrightarrow{ROOR} CH_3CH_2CH_2Br} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2CH_2Br \xrightarrow{Mg/dry\ ether} CH_3CH_2CH_2MgBr} \)

      Peroxide effect is characteristic for \(\mathrm{HBr}\) and gives the less substituted bromide.

    13. Williamson ether synthesis versus elimination

      \( \mathrm{RONa + R'X \rightarrow ROR' + NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_3CONa + CH_3Br \rightarrow (CH_3)_3COCH_3 + NaBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3ONa + (CH_3)_3CBr \xrightarrow{\Delta} (CH_3)_2C{=}CH_2} \)

      Primary or methyl halides favour ether formation; tertiary halides with strong base favour elimination.

    14. Alkyl halide reduction to alkane

      \( \mathrm{R{-}Cl \xrightarrow{Zn/dil.\ HCl} R{-}H} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{ClCH_2CH_2CH(CH_3)CH_3 \xrightarrow{Zn/dil.\ HCl} CH_3CH_2CH(CH_3)CH_3} \)

      The halogen is replaced by hydrogen without changing the carbon skeleton.

    15. Wurtz reaction

      \( \mathrm{2R{-}X + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} R{-}R + 2NaX} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{2CH_3CH_2CH_2Cl + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} CH_3(CH_2)_4CH_3 + 2NaCl} \)

      Used for symmetrical alkane formation; straight-chain propyl halide gives \(n\)-hexane.

    16. Fittig reaction

      \( \mathrm{2C_6H_5Cl + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} C_6H_5{-}C_6H_5 + 2NaCl} \)

      Chlorobenzene gives biphenyl under Fittig coupling conditions.

    17. Wurtz-Fittig reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5Cl + CH_3Cl + 2Na \xrightarrow{dry\ ether} C_6H_5CH_3 + 2NaCl} \)

      Aryl halide plus alkyl halide gives alkylbenzene.

    18. Sandmeyer reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{Cu_2Cl_2/HCl} C_6H_5Cl + N_2} \)

      Diazonium salt replacement gives aryl chloride, often followed by Wurtz-Fittig or ring substitution steps.

    19. Aryl halide nitration followed by nucleophilic substitution

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5Cl \xrightarrow{HNO_3/H_2SO_4} o/p{-}ClC_6H_4NO_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{p{-}ClC_6H_4NO_2 \xrightarrow[443\,K]{NaOH} p{-}HOC_6H_4NO_2 \xrightarrow{H^+} p{-}nitrophenol} \)

      Para \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) activates aryl chloride towards nucleophilic substitution by \(\mathrm{OH^-}\).

    20. Benzylic halogenation and hydrolysis

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 + Br_2 \xrightarrow{h\nu} C_6H_5CH_2Br + HBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_2Br + KOH(aq.) \rightarrow C_6H_5CH_2OH + KBr} \)

      Light gives side-chain bromination; aqueous alkali gives benzyl alcohol.

    21. Benzylic Grignard chain extension

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_2Br \xrightarrow{Mg/dry\ ether} C_6H_5CH_2MgBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_2MgBr + HCHO \xrightarrow{H_3O^+} C_6H_5CH_2CH_2OH} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_2CH_2OH \xrightarrow{PBr_3} C_6H_5CH_2CH_2Br} \)

      Formaldehyde with Grignard reagent adds one \(\mathrm{CH_2OH}\) unit.

    22. Haloarene photochemical addition versus electrophilic substitution

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + 3Cl_2 \xrightarrow{h\nu} C_6H_6Cl_6} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{FeCl_3} C_6H_5Cl + HCl} \)

      \(\mathrm{Cl_2/h\nu}\) gives addition product BHC; \(\mathrm{Cl_2/FeCl_3}\) gives chlorobenzene.

    23. DDT chlorine-count anchor

      DDT \(=\) dichloro diphenyl trichloroethane.

      Total chlorine atoms \(=2\) on the two \(p\)-chlorophenyl rings \(+3\) on the trichloroethane part \(=5\).


    1. Alcohol vapours over copper at \(573\,K\)

      \( \mathrm{RCH_2OH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} RCHO + H_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_2CHOH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} R_2C{=}O + H_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_3COH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} alkene + H_2O} \)

      Primary alcohols give aldehydes, secondary alcohols give ketones, and tertiary alcohols dehydrate to alkenes.

    2. Alcohol dehydration to alkene

      \( \mathrm{C_3H_7OH \xrightarrow[443\,K]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + H_2O} \)

      \( \mathrm{2\text{-}methyl\text{-}2\text{-}butanol \xrightarrow[358\,K]{20\%\ H_3PO_4} 2\text{-}methyl\text{-}2\text{-}butene + H_2O} \)

      Dehydration usually gives the more substituted alkene as major product.

    3. Hydroboration-oxidation to anti-Markovnikov alcohol

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 \xrightarrow[2.\ H_2O_2/NaOH]{1.\ B_2H_6} CH_3CH_2CH_2OH} \)

      This gives propan-1-ol, not propan-2-ol.

    4. Acid-catalysed hydration to Markovnikov alcohol

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CH_2 + H_2O \xrightarrow{H^+} CH_3CH(OH)CH_3} \)

      Markovnikov hydration gives the more substituted alcohol.

    5. Lucas test for alcohol class

      \( \mathrm{R{-}OH + HCl \xrightarrow{anhy.\ ZnCl_2} R{-}Cl + H_2O} \)

      \(3^\circ\) alcohol: immediate turbidity; \(2^\circ\): slower turbidity; \(1^\circ\): very slow/no turbidity at room temperature.

    6. Alcohol to alkyl iodide in good yield

      \( \mathrm{R{-}OH + HI \rightarrow R{-}I + H_2O} \)

      Use \(\mathrm{KI + 95\%\ H_3PO_4}\) to generate \(\mathrm{HI}\) in situ.

      \(\mathrm{H_3PO_4}\) is preferred over \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4}\) because it is non-oxidising.

    7. Anisole cleavage with \(\mathrm{HI}\)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OCH_3 + HI \rightarrow C_6H_5OH + CH_3I} \)

      Aryl-alkyl ether cleavage occurs at the alkyl-O bond; phenyl iodide is not formed.

    8. Ether cleavage with \(\mathrm{HBr}\)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}O{-}R' + HBr \rightarrow R{-}Br + R'{-}OH} \)

      Aryl-alkyl ethers cleave on the alkyl side because the aryl-O bond has partial double-bond character.

    9. Williamson synthesis of ether

      \( \mathrm{RONa + R'X \rightarrow ROR' + NaX} \)

      \( \mathrm{(CH_3)_3CONa + CH_3Br \rightarrow (CH_3)_3COCH_3 + NaBr} \)

      Methyl or primary halide works best; tertiary halide with strong base favours elimination.

    10. Cumene process for phenol

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH(CH_3)_2 \xrightarrow{O_2} cumene\ hydroperoxide \xrightarrow{dil.\ acid} C_6H_5OH + CH_3COCH_3} \)

      Industrially important route to phenol; acetone is the co-product.

    11. Phenol from benzenesulphonic acid

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5SO_3H \xrightarrow{NaOH,\ fusion} C_6H_5ONa \xrightarrow{H^+} C_6H_5OH} \)

      Fusion with alkali followed by acidification gives phenol.

    12. Phenol from diazonium salt

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- + H_2O \xrightarrow{warm} C_6H_5OH + N_2 + HCl} \)

      Warm water hydrolysis of diazonium salt gives phenol.

    13. Phenol reduction with zinc dust

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow{Zn,\ \Delta} C_6H_6} \)

      Zinc dust removes oxygen from phenol to form benzene.

    14. Phenol oxidation to quinone

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow{Na_2Cr_2O_7/H_2SO_4} p\text{-}benzoquinone} \)

      Oxidation of phenol gives \(p\)-benzoquinone in the repeated PYQ pattern.

    15. Reimer-Tiemann reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow[2.\ H^+]{1.\ CHCl_3/aq.\ NaOH} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4CHO} \)

      Reimer-Tiemann introduces \(-\mathrm{CHO}\), mainly at ortho position, giving salicylaldehyde.

    16. Kolbe-Schmitt reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5OH \xrightarrow{NaOH} C_6H_5ONa \xrightarrow{CO_2} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COONa \xrightarrow{H^+} o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COOH} \)

      Kolbe-Schmitt introduces \(-\mathrm{COOH}\), giving salicylic acid.

    17. Aspirin formation from salicylic acid

      \( \mathrm{o\text{-}HOC_6H_4COOH + (CH_3CO)_2O \rightarrow o\text{-}CH_3COOC_6H_4COOH + CH_3COOH} \)

      Acetylation of phenolic \(-\mathrm{OH}\) gives acetylsalicylic acid.

    18. Etard reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 \xrightarrow[2.\ H_3O^+]{1.\ CrO_2Cl_2/CS_2} C_6H_5CHO} \)

      Etard oxidation stops at benzaldehyde, not benzoic acid.

    19. Side-chain oxidation of toluene

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CH_3 \xrightarrow[2.\ H_3O^+]{1.\ KMnO_4/KOH,\ \Delta} C_6H_5COOH} \)

      Hot alkaline permanganate fully oxidises benzylic side chain to benzoic acid.

    20. Gattermann-Koch reaction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_6 + CO + HCl \xrightarrow{AlCl_3/CuCl} C_6H_5CHO} \)

      Formylation of benzene gives benzaldehyde.

    21. Rosenmund reduction

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5COCl + H_2 \xrightarrow{Pd/BaSO_4} C_6H_5CHO + HCl} \)

      Poisoned catalyst stops acid chloride reduction at aldehyde.

    22. DIBAL-H reduction of nitrile to aldehyde

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CN \xrightarrow[2.\ H_2O]{1.\ DIBAL-H} C_6H_5CHO} \)

      DIBAL-H gives aldehyde from nitrile; complete reduction gives amine.

    23. PCC oxidation of primary alcohol to aldehyde

      \( \mathrm{RCH_2OH \xrightarrow{PCC} RCHO} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH{=}CHCH_2OH \xrightarrow{PCC} CH_3CH{=}CHCHO} \)

      PCC stops primary alcohol oxidation at aldehyde.

    24. Strong oxidation of primary alcohol or aldehyde to acid

      \( \mathrm{RCH_2OH \xrightarrow{KMnO_4/H^+} RCOOH} \)

      \( \mathrm{RCHO \xrightarrow{KMnO_4/H^+} RCOOH} \)

      Strong oxidants usually over-oxidise primary alcohols and aldehydes to carboxylic acids.

    25. Cannizzaro reaction

      \( \mathrm{2C_6H_5CHO + NaOH(conc.) \rightarrow C_6H_5CH_2OH + C_6H_5COONa} \)

      \( \mathrm{2HCHO + NaOH(conc.) \rightarrow CH_3OH + HCOONa} \)

      Aldehydes without \(\alpha\)-hydrogen undergo Cannizzaro reaction.

    26. Aldol condensation of aldehydes

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3CHO \xrightarrow{dil.\ NaOH} CH_3CH(OH)CH_2CHO \xrightarrow{\Delta} CH_3CH{=}CHCHO + H_2O} \)

      Aldehydes with \(\alpha\)-hydrogen give aldol products and then \(\alpha,\beta\)-unsaturated aldehydes.

    27. Aldol condensation of acetone

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3COCH_3 \xrightarrow[\Delta]{Ba(OH)_2} CH_3COCH{=}C(CH_3)_2 + H_2O} \)

      Acetone self-condensation followed by dehydration gives mesityl oxide.

    28. Crossed aldol condensation to benzalacetophenone

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CHO + C_6H_5COCH_3 \xrightarrow{base} C_6H_5CH{=}CHCOC_6H_5 + H_2O} \)

      Benzaldehyde plus acetophenone gives benzalacetophenone/chalcone type product.

    29. Cyanohydrin formation

      \( \mathrm{RCHO + HCN \rightarrow RCH(OH)CN} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O + HCN \rightarrow R_2C(OH)CN} \)

      \(-\mathrm{OH}\) and \(-\mathrm{CN}\) attach to the former carbonyl carbon.

    30. Carbonyl derivatives with nitrogen reagents

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O + NH_2OH \rightarrow R_2C{=}NOH + H_2O} \)

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O + NH_2NH_2 \rightarrow R_2C{=}NNH_2 + H_2O} \)

      \( \mathrm{RCHO + C_6H_5NH_2 \rightarrow RCH{=}NC_6H_5 + H_2O} \)

      \(\mathrm{NH_2OH}\) gives oxime; hydrazine gives hydrazone; primary amine gives Schiff base.

    31. Clemmensen reduction

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O \xrightarrow{Zn{-}Hg/conc.\ HCl} R_2CH_2} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{C_6H_5COCH_3 \xrightarrow{Zn{-}Hg/conc.\ HCl} C_6H_5CH_2CH_3} \)

      Acidic reduction converts carbonyl to methylene.

    32. Wolff-Kishner reduction

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O \xrightarrow[\Delta]{NH_2NH_2/KOH,\ glycol} R_2CH_2} \)

      Basic reduction converts carbonyl to methylene.

    33. Iodoform test

      \( \mathrm{CH_3COCH_3 + 3NaOI \rightarrow CHI_3\downarrow + CH_3COONa + 2NaOH} \)

      Ethanol, ethanal, methyl ketones and suitable secondary alcohols give yellow \(\mathrm{CHI_3}\).

    34. \(2,4\)-DNP and aldehyde/ketone distinction

      \( \mathrm{R_2C{=}O + 2,4\text{-}DNP \rightarrow 2,4\text{-}dinitrophenylhydrazone + H_2O} \)

      \(2,4\)-DNP detects carbonyl compounds but does not distinguish aldehydes from ketones.

    35. Tollens' and Fehling's aldehyde tests

      \( \mathrm{RCHO + 2[Ag(NH_3)_2]^+ + 3OH^- \rightarrow RCOO^- + 2Ag\downarrow + 4NH_3 + 2H_2O} \)

      Aldehydes reduce Tollens' reagent; most ketones do not.

    36. Acid chloride formation from carboxylic acid

      \( \mathrm{RCOOH + SOCl_2 \rightarrow RCOCl + SO_2 + HCl} \)

      \(\mathrm{SOCl_2}\) is a standard reagent for converting carboxylic acids to acid chlorides.

    37. Soda-lime Decarboxylation

      \( \mathrm{RCOONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\Delta]{CaO} RH + Na_2CO_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5COONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[\Delta]{CaO} C_6H_6 + Na_2CO_3} \)

      Product has one carbon less than the sodium carboxylate.

    38. Kolbe electrolysis of acetate

      \( \mathrm{2CH_3COO^- \xrightarrow{electrolysis} C_2H_6 + 2CO_2 + 2e^-} \)

      Acetate radicals couple to ethane; do not expect methane.

    39. HVZ reaction

      \( \mathrm{RCH_2COOH \xrightarrow{Br_2/red\ P} RCHBrCOOH} \)

      Carboxylic acids with \(\alpha\)-hydrogen undergo \(\alpha\)-halogenation.

    40. Formic acid dehydration to carbon monoxide

      \( \mathrm{HCOOH \xrightarrow[373\,K]{conc.\ H_2SO_4} CO + H_2O} \)

      The gas formed is \(\mathrm{CO}\), not \(\mathrm{CO_2}\).

    41. Acidity anchors for phenols and carboxylic acids

      Lower \(pK_a\) means stronger acid.

      Electron-withdrawing groups such as \(-\mathrm{NO_2}\), \(-\mathrm{CN}\) increase acidity.

      Electron-donating groups such as \(-\mathrm{CH_3}\), \(-\mathrm{OCH_3}\) generally decrease acidity.

      Carboxylic acids are stronger than phenols, and phenols are stronger than alcohols.


    1. Amide reduction versus Hofmann bromamide reaction

      \( \mathrm{RCONH_2 \xrightarrow[H_2O]{LiAlH_4} RCH_2NH_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{RCONH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/NaOH} RNH_2} \)

      \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\) keeps the carbonyl carbon as \(\mathrm{-CH_2-}\); Hofmann bromamide loses one carbon.

      Example: benzamide gives benzylamine with \(\mathrm{LiAlH_4}\), but aniline with \(\mathrm{Br_2/NaOH}\).

    2. Reverse carbon-count rule in Hofmann bromamide reaction

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CONH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/NaOH} CH_3NH_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2CONH_2 \xrightarrow{Br_2/KOH} CH_3CH_2NH_2} \)

      To obtain a \(C_n\) amine, choose a \(C_{n+1}\) primary amide.

    3. Carbylamine test for primary amines

      \( \mathrm{RNH_2 + CHCl_3 + 3KOH \xrightarrow{alc.} RNC + 3KCl + 3H_2O} \)

      Only \(1^\circ\) aliphatic and aromatic amines give the foul-smelling isocyanide.

      Secondary amines, tertiary amines, quaternary ammonium salts and amides do not give this test.

    4. Hinsberg test reagent and outcomes

      Hinsberg reagent \(=\mathrm{C_6H_5SO_2Cl}\), benzene sulphonyl chloride.

      \(1^\circ\) amine gives sulphonamide soluble in alkali.

      \(2^\circ\) amine gives sulphonamide insoluble in alkali.

      \(3^\circ\) amine does not form the same sulphonamide.

    5. Sulphonamide formation from secondary amines

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5SO_2Cl + HN(CH_3)_2 \rightarrow C_6H_5SO_2N(CH_3)_2 + HCl} \)

      If the product contains \(\mathrm{ArSO_2N{-}}\), the reagent is usually an aryl sulphonyl chloride.

    6. Nitrile hydrolysis and reduction

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CN \xrightarrow[\,\Delta\,]{H_3O^+} R{-}COOH} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CN \xrightarrow[H_2O]{LiAlH_4} R{-}CH_2NH_2} \)

      Nitrile hydrolysis gives carboxylic acid; strong reduction gives primary amine.

    7. One-carbon chain extension using \(\mathrm{KCN}\)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}Br + KCN \rightarrow R{-}CN + KBr} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CN \xrightarrow[\,\Delta\,]{aq.\ acid} R{-}COOH} \)

      \(\mathrm{KCN}\) adds one carbon because the carbon of \(-\mathrm{CN}\) becomes part of the final carboxyl group.

    8. \(\mathrm{AgCN}\): nitrile versus isocyanide products

      \( \mathrm{R{-}X + KCN \rightarrow R{-}CN + KX} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}X + AgCN \rightarrow R{-}NC + AgX} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CN \xrightarrow{reduction} RCH_2NH_2} \)

      \( \mathrm{R{-}NC \xrightarrow{reduction} RNHCH_3} \)

      \(\mathrm{KCN}\) mainly gives nitrile; \(\mathrm{AgCN}\) mainly gives isocyanide.

    9. DIBAL-H reduction of nitrile to aldehyde

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CN \xrightarrow[2.\ H_2O]{1.\ DIBAL-H} C_6H_5CHO} \)

      DIBAL-H gives partial reduction of nitrile to aldehyde.

      Complete reduction gives amine, not aldehyde.

    10. Stephen reduction of nitrile

      \( \mathrm{R{-}CN \xrightarrow[H_3O^+]{SnCl_2/HCl} R{-}CHO} \)

      With further strong reduction by \(\mathrm{HI/red\ P}\), aldehyde can be reduced to hydrocarbon level.

      Overall: \(\mathrm{-CN}\rightarrow-\mathrm{CHO}\rightarrow-\mathrm{CH_3}\).

    11. Diazotisation of aromatic primary amines

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NH_2 + NaNO_2 + 2HCl \xrightarrow{273{-}278\,K} C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- + NaCl + 2H_2O} \)

      Benzene diazonium chloride is stable only at low temperature, around \(273{-}278\,\mathrm{K}\).

    12. Diazonium salt to phenol

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- + H_2O \xrightarrow{warm} C_6H_5OH + N_2 + HCl} \)

      Warm water hydrolysis of diazonium salt gives phenol.

    13. Sandmeyer reaction: diazonium to aryl chloride

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{Cu_2Cl_2/HCl} C_6H_5Cl + N_2} \)

      Diazonium replacement by chlorine is often used in aromatic substitution sequences.

    14. Diazonium cyanation followed by hydrolysis

      \( \mathrm{ArNH_2 \xrightarrow{NaNO_2/HCl,\ 273\,K} ArN_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{CuCN/KCN} ArCN \xrightarrow{H_3O^+} ArCOOH} \)

      This converts aromatic amine into aromatic carboxylic acid through nitrile formation.

    15. Diazonium replacement by hydrogen

      \( \mathrm{ArN_2^+Cl^- \xrightarrow{H_3PO_2/H_2O} ArH + N_2 + HCl} \)

      Hypophosphorous acid removes the diazonium group and replaces it by hydrogen.

      Example: nitrobenzene \(\rightarrow\) aniline \(\rightarrow\) diazonium salt \(\rightarrow\) benzene.

    16. Aliphatic primary amine with nitrous acid

      \( \mathrm{RNH_2 \xrightarrow{NaNO_2/HCl,\ 0^\circ C} ROH + N_2 + H_2O} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{C_2H_5NH_2 \rightarrow C_2H_5OH \xrightarrow{Cu,\ 573\,K} CH_3CHO} \)

      Aliphatic diazonium salts are unstable and usually give alcohols.

    17. Aniline protection before nitration

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NH_2 \xrightarrow{(CH_3CO)_2O} C_6H_5NHCOCH_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NHCOCH_3 \xrightarrow{HNO_3/H_2SO_4} p\text{-}NO_2C_6H_4NHCOCH_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{p\text{-}NO_2C_6H_4NHCOCH_3 \xrightarrow{H^+,\ hydrolysis} p\text{-}NO_2C_6H_4NH_2} \)

      Acetylation protects aniline and gives controlled para nitration.

    18. Aniline protection before bromination

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NH_2 \xrightarrow{(CH_3CO)_2O} C_6H_5NHCOCH_3} \)

      \( \mathrm{C_6H_5NHCOCH_3 \xrightarrow{Br_2/CH_3COOH} p\text{-}BrC_6H_4NHCOCH_3} \)

      Protection avoids uncontrolled tribromination and favours the para product.

    19. Ammonolysis of alkyl halide

      \( \mathrm{C_2H_5Br + NH_3(excess) \rightarrow C_2H_5NH_2 + HBr} \)

      Excess ammonia favours primary amine formation.

    20. Gabriel phthalimide synthesis

      \( \mathrm{potassium\ phthalimide + R{-}X \rightarrow N\text{-}alkylphthalimide \rightarrow RNH_2} \)

      Gabriel synthesis is used for aliphatic primary amines.

      It is not used as a direct preparation method for aromatic primary amines.

    21. Schiff base formation

      \( \mathrm{RCHO + R'NH_2 \rightarrow RCH{=}NR' + H_2O} \)

      Example: \( \mathrm{C_6H_5CHO + C_6H_5NH_2 \rightarrow C_6H_5CH{=}NC_6H_5 + H_2O} \)

      Aldehyde plus primary amine gives imine or Schiff base.

    22. Secondary amine preparation through imine reduction

      \( \mathrm{RCHO + R'NH_2 \rightarrow RCH{=}NR' \xrightarrow{reduction} RCH_2NHR'} \)

      This route is useful when the target is a secondary amine, not a primary amine.

    23. Benzoylation of amines

      \( \mathrm{RNH_2 + C_6H_5COCl \rightarrow RNHCOC_6H_5 + HCl} \)

      Example: \(p\)-methylaniline gives \( \mathrm{p\text{-}CH_3C_6H_4NHCOC_6H_5} \).

      The introduced acyl group is benzoyl, \(\mathrm{C_6H_5CO{-}}\).

    24. Amine basicity anchors

      Lower \(pK_b\) means stronger base.

      Aliphatic amines are stronger bases than aniline-type aromatic amines because the aniline lone pair is resonance-delocalised into the ring.

      Basicity trend by hybridisation/lone-pair availability: \(\mathrm{RNH_2 > RN{=}CHR > RC{\equiv}N}\).

    25. IUPAC naming of tertiary aromatic amines

      When nitrogen is directly attached to benzene, parent name is benzenamine.

      Groups attached to nitrogen are written as \(N\)-substituents.

      Example: \( \mathrm{C_6H_5N(CH_3)(C_2H_5)} \) is \(N\)-ethyl-\(N\)-methylbenzenamine.

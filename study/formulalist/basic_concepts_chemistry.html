Basic Concepts of Chemistry

  1. Significant digits are the meaningful digits in a number, including all known digits plus one estimated digit, indicating the precision of a measurement.

    (Check this video tutorial)

    1. All non-zero digits are significant. For example in 285 cm, there are three significant figures and in 0.25 mL, there are two significant figures.
    2. Zeros preceding to first non-zero digit are not significant. Such zero indicates the position of decimal point. Thus, 0.03 has one significant figure and 0.0052 has two significant figures.
    3. Zeros between two non-zero digits are significant. Thus, 2.005 has four significant figures.
    4. Zeros at the end or right of a number are significant, provided they are on the right side of the decimal point. For example, \(0.200 \, \text{g}\) has three significant figures. But, if otherwise, the terminal zeros are not significant if there is no decimal point. For example, 100 has only one significant figure, but 100.0 has four significant figures. Such numbers are better represented in scientific notation. We can express the number 100 as \(1 \times 10^2\) for one significant figure, \(1.0 \times 10^2\) for two significant figures, and \(1.00 \times 10^2\) for three significant figures.
    5. Counting the numbers of objects, for example, 2 balls or 20 eggs, have infinite significant figures as these are exact numbers and can be represented by writing an infinite number of zeros after placing a decimal, i.e., 2 = 2.000000 or 20 = 20.000000.
    6. All constants (like Avogadro's Number, or Planck's constant or refractive index of a medium etc or ) also have infinite significant figures as these are "defined" values.
    2. Dalton's Atomic theory

    1. Matter consists of indivisible atoms.
    2. All atoms of a given element have identical properties, including identical mass. Atoms of different elements differ in mass.
    3. Compounds are formed when atoms of different elements combine in a fixed ratio.
    4. Chemical reactions involve reorganisation of atoms. These are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
    1. One atomic mass unit is defined as a mass exactly equal to one-twelfth of the mass of one carbon – 12 atom; \(1 \text{ amu} = 1.66056 \times 10^{-24} \text{ g}\)
    2. Mass of an atom of hydrogen \[ = 1.6736 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{g} \] Thus, in terms of amu, the mass of hydrogen atom \[ = \dfrac{1.6736 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{g}}{1.66056 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{g}} \] \[ = 1.0078 \, \text{amu} \] \[ = 1.0080 \, \text{amu} \] Similarly, the mass of oxygen-16 (\(^{16}\text{O}\)) atom would be 15.995 amu.
    3. Average Atomic Mass
      Many naturally occurring elements exist as more than one isotope. When we take into account the existence of these isotopes and their relative abundance (per cent occurrence), the average atomic mass of that element can be computed.
    4. Formula mass
      Used for ionic compounds, which contain metals and non-metals. It's the sum of the atomic masses of the ions in the compound's simplest formula. Formula mass is a more general term and can refer to all compounds.
    5. Molecular mass
      Used for covalent compounds, which contain only non-metals. It's the sum of the atomic masses of the atoms in a molecule's actual formula. Molecular mass is also used to identify unknown chemicals and determine a compound's formula.
    4. Molar Mass
    1. One mole contains exactly \(6.02214076 \times 10^{23}\) elementary entities.
    2. This number is the fixed numerical value of the Avogadro constant, \(N_A\), when expressed in the unit \(\text{mol}^{-1}\) and is called the Avogadro number.
    3. In order to determine this number precisely, the mass of a carbon–12 atom was determined by a mass spectrometer and found to be equal to \(1.992648 \times 10^{-23}\) g. Knowing that one mole of carbon weighs 12 g, the number of atoms in it is equal to:

      4. The amount of substance, symbol \(n\), of a system is a measure of the number of specified elementary entities. An elementary entity may be an atom, a molecule, an ion, an electron, any other particle or specified group of particles. It may be emphasized that the mole of a substance always contains the same number of entities, no matter what the substance may be.

      \[ \dfrac{12 \, \text{g/mol} \, ^{12}\text{C}}{1.992648 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{g}/^{12}\text{C atom}} = 6.0221367 \times 10^{23} \, \text{atoms/mol} \]

    4. Hence, so many entities (atoms, molecules or any other particle) constitute one mole of a particular substance.
    5. We can, therefore, say that 1 mol of hydrogen atoms = \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) atoms
    6. 1 mol of water molecules = \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) water molecules
    7. 1 mol of sodium chloride = \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) formula units of sodium chloride
    8. The mass of one mole of a substance in grams is called its molar mass.
    9. The molar mass in grams is numerically equal to atomic/molecular/formula mass in u.
    10. Molar mass of water = 18.02 g mol\(^{-1}\)
    11. Molar mass of sodium chloride = 58.5 g mol\(^{-1}\)
    5. Percentage Composition
    1. Mass % of an element = \[ \dfrac{\text{mass of that element in the compound} \times 100}{\text{molar mass of the compound}} \]
    2. Molar mass of water = 18.02 g \[ \text{Mass % of hydrogen} = \dfrac{2 \times 1.008}{18.02} \times 100 = 11.18 \] \[ \text{Mass % of oxygen} = \dfrac{16.00}{18.02} \times 100 = 88.79 \]
    3. Let us take one more example. What is the percentage of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in ethanol?
      Molecular formula of ethanol is: \( \text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH} \)
      Molar mass of ethanol is:
      \( (2 \times 12.01 + 6 \times 1.008 + 16.00) \, \text{g} = 46.068 \, \text{g} \)
      Mass per cent of carbon:
      \[ = \dfrac{24.02 \, \text{g}}{46.068 \, \text{g}} \times 100 = 52.14\% \]
      Mass per cent of hydrogen:
      \[ = \dfrac{6.048 \, \text{g}}{46.068 \, \text{g}} \times 100 = 13.13\% \]
      Mass per cent of oxygen:
      \[ = \dfrac{16.00 \, \text{g}}{46.068 \, \text{g}} \times 100 = 34.73\% \]
    6. Empirical vs Molecular Formula
    1. An empirical formula represents the simplest whole number ratio of various atoms present in a compound, whereas, the molecular formula shows the exact number of different types of atoms present in a molecule of a compound.
      2. A compound contains 4.07% hydrogen, 24.27% carbon and 71.65% chlorine. Its molar mass is 98.96 g. What are its empirical and molecular formulas?

      1. Step 1. Conversion of mass per cent to grams Since we are having mass per cent, it is convenient to use 100 g of the compound as the starting material. Thus, in the 100 g sample of the above compound, 4.07g hydrogen, 24.27g carbon and 71.65g chlorine are present.
      2. Step 2. Convert into number moles of each element
        Divide the masses obtained above by respective atomic masses of various elements. This gives the number of moles of constituent elements in the compound
        Moles of hydrogen \[ = \dfrac{4.07 \, \text{g}}{1.008 \, \text{g}} = 4.04 \]
        Moles of carbon \[ = \dfrac{24.27 \, \text{g}}{12.01 \, \text{g}} = 2.021 \]
        Moles of chlorine \[ = \dfrac{71.65 \, \text{g}}{35.453 \, \text{g}} = 2.021 \]
      3. Step 3. Divide each of the mole values obtained above by the smallest number amongst them
        Since 2.021 is smallest value, division by it gives a ratio of 2:1:1 for H:C:Cl.
        In case the ratios are not whole numbers then they may be converted into whole number by multiplying by the suitable coefficient.
      4. Step 4. Write down the empirical formula by mentioning the numbers after writing the symbols of respective elements
        \( \text{CH}_2\text{Cl} \) is, thus, the empirical formula of the above compound.
      5. Step 5. Writing molecular formula
        (a) Determine empirical formula mass by adding the atomic masses of various atoms present in the empirical formula. For \( \text{CH}_2\text{Cl} \), empirical formula mass is \( 12.01 + (2 \times 1.008) + 35.453 = 49.48 \, \text{g} \)
      6. (b) Divide Molar mass by empirical formula mass
        \[ \dfrac{\text{Molar mass}}{\text{Empirical formula mass}} = \dfrac{98.96 \, \text{g}}{49.48 \, \text{g}} = 2 = (n) \]
      7. (c) Multiply empirical formula by \( n \) obtained above to get the molecular formula
        Empirical formula = \( \text{CH}_2\text{Cl} \), \( n = 2 \). Hence molecular formula is \( \text{C}_2\text{H}_4\text{Cl}_2 \).

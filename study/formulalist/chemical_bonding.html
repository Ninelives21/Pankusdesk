  2. LATTICE ENERGY AND BORN-HABER CYCLE
    1. Lattice Energy
      • Lattice energy is the amount of energy released when one mole of an ionic compound is formed.
      • Conversely, lattice energy is the amount of energy required to break 1 mole of an ionic compound into its gaseous cation and gaseous anion.
      • No direct method to determine lattice energy.
    2. Born-Haber Cycle of NaCl
      • Born Haber Cycle is an indirect method of finding Lattice energy.
      • Heat/Enthalpy of formation (∆Hf) This is different from Lattice energy which was formed from gaseous ions.

      • Heat/Enthalpy of Formation (ΔHf)

        This is different from Lattice energy which was formed from gaseous ions.

        • Amount of heat released when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their pure and stable states.
        • Example reaction: \( \text{Na (s)} + \frac{1}{2} \text{Cl}_2 \text{(g)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl (s)} \)
      • How are Lattice Energy and Heat of Formation related
        • Na -> Na (g) needs sublimation energy (+ SE).
        • Na = Na+ needs Ionisation Energy (+IE).
        • Cl2 -> Cl needs Bond Dissociation Energy (+BE/2).
        • Cl -> Cl- needs Electron Affinity (-EA).
        • Na+ + Cl- -> NaCl releases Lattice Energy (- LE).
    3. Introduction to Hess’s Law
      • ∆Hf = SE + IE + BE/2 - EA - LE
    4. Born-Haber Cycle of MgO
    5. Factors affecting Lattice Energy Lattice energy is also calculated using the charge and distance between ions.
      • \[ \text{Lattice energy} = \frac{-K \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2} \]
      • K = Proportionality constant
      • q1 = Charge of anion
      • q2 = Charge of cation
      • r = Internuclear distance
      • Lattice energy is proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the distance squared.
    6. Note:
      • Lattice Energy is proportional to stability
      • Lattice Energy is proportional to melting point (M.Pt)
      • Lattice Energy is proportional to hardness

  3. LEWIS DOT Structure
    1. Lewis Dot Structure - Intro
      • Structure → Only see outermost e⁻
      • Octet Rule (8e⁻ → Valence Shell) (Lewis theory)
      • Octet Rule can be broken for central atom; but central can have 8e⁻ or more
    2. Rules for Selecting Central Atom (C.A)
      • Least in number OR least electronegativity OR Size largest OR Atomic No. highest
      • H and F can never be central atom
      • Common C.A's are : O,S,N,P,C,Si,Cl
      • Octet of central atom must be complete (duplet in case of H)
      • C.A can have 8 or more electrons
      • C.A tries to remain in maximum covalency (covalency = number of shared electrons)
      • Some imp data of common central atoms:
        Element Number of valence e- Co-valencies Max Co-valency
        O 6 1,2 2
        S 6 2,4,6 6
        N 5 3,4 4
        P 5 3,5 5
        C 4 4 4
        Si 4 4 4
        Cl 7 1,3,5,7 7
    3. Rule for +ve and -ve
      • If +ve charge is there, it will be on the central atom (e.g., H₃O⁺, NH₄⁺).
      • If -ve charge is there, it will be on the corner atom (NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻).
    4. Calculation rules
      • Find Q = Total electrons = Valence electrons of all atoms (-ve charge) - (+ve charge)
      • Bond pair electrons (BPE) = 2 × No. of bonds
      • one pair electrons (LPE) = Q - BPE
      • Remember that LPE is used to either (1) Create lone pair OR (2) Get used in double or triple bond.
    5. Formal Charge (inside example 5 : \( \text{NO}_3^- \))
      • \( \text{FC} = V - \text{LPe} - \dfrac{\text{BPe}}{2} \) (where V = number of valence electrons)

  2. IONIC BONDS

    1. Ionic bond/Electrovalent Bond : Click here
      • Between metal and non-metal
      • Complete transfer of electron - octet is complete
      • Ionic bonds are non directional - no "dash" between atoms of an ionic bond
      • example: \( \text{Na} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+ + \text{e}^- \)
        example: \( \text{Cl}+ \text{e}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}^- \)
        Note: This transfer of electron is happening in gaseous states of Na and Cl.
      • \( \text{Na}^+ \text{and} + \text{Cl}^- \) attract each other with electrostatic force of attraction - which is very strong.
      • Electrostatic force: \( F = \dfrac{K q_1 q_2}{r^2} \) ,
        Note that 'r' will be very small
      • Ionic bonds do not have a shape or direction

    2. Lewis Dot Structure In Ionic bonds - example, NaCl and \(\text{Mg Cl}_2 \): Click here

    3. Electrovalency : Click here
      • Number of electrons given by the metal or the number of electrons gained by the non-metal during the formation of an electrovalent bond.
      • Electrovalency is always taken as positive.
      • Electrovalency of elements groupwise:
        1. Group 1 elements Li, Na, K etc \(\rightarrow\) 1
        2. Group 2 elements Be, Mg, Ca, Sr etc \(\rightarrow\) 2
        3. Group 13 elements B, Al, Ga etc \(\rightarrow\) 3
        4. Group 14 elements C, Si, Ge etc \(\rightarrow\) do not form ionic bonds
        5. Group 15 elements N, P, As etc \(\rightarrow\) 3
        6. Group 16 elements O, S, Se etc \(\rightarrow\) 2
        7. Group 17 elements F, Cl, Br etc \(\rightarrow\) 1
        8. Group 3 to 12 (d block) elements \(\rightarrow\) variable valencies
          1. example Fe \(\rightarrow\) 2,3
          2. example Cu \(\rightarrow\) 1,2

    4. Energy Terms involved in Ionic Bond Formation / Favourable conditions in formation of Ionic bonds : Click here
      • When energy is relased, a stable compound is formed; if energy is to be absorbed, it should be as low as possible.
      • Ionization Energy: energy absorbed in order to remove and electron from a metal in its gaseous state.
        • For stable compound, IE should be as less as possible \(\rightarrow\) min
      • Electron Affinity : energy relased when a non-metal accepts an electron.
        • For stable compound, EA should be as high as possible \(\rightarrow\) max
      • Lattice Energy : energy released when a gaseous cation and a gaseous anion combine to form one mole of a solid ionic compound
        • Lattice energy can be conversely thought of as the energy needed to break an ionic compound
        • For stable compound, LE should be as high as possible \(\rightarrow\) max

  3. LATTICE ENERGY AND BORN-HABER CYCLE

    1. Lattice Energy
      • Two elements in their gaseous state (example, Na and Cl) will combine in order to lose energy and become more stable. This energy lost is the 'Lattice Energy'
      • Lattice energy is the amount of energy released when one mole of an ionic compound is formed.
      • Conversely, lattice energy is the amount of energy required to break 1 mole of an ionic compound into its gaseous cation and gaseous anion.
      • No direct method to determine lattice energy. There's an indirect method which is called Born Haber cycle.

    2. Born-Haber Cycle of NaCl
      • Born Haber Cycle is an indirect method of finding Lattice energy.
      • Heat/Enthalpy of Formation (ΔHf)
        • Amount of heat released when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their pure and stable states.
        • This is different from Lattice energy which was formed from gaseous ions.
        • Example reaction: \( \text{Na (s)} + \frac{1}{2} \text{Cl}_2 \text{(g)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl (s)} \)
      • How are Lattice Energy and Heat of Formation related
        • Na -> Na (g) needs sublimation energy (+ SE).
        • Na = \(\text{Na}^+\) needs Ionisation Energy (+IE).
        • Cl2 -> Cl needs Bond Dissociation Energy (+BE/2).
        • Cl -> Cl- needs Electron Affinity (-EA).
        • Na+ + Cl- -> NaCl releases Lattice Energy (- LE).

    3. Introduction to Hess’s Law
      • Hess's law states that regardless of the multiple stages or steps of a reaction, the total enthalpy change for the reaction is the sum of all changes
      • ∆Hf = SE + IE + BE/2 - EA - LE

    4. Born-Haber Cycle of MgO
      • Two electrons have to be removed/added
      • ΔHf = SE + \(\text{IE}_1\) + \(\text{IE}_2\) + \(\dfrac{DE}{2} \) - \(\text{EA}_1\) + \(\text{EA}_2\) - LA))

    5. Factors affecting Lattice Energy Lattice energy is also calculated using the charge and distance between ions.
      • \( \text{Eletrostatic force of attraction} = \dfrac{-K \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2} \)
      • K = Proportionality constant
      • q1 = Charge of anion
      • q2 = Charge of cation
      • r = Internuclear distance
      • Lattice energy is proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the distance between them.
      • L.E. \( \propto \) Charge
      • L.E. \( \propto \dfrac{1}{\text{r}} \)(and not \(\text{r}^2\); remember F \( \propto \dfrac{1}{\text{r}^2} \), not LE)
      • Lattice Energy is proportional to stability
      • Lattice Energy is proportional to melting point (M.Pt)
      • Lattice Energy is proportional to hardness

  4. FAJAN’S RULE

    1. Intro to Fajan’s Rule
      • Ionic bonds have some cobalent character
      • No bond is completely ionic or completely covalent
      • Fajan's rule is used to determine which of two given ionic compounds is more covalent
      • Consequently it will tell which of the two ionic compounds have greater
        1. melting boiling point
        2. solubility in water
      • Limitation of Fajan's rule is that it is a qualitative and not quantitative law.
      • You can only compare the covalent character of two ionic compounds.
    2. Why do ionic bonds have covalent character?
      • The extra electrons in the anion starts to get "polarized" towards the cations.
      • The sperical shape of the electron cloud is distorted into an ellipse.
      • This is called polarization of anions.
      • This sort of ends up in sharing of electrons.
      • This happens due to the polarizing power of the cation.
      • The anion is said to have been polarized.
      • More the polarizing power of the cation, more is the covalaent character of the compound.
      • More the polarization of the anion, more is the covalaent character of the compound.
      • Every ioning ionic compounds goes through this.

    3. Cation - factors affecting polarising power
      1. Size of cation \(\rightarrow\) smaller the cation, more is the polarizing power and more is the covalent character.
      2. Charge of cation \(\rightarrow\) more the charge on cation, more is the polarizing power and more is the covalent character.
      3. Pseudo Noble Gas Configuration (CNGC) :
        1. PNGC is a configuration like noble gas \(\rightarrow\) 8 electrons in the outermost shell : \(\text{ns}^2\text{np}^6\text{nd}^{10}\) (18 electrons)
        2. Any cation with the above configuration will have very high polarizing power.
        3. Let's take example of Cu:
          • Cu = \(\text{[Ar]3d}^{10}\text{4s}^1\)
          • Remember, Cu's electronic configuration is an exception; so it is not \(\text{[Ar]3d}^{9}\text{4s}^2\)
          • Electronic configuration of \(\text{Cu}^{+}\) will be \(\text{[Ar]3d}^{10}\) as the outermost \(\text{4s}^1\) will jump out.
          • Electronic configuration of \(\text{Cu}^{+}\) is now a PNGC.
          • Same goes for \(\text{Ag}^{+}\) and \(\text{Au}^{+}\).
          • All three, \(\text{Cu}^{+}\), \(\text{Ag}^{+}\) and \(\text{Au}^{+}\), have very strong polarizing power and compunds with them are more covalent character.
        4. Next let's take example of Zn:
          • Zn = \(\text{[Ar]3d}^{10}\text{4s}^2\)
          • Electronic configuration of \(\text{Zn}^{2+}\) will be \(\text{[Ar]3d}^{10}\) as the outermost \(\text{4s}^2\) will jump out.
          • Electronic configuration of \(\text{Zn}^{2+}\) is now a PNGC.
          • Same goes for \(\text{Cd}^{2+}\) and \(\text{Hg}^{+}\).
          • All three, \(\text{Zn}^{2+}\), \(\text{Cd}^{2+}\) and \(\text{Hg}^{2+}\), have very strong polarizing power and compunds with them are more covalent character.
      4. Examples:
        • Arrange the below in ascending order of Polarizing power
          \(\text{Li}^+\) > \(\text{Na}^+\) > \(\text{K}^+\) > \(\text{Rb}^+\)
        • Arrange the below in ascending order of Polarizing power
          \(\text{Be}^{2+}\) > \(\text{Mg}^{2+}\) > \(\text{Ca}^{2+}\) > \(\text{Sr}^{2+}\)
        • Arrange the below in ascending order of Polarizing power
          \(\text{Na}^{+}\) < \(\text{Mg}^{2+}\) < \(\text{Al}^{3+}\)
    4. Anion - factors affecting polarisation
      • Size of anion \(\rightarrow\) larger the cation
      • Charge of anion \(\rightarrow\) more the charge on anion, more is the polarization and more is the covalent character.
    5. Fajan’s Rule summary
      • According to Fajan's Rule, covalent character of an ionic bond will be more if
        • Cation's polarizing power is more
        • Anion's polarization is more
      • More covalent character if:
        • Cation - smaller size
        • Cation - More charge
        • Cation - has PNGC (Pseudo Noble Gas Configuration
          1. \(\text{Cu}^+\), \(\text{Ag}^+\), \(\text{Au}^+\)
          2. \(\text{Zn}^{2+}\), \(\text{Cd}^{2+}\), \(\text{Hg}^{2+}\)
        • Anion - larger size
        • Anion - more charge
    6. Applications
      • More covalant character \(\rightarrow\) less ionic character and vice versa.
      • More Covalent character \(\rightarrow\) weaker bond.
      • More Covalent character \(\rightarrow\) less solubility in water.
      • More Covalent character \(\rightarrow\) low melting and bolining point.
      • More Covalent character \(\rightarrow\) more colour imparting nature.

  5. LEWIS DOT STRUCTURE

    1. Lewis Dot Structure - Intro
      • Structure → Only see outermost e⁻
      • Octet Rule (8e⁻ → Valence Shell) (Lewis theory)
      • Octet Rule can be broken for central atom; but central can have 8e⁻ or more
    2. Rules for Selecting Central Atom (C.A)
      • Least in number OR least electronegativity OR Size largest OR Atomic No. highest
      • H and F can never be central atom
      • Common C.A's are : O,S,N,P,C,Si,Cl
      • Octet of central atom must be complete (duplet in case of H)
      • C.A can have 8 or more electrons
      • C.A tries to remain in maximum covalency (covalency = number of shared electrons)
      • Some imp data of common central atoms:
        Element Number of valence e- Co-valencies Max Co-valency
        O 6 1,2 2
        S 6 2,4,6 6
        N 5 3,4 4
        P 5 3,5 5
        C 4 4 4
        Si 4 4 4
        Cl 7 1,3,5,7 7
    3. Rule for +ve and -ve
      • If +ve charge is there, it will be on the central atom (e.g., H₃O⁺, NH₄⁺).
      • If -ve charge is there, it will be on the corner atom (NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻).
    4. Calculation rules
      • Find Q = Total electrons = Valence electrons of all atoms (-ve charge) - (+ve charge)
      • Bond pair electrons (BPE) = 2 × No. of bonds
      • one pair electrons (LPE) = Q - BPE
      • Remember that LPE is used to either (1) Create lone pair OR (2) Get used in double or triple bond.
    5. Formal Charge (inside example 5 : \( \text{NO}_3^- \))
      • \( \text{FC} = V - \text{LPe} - \dfrac{\text{BPe}}{2} \) (where V = number of valence electrons)

  6. VBT

    1. VBT Intro
      • Lewis Dot structure failed because it did not give information about the geometry of the molecute.
      • The VBT explains formation of covalent bonds in terms of how the atomic orbitals overlap resulting in sharing of electrons.
      • VBT was proposed by scientists Pauling and Slater and extended by Heitler and ???
      • Mains points of the VBT are :
        1. Covalent bond is formed by overlapping of atomic orbitals.
        2. Atoms should come very close so that orbitals can overlap.
        3. Only half-filled orbitals with electrons of opposite spins overlap.
        4. Overlapping must be less than 50% as otherwise nuclei of the participating atoms with get too close and repulsion will start. The bond will become unstable.
        5. Extent of overlapping decides how strong the bond is - and also the bond length. More overlap \(\rightarrow\) stronger bond \(\rightarrow\) shorter bond length.
        6. Two types of overlapping are possible
          • axial or head-on (sigma, \(\sigma\))
          • lateral or parallel or lateral (pi, \(\pi\)))
        7. The overlapping orbitals must have same sign. Sign implies the "+" or "-" of the (sine) wave which the orbital actual is. "+" imples upper (above x-axis) part of the wave and "-" implies the lower part of the wave.
        8. Also, p-bond (and d-bond etc), should be oriented either head-on or parallel. If the two p-orbitals are perpedicular to each other, they won't overlap.
    2. Two types of overlapping

      1. Axial Overlapping
        • This results in sigma \(\rightarrow\) "\(\sigma\)" bond
        • Takes place when s-orbital overlaps with another s-orbital.
          • s-s bond
            • s-s always overlap axially as, being spherically symmetric, they have no direction or orientation.
            • Example formation of \(\text{H}_2\) is a 1s-1s \(\sigma\) bond.
          • s-p Bond
            • Even s-p bond will always be \(\sigma\)
            • Example formation of HF is a 1s-2p \(\sigma\) bond. (note: convention is that if we have to make just one bond, we take the \(\text{p}_z\) orbital. So this is a \(\text{1s-2p}_z\) \(\sigma\) bond.)
              Similarly:
              • HCl is a 1s-3p \(\sigma\) bond.
              • HBr is a 1s-4p \(\sigma\) bond.
              • HI is a 1s-5p \(\sigma\) bond.
            • Question Which bond has more bond energy of :
              1. HF
              2. HCl
              3. HBr
              4. HI
              Hint: The closer the shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the bond.
            • Question Which bond has more bond energy of :
              1. 1s-2p
              2. 2p-2p
              3. 3s-3p
              Hint: The closer the shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the bond.
            • Conversely, if asked which will have the shortest bond length. Ans: It will the bond with the maximun strength.
          • p-p bond
            • When two p orbitals meet head-on, they will also form \(\sigma\) bond.
            • The two p-orbitals should have the same direction.
            • This implies that \(\text{p}_x\)-\(\text{p}_x\) will bond, OR \(\text{p}_y\)-\(\text{p}_y\) will bond, OR \(\text{p}_z\)-\(\text{p}_z\) will bond.
            • Only one of the above is possible; so only one p-p \(\sigma\) is possible.
            • The other two orbitals, if present, will overlap laterally only. So they will form \(\pi\) bonds.
            • Only the first bond will be \(\sigma\). The rest will be \(\pi\)
            • Example \(\text{BF}_3\) <
        • s-orbital will never form \(\pi\) bond
        • Note: only the outermost shell with take part in the overlapping. Ignore inner shells.

      2. Lateral Overlapping
        • This results in pi \(\rightarrow\) "\(\pi\)" bond

  10. MOT PART I

    1. Introduction to Molecular Orbital Theory
      • When two atoms form a covalent bond, their orbitals are not overlapping (like VBT says); instead they are forming two new orbitals.
      • So, "atomic orbitals" are overlapping/combining to form "molecular orbitals".
      • Knowing the electronic configuration of atoms helps us understand/predict it's properties. Similarly, if we can get the electronic configuration of molecules from the MOT, we can get information about the properties of the molecule, after the molecule has been formed.
      • VBT vs MOT
        1. Could not give the "molecular orbital".
        2. Could not predict the magnetic nature of molecules. or example VBT predicted that \(\text{O}_2\) is diamagnetic (gets slightly repelled by magnets). Instead, from MOT, it was found that \(\text{O}_2\) in fact is paramegnetic (gets slightly attracted by magnets).
        3. VBT did not talk about stability of molecules. For example VBT could not tell which was the most stable amonst \(\text{H}_2\), \(\text{H}{_2}^{+}\), \(\text{H}{_2}^{-}\). MOT can tells us which of these is more stable.
        4. VBT can tell whether bonds between two atoms are single, double or triple. MOT on the other hand can tell that there are fractional (ex: 1.5, 2.5) number of bonds in molecules.
        5. MOT supports wave mechanics. It can not only tell us how atoms overlap, but also how two waves overlap (which is useful as orbitals are waves too).

    2. Main points of MOT
      1. Atomic orbitals are in atoms; Molecular orbitals are in molecules (!). There are no atomic orbitals in molecules.
      2. Atomic orbitals are : 1s, 2s, 2p etc whereas molecular orbitals have names like \(\sigma\), \(\pi\), \(\delta\), and so on. The \(\sigma\) and \(\pi\) are roughly equivalent to those that we learnt in VBT.
      3. Number of molecular orbitals formed are equal to number of atomic orbitals combined.
      4. IMP
        Constructive Destructive
        When two waves \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) add, it is a "constructive" interference. Example, two similar sine waves at same phase add to give a sine wave of double amplitude: \(\psi_1\) + \(\psi_2\). When two waves \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) get subtracted, it is a "destructive" interference. Example, two similar sine waves at same phase get subtracted to give a flat line: \(\psi_1\) - \(\psi_2\).
        Constructive interference gives "Bonding Molecular Orbital" \(\rightarrow\) BOD Destructive interference gives "Anti Bonding Molecular Orbital" \(\rightarrow\) ABOD

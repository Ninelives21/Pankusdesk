ATOMIC STRUCTURE - THINGS TO REMEMBER

Constants

    1. The mass of a single electron is approximately \(9.109 \times 10^{-31}\) kg.
    2. Avogadro's number, which is the number of particles in one mole of a substance, is approximately \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\).
    3. Planck's constant (h) is \(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) J·s.
    4. 1 electron volt (eV) is equal to \(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\) J.

Formulae

    1. Isoneutronic nuclides have the same number of neutrons.
    2. The speed of light (\(c\)) is \(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\).
    3. The wavelength (\(\lambda\)) is often given in nanometers (nm) and must be converted to meters (m) for calculations.
    4. The frequency (\(\nu\)) is calculated using the formula: \(\nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda}\).
    5. The wave number (\(\tilde{\nu}\)) is calculated using the formula: \(\tilde{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda}\).
    6. Radiowaves have the longest wavelengths in the electromagnetic spectrum, ranging from 1 mm to 100 km.
    7. To find the fraction of an atom that is occupied by the nucleus, compare their volumes using the formula for the volume of a sphere: \(\dfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3\).
    8. Wavelength (\(\lambda\)) can be converted from angstroms (Å) to meters (m) by multiplying by \(10^{-10}\).
    9. The energy of a photon (\(E_{\text{photon}}\)) is calculated using the formula \(E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).
    10. The photoelectric equation is \(E_{\text{photon}} = \text{Work function} + \text{Kinetic energy}\).
    11. The number of photons (\(N\)) can be calculated by dividing the total energy by the energy of one photon: \(N = \dfrac{\text{Total Energy}}{\text{Energy per photon}}\).
    12. To calculate the number of photons emitted, use the formula for the energy of a photon: \(E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\) and then calculate the total number of photons emitted based on the total energy emitted by the light source.
    13. The threshold frequency (\(\nu_0\)) for a metal in a photoelectric experiment can be calculated using the formula \(h\nu_0 = E_{\text{photon}} - \text{Kinetic energy}\).

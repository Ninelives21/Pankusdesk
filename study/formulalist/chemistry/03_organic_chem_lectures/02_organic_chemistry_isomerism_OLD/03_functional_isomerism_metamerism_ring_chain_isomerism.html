  1. INTRODUCTION — FUNCTIONAL, RING–CHAIN, AND METAMERISM
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Today we will discuss:
      • Functional isomerism
      • Ring–chain isomerism
      • Metamerism
    2. We will take functional isomerism as the base, and in between, we will also show how ring–chain isomerism and metamerism appear.
    3. Definitions:
      • Functional isomers are compounds that definitely have the same molecular formula (first condition), but have different functional groups.
      • Metamerism :
        • Introduction :
          • In metamerism, the compounds have the same functional group.
          • So the conditions are:
            • same molecular formula,
            • same functional group,
            • but different sizes of alkyl groups on either side of a polyvalent atom or group.
          • This means there must be an atom (or group) whose valency is greater than one.
          • On both sides of this atom/group, the alkyl groups are of different sizes (even if the diagram looks a bit unclear).
        • Examples (throughout the lecture within different Functional Isomers)
          1. Example 1 inside \(\left(C_4H_{10}O\right)\)
        • For metamerism, a polyvalent atom or group is required.
          • This polyvalent atom must be present between two alkyl groups.
          • In metamers, commonly observed polyvalent atoms or groups include:
            • oxygen
            • sulfur
            • secondary amines (to be studied later)
            • carbonyl group
          • In the case of a carbonyl group (as in ketones), there is a carbon atom on both sides of the carbonyl carbon.
          • When such a structure exists—two alkyl groups connected through a polyvalent atom or group—metamerism is possible.
    4. Remember the priority order (RTFMCP):
      • Ring–chain
      • Tautomerism
      • Functional
      • Metamerism
      • Chain isomerism
      • Position isomerism
  2. FUNCTIONAL ISOMERS 1 : ALCOHOLS AND ETHERS
    (Back to topic index)
    1. First example: alcohols and ethers.
    2. Both have the same general formula:
      • \(\left(C_nH_{2n+2}O\right)\)
    3. Example 1 : \(\left(C_2H_6O\right)\)
      • From this formula, you can make an alcohol and also an ether.
      • There is no double bond here (DBE = 0)
      • One alcohol can be written as \(\mathrm{C-C-OH}\), and one ether can be written as \(\mathrm{C-O-C}\).
      • So alcohol and ether are functional isomers:
        • same molecular formula
        • different functional groups
    4. Example 2 : \(\left(C_3H_8O\right)\)
      • Next, take the next compound in the same homologous series (same family): \(\left(C_3H_8O\right)\).
      • DBE = 0; so no double bonds are present.
      • From the figure:
        • A and C are functional isomers (one is alcohol, one is ether).
        • A and B are position isomers (in both, the functional group is alcohol; in one it is at carbon 1, in the other at carbon 2).
        • B and C are functional isomers (alcohol vs ether).
          Note : Even if it feels like position difference is also present, still functional-group priority comes before position.
    5. Example 3 : \(\left(C_4H_{10}O\right)\)
      • Next question: alcohols and ethers for \(\left(C_4H_{10}O\right)\).
      • There is no double bond here as DBE = 0
      • Draw all alcohols and all ethers.
      • Quick identification (as asked):
        • A and E → functional isomers
        • B and E → functional isomers
        • A and B → position isomers (OH at carbon 1 vs carbon 2)
        • A and C → chain isomers (main chain 4 carbons vs main chain 3 carbons)
        • C and D → position isomers (same main chain 3 carbons; OH at carbon 1 vs carbon 2)
        • A and D → chain isomers (main chain differs)
        • E and F:
          • In E, the ether oxygen is at the 3rd position; in F, it is at the 2nd position.
          • It may look like position isomerism, but this is a new type called metamerismmp.
          • Even if you feel “position is also changing”, metamerism has higher priority than position.
          • Looking carefully:
            • In E, there are alkyl groups of size 2 and 2 on both sides.
            • In F, one side increases and the other side decreases (one side 1, other side 3).
            • The connecting atom is oxygen (polyvalent; valency \(=2\)).
            • Different size of alkyl group on either side of a polyvalent atom/group.
          • E and F → metamers
        • E and G → metamers (2–2 vs 1–3)
        • F and G:
          • Both have 1 and 3 on the two sides, so “different sizes” are not happening between F and G.
          • So F and G are not metamers.
          • Answer: F and G are chain isomers.

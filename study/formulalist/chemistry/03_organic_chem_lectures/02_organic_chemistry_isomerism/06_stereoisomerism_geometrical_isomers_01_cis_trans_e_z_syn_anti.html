  2. Introduction To Stereoisomers
    • same molecular formula
    • same structural formula
    • different spatial arrangement of atoms/groups
    Examples:
    – structural isomerism: A–B–C–D ⇄ A–B–D–C (different connectivity)
    – stereoisomerism: same connectivity; different 3-D orientation of groups (illustrative sketch)

  3. Types
    Stereoisomers → (a) configurational (separable) and (b) conformation (non-separable)
    Under configurational:
    – enantiomers (mirror images) — O.I
    – diastereomers (non-mirror images) — G.I and O.I
    Notes: OI = optical isomers; GI = geometrical isomers

  4. Geometrical Isomerism
    • also known as cis–trans isomerism
    • if same kind of atoms/groups are on the same side → “cis”
    • on opposite sides → “trans”
    • illustrations with H/Cl around C=C (cis vs trans)
    • example names: cis-but-2-ene and trans-but-2-ene

  5. Important Condition For GI — Double Bond
    • π-bond in a double bond causes restricted rotation
    • restricted rotation allows isolable (configurational) cis/trans isomers
    • single bond permits free rotation → gives conformational isomers (not GI)

  6. Question: Draw Cis–Trans For Hex-2-ene

  7. Question — Will This Show Cis–Trans Isomerism? (Pent-2-ene)
    • asks whether pent-2-ene exhibits cis–trans isomerism
    • answer: "Yes"

  8. Question — Will This Show GI When CH₃ Are On The Same Side Of A Double Bond?
    • conceptual check: two CH₃ on the "same" side of C=C but attached to the "same carbon" does "not" give GI
    • to show cis/trans, substituents must be "across/about" the double bond on "opposite sides" (one on each sp² carbon)
    • answer: "No"

  9. Important Condition For Cis–Trans
    • the two groups on "each" alkene carbon must be "different"
    • if either carbon bears two identical groups → "no" cis–trans isomerism

  10. Famous Example — Maleic Acid & Fumaric Acid
    • these are geometric (cis–trans) isomers
    • maleic acid = cis; fumaric acid = trans
    • another example mentioned: cinnamic acid

  11. Example — Will This Compound Show GI?
    • alkene with four halogens around C=C considered
    • conclusion: qualifies for E–Z (geometric) isomerism when suitable substituents are across the double bond

  12. E–Z
    • When cis-trans nomenclature is ambiguous, it is "E-Z" type of GI
    • "E" equivalent of trans ("Entgegen" = "opposite")
    • "Z" equivalent of cis ("Zusammen" = "together")
    • CIP scheme: assign priority to both groups on each sp² carbon based on "atomic weight" (note: when there are no isotopes — in that case, use "atomic number")

  13. Applying CIP To The Above Example (Cl, F, I, Br)
    • between Cl and I → I gets higher priority (at wt I > Cl)
    • between F and Br → Br gets higher priority
    • priorities lead to identifying the configurations as Z or E for the two depictions

  16. E–Z
    • "E" equivalent of trans ("Entgegen")
    • "Z" equivalent of cis ("Zusammen")
    • CIP scheme: assign priority to both groups on each sp² carbon based on "atomic weight" (note: when there are no isotopes — in that case, use "atomic number")

  17. Applying CIP To The Above Example (Cl, F, I, Br)
    • between Cl and I → I gets higher priority (at wt I > Cl)
    • between F and Br → Br gets higher priority
    • priorities lead to identifying the configurations as Z or E for the two depictions

  18. Example — E–Z Using CIP
    • in case of a group of atoms, give priority to the atom directly attached to the double-bonded carbon
    • between OH and NH₂, OH wins because O has greater atomic weight than N

  19. Important Example — E–Z With CIP For Compound Atoms
    • CIP New Rule : if both atoms attached are the same (e.g., OH vs OCH₃), compare the next atom
    • example: OH vs OCH₃ → O is same, compare H vs C; CH₃ has higher priority
    • example: NH₃ vs N(CH₃)₂ → N(CH₃)₂ gets higher priority

  20. Example using CIP New Rule when double and triple bonds are present
    • When double/triple bonds exist in attached groups: treat C=C as "two" carbons attached to one C
    • Treat C≡C as "three" carbons attached to one C.
    • C≡C gets higher priority

  21. Example — E–Z For Isotopes (H, D, T)
    • priority order: T > D > H (heavier isotope gets higher priority)

  22. Example Problem — Find E/Z In A Given Compound
    • results illustrated as either "(2E, 4Z) hex-2,4-diene" or "(2Z, 4E) hex-2,4-diene" depending on numbering direction

  23. GI About C=N : (1) Aldoxime
    • Aldoxime is formed when aldehyde reacts with hydroxyl amine in basic medium.
    • new view of GI: consider "electron density" differences
    • a lone pair can be treated as a substituent/group
    • use Syn–Anti nomenclature: "Syn" ≡ cis, "Anti" ≡ trans
    • special rule for aldoximes: H and OH opposite → "Anti-aldoxime"; same side → "Syn-aldoxime"

  24. GI About C=N : (2) Ketoxime
    • Ketoxime is formed when ketome reacts with hydroxyl amine in basic medium.
    • naming approach: write "Syn" and take the group syn with OH (CH₃ in example), or write "Anti" and take the group anti to OH (C₂H₅ in example)
    • names: "Syn methyl ethyl ketoxime" or "Anti ethyl methyl ketoxime"

  25. Question — Syn/Anti (Ketoxime)
    • practice naming using Syn/Anti convention for a ketoxime derivative

  26. N=N, or the "Azo" Group
    • lone pairs on the same side → "Syn"; on opposite sides → "Anti"
    • example names illustrated: Syn azo benzene and Anti azo benzene (lone pairs assumed on N even if not drawn)

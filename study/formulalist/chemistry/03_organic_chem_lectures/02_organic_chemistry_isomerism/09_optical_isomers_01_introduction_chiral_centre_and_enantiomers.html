OPTICAL ISOMERS 01: INTRODUCTION - CHIRAL CENTRE AND ENANTIOMERS

YOUTUBE LECTURE LINK
TOPICS IN THIS LECTURE :

  1. Introduction
    • PPL (plane-polarized light)
    • Optically active substance
    • Enantiomers
    • Chiral center
    • Chirality

  2. Optical Isomers
    • Same molecular & structural formula; similar physical/chemical properties
    • Differ in behavior toward plane-polarized light
    • Differ in optically active chemical reactions

  3. Nature Of Light
    • Light = EMR (E & M field vibrations)
    • Vibrates in all directions
    • E-field planes ⟂ to propagation
    • Polarizer (Nicol prism) selects one plane → plane-polarized light (PPL)

  4. Optically Active Substance
    • Rotates PPL
    • Polarimeter measures optical activity
    • Results: unaffected → inactive; clockwise → dextrorotatory (+); anticlockwise → levorotatory (−)
    • Specific rotation α = θ/(l×c); l in dm, c in g/mL; units ≈ deg·mL·dm⁻¹·g⁻¹

  5. How To Know — Optically Active?
    • Look for dissymmetry
    • Dissymmetrical compounds are optically active
    • Chirality ⇒ non-superimposable mirror image

  6. Chirality → Dissymmetric Compound
    • Symmetrical compounds do not favor OA
    • Dissymmetrical compounds favor OA
    • Chirality = dissymmetry + non-superimposability

  7. Example of an optically active compound — Lactic Acid
    • 2-hydroxy propan-1-oic acid
    • Mirror image non-superimposable (even after 180°)
    • Each member and its mirror image are optically active
    • d (+) rotates right; l (−) rotates left
    • Pair called enantiomers; chiral compounds

  8. Enantiomers
    • Non-superimposable mirror images
    • Same magnitude, opposite direction of PPL rotation
    • Differ only in light-involving reactions

  9. Chiral Centre
    • sp³ center with four different substituents (or N: 3 bonds + lone pair)
    • One chiral center ⇒ definitely optically active
    • >1 chiral center ⇒ optical activity may or may not occur

  10. Question — Are These Compounds Optically Active?
    • sec-butyl chloride: Yes
    • tert-pentyl chloride: No
    • iso-butyl chloride: No

  11. Question — Is This Compound Optically Active? (Cyclopropane)
    • Symmetric; no chiral center → not optically active

  12. Question — Is This Compound Optically Active? (Methyl Cyclopropane)
    • Not optically active; no chiral center

  13. Question — Is This Compound Optically Active? (CH₃ & OH On Cyclopropane)
    • Two chiral centers (overall OA evaluated below)

  14. Question — How Many Chiral Centers? (2-methylcyclopropane-1,3-diol)
    • two chiral centers
    • overall OA uncertain

  15. Question — Chiral Center In Methyl Cyclohexane?
    • No chiral center

  16. Question — Chiral Center In 1,2-Dimethylcyclohexane?
    • Two chiral centers

  17. Question — Chiral Center In 1-Bromo-1-Chloro Cyclobutane?
    • No chiral centers

  18. Question — Chiral Center In 1-Bromo-3-Chloro Cyclobutane?
    • No chiral center

  19. Question — Chiral Center In Methyl Hydrogen Sulphate (CH₃HSO₄)?
    • Has a chiral center (at S)

  20. Question — Chiral Center In A Tertiary Amine?
    • N-ethyl-N-methyl-propanamine: no chiral center
    • Tertiary Nitrogen has no chiral centers

  21. Summary Rule
    • One chiral center → chirality → dissymmetric
    • optically active → Enantiomers: d’ (+) and l’ (−)

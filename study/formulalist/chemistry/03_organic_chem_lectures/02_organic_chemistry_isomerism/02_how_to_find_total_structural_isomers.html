  1. INTRODUCTION
    (Back to topic index)
    1. In this chapter, we will learn how to draw all possible structural isomers.
      • Example: draw all structural isomers of \(\left(C_3H_6O\right)\).
    2. Structural isomerism to be covered:
      • Chain isomerism
      • Position isomerism
      • Functional isomerism
      • Metamerism
      • Tautomerism
      • Ring–chain isomerism
    3. If the concept of DBE (double bond equivalent) is clear, then by just looking at a molecular formula, you can immediately understand what kinds of structures are possible.
    4. For the formula \(\left(C_3H_6O\right)\), seeing oxygen suggests possible functional groups:
      • Alcohol
      • Aldehyde
      • Ketone
      • Ether
    5. Carboxylic acid is not possible because it requires two oxygen atoms.
      • Ester is also not possible for the same reason (two oxygen).
    6. So, alcohol, ether, aldehyde, and ketone are possible.
    7. Aldehydes and ketones require a double bond \(\left(C=O\right)\).
      • From the molecular formula alone, it is not immediately clear whether a double bond is present.
    8. Here, the double bond equivalent (DBE) expression is very useful, as it tells how many double bonds can exist inside the compound.
    9. Using DBE, we can also determine whether a cyclic structure is possible, and if so, what kind of cyclic structure can be formed.
    10. All these ideas will be taught step by step in sequence.
  2. DBE (DOUBLE BOND EQUIVALENT)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Today we will learn DBE and how to make and count all structural isomers.
    2. DBE gives us an idea of the number of double bonds possible in a compound.
    3. What is the formula?
      • Count the number of carbons, subtract half the number of hydrogens, add half the number of nitrogens, and then add one.
      • \(\text{DBE} = C - \frac{H}{2} + \frac{N}{2} + 1\)
    4. Oxygen and sulfur have no role here.
      • If a compound contains oxygen, DBE has nothing to do with it.
      • Sulfur also has nothing to do with DBE.
    5. You only have to look at hydrogen and nitrogen.
    6. If any halogen is present—chlorine, bromine, fluorine, etc.—their valency is one, so you treat them like hydrogen.
      • If a halogen is present, you count it in the position of hydrogen.
    7. DBE concept when value = 3
      • The possibilities include:
        1. three double bonds
        2. three rings
        3. one double bond + two rings
        4. two double bonds + one ring
      • Two double bonds together correspond to one triple bond.
        • Triple bond inside a ring is a very unstable structure.
  3. EXAMPLES / QUESTIONS DISCUSSED
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Answer Video
      • If we know whether this compound contains a double bond or not, it becomes very helpful in making isomers.
      • Also, whether a cyclic structure is possible or not.
      • Let’s calculate its DBE:
        • Number of carbons \(= 3\)
        • Hydrogen \(= 6 \Rightarrow \frac{6}{2}\)
        • Nitrogen \(= 0\)
        • \(\text{DBE} = 3 - 3 + 1 = 1\)
      • If a compound has \(\text{DBE} = 1\), it means:
        • it can have one double bond, or
        • it can have one ring.
      • One double bond is equivalent to one ring.
      • So either:
        • make a straight chain and add one double bond, or
        • make a ring.
      • For \(\left(C_3H_6\right)\): make a 3-carbon chain and place one double bond, then add hydrogens and check the count → it fits \(\left(C_3H_6\right)\).
      • A 3-carbon ring is also possible (rings smaller than 3 carbons are not possible). Complete valencies, count hydrogens → \(\left(C_3H_6\right)\).
      • Therefore, the number of structural isomers possible for \(\left(C_3H_6\right)\) is 2.
      • No other distinct structure is possible here.
        • Shifting the double bond position does not create a new isomer in a 3-carbon chain (it will still be the same compound by numbering).

        • Nitrogen \(= 0\)
        • \(\text{DBE} = 4 - 4 + 1 = 1\)

    3. Answer Video
      • First calculate DBE:
        • Number of carbons \(= 4\)
        • Hydrogen \(= 6 \Rightarrow \frac{6}{2}\)
        • Nitrogen \(= 0\)
        • \(\text{DBE} = 4 - 3 + 1 = 2\)
      • DBE = 2 means the following possibilities:
        • two double bonds, or
        • two rings, or
        • one double bond and one ring.
      • DBE = 2 can also represent:
        • one triple bond.
      • So, from this formula, the following types of structures are possible:
        • straight-chain structure with two double bonds
        • straight-chain structure with one triple bond
        • structure with two rings
        • structure with one ring and one double bond
      • For structures with two rings, an additional condition applies:
        • At least four carbon atoms are required to form two rings.
      • Total structural isomers = 9
    4. Answer Video
      • First calculate DBE:
        • Number of carbons \(= 5\)
        • Hydrogen \(= 10 \Rightarrow \frac{10}{2}\)
        • Nitrogen \(= 0\)
        • \(\text{DBE} = 5 - 5 + 1 = 1\)
      • DBE = 1 means:
        • either one double bond, or
        • one ring.
      • There is no possibility of a triple bond here.
        • A triple bond requires DBE = 2.
      • So, all structural isomers will be:
        • straight-chain structures with one double bond, and
        • cyclic structures with one ring.
      • For \(\left(C_5H_{10}\right)\):
        • Number of straight-chain (alkene) structures = 5
        • Number of cyclic structures = 5
      • Total structural isomers = 10

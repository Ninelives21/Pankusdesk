  1. Introduction to GOC and Its Importance (00:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General Organic Chemistry (GOC) is the foundation of organic chemistry.
    • It is used to explain:
      1. reactivity,
      2. stability of intermediates,
      3. acidity/basicity,
      4. comparative stability of compounds.
    • Many later chapters depend on GOC concepts.
    • Basic IUPAC nomenclature should be known before starting GOC.
  2. What Is Studied in GOC (04:28)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Main areas of GOC:
      1. Electronic effects
      2. Reaction intermediates
      3. Reaction mechanisms
    • Electronic effects discussed in GOC:
      1. Inductive effect
      2. Electromeric effect
      3. Hyperconjugation
      4. Resonance / mesomeric effect
    • Among these, electromeric effect is temporary; the others are treated as permanent.
    • This lecture starts with the inductive effect.
  3. Inductive Effect: Definition and Basic Idea (07:43)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Definition: Inductive effect is the partial displacement of \( \sigma \)-bond electrons due to electronegativity difference.
    • It operates through \( \sigma \) bonds only.
    • For \( \mathrm{C-X} \), if \( X \) is more electronegative than carbon:
      \( \mathrm{C^{\delta +}-X^{\delta -}} \)
    • If the attached atom/group is less electronegative than carbon, electron density shifts toward carbon.
    • It is a partial effect, not complete bond breaking.
  4. Plus I, Minus I, and Transmission Through Sigma Bonds (10:18)
    (Back to topic index)
    • \(-I\) effect: electron-withdrawing effect through \( \sigma \) bonds.
    • \(+I\) effect: electron-donating effect through \( \sigma \) bonds.
    • Reference point is carbon:
      1. group more electronegative than carbon \( \rightarrow -I \)
      2. group less electronegative than carbon \( \rightarrow +I \)
    • Inductive effect is transmitted through \( \sigma \) bonds.
    • Its strength decreases with distance.
  5. Stability of Charged Species by Minus I Effect (14:23)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Charged species are generally less stable than neutral species.
    • Greater charge dispersal \( \rightarrow \) greater stability.
    • \(-I\) groups stabilize a nearby negative charge by withdrawing electron density.
    • For halogens:
      \( \mathrm{F > Cl > Br > I} \) in \(-I\) effect
    • Hence, stronger \(-I\) effect gives greater stabilization of a nearby anion.
    • Common \(-I\) order:
    • Hydrogen is taken as the reference point.
  6. Acidic Strength and Conjugate-Base Stability (22:55)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For an acid:
      \( \mathrm{HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-} \)
    • Acid strength depends on conjugate-base stability.
    • More stable \( \mathrm{A^-} \) \( \rightarrow \) stronger acid.
    • \(-I\) effect generally increases acidity by stabilizing the conjugate base.
    • Thus, stronger \(-I\) substituent at the same position usually gives stronger acid.
    • Application:
      1. \( \mathrm{NO_2CH_2COO^- > FCH_2COO^- > CH_2{=}CHCOO^-} \)
      2. Corresponding acidity follows the same stabilizing trend of the conjugate base.
  7. Plus I Effect and Acidity of Alcohols (25:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • \(+I\) effect is shown mainly by alkyl groups.
    • Order of \(+I\) effect among alkyl groups:
      \( \mathrm{3^\circ\ alkyl > 2^\circ\ alkyl > 1^\circ\ alkyl > CH_3} \)
    • For alcohols, acidity depends on stability of the alkoxide ion.
    • \(+I\) groups push electron density toward oxygen and destabilize \( \mathrm{O^-} \).
    • Therefore:
      more \(+I\) effect \( \rightarrow \) less acidic alcohol
    • Acidity order:
      methyl alcohol \( > \) primary alcohol \( > \) secondary alcohol \( > \) tertiary alcohol
  8. Net Effect of Substituents in Carboxylic Acids (38:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Always consider the net inductive effect of the whole substituent.
    • \( \mathrm{ClCH_2{-}} \): overall \(-I\) effect.
    • \( \mathrm{HOCH_2{-}} \): in this lecture, treated overall as weak \(+I\) in the given comparison.
    • Representative acidity trend:
      1. \( \mathrm{ClCH_2COOH} \): most acidic
      2. \( \mathrm{HOCH_2COOH} \): intermediate
      3. \( \mathrm{CH_3COOH} \): least acidic
    • Reason: net \(-I\) effect increases acidity; net \(+I\) effect decreases acidity.
  9. Ka, pKa, and the Reliable Measure of Acidity (43:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For
      \( \mathrm{HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-} \),
      \( \mathrm{K_a = \dfrac{[H^+][A^-]}{[HA]}} \)
    • Larger \( \mathrm{K_a} \) \( \rightarrow \) stronger acid.
    • \( \mathrm{p}K_a = -\log K_a \)
    • Smaller \( \mathrm{p}K_a \) \( \rightarrow \) stronger acid.
    • For broad comparisons across different classes, \( \mathrm{K_a} \) / \( \mathrm{p}K_a \) is more reliable than a single inductive-effect argument.
  10. Memorized Acidity Order of Common Organic Species (48:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Useful memorized acidity order:
      1. \( \mathrm{ArSO_3H} \)
      2. \( \mathrm{HCOOH} \)
      3. Benzoic acid
      4. \( \mathrm{RCOOH} \)
      5. \( \mathrm{H_2CO_3} \)
      6. Phenol
      7. \( \mathrm{H_2O} \)
      8. \( \mathrm{ROH} \)
      9. \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH} \)
      10. \( \mathrm{H_2C{=}CH_2} \)
      11. \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_3} \)
    • In the lecture, methanol is treated as slightly more acidic than water in the cited comparison.
    • Only terminal alkynes show acidic hydrogen in this context.
    • Example:
      \( \mathrm{H_2O > C_2H_5OH > HC{\equiv}CH} \)
  11. Mixed Comparisons: Carboxylic Acids and Phenols (53:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • First compare the class of compound, then the substituent effect.
    • Carboxylic acids are generally much more acidic than phenols.
    • Within the same class, substituent effects can significantly change acidity.
    • In nitro-substituted phenols:
      1. dinitrophenol \( > \) mononitrophenol in acidity
      2. para-nitrophenol \( > \) meta-nitrophenol in the lecture comparison
    • Reason: at ortho/para positions, mesomeric effect also operates; at meta, mainly inductive effect is considered.
  12. When Atom Type Matters More Than Inductive Effect (62:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Inductive effect should be compared only when the charged atom is the same or comparable.
    • Example:
      1. alcohol \( \rightarrow \mathrm{RO^-} \)
      2. amine \( \rightarrow \mathrm{RNH^-} \)
    • These anions carry charge on different atoms.
    • Rule:
      1. negative charge is more stable on a more electronegative atom
      2. positive charge is more stable on a less electronegative atom
    • Therefore, alkoxide ion is more stable than the corresponding amide-type anion.
    • Hence: alcohols are more acidic than amines.
    • Example: ethanol is more acidic than methylamine.
  13. Hybridization, Terminal Alkynes, and Benzene (67:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For hydrocarbons, acidity depends on stability of the carbanion formed after deprotonation.
    • Relevant conjugate bases:
      1. \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}C^-} \)
      2. \( \mathrm{H_2C{=}CH^-} \)
      3. \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2^-} \)
    • Electronegativity of carbon by hybridization:
      \( \mathrm{sp > sp^2 > sp^3} \)
    • More \( s \)-character \( \rightarrow \) greater electronegativity \( \rightarrow \) better stabilization of negative charge.
    • Therefore:
      \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH > H_2C{=}CH_2 > CH_3CH_3} \) in acidity
    • Only terminal alkynes are acidic in this comparison.
    • Lecture order including benzene:
      \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH > C_6H_6 > H_2C{=}CH_2 > CH_3CH_3} \)
  14. Period-2 Hydrides and Final Homework (75:52)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Compare acidity of period-2 hydrides using stability of conjugate bases.
    • Conjugate bases:

      2. \( \mathrm{NH_2^-} \)
      3. \( \mathrm{OH^-} \)
      4. \( \mathrm{F^-} \)
    • Since negative charge is more stable on the more electronegative atom:
      \( \mathrm{HF > H_2O > NH_3 > CH_4} \)

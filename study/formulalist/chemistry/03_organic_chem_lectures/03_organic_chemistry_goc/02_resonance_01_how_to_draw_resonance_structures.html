  1. Introduction to Resonance (00:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Electronic effects in organic molecules explain how electrons shift and influence reactivity.
    • Main electron-displacement effects studied in GOC:
      1. Inductive effect

      2. Electromeric effect

      3. Resonance (mesomeric effect)

      4. Hyperconjugation
    • Resonance explains delocalization of electrons within a molecule.
    • Electron delocalization stabilizes molecules, ions, and reaction intermediates.
    • Many stability trends in organic chemistry depend on resonance.
  2. Examples of Resonating Structures (01:40)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Example: allylic carbocation.
    • Structure:
      \( CH_2 = CH - CH_2^{+} \)
    • π electrons shift toward the positive center.
    • New resonance structure formed:
      \( CH_2^{+} - CH = CH_2 \)
    • Both structures contribute to the resonance hybrid.
    • Example with negative charge:
      \( CH_2 = CH - CH_2^{-} \)
    • Lone pair electrons move to form alternate structures.
    • Example: carbonate ion \( CO_3^{2-} \) shows three equivalent resonance structures.
  3. Definition and Key Features of Resonance (07:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Resonance definition:
    • Delocalization or displacement of π electrons or lone pair electrons within a conjugated system.
    • Important characteristics:
      1. Only π electrons or lone pair electrons participate.

      2. σ bonds remain unchanged.

      3. Atomic positions do not change.

      4. Electron redistribution occurs within the molecule.
    • Resonance structures are theoretical contributors.
    • The real structure is a hybrid of all contributors.
  4. Resonance Hybrid Concept (11:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The actual molecule is represented by the resonance hybrid.
    • Resonance hybrid represents average electron distribution.
    • Characteristics of resonance hybrid:
      1. More stable than any individual resonance structure.

      2. Electron density becomes delocalized.

      3. Bond lengths become intermediate.
    • Example: carbonate ion.
    • All three C–O bonds have identical bond length.
  5. Condition for Resonance — Conjugation (15:59)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Resonance occurs only in conjugated systems.
    • Conjugation means continuous overlap of p orbitals.
    • Basic conjugation patterns:
      1. π–σ–positive charge

      2. π–σ–lone pair

      3. π–σ–free radical

      4. π–σ–π

      5. lone pair–σ–positive charge
  6. Type 1 Conjugation: π–σ–Positive Charge (20:55)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Pattern: π bond – single bond – positive charge.
    • Example:
      \( CH_2 = CH - CH_2^{+} \)
    • π electrons shift toward the positively charged carbon.
    • Positive charge becomes distributed across multiple atoms.
    • This stabilizes carbocations.
  7. Type 2 Conjugation: π–σ–Lone Pair / Negative Charge (41:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Pattern: π bond – single bond – lone pair.
    • Example:
      \( CH_2 = CH - CH_2^{-} \)
    • Lone pair electrons interact with the π system.
    • Negative charge becomes delocalized.
    • This stabilizes carbanions.
  8. Rules for Valid Resonating Structures (45:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rules for drawing resonance structures:
      1. Total number of electrons must remain constant.

      2. Total charge must remain unchanged.

      3. Only electrons move, atoms do not move.

      4. σ bonds remain intact.
    • Resonance contributors must represent the same molecular skeleton.
  9. Resonance vs Tautomerism (46:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Resonance: movement of electrons only.
    • Tautomerism: movement of atoms (usually hydrogen).
    • In resonance, atoms remain fixed.
    • If atoms shift positions, it represents isomerism rather than resonance.
  10. Type 3 Conjugation: π–σ–Free Radical (01:02:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Pattern: π bond – single bond – radical.
    • Radicals are species containing an unpaired electron.
    • Radicals form through homolytic bond cleavage.
    • Electron delocalization stabilizes allylic radicals.
  11. Type 4 Conjugation: π–σ–π System (01:08:31)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Pattern: double bond – single bond – double bond.
    • Example: conjugated diene systems.
    • π electrons move across the entire conjugated system.
    • This produces delocalized electron density.
  12. Type 5 Conjugation: Lone Pair–σ–Positive Charge (01:19:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Pattern: lone pair – single bond – positive charge.
    • Example:
      \( CH_3 - NH - CH_2^{+} \)
    • Lone pair electrons shift toward the positive center.
    • New resonance structure:
      \( CH_3 - NH^{+} = CH_2 \)
    • Positive charge becomes delocalized between atoms.

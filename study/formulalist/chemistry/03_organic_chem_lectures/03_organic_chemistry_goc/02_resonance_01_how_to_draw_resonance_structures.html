  1. Introduction to GOC and Its Importance (00:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General Organic Chemistry (GOC) is the foundation of organic chemistry.
    • It explains general principles used to predict reactivity, stability of intermediates, acidic character, and basic character.
    • Many later chapters such as hydrocarbons, haloalkanes, alcohols, phenols, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, and nitrogen compounds rely on GOC concepts.
    • For higher-level problem solving, conceptual understanding of GOC is essential.
  2. What Is Studied in GOC (04:28)
    (Back to topic index)
    • GOC is discussed here under three broad areas:
      1. Electronic effects

      2. Reaction intermediates

      3. Reaction mechanisms
    • The four electron-displacement effects listed are:
      1. Inductive effect

      2. Electromeric effect

      3. Hyperconjugation

      4. Resonance or mesomeric effect
    • This lecture begins with inductive effect and its applications.
  3. Inductive Effect: Definition and Basic Idea (07:43)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Inductive effect is the partial displacement of sigma-bond electrons toward the more electronegative atom in a sigma bond.
    • The displacement is partial, so partial charges develop instead of complete ionic charges.
    • If \( X \) is more electronegative than carbon in \( \mathrm{C-X} \), electron density shifts toward \( X \).
      \( \mathrm{C^{\delta +}-X^{\delta -}} \)
    • If the attached atom or group is less electronegative than carbon, electron density shifts toward carbon.
    • This discussion is restricted to sigma bonds.
  4. Plus I, Minus I, and Transmission Through Sigma Bonds (10:18)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A group that withdraws electron density from carbon is an electron-withdrawing group and shows minus I effect.
    • A group that donates electron density toward carbon is an electron-donating group and shows plus I effect.
    • Relative to carbon:
      1. More electronegative group \( \rightarrow \) minus I

      2. Less electronegative group \( \rightarrow \) plus I
    • Inductive effect travels through sigma bonds and decreases with distance.
    • The atom directly attached to the substituent is affected the most, and more distant atoms are affected less strongly.
    • This distance dependence is important in predicting stability and acidity.
  5. Stability of Charged Species by Minus I Effect (14:23)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Charged species are generally less stable than corresponding neutral species.
    • A localized charge gives lower stability, whereas a dispersed charge gives greater stability.
    • Minus I groups can stabilize a nearby negative charge by withdrawing electron density and reducing charge concentration.
    • For halogens, the minus I order used in the lecture is:
      \( \mathrm{F > Cl > Br > I} \)
    • A longer memorized minus I order is introduced, beginning with strongly electron-withdrawing positively charged nitrogen groups, then \( \mathrm{NO_2} \), \( \mathrm{CN} \), carbonyl-containing groups, halogens, \( \mathrm{OR} \), \( \mathrm{OH} \), alkynyl, \( \mathrm{NH_2} \), phenyl, vinyl, and finally hydrogen as reference.
    • Hydrogen is treated as the zero reference for inductive effect.
  6. Acidic Strength and Conjugate-Base Stability (22:55)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For an acid \( \mathrm{HA} \), acidity is judged by the stability of the conjugate base formed after loss of \( \mathrm{H^+} \).
      \( \mathrm{HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-} \)
    • A more stable \( \mathrm{A^-} \) means easier proton loss and hence stronger acidity.
    • Minus I effect generally increases acidity because it stabilizes the conjugate base.
    • This is a useful trend for comparable compounds, but it is not the only factor in every acidity comparison.
      1. Question 1: Compare the stability of similarly substituted carboxylate ions containing groups such as \( \mathrm{NO_2} \), \( \mathrm{F} \), and vinyl.

      2. Greater electron withdrawal gives greater stabilization of the anion.

      3. Question 2: Compare acidity among analogous acids with different withdrawing substituents.

      4. Stronger minus I effect gives stronger acidity when the compounds are otherwise comparable.
  7. Plus I Effect and Acidity of Alcohols (25:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Plus I effect is shown by electron-donating groups, especially alkyl groups.
    • The lecture emphasizes the common alkyl-group order:
      \( \mathrm{3^\circ\ alkyl > 2^\circ\ alkyl > 1^\circ\ alkyl > CH_3} \)
    • More alkyl substitution gives stronger plus I effect.
    • For alcohols, acidity is judged by the stability of the alkoxide ion formed after loss of \( \mathrm{H^+} \).
    • Plus I groups push electron density toward oxygen and destabilize \( \mathrm{O^-} \), so stronger plus I effect means lower acidity.
    • Thus, within a homologous set of alcohols, increasing alkyl substitution lowers acidic strength.
      1. Question 3: Arrange tertiary, secondary, primary, and methyl alcohols in acidic strength.

      2. Greater plus I effect gives lower acidity; methyl alcohol is most acidic and tertiary alcohol is least acidic within this set.

      3. Question 4: Compare acidity when one alcohol framework is influenced by plus I groups and another by a minus I substituent.

      4. The structure with stronger stabilization of \( \mathrm{O^-} \) is more acidic.
  8. Net Effect of Substituents in Carboxylic Acids (38:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The net inductive effect of a whole substituent must be considered, not only one atom inside it.
    • For \( \mathrm{ClCH_2{-}} \), chlorine contributes minus I while \( \mathrm{CH_2} \) contributes plus I; the overall effect remains minus I.
    • For \( \mathrm{HOCH_2{-}} \), oxygen contributes minus I but \( \mathrm{CH_2} \) contributes plus I; the lecture treats the net effect as plus I in the given comparison.
    • This explains why some substituents that contain electronegative atoms may still show net behavior different from first expectation.
  9. Ka, pKa, and the Reliable Measure of Acidity (43:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Inductive reasoning is useful, but the sure method for acidity comparison is the acid dissociation constant.
    • For \( \mathrm{HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-} \),
      \( \mathrm{K_a = \dfrac{[H^+][A^-]}{[HA]}} \)
    • Larger \( \mathrm{K_a} \) means stronger acid.
    • \( \mathrm{p}K_a \) is inversely related to acidity:
      \( \mathrm{p}K_a = -\log K_a \)
    • Smaller \( \mathrm{p}K_a \) means stronger acid.
    • When compounds belong to very different classes, direct \( \mathrm{K_a} \) or \( \mathrm{p}K_a \) trends are more reliable than using a single electronic effect alone.
  10. Memorized Acidity Order of Common Organic Species (48:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A broad decreasing acidity order presented in the lecture is:
      1. \( \mathrm{ArSO_3H} \)

      2. \( \mathrm{HCOOH} \)

      3. Benzoic acid

      4. \( \mathrm{RCOOH} \)

      5. \( \mathrm{H_2CO_3} \)

      6. Phenol

      7. \( \mathrm{H_2O} \)

      8. \( \mathrm{ROH} \)

      9. \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH} \)

      10. \( \mathrm{H_2C{=}CH_2} \)

      11. \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_3} \)
    • This is a practical revision list for common organic comparisons.
    • A stated exception in the lecture is that methanol is treated as slightly more acidic than water in the cited comparison.
    • Only terminal alkynes are acidic in this context because an acidic hydrogen must be present on the triply bonded carbon.
      1. Question 5: Arrange \( \mathrm{H_2O} \), \( \mathrm{C_2H_5OH} \), and \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH} \) in decreasing acidity.

      2. Order: \( \mathrm{H_2O > C_2H_5OH > HC{\equiv}CH} \).
  11. Mixed Comparisons: Carboxylic Acids and Phenols (53:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Class identity matters in acidity comparison.
    • A benzoic acid derivative remains significantly more acidic than phenol, even if the phenol carries a strong minus I substituent and the benzoic acid carries a plus I substituent.
    • Within the same class of acids, substituent effects can strongly change acidity order.
    • For nitro-substituted phenols, the lecture notes that ortho and para positions can have stronger overall effect than meta position because mesomeric contribution becomes relevant in addition to distance-based inductive effect.
  12. When Atom Type Matters More Than Inductive Effect (62:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Inductive effect should not be used blindly when the charged atom in the conjugate bases is different.
    • Example: alcohol versus amine acidity cannot be decided only by counting alkyl groups.
    • After deprotonation:
      1. Alcohol gives \( \mathrm{RO^-} \)

      2. Amine gives \( \mathrm{RNH^-} \)
    • The more stable anion is the one with negative charge on the more electronegative atom.
    • General rule:
      1. Negative charge on a more electronegative atom \( \rightarrow \) greater stability

      2. Positive charge on a less electronegative atom \( \rightarrow \) greater stability
    • Therefore, alcohols are more acidic than amines in the compared example because oxygen stabilizes negative charge better than nitrogen.
      1. Question 6: Which is more acidic, ethanol or methylamine?

      2. Ethanol is more acidic because \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2O^-} \) is more stable than \( \mathrm{CH_3NH^-} \).
  13. Hybridization, Terminal Alkynes, and Benzene (67:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For hydrocarbon acidity, compare the stability of the carbanion formed after proton removal.
    • The conjugate bases are:
      1. \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}C^-} \)

      2. \( \mathrm{H_2C{=}CH^-} \)

      3. \( \mathrm{CH_3CH_2^-} \)
    • The hybridization of the negatively charged carbon affects its electronegativity:
      1. \( \mathrm{sp > sp^2 > sp^3} \)
    • Higher \( s \)-character places electron density closer to the nucleus, increasing electronegativity and stabilizing negative charge better.
    • Therefore:
      1. \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH > H_2C{=}CH_2 > CH_3CH_3} \)
    • Only terminal alkynes are acidic because the proton must be attached directly to the triply bonded carbon.
    • Benzene is placed between alkyne and alkene in the lecture’s acidity comparison.
    • For the cited sequence, the order becomes:
      1. \( \mathrm{HC{\equiv}CH > C_6H_6 > H_2C{=}CH_2 > CH_3CH_3} \)
  14. Period-2 Hydrides and Final Homework (75:52)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For period-2 hydrides \( \mathrm{CH_4} \), \( \mathrm{NH_3} \), \( \mathrm{H_2O} \), and \( \mathrm{HF} \), acidity is compared by stability of the conjugate base formed after loss of \( \mathrm{H^+} \).
    • The conjugate bases are:
      1. \( \mathrm{CH_3^-} \)

      2. \( \mathrm{NH_2^-} \)

      3. \( \mathrm{OH^-} \)

      4. \( \mathrm{F^-} \)
    • Since negative charge is most stable on the most electronegative atom, the acidity order is:
      1. \( \mathrm{HF > H_2O > NH_3 > CH_4} \)
    • This again follows the rule that negative charge on a more electronegative atom gives greater stability.
      1. Question 7: Arrange the given amines in decreasing basic strength in gaseous phase.

      2. This is left as homework for application of inductive effect in basicity.

  1. Introduction to Stability of Resonating Structures (00:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Topic: stability order of resonating structures.
    • This topic is important for objective questions asking:
      1. most stable resonating structure,

      2. least stable resonating structure,

      3. impossible resonating structure,

      4. stability order among given contributors.
    • Resonance structures must already satisfy basic resonance conditions:
      1. same atomic arrangement,

      2. same total charge,

      3. electron movement only.
    • Stability is judged by applying rules in a fixed priority order.
  2. Rule 1: Greater Number of Covalent Bonds (02:50)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Highest-priority rule: the resonating structure with the greater number of covalent bonds is more stable.
    • Bond formation lowers energy; therefore, a structure containing more covalent bonding is usually lower in energy.
    • Example:
      \( \mathrm{CH_3-O-CH_2^{+}} \)
      \( \mathrm{CH_3-O^{+}=CH_2} \)
    • Comparison:
      1. first structure contains fewer covalent bonds,

      2. second structure contains one extra covalent bond.
    • Hence the second contributor is more stable.
    • This rule is checked before all other stability rules.
  3. Rule 2: Neutral Species Are More Stable (05:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If two resonating structures have the same number of covalent bonds, compare neutral and charged forms.
    • Neutral species are more stable than charged species.
    • Example:
      \( \mathrm{CH_2=CH-NH_2} \)
      \( \mathrm{CH_2^{-}-CH=NH_2^{+}} \)
    • Both contributors have the same number of covalent bonds.
    • The first contributor is neutral, whereas the second contains separated charges.
    • Therefore:
      neutral contributor \( \mathrm{>} \) charged contributor in stability.
  4. Rule 3: Charge on Electronegative Atoms (06:58)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If the number of covalent bonds is equal and all contributors are charged, then examine the position of charge.
    • Negative charge is stabilized on a more electronegative atom.
    • Positive charge is destabilized on a more electronegative atom.
    • Example:
      \( \mathrm{CH_2=CH-O^{-}} \)
      \( \mathrm{CH_2^{-}-CH=O} \)
    • In the first contributor, negative charge is on oxygen.
    • In the second contributor, negative charge is on carbon.
    • Since oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, the first contributor is more stable.
    • Equivalent statement:
      1. negative charge should prefer a more electronegative atom,

      2. positive charge should prefer a less electronegative atom.
  5. Carbonate and Formic Acid Comparisons (11:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • These examples illustrate the ordered use of the rules.
    • Carbonate ion contributors:
      1. \( \mathrm{O=C(O^{-})O^{-}} \)

      2. \( \mathrm{^{-}O-C^{+}(O^{-})-O^{-}} \)

      3. \( \mathrm{O^{-}-C^{-}(=O^{+})-O^{-}} \)
    • Comparison logic:
      1. first contributor has more covalent bonds, so it is most stable,

      2. between the remaining charged contributors, the one with negative charge on oxygen is more stable than the one with negative charge on carbon.
    • Stability order:
      1 \( \mathrm{>} \) 2 \( \mathrm{>} \) 3
    • Formic acid contributors:
      1. \( \mathrm{H-C(=O)-OH} \)

      2. \( \mathrm{H-C(-O^{-})=O^{+}H} \)

      3. \( \mathrm{H-C^{+}(-O^{-})-OH} \)

      4. \( \mathrm{H-C^{-}(-O^{+})-OH} \)
    • First compare total covalent bonds:
      1. contributors 1 and 2 have more covalent bonds,

      2. contributors 3 and 4 have fewer covalent bonds.
    • Between 1 and 2, contributor 1 is neutral and is therefore more stable.
    • Between 3 and 4, contributor 3 is more stable because negative charge is on oxygen, not carbon.
    • Final stability order:
      1 \( \mathrm{>} \) 2 \( \mathrm{>} \) 3 \( \mathrm{>} \) 4
  6. IIT 2009 Stability Order Problem (15:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A JEE/IIT-type problem can be solved by systematic rule application.
    • Method:
      1. count covalent bonds,

      2. check neutral vs charged character,

      3. check whether negative charge is on the more electronegative atom.
    • In the discussed 2009 problem, contributors with more covalent bonds were placed above those with fewer bonds.
    • Among contributors with equal bond count, the one carrying negative charge on nitrogen was preferred over the one carrying negative charge on carbon.
    • This shows that the same three rules are enough to solve many past-paper questions.
  7. Valency Restriction: Nitrogen Cannot Be Pentavalent (18:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Before applying stability rules, first check whether the proposed resonating structure is even valid.
    • Nitrogen cannot be pentavalent in these normal organic resonance contributors.
    • If a proposed contributor shows five bonds around nitrogen, that contributor should be rejected.
    • Example type:
      \( \mathrm{CH_2=CH-NH_3^{+}} \) and a proposed contributor that forces nitrogen to become pentavalent.
    • Such a contributor is not accepted as a valid resonating structure.
    • This check has very high priority because an impossible structure cannot be compared for stability.
  8. Rule 4: Like Charges Far, Unlike Charges Near (21:51)
    (Back to topic index)
    • This rule is used when earlier rules do not distinguish the contributors.
    • Like charges should be far apart.
    • Unlike charges should be closer together.
    • If two positive charges or two negative charges are placed very near each other, the contributor becomes less stable because of repulsion.
    • If opposite charges are closer, electrostatic attraction gives extra stabilization.
    • Hence:
      1. \( \mathrm{+ \ ... \ +} \) is better than \( \mathrm{++} \),

      2. \( \mathrm{+ -} \) proximity is better than keeping unlike charges unnecessarily far apart.
    • In the discussed multi-charge example, the contributor with adjacent positive charges was identified as the most unstable one.
  9. Rule 5: Inductive Effect in Resonance Stability (26:38)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If all previous rules fail to differentiate two contributors, inductive effect can be used.
    • Example comparison involves a positively charged carbon attached to different substituents.
    • A \( \mathrm{+I} \) group donates electron density and reduces positive charge, thus increasing stability.
    • A \( \mathrm{-I} \) group withdraws electron density and increases positive charge, thus decreasing stability.
    • Therefore:
      1. \( \mathrm{+I} \) near a positively charged center \( \rightarrow \) stabilizing,

      2. \( \mathrm{-I} \) near a positively charged center \( \rightarrow \) destabilizing.
    • In the discussed example, the contributor adjacent to a \( \mathrm{CH_3} \) group was more stable than the one lacking that \( \mathrm{+I} \) support.
  10. Final Priority Order of Stability Rules (29:24)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Priority order for judging stability of resonating structures:
      1. greater number of covalent bonds \( \rightarrow \) more stable,

      2. neutral species \( \rightarrow \) more stable than charged species,

      3. negative charge on more electronegative atom \( \rightarrow \) more stable,

      4. positive charge on more electronegative atom \( \rightarrow \) less stable,

      5. invalid valency must be rejected,

      6. like charges should be farther apart and unlike charges should be closer,

      7. inductive effect may be used if required.
    • Additional aromaticity-based comparison is mentioned as a later topic and is not used here.
    • Practical conclusion: always apply the rules in sequence, not randomly.

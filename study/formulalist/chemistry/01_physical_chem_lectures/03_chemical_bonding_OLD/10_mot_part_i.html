    1. Introduction to Molecular Orbital Theory
      • When two atoms form a covalent bond, their orbitals are not overlapping (like VBT says); instead they are forming two new orbitals.
      • So, "atomic orbitals" are overlapping/combining to form "molecular orbitals".
      • Knowing the electronic configuration of atoms helps us understand/predict it's properties. Similarly, if we can get the electronic configuration of molecules from the MOT, we can get information about the properties of the molecule, after the molecule has been formed.
      • VBT vs MOT
        1. Could not give the "molecular orbital".
        2. Could not predict the magnetic nature of molecules. or example VBT predicted that \(\text{O}_2\) is diamagnetic (gets slightly repelled by magnets). Instead, from MOT, it was found that \(\text{O}_2\) in fact is paramegnetic (gets slightly attracted by magnets).
        3. VBT did not talk about stability of molecules. For example VBT could not tell which was the most stable amonst \(\text{H}_2\), \(\text{H}{_2}^{+}\), \(\text{H}{_2}^{-}\). MOT can tells us which of these is more stable.
        4. VBT can tell whether bonds between two atoms are single, double or triple. MOT on the other hand can tell that there are fractional (ex: 1.5, 2.5) number of bonds in molecules.
        5. MOT supports wave mechanics. It can not only tell us how atoms overlap, but also how two waves overlap (which is useful as orbitals are waves too).

    2. Main points of MOT
      1. Atomic orbitals (AO) are in atoms; Molecular orbitals (MO) are in molecules (!). There are no AO in molecules.
      2. AO are : 1s, 2s, 2p etc whereas MO have names like \(\sigma\), \(\pi\), \(\delta\), and so on. The \(\sigma\) and \(\pi\) are roughly equivalent to those that we learnt in VBT.
      3. Number of MO formed are equal to number of AO combined.
      4. Combining AO must have same or nearly same energy (example 1s of C and 1s of O) and same shape (example s-s or p-p, never s-p)
      5. As per Schrodinger, AO can be considered as waves (\(\psi_1\), \(\psi_2\) etc). We get different types of MO when AO waves are added vs subtracted.

      6. Constructive Destructive
        When two waves \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) add, it is a "constructive" interference. Example, two similar sine waves at same phase add to give a sine wave of double amplitude: \(\psi_1\) + \(\psi_2\). When two waves \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) get subtracted, it is a "destructive" interference. Example, two similar sine waves at same phase get subtracted to give a flat line: \(\psi_1\) - \(\psi_2\).
        Constructive interference gives "Bonding Molecular Orbital" \(\rightarrow\) BOD Destructive interference gives "Anti Bonding Molecular Orbital" \(\rightarrow\) ABOD
        BMO is stable (less energy) ABMO is less stable (more energy)
        BMO is placed below in the combined MO represenation ABMO is placed above in the combined MO represenation, and denoted with an asterisk

    3. Types Of MO : 1) \(\sigma \) MO = Axial AO Interference

      1. s-s \(\sigma \) MO
        • \(1s + 1s \rightarrow \sigma 1s\) BMO
        • \(1s - 1s \rightarrow\) there will be a 'nodal plane' where the probability of finding an electron is zero \(\sigma^{*} 1s\)
        Representation

      2. \(p_z\)–\(p_z\) \(\sigma \) MO
        • N≡N Has One \(p_z\)–\(p_z\) Axial Overlapping
        • \(p_z - p_z\) form \(\sigma\) bonds ( \(p_z\) chosen as per convention )
        • \(p_x - p_x\) & \(p_y - p_y\) form \(\pi\) bonds
        • In \(2p_z - 2p_z\) orbitals :-
          1. \(2p_z + 2p_z = \sigma 2p_z\)
            has 2 nodal planes when added (CI)
          2. \(-2p_z - 2p_z = \sigma^{*} 2p_z\)
            has 3 nodal planes when subtracted (DI)


    4. Types Of MO : 2) \(\pi\) Molecular Orbitals - When There Is Sideways Overlapping / Interference Of AO
      1. Example \(p_x - p_x\) or \(p_y - p_y\) lateral overlapping
        • CI results in 1 nodal plane and is called \(\pi 2p_x\)
        • DI results in 2 nodal planes and is called \(\pi^{*} 2p_x\)

    1. Ionic bond/Electrovalent Bond : Click here
      • Between metal and non-metal
      • Complete transfer of electron - octet is complete
      • Ionic bonds are non directional - no "dash" between atoms of an ionic bond
      • example: \( \text{Na} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+ + \text{e}^- \)
        example: \( \text{Cl}+ \text{e}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}^- \)
        Note: This transfer of electron is happening in gaseous states of Na and Cl.
      • \( \text{Na}^+ \text{and} + \text{Cl}^- \) attract each other with electrostatic force of attraction - which is very strong.
      • Electrostatic force: \( F = \dfrac{K q_1 q_2}{r^2} \) ,
        Note that 'r' will be very small
      • Ionic bonds do not have a shape or direction

    3. Electrovalency : Click here
      • Number of electrons given by the metal or the number of electrons gained by the non-metal during the formation of an electrovalent bond.
      • Electrovalency is always taken as positive.
      • Electrovalency of elements groupwise:
        1. Group 1 elements Li, Na, K etc \(\rightarrow\) 1
        2. Group 2 elements Be, Mg, Ca, Sr etc \(\rightarrow\) 2
        3. Group 13 elements B, Al, Ga etc \(\rightarrow\) 3
        4. Group 14 elements C, Si, Ge etc \(\rightarrow\) do not form ionic bonds
        5. Group 15 elements N, P, As etc \(\rightarrow\) 3
        6. Group 16 elements O, S, Se etc \(\rightarrow\) 2
        7. Group 17 elements F, Cl, Br etc \(\rightarrow\) 1
        8. Group 3 to 12 (d block) elements \(\rightarrow\) variable valencies
          1. example Fe \(\rightarrow\) 2,3
          2. example Cu \(\rightarrow\) 1,2

    4. Energy Terms involved in Ionic Bond Formation / Favourable conditions in formation of Ionic bonds : Click here
      • When energy is relased, a stable compound is formed; if energy is to be absorbed, it should be as low as possible.
      • Ionization Energy: energy absorbed in order to remove and electron from a metal in its gaseous state.
        • For stable compound, IE should be as less as possible \(\rightarrow\) min
      • Electron Affinity : energy relased when a non-metal accepts an electron.
        • For stable compound, EA should be as high as possible \(\rightarrow\) max
      • Lattice Energy : energy released when a gaseous cation and a gaseous anion combine to form one mole of a solid ionic compound
        • Lattice energy can be conversely thought of as the energy needed to break an ionic compound
        • For stable compound, LE should be as high as possible \(\rightarrow\) max

    5. Which is a stronger Ionic bond? : Click here
      • Metal should have more metallic character
      • Example: Francium is considered the most metallic element because of its position at the bottom of the first group of alkali metals on the periodic table. This position gives it a large atomic radius, which results in a weak nuclear charge and high metallic character
      • Non-Metal should have more non-metallic character
      • Example of strong ionic bond: FrF
      • Ques: Which has more ionic character among NaF, NaCl, NaBr, NaI \(\rightarrow\) NaF
      • Ques: Which has more covalent character among NaF, NaCl, NaBr, NaI \(\rightarrow\) NaI

    6. Properties of Ionic Compounds : Click here
      • Physical State - solid lattice due to strong electrostatic forces : Click here
      • Melting and Boiling point - very high (same reason \(\rightarrow\) due to strong electrostatic forces): Click here
      • Conductivity Click here
        • Bad conductors in solid state (as they are held together by strong electrostatic forces and are not free to move in solid state)
        • But good conductors in molten/aqueous state
        • In molten state you have given it heat energy and the ions move/conduct electricity
        • In aqueous state, water reduces the force of attraction between the ions by a factor of 81 \(\rightarrow\) dielectric property
      • Isomerism (same molecular formula but different structural formula) is not displayed as they have no structure: Click here
      • Isomorphism (Similar lattice or same 3D structure) : Click here
        Isomorphism takes place when :
        1. ions have similar formula
        2. size of ions is nearly the same (Na and Mg have nearly same size)
        3. example NaCl and \(\text{MgCl}_2\)
        4. example NaF and MgO
        5. Not displaying isomorphism : NaCl, KCl (as Na and K are very different in size
      • Type of reaction : Click here
        1. Ionic reactions are very fast; especially precipitation reactions (approx 10-6 seconds to complete)
        2. It is difficult to measure their speed
      • Solubility - Hydration energy/Solvation energy: Click here
        • Ionic compounds are generally more soluble in polar solvents than covalent compounds.

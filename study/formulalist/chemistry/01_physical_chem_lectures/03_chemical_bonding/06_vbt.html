    1. VBT Intro
      • Lewis Dot structure failed because it did not give information about the geometry of the molecute.
      • The VBT explains formation of covalent bonds in terms of how the atomic orbitals overlap resulting in sharing of electrons.
      • VBT was proposed by scientists Pauling and Slater and extended by Heitler and London.

      • Mains points of the VBT are :
        1. Covalent bond is formed by overlapping of atomic orbitals.
        2. Atoms should come very close so that orbitals can overlap.
        3. Only half-filled orbitals with electrons of opposite spins overlap.
        4. Overlapping must be less than 50% as otherwise nuclei of the participating atoms with get too close and repulsion will start. The bond will become unstable.
        5. Extent of overlapping decides how strong the bond is - and also the bond length. More overlap \(\rightarrow\) stronger bond \(\rightarrow\) more bond energy \(\rightarrow\) shorter bond length.
        6. Two types of overlapping are possible
          • axial or head-on (sigma, \(\sigma\))
          • lateral or parallel or lateral (pi, \(\pi\)))
        7. The overlapping orbitals must have same sign. Sign implies the "+" or "-" of the (sine) wave which the orbital actual is. "+" imples upper (above x-axis) part of the wave and "-" implies the lower part of the wave.
        8. Also, p-bond (and d-bond etc), should be oriented either head-on or parallel. If the two p-orbitals are perpedicular to each other, they won't overlap.
    2. Two types of overlapping : (1) Axial Overlapping

      • This results in sigma \(\rightarrow\) "\(\sigma\)" bond
      • Takes place when s-orbital overlaps with another s-orbital.
        • s-s bond
          • s-s always overlap axially as, being spherically symmetric, they have no direction or orientation.
          • Example formation of \(\text{H}_2\) is a 1s-1s \(\sigma\) bond.
        • s-p Bond
          • Even s-p bond will always be \(\sigma\)
          • Example formation of HF is a 1s-2p \(\sigma\) bond. (note: convention is that if we have to make just one bond, we take the \(\text{p}_z\) orbital. So this is a \(\text{1s-2p}_z\) \(\sigma\) bond.)
            Similarly:
            • HCl is a 1s-3p \(\sigma\) bond.
            • HBr is a 1s-4p \(\sigma\) bond.
            • HI is a 1s-5p \(\sigma\) bond.
          • Question Which bond has more bond energy of :
            1. HF
            2. HCl
            3. HBr
            4. HI
            Hint: The closer the shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the bond.
          • Question Which bond has more bond energy of :
            1. 1s-2p
            2. 2p-2p
            3. 3s-3p
            Hint: The closer the shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the bond.
          • Conversely, if asked which will have the shortest bond length. Ans: It will the bond with the maximun strength.
        • p-p bond
          • When two p orbitals meet head-on, they will also form \(\sigma\) bond.
          • The two p-orbitals should have the same direction.
          • This implies that \(\text{p}_x\)-\(\text{p}_x\) will bond, OR \(\text{p}_y\)-\(\text{p}_y\) will bond, OR \(\text{p}_z\)-\(\text{p}_z\) will bond.
          • Only one of the above is possible; so only one p-p \(\sigma\) is possible.
          • The other two orbitals, if present, will overlap laterally only. So they will form \(\pi\) bonds.
          • Only the first bond will be \(\sigma\). The rest will be \(\pi\)
          • Example \(\text{BF}_3\) <
      • s-orbital will never form \(\pi\) bond
      • Note: only the outermost shell with take part in the overlapping. Ignore inner shells.

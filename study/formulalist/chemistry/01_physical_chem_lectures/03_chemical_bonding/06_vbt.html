    1. VBT Intro
      • Lewis Dot structure failed because it did not give information about the geometry of the molecute.
      • The VBT explains formation of covalent bonds in terms of how the atomic orbitals overlap resulting in sharing of electrons.
      • VBT was proposed by scientists Pauling and Slater and extended by Heitler and London.

      • Mains points of the VBT are :
        1. Covalent bond is formed by overlapping of atomic orbitals.
        2. Atoms should come very close so that orbitals can overlap.
        3. Only half-filled orbitals with electrons of opposite spins overlap.
        4. Overlapping must be less than 50% as otherwise nuclei of the participating atoms with get too close and repulsion will start. The bond will become unstable.
        5. Extent of overlapping decides how strong the bond is - and also the bond length.
          More overlap \(\rightarrow\) stronger bond \(\rightarrow\) more bond energy \(\rightarrow\) shorter bond length.
        6. Two types of overlapping are possible
          • axial or head-on (sigma, \(\sigma\))
          • lateral or parallel or lateral (pi, \(\pi\)))
        7. The overlapping orbitals must have same sign.
          • Sign implies the "+" or "-" of the (sine) wave which the orbital actually is.
          • "+" imples upper (above x-axis) part of the wave and "-" implies the lower part of the wave.
          • Also, p-bond (and d-bond etc), should be oriented either head-on or parallel. If the two p-orbitals are perpedicular to each other, they won't overlap.
    2. Two types of overlapping : (1) Axial Overlapping

      • This results in sigma \(\rightarrow\) "\(\sigma\)" bond
      • Takes place when s-orbital overlaps with another s-orbital.
        • s-s bond
          • s-s always overlap axially as, being spherically symmetric, they have no direction or orientation.
          • Example formation of \(\text{H}_2\) is a 1s-1s \(\sigma\) bond.
        • s-p Bond
          • Even s-p bond will always be \(\sigma\)
          • Example formation of HF is a 1s-2p \(\sigma\) bond. (note: convention is that if we have to make just one bond, we take the \(\text{p}_z\) orbital. So this is a \(\text{1s-2p}_z\) \(\sigma\) bond.)
            Similarly:
            • HCl is a 1s-3p \(\sigma\) bond.
            • HBr is a 1s-4p \(\sigma\) bond.
            • HI is a 1s-5p \(\sigma\) bond.
          • Question Which bond has more bond energy of :
            1. HF
            2. HCl
            3. HBr
            4. HI
            Hint: The closer the shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the bond.
          • Question Which bond has more bond energy of :
            1. 1s-2p
            2. 2p-2p
            3. 3s-3p
            Hint: The closer the shell is to the nucleus, the stronger the bond.
          • Conversely, if asked which will have the shortest bond length. Ans: It will the bond with the maximun strength.
        • p-p bond
          • When two p orbitals meet head-on, they will also form \(\sigma\) bond.
          • The two head-on overlapping p-orbitals should have the same direction.
          • This implies that \(\text{p}_x\)-\(\text{p}_x\) will bond, OR \(\text{p}_y\)-\(\text{p}_y\) will bond, OR \(\text{p}_z\)-\(\text{p}_z\) will bond.
          • Only one of the above is possible; so only one p-p \(\sigma\) is possible.
          • The other two orbitals, if present, will overlap laterally only. So they will form \(\pi\) bonds.
          • Only the first bond will be \(\sigma\). The rest will be \(\pi\)
          • Example \(\text{BF}_3\)
            • B atom gets into first excited state; one 2s electron with jump to a p orbital; So B has 3 unpaired electron: 2s, 2\(\text{p}_x\), 2\(\text{p}_y\)
            • 2s of B will bond with 2\(\text{p}_z\) of F to form a 2s-2\(\text{p}_z\) \(\sigma\) bpnd
            • 2\(\text{p}_x\) of B will bond with 2\(\text{p}_x\) of F to form a 2\(\text{p}_x\)-2\(\text{p}_z\) \(\pi\) bpnd
            • 2\(\text{p}_y\) of B will bond with 2\(\text{p}_y\) of F to form a 2\(\text{p}_x\)-2\(\text{p}_z\) \(\pi\) bpnd
            • Only one sigma bond is possible in a molecule; the rest will be pi bonds
      • s-orbital will never form \(\pi\) bond
      • Note: only the outermost shell with take part in the overlapping. Ignore inner shells.

    3. Two Types Of Overlapping: (2) Lateral Overlapping
      • also called sidewise / parallel overlapping
      • never found in s (as s is spherical – no sides)
      • sidewise overlapping found in p and d (and f)
      • will form \(p_y\)–\(p_y\) bond
      • overlapping is less compared to axial \(\sigma\)
      • so, \(\sigma\) bond is stronger than \(\pi\)
      • Example \(\mathrm{O_2}\) : \( \mathrm{O=O} \)
        • in the double bond, 1ˢᵗ is \(\sigma\), 2ⁿᵈ is \(\pi\)
        • O : \(1s^2\ 2p^2\ 2p^4\)
        • \(p_y\)–\(p_y\) overlap and \(p_z\)–\(p_z\) overlap
        • \(p_z\)–\(p_z\) \(=\) \(\sigma\), \(p_y\)–\(p_y\) \(=\) \(\pi\)
      • Example nitrogen – \(\mathrm{N_2}\) : \( \mathrm{N\equiv N} \)
        • in the triple bond between N–N
        • the first is \(\sigma\) and the rest two are \(\pi\)
        • N \(=\) \((1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^3)\) \(\Rightarrow\) 3 half-filled orbitals
        • \(p_z\)–\(p_z\) axial overlap \(\sigma\)
        • \(p_x\)–\(p_x\) lateral overlap \(\pi\)
        • \(p_y\)–\(p_y\) lateral overlap \(\pi\)

    5. Difference Between \(\sigma\) And \(\pi\) Bonds
      \(\sigma\) bond \(\pi\) bond
      Axial / head-on overlapping Sidewise / parallel overlapping
      s–s, s–p, p–p p–p, p–d, d–d
      Extent of overlapping is more \(\Rightarrow\) stronger bond \(\Rightarrow\) bond energy is more \(\Rightarrow\) bond length is less \(\Rightarrow\) more stable bond BE \(\approx 90\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\) Extent of overlapping is less \(\Rightarrow\) weaker bond \(\Rightarrow\) bond energy is less \(\Rightarrow\) bond length is more \(\Rightarrow\) less stable bond BE \(\approx 65\ \mathrm{kcal\ mol^{-1}}\)
      Only 1 \(\sigma\) bond can be formed between two atoms 1 or 2 \(\pi\) bonds can be formed between two atoms \( \mathrm{O=O} \) \((\sigma+\pi)\) \( \mathrm{N\equiv N} \) \((\sigma+2\pi)\)
      \(\sigma\) can be formed on its own \(\pi\) bond formed only after \(\sigma\) bond already exists
      In \(\sigma\) bond \(\rightarrow\) rotation is possible about the bond \(\pi\) bond has restricted rotation about it; no rotation is possible

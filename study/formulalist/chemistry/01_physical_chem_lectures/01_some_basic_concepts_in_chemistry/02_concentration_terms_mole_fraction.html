CONCENTRATION TERMS: MOLE FRACTION

  1. Intoduction : Solutions and Concentration Terms

    1. Solution = solute + solvent.
    2. The substance that has more number of moles is the solvent.
    3. Number of moles = given mass / molecular mass.
    4. For example, in a solution of 98 gms of \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) and 54 gm of \(\text{H}_2{O}\), which is the solute and which is the solvent? Since there 1 more of \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) and 3 moles of \(\text{H}_2{O}\), \(\text{H}_2{O}\) is the solvent, and \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) is the solute.
    5. Concentration terms are used in a solution to tell how much solute and solvent is present.
    6. Some concentration terms are :
      1. weight by weight %
      2. volume by volume %
      3. weight by volume %
      4. ppm
      5. ppb
      6. mole fraction
      7. molarity
      8. molality
      9. normality


  2. Weight by weight %

    1. Weight by weight % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}} * 100 \)
      • Mass of solute = 10 gm
      • Mass of solution = 10 + 100 = 110 gm
      • Wt by wt % = \(\dfrac{10}{110} * 100 \) = 9.09 %
      • Weight by weight % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}} * 100 \)
      • Mass of solute = 10 gm
      • Mass of solution = density * volume = 1.2 * 100 = 120 gm
      • Wt by wt % = \(\dfrac{10}{120} * 100 \) = 8.33 %


  3. Volume by volume %

    1. Volume by volume % = \(\dfrac{\text{Volume of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} * 100 \)
    2. For example, if we are told that an HCl aquaeus solution is 7 % volume by volume \(\implies\) 7 ml HCl is present in 100 ml of solution.


  4. Weight by volume %

    1. Weight by volume % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} * 100 \)
    2. For example, a sugar solution is 3% weight by volume \(\implies\) 3 gm of sugar (solute) is present in 100 ml of solution
    3. Weight by weight % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}} * 100 \)
    4. Mass of solute = 10 gm and volume of solution = 100
    5. Wt (mass) of solution = density * volume = 1.25 * 100 = 125 gm
    6. Wt by wt % = \(\dfrac{10}{125} * 100\) = 8%
    7. Weight by volume % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} * 100 \)
    8. Mass of solute = 10 gm and mass of solution = 100
    9. Volume of solution = mass / density = 100/1.25 = 80 ml
    10. Wt by wt % = \(\dfrac{10}{80} * 100\) = 12.5%
    11. Weight by volume % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} * 100 \)
    12. When mass of solute = 5 gm, mass of solution = 100 gm
    13. Volume of solution = mass/density = 100/1.4 ml
    14. 100/1.4 ml solution has 5 gm \(\text{HNO}_3\)
    15. 100 ml solution has \(\dfrac{100 * 5 * 1.4}{100}\) = 7 gm \(\text{HNO}_3\)


  5. Ppm

    1. PPM = Parts per million = very small quantity
    2. Example : sugar in solution = 2 ppm \(\implies\) 2 gm sugar in \(\text{10}^6\) gm solution.
    3. No pf ppm = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}}\) * \(\text{10}^6\)
      • No pf ppm = \( \dfrac{8 \times 10^{-4} \text{ g}}{100 \text{ g}} \times 10^6 \) = 8 ppm
      • If not specified, glass of water = 250 ml = 250 gm
      • No pf ppm = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}}\) * \(\text{10}^6\)
      • So, 53 = \(\dfrac{x}{250}\) * \(\text{10}^6\)


  6. Ppb

    1. PPM = Parts per million = very small quantity
    2. No pf ppb = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}}\) * \(\text{10}^9\)
    3. Example : NaCl in solution = 5 ppb \(\implies\) 5 gm NaCl in \(\text{10}^9\) gm solution.


  7. Mole fraction

    1. In a solution, let there be two components : solute A, and solvent B. Mole fraction of A = \(\dfrac{\text{Number of moles of A}}{\text{Number of moles of A + Number of moles of B}} \)
    2. For example, a solution has three components A, B, C with mole fraction \(\text{n}_A\), \(\text{n}_B\), \(\text{n}_C\) respectively. Then :
      • Mole fraction of A = \(\chi_A\) = \(\dfrac{\text{n}_A}{\text{n}_A + \text{n}_B + \text{n}_C}\)
      • Mole fraction of B = \(\chi_B\) = \(\dfrac{\text{n}_B}{\text{n}_A + \text{n}_B + \text{n}_C}\)
      • Mole fraction of C = \(\chi_C\) = \(\dfrac{\text{n}_C}{\text{n}_A + \text{n}_B + \text{n}_C}\)
      • Note that \(\chi_A\) + \(\chi_B\) + \(\chi_C\) = 1
      • \(\text{n}_{\text{NaOH}}\) = \(\dfrac{40}{23 + 16 + 1}\) = \(\dfrac{40}{40}\) = 1
      • \(\text{n}_{\text{H}_2{O}}\) = \(\dfrac{54}{2 + 16}\) = \(\dfrac{54}{18}\) = 3
      • \(\chi_{NaOH}\) = \(\dfrac{1}{4}\) = 0.25
      • \(\chi_{\text{H}_2{O}}\) = \(\dfrac{3}{4}\) = 0.75
      • Alternatively, \(\chi_{\text{H}_2{O}}\) = 1 - \(\chi_{NaOH}\) = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75
      • 46 gm of \(\text{C}_2{H}_5{OH}\) in 100 gm of solution.
      • 100 - 46 = 54 gm of \(\text{H}_2{O}\)
      • Moles of \(\text{C}_2{H}_5{OH}\) = \(\dfrac{46}{24 + 5 + 16 + 1}\) = \(\dfrac{46}{46}\) = 1
      • Moles of \(\text{H}_2{O}\) = \(\dfrac{54}{2 + 16}\) = \(\dfrac{54}{18}\) = 3
      • Mole fraction of ethyl alcohol = \(\dfrac{1}{4}\) = 0.25
      • Mole fraction of water = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75

  1. Atomic spectrum and discrete lines (00:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Spectrum: white light splits into different colours; similarly, atoms show characteristic spectra.
    • When a gas in a discharge/heat tube is excited, emitted radiations correspond to only certain wavelengths; many wavelengths are absent.
    • Two types:
      1. Continuous spectrum: all wavelengths/colours present.
      2. Discrete (line) spectrum: only specific lines appear; intensity differs line to line.
    • Atomic spectrum is a line (discrete) spectrum, not continuous.
  2. Bohr explanation: absorption and emission (02:24)
    (Back to topic index)
    • In an atom, electrons occupy discrete energy levels (Bohr orbits).
    • Heating/excitation supplies energy; an electron absorbs energy and jumps from a lower level to a higher level.
    • Electron tends to return to a lower (more stable) energy level; during return it emits energy as electromagnetic radiation.
      \( \Delta E = E_{\text{higher}} - E_{\text{lower}} \)
    • Discreteness arises because only specific energy levels exist; hence only specific \(\Delta E\) values are possible, giving only certain emitted wavelengths.
    • Emission spectrum: obtained when excited atoms return to lower energy levels and emit radiation.
    • Absorption spectrum (mentioned for later): related to absorption of specific wavelengths, but focus here is emission.
  3. Energy of emitted radiation and \(E=\frac{hc}{\lambda}\) (09:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a hydrogen atom transition from \(n=2\) to \(n=1\), emitted energy equals the energy difference:
      \( \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 \)
    • Energy of an electron in a hydrogen-like species:
      \( E_n = -13.6\,\frac{Z^2}{n^2}\,\text{eV} \)
    • Example result (H atom, \(Z=1\)):
      \( \Delta E = -13.6\left(\frac{1}{2^2}\right) - \left[-13.6\left(\frac{1}{1^2}\right)\right] = 10.2\,\text{eV} \)
    • Photon energy-wavelength relation:
      \( E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \)
    • Shortcut relation used in problems (with \(E\) in eV and \(\lambda\) in \(\text{\AA}\)):
      \( E = \frac{12375}{\lambda} \)
      (units and constant form assumed from lecture; use as given)
    • Key trend:
      \( \Delta E \propto \frac{1}{\lambda} \Rightarrow \) larger \(\Delta E\) gives smaller \(\lambda\).
  4. Rydberg formula derivation and final form (14:24)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a hydrogen-like atom, transition from \(n_2\) to \(n_1\) (\(n_2>n_1\)):
      \( \Delta E = E_{n_2} - E_{n_1} = -13.6\frac{Z^2}{n_2^2} - \left(-13.6\frac{Z^2}{n_1^2}\right) \)
    • Simplify:
      \( \Delta E = 13.6Z^2\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2}-\frac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\,\text{eV} \)
    • Since emitted photon energy \(E=\frac{hc}{\lambda}\), equate and rearrange to get:
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{13.6}{hc}\,Z^2\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2}-\frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \)
    • Define Rydberg constant \(R=\frac{13.6}{hc}\) (as used in the lecture):
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda} = RZ^2\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2}-\frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \)
    • Given value (do not memorize per lecture):
      \( R \approx 1.09\times10^{-7}\,\text{m}^{-1} \)
    • Useful shortcut stated:
      \( \frac{1}{R} \approx 911\,\text{\AA} \)
  5. Lyman series: first line, limit, trends (21:31)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Lyman series: all transitions ending at \(n_1=1\) (from \(n_2=2,3,4,\dots,\infty\) to 1).
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda} = RZ^2\left(1-\frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \) for H (\(Z=1\))
    • First line (smallest transition \(2\to1\)):
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda}=R\left(1-\frac{1}{2^2}\right)=R\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) \Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{4}{3}\frac{1}{R}\approx \frac{4}{3}\times 911 \approx 1216\,\text{\AA} \)
    • As \(n_2\) increases (\(3\to1,4\to1,\dots\)), \(\lambda\) decreases (energy gap increases).
    • Series limit (largest transition \(\infty\to1\)):
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda}=R\left(1-\frac{1}{\infty}\right)=R \Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{1}{R}\approx 911\,\text{\AA} \)
    • Range (lecture): \(1216\,\text{\AA}\) to \(911\,\text{\AA}\); these lie in the ultraviolet region.
    • Largest wavelength in a series corresponds to smallest energy gap (closest upper level): here \(2\to1\).
    • Shortest wavelength in a series corresponds to largest energy gap (from \(\infty\)): here \(\infty\to1\).
  6. Balmer series: shortest and longest wavelength (31:41)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Balmer series: all transitions ending at \(n_1=2\) (from \(n_2=3,4,5,\dots,\infty\) to 2).
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda} = RZ^2\left(\frac{1}{2^2}-\frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \) for H (\(Z=1\))
    • Shortest wavelength (largest transition \(\infty\to2\)):
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda}=R\left(\frac{1}{4}-0\right)=\frac{R}{4}\Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{4}{R}=4\frac{1}{R}\approx 4\times 911=3644\,\text{\AA} \)
    • Longest wavelength (smallest transition \(3\to2\)):
      \( \frac{1}{\lambda}=R\left(\frac{1}{4}-\frac{1}{9}\right)=R\left(\frac{5}{36}\right)\Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{36}{5}\frac{1}{R}\approx \frac{36}{5}\times 911 \approx 6552\,\text{\AA} \)
    • Balmer wavelengths fall largely in the visible region (lecture uses \(\sim 4000\) to \(\sim 8000\,\text{\AA}\) as visible range estimate), so Balmer lines are visible.
  7. Spectral regions and named series summary (36:23)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Named hydrogen spectral series (by final level \(n_1\)):
      1. Lyman: \(n_1=1\) (ultraviolet).
      2. Balmer: \(n_1=2\) (visible).
      3. Paschen: \(n_1=3\) (infrared).
      4. Brackett: \(n_1=4\) (short infrared).
      5. Pfund: \(n_1=5\) (long infrared).
      6. Humphreys: \(n_1=6\) (long infrared).
    • Visual range estimate used in lecture: \(\sim 4000\,\text{\AA}\) to \(\sim 8000\,\text{\AA}\); below is UV, above is IR.
    • Ordering of wavelengths (typical trend): Lyman < Balmer < Paschen < Brackett < Pfund < Humphreys.
  8. Number of spectral lines for transitions (43:46)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When an electron de-excites from a higher level to a lower level, multiple paths (stepwise or direct) can occur, producing different wavelengths.
    • Example: electron starting at \(n=4\) and ending at \(n=1\).
      1. Possible distinct transitions include: \(4\to1\), \(4\to2\), \(4\to3\), \(3\to2\), \(3\to1\), \(2\to1\).
      2. Total distinct spectral lines for \(4\to1\) case: \(6\).
    • Shortcut for number of distinct spectral lines when transitions are possible between levels from \(n_2\) down to \(n_1\):
      \( N = \frac{(n_2-n_1)\left((n_2-n_1)+1\right)}{2} \)
    • Check:
      1. For \(n_2=4\), \(n_1=1\): \(N=\frac{(3)(4)}{2}=6\).
      2. For \(n_2=5\), \(n_1=1\): \(N=\frac{(4)(5)}{2}=10\).

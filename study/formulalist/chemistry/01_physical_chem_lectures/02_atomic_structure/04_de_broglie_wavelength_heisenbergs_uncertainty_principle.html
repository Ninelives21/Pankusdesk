  1. Planck’s quantum idea and photon energy (00:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Classical view treated light/radiation as a wave; Planck introduced a particle-like description for energy exchange.
    • Radiation consists of tiny packets of energy called quanta or photons.
    • Energy of one photon depends on frequency:
      \( E = h f \)
    • Using wave relation \( v = f \lambda \); for electromagnetic radiation \( v = c \), so:
      \( f = \frac{c}{\lambda} \)
    • Photon energy can be written as:
      \( E = h f = \frac{h c}{\lambda} \)
    • Planck constant (approximate):
      \( h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J s} \)
    • Key implication: light shows particle behavior in many phenomena; later it is understood to have dual nature (wave + particle).
  2. Wave–particle duality and de Broglie hypothesis (06:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Light can behave as a wave in some contexts and as photons (particles) in others: dual nature of light.
    • Inspired by this, de Broglie proposed: if light (a wave) can show particle nature, then moving particles may show wave nature.
    • For an electron (microscopic, fast-moving), wave behavior becomes plausible; this led to the idea of dual nature of electron.
    • de Broglie hypothesis: a moving particle has an associated wavelength:
      \( \lambda = \frac{h}{m v} = \frac{h}{p} \)
    • Where \( p = m v \) is momentum.
    • Experimental support: electron diffraction (Davisson–Germer experiment) demonstrates wave property because diffraction is a wave phenomenon.
    • Extension idea: all moving particles have wave nature, but observability depends on how large the wavelength is.
  3. de Broglie wavelength examples: cricket ball vs electron (12:42)
    (Back to topic index)
    • de Broglie wavelength depends inversely on momentum:
      \( \lambda = \frac{h}{m v} \)
    • Macroscopic object example (cricket ball):
      • Take \( m \approx 100\ \text{g} = 0.1\ \text{kg} \), \( v \approx 20\ \text{m s}^{-1} \).
      • \( \lambda \approx \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{0.1 \times 20} \approx 3.3 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{m} \)
      • In angstrom:
        \( 1\ \text{m} = 10^{10}\ \text{\AA} \Rightarrow \lambda \approx 3.3 \times 10^{-24}\ \text{\AA} \)
      • This is extremely small, effectively unmeasurable → wave nature is not observed for such macroscopic bodies.
    • Microscopic object example (electron):
      • Given \( m_e \approx 9.1 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg} \), \( v \approx 2.18 \times 10^{6}\ \text{m s}^{-1} \).
      • Using \( \lambda = \frac{h}{m v} \), the wavelength is of the order of angstroms (measurable).
      • Lecture approximation:
        \( \lambda \approx 3.2\ \text{\AA} \)
    • Conclusion: wave nature becomes experimentally significant mainly for microscopic, fast-moving particles (small \( m \), appreciable \( v \)).
  4. Kinetic-energy form and % change in de Broglie wavelength (20:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Sometimes the particle’s kinetic energy \( K \) is given instead of velocity or momentum.
    • Relations:
      • \( K = \frac{1}{2} m v^{2} \)
      • \( p = m v \Rightarrow K = \frac{p^{2}}{2m} \)
      • Hence:
        \( p = \sqrt{2 m K} \)
    • Substitute into de Broglie relation \( \lambda = \frac{h}{p} \):
      \( \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 m K}} \)
    • Percentage-change type result: since \( \lambda \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{K}} \), increasing \( K \) decreases \( \lambda \).
    • Example from lecture: if kinetic energy increases by 44% (e.g., \( K: 100 \to 144 \)):
      • \( \frac{\lambda_f}{\lambda_i} = \sqrt{\frac{K_i}{K_f}} = \sqrt{\frac{100}{144}} = \frac{10}{12} = \frac{5}{6} \)
      • Percentage decrease:
        \( \frac{\lambda_i - \lambda_f}{\lambda_i} \times 100 = \left(1 - \frac{5}{6}\right)\times 100 = \frac{1}{6}\times 100 \approx 16.67\% \)
  5. Heisenberg uncertainty principle and numericals (24:19)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a moving microscopic particle (showing wave nature), position and momentum cannot be simultaneously measured with exact certainty.
    • If \( \Delta x \) is uncertainty (error) in position and \( \Delta p \) is uncertainty in momentum, then:
      \( \Delta x \, \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{4\pi} \)
    • Key consequences:
      • If \( \Delta p \to 0 \), then \( \Delta x \to \infty \) (position becomes highly uncertain).
      • If \( \Delta x \to 0 \), then \( \Delta p \to \infty \) (momentum becomes highly uncertain).
    • Common numerical pattern: given \( \Delta x \), estimate minimum \( \Delta p \) using:
      \( \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{4\pi \Delta x} \)
    • If uncertainty in velocity is asked, use \( p = m v \Rightarrow \Delta p = m \Delta v \) (for fixed \( m \)).
    • Special case (lecture): if uncertainties in position and momentum are equal (\( \Delta x = \Delta p \)):
      \( (\Delta p)^2 \ge \frac{h}{4\pi} \Rightarrow \Delta p \ge \sqrt{\frac{h}{4\pi}} \)
      1. Question 1 Find the minimum uncertainty in momentum if the uncertainty in position of an electron is \( 1.1\ \text{\AA} \). Use \( 1\ \text{\AA} = 10^{-10}\ \text{m} \).

      2. Question 2 If the errors in momentum and position are equal, obtain the minimum possible value of the error in momentum.
  6. Why Bohr model fails: key limitations (36:13)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Applicability limited mainly to one-electron species (hydrogen-like): \( \text{H}, \text{He}^+, \text{Li}^{2+}, \text{Be}^{3+} \). For multi-electron atoms, predictions do not match experiments well.
    • Bohr treated the electron as a particle in definite circular orbits; later evidence shows electrons also have wave nature (de Broglie), which is not incorporated.
    • Conflict with uncertainty principle: Bohr model effectively assigns both exact radius (position) and exact velocity (momentum) at the same time.
      • Radius of nth orbit (as stated):
        \( r_n = 0.53 \times \frac{n^2}{Z}\ \text{\AA} \)
      • Electron speed (as stated):
        \( v_n = 2.18 \times 10^{6} \times \frac{Z}{n}\ \text{m s}^{-1} \)
    • Cannot explain splitting of spectral lines:
      • In magnetic field: Zeeman effect.
      • In electric field: Stark effect.
    • Cannot explain ultra-fine structure (additional fine splitting within spectral lines).
    • Leads to the need for a better model: quantum mechanical (wave mechanical) model of atom, described via quantum numbers and orbitals (\( s, p, d, f \)).

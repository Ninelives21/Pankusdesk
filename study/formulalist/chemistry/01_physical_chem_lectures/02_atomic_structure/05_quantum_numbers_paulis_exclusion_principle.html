  1. Why quantum numbers (wave mechanical model) (00:41)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Bohr/Rutherford-type models treated the electron as a particle moving around the nucleus in (approximately) fixed circular orbits.
    • De Broglie + Heisenberg viewpoint: electron shows wave nature, so a fixed “revolving orbit” picture becomes inadequate for microscopic particles.
    • Wave mechanical (quantum mechanical) model: electron is described as a wave; measurable statements are probabilistic.
  2. Schrödinger wave equation & probability meaning (02:19)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Electron matter wave is described by a wave equation (Schrödinger wave equation). In the transcript, the wavefunction symbol is spoken as “z” (commonly written as \(\psi\)).
    • Probability interpretation:
      • Wavefunction: \(\psi\) (transcript uses \(z\)).
      • Probability density \(\propto \psi^2\) (more standard: \(|\psi|^2\)).
      • \(\psi^2\) represents the probability of finding the electron at a point/region in space.
    • Schrödinger wave equation (as stated in the lecture; transcription is imperfect):
      \( \dfrac{d^2 z}{dx^2} + \dfrac{d^2 z}{dy^2} + \dfrac{d^2 z}{dz^2} + \dfrac{8\pi^2 m}{h^2}(E - V)\,z = 0 \)
      (exact symbols/denominators in the spoken equation are unclear in transcript; form captured as lecture-intended)
    • Meaning of symbols in the lecture context:
      • \(m\): mass of the electron
      • \(h\): Planck’s constant
      • \(E\): total energy
      • \(V\): potential energy
      • \(z\) / \(\psi\): wavefunction
    • Key takeaway: solving the wave equation yields a mathematical solution with variables tied to electron “position” in 3D space; these variables lead to quantum numbers.
  3. Orbit vs orbital (3D probability region) (09:58)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Orbit: fixed circular path used in Bohr-type models.
    • Orbital: three-dimensional region where probability of finding electron is maximum.
      \( \text{Orbital} = \text{3D space region with max probability} \)
    • Orbitals differ in: size, shape, and orientation (which is why multiple quantum numbers are required).
  4. Principal quantum number \(n\): size & energy (12:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Four quantum numbers: \(n, l, m, s\) collectively specify the electron’s state (probability-based “address”).
    • Principal quantum number \(n\):
      • Allowed values: \(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) (positive integers only); \(n \ne 0\).
      • Shell names: \(K, L, M, N, \ldots\) correspond to \(n = 1,2,3,4,\ldots\)
      • Gives orbital size (general trend: size increases as \(n\) increases).
      • Gives orbital/electron energy (general trend: energy increases as \(n\) increases).
    • For hydrogen-like (one-electron) species:
      \( E_n = -13.6 \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \)
      where \(Z\) is atomic number (nuclear charge) and \(n\) is principal quantum number.
  5. Azimuthal quantum number \(l\): shape; subshells (15:04)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Azimuthal / angular momentum quantum number \(l\): determines shape of the orbital.
    • Subshell mapping:
      • \(l=0 \Rightarrow s\)
      • \(l=1 \Rightarrow p\)
      • \(l=2 \Rightarrow d\)
      • \(l=3 \Rightarrow f\)
    • Shape summary (lecture-level):
      • \(s\): spherical
      • \(p\): dumbbell
      • \(d\): “double dumbbell” type (multiple orientations)
      • \(f\): complex multi-lobed shapes
  6. Rules for \(l\): \(0 \le l \le n-1\) and subshell count (20:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Range of \(l\) for a given shell \(n\):
      \( l = 0,1,2,\ldots,(n-1) \)
    • Subshells present (examples):
      • \(n=1\): \(1s\)
      • \(n=2\): \(2s, 2p\)
      • \(n=3\): \(3s, 3p, 3d\)
      • \(n=4\): \(4s, 4p, 4d, 4f\)
    • Number of subshells in shell \(n\):
      \(\text{No. of subshells} = n\)
  7. Orbital angular momentum formula (23:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Orbital angular momentum magnitude:
      \( L = \dfrac{h}{2\pi}\sqrt{l(l+1)} \)
    • Examples:
      • \(s\) subshell: \(l=0 \Rightarrow L=0\)
      • \(3p\) subshell: \(l=1 \Rightarrow L=\dfrac{h}{2\pi}\sqrt{2}\)
  8. Magnetic quantum number \(m\): orientation & orbitals (25:45)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Magnetic quantum number \(m\): determines orbital orientation in space; also helps count orbitals.
    • Range:
      \( m = -l, -(l-1), \ldots, 0, \ldots, (l-1), l \)
    • Orbitals per subshell:
      \(\text{No. of orbitals in a subshell} = 2l+1\)
    • Mapping:
      • \(l=0\) (\(s\)) \(\Rightarrow\) \(m=0\) (1 orbital)
      • \(l=1\) (\(p\)) \(\Rightarrow\) \(m=-1,0,1\) (3 orbitals)
      • \(l=2\) (\(d\)) \(\Rightarrow\) 5 orbitals
      • \(l=3\) (\(f\)) \(\Rightarrow\) 7 orbitals
  9. Constructing \(n,l,m\) sets; examples up to \(n=4\) (31:04)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Shell \(n=1\):
      • \(l=0\) only
      • \(m=0\) only
      • Orbital: \(1s\)
    • Shell \(n=2\):
      • \(l=0,1\) \(\Rightarrow 2s,2p\)
      • \(2s\): \(m=0\) (1 orbital)
      • \(2p\): \(m=-1,0,1\) (3 orbitals)
    • Shell \(n=3\):
      • \(l=0,1,2\) \(\Rightarrow 3s,3p,3d\)
      • \(3s\): 1 orbital; \(3p\): 3 orbitals; \(3d\): 5 orbitals
    • Shell \(n=4\):
      • \(l=0,1,2,3\) \(\Rightarrow 4s,4p,4d,4f\)
      • \(4s\): 1; \(4p\): 3; \(4d\): 5; \(4f\): 7 orbitals
    • Orientation naming for \(p\) orbitals:
      \(p_x, p_y, p_z\) (commonly linked to x-, y-, z-axis orientation).
  10. Counting orbitals in a shell: \(n^2\) (35:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Number of orbitals in shell \(n\):
      \(\text{No. of orbitals in shell } n = n^2\)
    • Example: For \(n=3\), orbitals \(= 1 (s) + 3 (p) + 5 (d) = 9 = 3^2\).
  11. Spin quantum number \(s\) and Pauli exclusion (39:13)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Spin quantum number \(s\): describes electron spin about its own axis (two possible spin states).
    • Values:
      \( s = +\dfrac{1}{2} \) or \( s = -\dfrac{1}{2} \)
    • Pauli exclusion principle:
      No two electrons in the same atom can have identical \((n,l,m,s)\).
    • Consequence:
      \(\text{Max electrons in one orbital} = 2\), and they must have opposite spins.
  12. Electron counting formulas: \(2n^2\), \(2(2l+1)\) (46:38)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Max electrons in shell \(n\):
      \( \text{No. of electrons} = 2n^2 \)
    • Max electrons in subshell \(l\):
      \( \text{No. of electrons} = 2(2l+1) \)
    • Linked results (from lecture):
      • \(\text{No. of subshells in shell } n = n\)
      • \(\text{No. of orbitals in a subshell } l = 2l+1\)
      • \(\text{No. of orbitals in shell } n = n^2\)
      • \(\text{No. of electrons in one orbital } = 2\)

  1. Bohr's Postulates

    1. Atom has a centre called Nucleus.
    2. Electron revolves only in fixed circular orbits with fixed energy and fixed velocity.
    3. Quantisation Condition: Electrons revolve only in those circular orbits for which the angular momentum (\(L\)) is an integral multiple of \(\dfrac{h}{2\pi}\), or \( L = n \dfrac{h}{2\pi} \)
      where \( h \) is Planck's constant \( (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s}) \)
      n → shell no
      n = 1 (K), n = 2 (L), n = 3 (M), n = 4 (N), etc

      • The orbital angular momentum of an electron is the vector quantity defined by the cross product of its position vector and its linear momentum.
      • \( \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} \) (momentum)
      • \( \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times m\vec{v} \)
      • \( \vec{L} = r m v \sin \theta = m v r \sin 90^\circ = m v r \)
        where the vector \( \vec{r} \) represents the position vector of the electron relative to a chosen origin, typically the nucleus of the atom.
      • \( L = mvr \)
      • \( mvr = \dfrac{nh}{2\pi} \) → Equation 1
    4. While revolving, the electrostatic force between the electron (\( e^- \)) and nucleus provides centripetal force.
      \( F_e = F_c \), where \( F_e \) is electrostatic force and \( F_c \) is centripetal force

      • \( F_e = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \)
        where \( q_1 \) and \( q_2 \) are the charges of the electron and nucleus, \( r \) is the distance between them, and \( \epsilon_0 \) is the permittivity of free space.
      • \( F_c = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} \)
        where \( m \) is the mass of the electron, \( v \) is the velocity of the electron, and \( r \) is the radius of the circular orbit.
      • \( \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \dfrac{Ze^2}{r^2} = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} \) → Equation 2
      • Note: Charge on electron is -e and that on proton is +Ze, where Z is the atomic number of the element. +Ze = "nuclear charge".
      • While revolving in a particular orbit, an electron neither gains nor loses energy (countering Maxwell theory).
      • Energy of an orbit/shell is fixed.
      • Shells → Stationary Energy Levels.
      • n=1 \(\implies\) stationary energy level = "K"
        n=2 \(\implies\) stationary energy level = "L"
        n=3 \(\implies\) stationary energy level = "M"
        n=4 \(\implies\) stationary energy level = "N"
        n=5 \(\implies\) stationary energy level = "O"
      • Electron can gain and lose energy when it jumps up to and down from a stationary energy level.
      • There are certain conditions to this :
        1. If an electron accepts energy, it jumps to higher energy level -> "Excitation of electron"
        2. If an electron loses energy, it recedes back to lower energy level -> "De-excitation of electron"
        3. It will gain or lose that amount of energy that enables it to jump to higher energy level, or recede to a lower energy level (= difference in energies of the energy levels).
        4. This explained the Atomic spectrum
    7. Limitation : Bohr's model is valid only on single electron species, example \(\text{H, He}^+\)

  2. Calculations

    1. Radius of shell of an electron in the nth shell \({r}_n\):
      • \( {r}_n = \dfrac{n^2 h^2 \epsilon_0}{\pi m Ze^2} \) → Equation 3
      • \( {r}_n = 0.53 * \dfrac{n^2}{Z} Å \) → Equation 4

      • Example on radius :
        Answer video here Radius of 1st shell of the H atom = 0.53 * 1 = 0.53
        1. \( n = 2 \), Li : 0.53 * \(\dfrac{\text{2}^2} {\text{3}}\) \(\ne\) 0.53
        2. \( n = 2 \), Be : 0.53 * \(\dfrac{\text{2}^2} {\text{4}}\) = 0.53
        3. \( n = 2 \), He : 0.53 * \(\dfrac{\text{2}^2} {\text{2}}\) \(\ne\) 0.53
        4. \( n = 3 \), He : 0.53 * \(\dfrac{\text{3}^2} {\text{3}}\) \(\ne\) 0.53
          5. \(\therefore\) (b), : \( n = 2 \), Be is correct


    2. Velocity of the electron in the nth shell.
      • \( mv_nr = \dfrac{n h}{2 \pi} \)
      • \( mv_n \left( \dfrac{n^2 h^2 \varepsilon_0}{\pi m 2 e^2} \right) = \dfrac{n h}{2 \pi} \)
      • \( v_n = \dfrac{Z e^2}{2 h \varepsilon_0 n} \) → Equation 5
      • \( v_n = 2.18 \times 10^6 \dfrac{Z}{n} \text{ m/s} \) → Equation 6

    3. Energy of the electron in the nth shell.

      • An electron moving in a shell around the nucleus has two kinds of energy: Kinetic and Potential

        1. K.E. : Kinetic Energy
          • \( \text{K.E.} = \dfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} m \left( \dfrac{Z e^2}{2 h \varepsilon_0 n} \right)^2 \)
          • \( \text{KE} = \dfrac{1}{2} \dfrac{m Z^2 e^4}{4 h^2 \varepsilon_0^2 n^2} \) → Equation 7
          • \( \text{KE} = \dfrac{1}{8} \dfrac{m Z^2 e^4}{h^2 \varepsilon_0^2 n^2} \)
          • \( \text{KE} = \dfrac{m e^4}{8 h^2 \varepsilon_0^2} \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} = 13.6 \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV} \) → Equation 8
        2. P.E. : Potential Energy (Electrostatic).
          • \( \text{P.E.} = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0} \dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r} = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0} \dfrac{-e \times Z e}{r} \)
          • \( = -\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0} \dfrac{Z e^2}{n^2 h^2 \varepsilon_0} \times \dfrac{m Z e^2}{1} \)
          • \( = -\dfrac{m Z^2 e^4}{8 \varepsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2} \times 2 \)
          • \( = -\dfrac{m e^4}{8 \varepsilon_0^2 h^2} \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \times 2 \)
          • \( - 2 \times 13.6 \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \) → Equation 9


        3. T.E. = K.E. + P.E.
          • \( = -2 \times 13.6 \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} + 13.6 \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \)
          • \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV} \) → Equation 10
          • We can see that Total Energy, T.E is negative

          • This refers to the "ionization energy", which is the energy required to remove an electon from the nth shell.


          • Example on Total Energy :
            • T.E = \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV} \)
            • = \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{1^2}{n^2} \text{ eV} \)
            • Substituting increasing values for n :
              1. n = 1 \(\implies\) T.E = \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{1^2}{1^2} \text{ eV} \) = \(-\)13.6 eV
              2. n = 2 \(\implies\) T.E = \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{1^2}{2^2} \text{ eV} \) = \(-\)3.4 eV
              3. n = 3 \(\implies\) T.E = \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{1^2}{3^2} \text{ eV} \) = \(-\)1.51 eV
              4. n = 4 \(\implies\) T.E = \( = -13.6 \times \dfrac{1^2}{4^2} \text{ eV} \) = \(-\)0.85 eV

          • Maximum Energy of an electron :
            • Maximum energy of electron is when \( n \to \infty \)
            • Max T.E = 0
            • \(\implies\) electron is free from the nucleus
            • \(\implies\) ionization has occured.

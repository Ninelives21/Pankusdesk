  1. Why Rutherford model failed; need for Bohr model (00:37)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rutherford model could not explain **atomic stability**:
      A revolving electron (accelerated charge) should radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus.
    • Rutherford model could not explain **hydrogen line spectrum**:
      Only specific wavelengths appear, not a continuous spectrum.
    • Bohr model was proposed to address both: stability + discrete spectral lines.
  2. Bohr postulates: fixed orbits; quantization of angular momentum (01:55)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Atom has a central **nucleus** (already known from Rutherford).
    • Electron can revolve only in **certain fixed circular orbits**:
      Each allowed orbit has a fixed radius, fixed speed, and fixed energy.
    • These allowed orbits are decided by **quantization (Bohr) condition**:
      Electron revolves only in those circular orbits for which the angular momentum is an integral multiple of \( \frac{h}{2\pi} \).
    • Shell number notation:
      • \(n=1\) → K shell
      • \(n=2\) → L shell
      • \(n=3\) → M shell
      • \(n=4\) → N shell
    • Quantized angular momentum form:
      \( L = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \), where \(n\) is an integer (shell number).
    • Fractional multiples like \( \frac{3}{2}\frac{h}{2\pi} \) are not allowed for Bohr orbits.
  3. Postulate 3 : Angular momentum: definition and Bohr quantization equation (09:25)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Orbital angular momentum is a vector quantity:
      \( \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} \)
    • Momentum \( \vec{p} = m\vec{v} \).
    • For a circular orbit, \( \vec{r} \perp \vec{v} \Rightarrow \sin 90^\circ = 1 \), so magnitude:
      \( L = mvr \)
    • Bohr quantization condition becomes:
      \( mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \)
    • Meanings of symbols:
      • \(m\): mass of electron
      • \(v\): speed of electron in that orbit
      • \(r\): radius of that orbit
      • \(h\): Planck constant
      • \(n\): integer shell number
  4. Postulate 4 : Coulomb force provides centripetal force; key equations (14:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For circular motion, a centripetal force toward the center is required:
      \( F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} \)
    • Electrostatic (Coulomb) force between two charges \(q_1, q_2\) separated by \(r\):
      \( F_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q_1q_2}{r^2} \)
    • In an atom, charges are:
      • electron charge magnitude: \(e\) (electron itself is negative)
      • nuclear charge: \(+Ze\) where \(Z\) is atomic number
    • Bohr model uses:
      \( F_e = F_c \)
    • Substituting charges (using magnitudes for force):
      \( \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Ze^2}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \)
    • This relation is used along with \( mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \) for derivations.
  5. Postulate 5 : Stationary energy levels and atomic stability (21:25)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Key stability postulate:
      While revolving in a particular allowed orbit, an electron **neither gains nor loses energy**.
    • Allowed shells are called **stationary energy levels** because energy remains fixed in that orbit.
    • Shell naming:
      • \(n=1\): K
      • \(n=2\): L
      • \(n=3\): M
      • \(n=4\): N
      • \(n=5\): O
    • Not all energy values are permitted:
      Only discrete energy levels exist; “in-between” energies do not correspond to any stable orbit.
    • Implication:
      Electron can exist only at specific radii, specific speeds, and specific energies (quantized).
  6. Calculation 1 : Radius of electton in nth orbit; shortcut form and examples (27:57)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Use the two key equations:
      • \( mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \)
      • \( \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Ze^2}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \)
    • Eliminating \(v\) gives the radius of the \(n\)th Bohr orbit:
      \( r = \frac{n^2h^2\varepsilon_0}{\pi mZe^2} \)
    • Common shortcut (after substituting constants):
      \( r_n = 0.53\frac{n^2}{Z}\ \text{Å} \)
    • Example (Hydrogen, \(Z=1\)):
      • \(r_1 = 0.53\ \text{Å}\)
      • \(r_2 = 0.53\times 4 = 2.12\ \text{Å}\)
      • \(r_3 = 0.53\times 9 = 4.77\ \text{Å}\)
    • Concept point:
      \(r_n\) increases as \(n^2\) and decreases as \(1/Z\).
    • Practice-type question idea (same radius condition):
      Equal radii require \( \frac{n^2}{Z} \) to be equal for the compared species.
    • Applicability note:
      Bohr model works for **single-electron species** (H and hydrogen-like ions such as \( \text{He}^+ \), \( \text{Li}^{2+} \), \( \text{Be}^{3+} \)).
  7. Calculation 2 : Velocity of electron velocity in nth orbit; shortcut form and examples (39:26)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Start from quantization:
      \( mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \)
    • Substitute \(r\) expression to get speed in the \(n\)th orbit:
      \( v_n = \frac{Ze^2}{2\varepsilon_0hn} \)
    • Shortcut numerical form:
      \( v_n = 2.18\times 10^6\frac{Z}{n}\ \text{m s}^{-1} \)
    • Example (Hydrogen, first orbit \(Z=1,n=1\)):
      \( v_1 = 2.18\times 10^6\ \text{m s}^{-1} \)
    • Scaling:
      • Increasing \(Z\) increases speed proportionally.
      • Increasing \(n\) decreases speed as \(1/n\).
    • Why full derivation matters (concept):
      If mass/charge values are altered in a problem statement (non-standard particle), shortcut constants may not apply.
  8. Calculation 3 : Energy in Bohr orbit: KE, PE, total energy; negative energy meaning (43:12
    (Back to topic index)
    • In a Bohr orbit, “energy of electron” is treated as the energy of that stationary level (shell).
    • Two contributions:
      • Kinetic energy (motion): \( K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
      • Electrostatic potential energy (attraction): \( U = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q_1q_2}{r} \)
    • Using \(v_n\) gives kinetic energy expression:
      \( K_n = 13.6\frac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \)
    • Potential energy for electron–nucleus pair (with sign):
      \( U_n = -2\times 13.6\frac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \)
    • Total energy:
      \( E_n = K_n + U_n = -13.6\frac{Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \)
    • Meaning of negative total energy:
      Electron is bound to nucleus; energy must be supplied to remove it to infinity (ionization).
  9. Total energy of electron in nth shell of Hydrogen atom (52:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For hydrogen, \(Z=1\), so:
      \( E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2}\ \text{eV} \)
    • Common values (useful for quick MCQ checks):
      • \(E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}\)
      • \(E_2 = -3.4\ \text{eV}\)
      • \(E_3 \approx -1.51\ \text{eV}\)
      • \(E_4 = -0.85\ \text{eV}\)
    • Trend:
      As \(n\) increases, energy increases (becomes less negative), meaning electron is less tightly bound.
    • Maximum possible energy occurs at \( n \to \infty \):
      \( E_{\infty} = 0 \ \text{eV} \)
    • Interpretation of \(E=0\):
      Electron is free (ionization has occurred).
    • Ionization idea from ground state:
      Providing \(13.6\ \text{eV}\) raises \(E_1=-13.6\ \text{eV}\) to \(0\ \text{eV}\) (electron removed).
  10. Postulate 6 : Excitation/de-excitation; allowed energy absorption (58:35)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Sixth postulate (transition rule):
      Electron can gain or lose energy only by changing its energy level (orbit).
    • Excitation:
      If electron absorbs energy, it jumps to a higher energy level (higher \(n\)).
    • De-excitation:
      If electron loses energy, it drops to a lower energy level (lower \(n\)).
    • Energy absorption is selective:
      Electron absorbs only such energy that matches the difference between two stationary levels.
    • Hydrogen example idea:
      • From \(n=1\) to \(n=2\): energy needed is \( (-3.4) - (-13.6) = 10.2\ \text{eV} \).
      • If supplied energy does not make the final energy equal to an allowed level, it is not absorbed as a stable transition.
      1. Question 1
        An electron in hydrogen ground state is given \(9\ \text{eV}\). Will it absorb and jump to a new stationary level?
        • It would lead to resultant energy \( -13.6 + 9 = -4.6\ \text{eV} \), which does not correspond to a standard hydrogen level (per lecture logic), so it will not be a stable absorption transition.

      2. Question 2
        What energy must be supplied to hydrogen ground-state electron to move it to \(n=2\)?
        • Required energy equals level difference:
          \( \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = (-3.4) - (-13.6) = 10.2\ \text{eV} \).

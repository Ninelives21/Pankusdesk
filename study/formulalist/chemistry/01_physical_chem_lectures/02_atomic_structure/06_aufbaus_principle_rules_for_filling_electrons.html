  1. Aufbau principle: meaning and core rule (00:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Aufbau is a German word meaning “construction”; not named after a scientist.
    • Purpose: decide electron filling order among subshells/orbitals (e.g., \(1s, 2s, 2p, 3s\) …).
    • Key basis: energy of orbitals/subshells.
    • Rule statement:
      \( \text{Electrons occupy lower-energy orbitals first, then higher-energy orbitals.} \)
    • Reminder facts used later:
      • Subshell labels: \(s, p, d, f\).
      • Orbitals per subshell: \(s:1\), \(p:3\), \(d:5\), \(f:7\).
  2. Energy order via \(n+l\) rule + tie-breaker (01:25)
    (Back to topic index)
    • To compare energies of subshells/orbitals, use the \(n+l\) rule.
    • Quantum numbers used:
      • \(n\) = principal quantum number (shell number).
      • \(l\) = azimuthal quantum number (subshell type).
      • Mapping:
        \( l(s)=0,\; l(p)=1,\; l(d)=2,\; l(f)=3 \)
    • Energy trend (multi-electron atoms):
      \( E \propto (n+l) \)
      Larger \(n+l\) \(\Rightarrow\) higher energy \(\Rightarrow\) filled later.
    • Tie-breaker:
      If \(n+l\) is same for two subshells, the subshell with larger \(n\) has higher energy.
    • Example comparison: \(2s\) vs \(3p\)
      • \(2s:\; n+l = 2+0 = 2\)
      • \(3p:\; n+l = 3+1 = 4\)
      • So \(E(2s) < E(3p)\) and electrons fill \(2s\) before \(3p\).
  3. Arrange subshells by energy: worked ordering (03:49)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Task: arrange given subshells in increasing order of energy using \(n+l\) (and tie-breaker by \(n\)).
    • Given: \(1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 3d, 4s, 4p, 4d, 5s\).
    • Compute \(n+l\):
      • \(1s:\; 1+0=1\)
      • \(2s:\; 2+0=2\)
      • \(2p:\; 2+1=3\)
      • \(3s:\; 3+0=3\)
      • \(3p:\; 3+1=4\)
      • \(4s:\; 4+0=4\)
      • \(3d:\; 3+2=5\)
      • \(4p:\; 4+1=5\)
      • \(5s:\; 5+0=5\)
      • \(4d:\; 4+2=6\)
    • Apply tie-breaker where needed:
      • \(n+l=3:\) compare \(2p\) vs \(3s\) \(\Rightarrow\) larger \(n\) has higher energy, so \(2p\) before \(3s\).
      • \(n+l=4:\) compare \(3p\) vs \(4s\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(3p\) before \(4s\).
      • \(n+l=5:\) compare \(3d, 4p, 5s\) \(\Rightarrow\) increasing energy by \(n\): \(3d < 4p < 5s\).
    • Final increasing energy order (same as filling order):
      \(1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d\)
  4. Diagonal method to remember filling order (07:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Memory method: write subshells by rows (shell-wise), then read diagonally.
    • Row layout (up to the shown level):
      • \(1s\)
      • \(2s\;\; 2p\)
      • \(3s\;\; 3p\;\; 3d\)
      • \(4s\;\; 4p\;\; 4d\;\; 4f\)
      • \(5s\;\; 5p\)
      • \(6s\)
    • Diagonal reading gives the same Aufbau order:
      \(1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s\)
  5. Limitation: single-electron systems (09:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For single-electron species (hydrogen-like): energy depends only on \(n\), not on \(l\).
    • Examples mentioned: \(H\), \(H^+\) (species list unclear in transcript).
    • Consequence:
      • If \(n\) is same, subshell energies are equal (degenerate), e.g., \(2s\) and \(2p\) have same energy in a single-electron system.
      • Therefore, the \(n+l\) ordering used for multi-electron atoms is not applied in the same way for single-electron systems.

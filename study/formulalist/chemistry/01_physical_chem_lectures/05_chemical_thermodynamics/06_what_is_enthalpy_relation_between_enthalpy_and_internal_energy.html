  1. Enthalpy: definition and meaning of \(H=U+PV\) (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Enthalpy is a thermodynamic variable denoted by \(H\), defined by:
      \(H = U + PV\)
    • Meaning of symbols:
      • \(U\): internal energy (also written as \(E\) in some books)
      • \(P\): external pressure
      • \(V\): volume of the system
    • Intuition (why \(PV\) is included):
      • \(U\) accounts for microscopic energies (molecular/atomic kinetic + potential interaction energies).
      • \(PV\) represents the “pressure–volume energy” associated with establishing volume against external pressure.
    • Units:
      • Energy unit: joule (often reported as \(\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}\) in thermochemistry).
    • Absolute \(H\) cannot be measured directly; thermochemistry focuses on changes:
      \(\Delta H\) (not the absolute value of \(H\)).
  2. Why \(\Delta H\) matters; general formula and special cases (08:50)
    (Back to topic index)
    • From \(H=U+PV\), the enthalpy change is:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(PV)\)
    • Most general two-state form (always valid for any process):
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + (P_2V_2 - P_1V_1)\)
    • Special case 1: constant volume (\(V\) constant)
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + V\,\Delta P\)
    • Special case 2: constant pressure (\(P\) constant) — common in chemistry
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + P\,\Delta V\)
    • Choosing the correct form:
      • If both \(P\) and \(V\) change → use \(P_2V_2 - P_1V_1\).
      • If one is constant → factor it out as above.
  3. Practice: variable \(P,V\) case (JEE 2002) (13:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given states of a gas (both \(P\) and \(V\) change) and \(\Delta U\) (or \(\Delta E\)):
      • State 1: \(P_1=2\,\mathrm{atm}\), \(V_1=3\,\mathrm{L}\)
      • State 2: \(P_2=4\,\mathrm{atm}\), \(V_2=5\,\mathrm{L}\)
      • \(\Delta U = 30\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\)
    • Use the general relation:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + (P_2V_2 - P_1V_1)\)
    • Compute the \(PV\) term:
      • \(P_2V_2 = 4\times 5 = 20\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\)
      • \(P_1V_1 = 2\times 3 = 6\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\)
      • \(P_2V_2-P_1V_1 = 14\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\)
    • Final:
      \(\Delta H = 30 + 14 = 44\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\)
      1. Question 1
        A gas goes from \((P_1,V_1,T_1)=(2\,\mathrm{atm},3\,\mathrm{L},95\,\mathrm{K})\) to \((P_2,V_2,T_2)=(4\,\mathrm{atm},5\,\mathrm{L},245\,\mathrm{K})\). If \(\Delta U=30\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\), find \(\Delta H\). Options: 41, 42, 43, 44 \(\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\).

      2. Answer: \(44\,\mathrm{atm\cdot L}\).
  4. Practice: ideal gas isothermal expansion gives \(\Delta H=0\) (16:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For an ideal gas:
      • Internal energy depends only on temperature.
      • If the process is isothermal (\(\Delta T=0\)), then \(\Delta U=0\).
    • Also for an ideal gas in an isothermal process:
      \(P_1V_1 = P_2V_2\) (Boyle’s law at constant \(T\))
      Hence \(P_2V_2 - P_1V_1 = 0\).
    • Therefore:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + (P_2V_2 - P_1V_1) = 0 + 0 = 0\)
      1. Question 2
        An ideal gas expands isothermally at \(25^\circ\mathrm{C}\) from \(1\,\mathrm{L}\) to \(10\,\mathrm{L}\). Find \(\Delta H\). Options: \(-7\,\mathrm{kJ}\), \(+7\,\mathrm{kJ}\), \(-\frac{44}{3}\,\mathrm{kJ}\), \(0\).

      2. Answer: \(0\).
  5. Properties of enthalpy: state function, cyclic process, extensive property (21:27)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Enthalpy is a state function:
      • \(\Delta H\) depends only on initial and final states.
      • Path does not affect \(\Delta H\).
      • \(\Delta H = H_{\text{final}} - H_{\text{initial}}\)
    • Cyclic process:
      • If the system returns to the initial state, then \(\Delta H=0\).
    • Enthalpy is an extensive property (depends on amount of substance):
      • Larger amount of matter → larger \(U\) and typically larger \(PV\) → larger \(H\).
  6. First law link: at constant pressure \(\Delta H=\Delta Q_p\) (25:26)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Start with constant-pressure enthalpy change:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + P\,\Delta V\) (for constant \(P\))
    • First law (chemistry sign convention used here):
      \(\Delta U = \Delta Q + \Delta W\)
    • For \(PV\) work at constant external pressure:
      \(\Delta W = -P_{\text{ext}}\,\Delta V\)
    • At constant pressure, heat supplied is written as \(\Delta Q_p\). Using the first law:
      \(\Delta U = \Delta Q_p - P\,\Delta V\)
    • Substitute into \(\Delta H=\Delta U+P\,\Delta V\):
      \(\Delta H = (\Delta Q_p - P\,\Delta V) + P\,\Delta V = \Delta Q_p\)
    • Key conclusion:
      • \(\Delta H\) equals heat only for constant-pressure processes.
      • In general, \(\Delta H \neq \Delta Q\) unless pressure is constant.
  7. Heat capacity link: \(\Delta H=nC_p\Delta T\) and \(C_p=(\frac{f}{2}+1)R\) (30:40)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For heating a gaseous system:
      \(\Delta Q = nC\,\Delta T\)
    • At constant pressure:
      \(\Delta Q_p = nC_p\,\Delta T\)
    • Using \(\Delta H=\Delta Q_p\) (constant \(P\)):
      \(\Delta H = nC_p\,\Delta T\)
    • Differential form:
      \(dH = nC_p\,dT\)
    • For an ideal gas (using degrees of freedom \(f\)):
      \(C_p = \left(\frac{f}{2}+1\right)R\)
    • Typical \(f\) values (as used in basic thermodynamics problems):
      • Monatomic gas: \(f=3\)
      • Diatomic gas: \(f=5\)
      • Polyatomic gas: \(f=6\)
    • Example setup (helium heated at constant pressure):
      • Given: \(n=2\) moles, \(0^\circ\mathrm{C}\to 50^\circ\mathrm{C}\) so \(\Delta T=50\) (same in K)
      • For helium (monatomic): \(f=3\), so \(C_p=\left(\frac{3}{2}+1\right)R=\frac{5}{2}R\)
      • Then: \(\Delta H = nC_p\Delta T = 2\times \frac{5}{2}R \times 50\)
    • Choice of \(R\) depends on required energy unit:
      • In joule: \(R=8.314\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}\)
      • In calorie (approx): \(R\approx 2\,\mathrm{cal\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}\)
      1. Question 3
        \(2\) moles of helium are heated at constant pressure from \(0^\circ\mathrm{C}\) to \(50^\circ\mathrm{C}\). Find \(\Delta H\).

      2. Answer (setup): \(\Delta H = 2\times \frac{5}{2}R \times 50\) (use \(R\) in required units).
  8. Reaction relation: \(\Delta H=\Delta U+\Delta n_gRT\) and how to compute \(\Delta n_g\) (37:14)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For chemical reactions involving gases (reactants/products may include gases), start from:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(PV)\)
    • For gas terms, use the ideal gas relation \(PV=nRT\) (for gaseous moles in the reaction context):
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(nRT)\)
    • In most reaction conditions, pressure and temperature are treated as constant. Then:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT\)
    • Definition of \(\Delta n_g\):
      \(\Delta n_g = (\text{moles of gaseous products}) - (\text{moles of gaseous reactants})\)
    • Only gaseous species are counted in \(\Delta n_g\); solids and liquids are excluded.
    • Practical use:
      • This relation is widely used for numericals on \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta U\).
      • Also remember: \(\Delta E\) may be used instead of \(\Delta U\) in some texts.
  9. Compare \(\Delta H\) vs \(\Delta U\) using \(\Delta n_g\) examples (42:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Core comparison rule (at constant \(T,P\)):
      \(\Delta H - \Delta U = \Delta n_g RT\)
    • If \(\Delta n_g<0\), then \(\Delta H<\Delta U\).
    • If \(\Delta n_g=0\), then \(\Delta H=\Delta U\).
    • If \(\Delta n_g>0\), then \(\Delta H>\Delta U\).
    • Examples (count only gaseous moles):
      • \( \mathrm{N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow 2NH_3(g)} \)
        • \(\Delta n_g = 2-(1+3) = -2\)
        • \(\Delta H = \Delta U - 2RT\Rightarrow \Delta U>\Delta H\)
      • \( \mathrm{H_2(g) + Br_2(g) \rightarrow 2HBr(g)} \)
        • \(\Delta n_g = 2-(1+1)=0\)
        • \(\Delta H=\Delta U\)
      • \( \mathrm{MgCO_3(s) \rightarrow MgO(s) + CO_2(g)} \)
        • \(\Delta n_g = 1-0=1\)
        • \(\Delta H = \Delta U + RT\Rightarrow \Delta H>\Delta U\)
  10. Practice: benzene combustion to find \(\Delta H-\Delta E\) (45:54)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Use:
      \(\Delta H - \Delta E = \Delta n_g RT\)
    • Balanced reaction (as written in the lecture):
      \(\mathrm{2C_6H_6(l) + 15O_2(g) \rightarrow 12CO_2(g) + 6H_2O(l)}\)
    • Compute \(\Delta n_g\) (gases only):
      • Gaseous products: \(12\) (from \(CO_2\))
      • Gaseous reactants: \(15\) (from \(O_2\))
      • \(\Delta n_g = 12-15 = -3\)
    • At \(25^\circ\mathrm{C}\), take \(T=298\,\mathrm{K}\). Then:
      \(\Delta H - \Delta E = -3RT\)
    • Approximate in kJ (lecture-style estimation):
      • \(-3\times 8.314\times 298 \approx -7.2\times 10^3\,\mathrm{J}\approx -7.2\,\mathrm{kJ}\)
      • Closest option: \(-7.4\,\mathrm{kJ}\)
      1. Question 4
        For \(\mathrm{2C_6H_6(l) + 15O_2(g) \rightarrow 12CO_2(g) + 6H_2O(l)}\) at \(25^\circ\mathrm{C}\), find \(\Delta H-\Delta E\) (in kJ). Options: \(-7.4\), \(-3.2\), \(+7.4\), \(+3.2\).

      2. Answer (approx): \(-7.4\,\mathrm{kJ}\).
  11. Heat at constant \(P\) vs constant \(V\): \(\Delta Q_p-\Delta Q_v=\Delta n_gRT\) (49:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Interpretation:
      • \(\Delta H\) is heat at constant pressure: \(\Delta H = \Delta Q_p\)
      • \(\Delta U\) is heat at constant volume: \(\Delta U = \Delta Q_v\)
    • Proof idea for constant volume:
      • First law: \(\Delta U=\Delta Q+\Delta W\)
      • \(\Delta W=-P\Delta V\)
      • At constant volume, \(\Delta V=0\Rightarrow \Delta W=0\)
      • So \(\Delta U=\Delta Q_v\)
    • Combine with reaction relation:
      \(\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT\)
    • Replace \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta U\) by constant-pressure/constant-volume heat:
      \(\Delta Q_p = \Delta Q_v + \Delta n_g RT\)
    • Therefore:
      \(\Delta Q_p - \Delta Q_v = \Delta n_g RT\)
  12. Practice: propane combustion; \(\Delta Q_p-\Delta Q_v\) in \(RT\) terms (53:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Required quantity:
      \(\Delta Q_p - \Delta Q_v = \Delta n_g RT\)
    • Balanced reaction written in the lecture (water as liquid):
      \(\mathrm{C_3H_8(g) + 5O_2(g) \rightarrow 3CO_2(g) + 4H_2O(l)}\)
    • Compute \(\Delta n_g\) (gases only):
      • Gaseous products: \(3\) (from \(CO_2\))
      • Gaseous reactants: \(1+5=6\) (from \(C_3H_8\) and \(O_2\))
      • \(\Delta n_g = 3-6 = -3\)
    • Therefore:
      \(\Delta Q_p - \Delta Q_v = -3RT\)
      1. Question 5
        For propane combustion, find \(\Delta Q_p-\Delta Q_v\) in terms of \(RT\) at temperature \(T\). Options: \(-3RT\), \(-2RT\), \(+2RT\), \(+3RT\).

      2. Answer: \(-3RT\).

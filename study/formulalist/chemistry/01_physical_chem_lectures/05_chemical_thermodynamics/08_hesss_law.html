  1. Hess's Law: Statement and Path Independence (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Hess's law states that the total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the path followed.
    • If a reaction occurs in one step or in several steps, the overall enthalpy change remains the same.
    • For a reaction \( A \rightarrow B \) with enthalpy change \( \Delta H \), if it proceeds through an intermediate \( C \), then:
      \( A \rightarrow C \; (\Delta H_1) \)
      \( C \rightarrow B \; (\Delta H_2) \)
      Overall, \( \Delta H = \Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 \).
    • This principle is especially useful when the enthalpy change of the required reaction cannot be measured directly.
  2. Basic Addition of Enthalpy Changes (03:53)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When two given reactions add up directly to the required reaction, their enthalpy changes also add directly.
    • Given:
      \( C(s) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow CO(g), \; \Delta H = -x \)
      \( CO(g) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g), \; \Delta H = -y \)
    • Adding both reactions gives:
      \( C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) \)
    • Therefore, the enthalpy of the required reaction is:
      \( \Delta H = -x - y \).
    • This is the simplest application of Hess's law: add equations and add their enthalpy changes in the same manner.
  3. Manipulating Equations to Obtain Required Reaction (06:26)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given:
      \( Fe_2O_3 + 3CO \rightarrow 2Fe + 3CO_2, \; \Delta H_1 = -26 \; \text{kJ} \)
      \( FeO + CO \rightarrow Fe + CO_2, \; \Delta H_2 = -16 \; \text{kJ} \)
    • Required:
      \( Fe_2O_3 + CO \rightarrow 2FeO + CO_2 \)
    • Multiply the second equation by 2:
      \( 2FeO + 2CO \rightarrow 2Fe + 2CO_2, \; 2\Delta H_2 \)
    • Subtract \( 2 \times \) second equation from the first equation:
      \( (i) - 2(ii) \Rightarrow Fe_2O_3 + CO \rightarrow 2FeO + CO_2 \)
    • Hence,
      \( \Delta H = \Delta H_1 - 2\Delta H_2 \)
      \( \Delta H = -26 - 2(-16) = -26 + 32 = +6 \; \text{kJ} \)
    • Rule: whatever algebraic operation is applied to chemical equations must be applied identically to enthalpy change.
    • Enthalpy is an extensive property, so multiplying a reaction by a factor multiplies \( \Delta H \) by the same factor.
  4. Enthalpy of Formation and Combustion of Ethane (11:50)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Required reaction:
      \( C_2H_6 + \frac{7}{2}O_2 \rightarrow 2CO_2 + 3H_2O \)
    • Given standard enthalpies of formation:
      \( \Delta H_f(C_2H_6) = -20 \; \text{kcal} \)
      \( \Delta H_f(CO_2) = -90 \; \text{kcal} \)
      \( \Delta H_f(H_2O) = -70 \; \text{kcal} \)
    • Meaning of enthalpy of formation: enthalpy change when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its constituent elements in their free natural states.
    • Examples of formation equations:
      \( 2C(s,\text{graphite}) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow C_2H_6(g) \)
      \( C(s,\text{graphite}) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) \)
      \( H_2(g) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(l) \)
    • To build the required combustion reaction:
      required equation \( = -1 \times \) formation of \( C_2H_6 \)
      \( + 2 \times \) formation of \( CO_2 \)
      \( + 3 \times \) formation of \( H_2O \)
    • Therefore,
      \( \Delta H = -\Delta H_f(C_2H_6) + 2\Delta H_f(CO_2) + 3\Delta H_f(H_2O) \)
      \( \Delta H = -(-20) + 2(-90) + 3(-70) \)
      \( \Delta H = 20 - 180 - 210 = -370 \; \text{kcal} \)
  5. Shortcut Formulas Using Formation or Combustion Data (22:25)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When standard enthalpies of formation of all reactants and products are known:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{reaction}} = \sum \Delta H_f(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta H_f(\text{reactants}) \)
    • Stoichiometric coefficients must always be included.
    • Elements in their free natural states have zero enthalpy of formation.
      Examples: \( O_2, H_2, N_2, Cl_2, Br_2, C(s,\text{graphite}) \).
    • Using the previous ethane combustion example:
      \( \Delta H = [2(-90) + 3(-70)] - [1(-20) + \frac{7}{2}(0)] = -370 \; \text{kcal} \)
    • When standard enthalpies of combustion of all species are known:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{reaction}} = \sum \Delta H_c(\text{reactants}) - \sum \Delta H_c(\text{products}) \)
    • Memory aid:
      Formation data \( \Rightarrow \) products minus reactants
      Combustion data \( \Rightarrow \) reactants minus products
  6. Formation Enthalpy of Acetylene from Combustion Data (27:53)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Required formation reaction of acetylene:
      \( 2C(s,\text{graphite}) + H_2(g) \rightarrow C_2H_2(g) \)
    • Given combustion enthalpies:
      \( C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g), \; \Delta H_1 = -400 \; \text{kJ} \)
      \( H_2(g) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(l), \; \Delta H_2 = -300 \; \text{kJ} \)
      \( C_2H_2(g) + \frac{5}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow 2CO_2(g) + H_2O(l), \; \Delta H_3 = -1300 \; \text{kJ} \)
    • To obtain the required equation:
      required equation \( = 2(i) + (ii) - (iii) \)
    • Hence,
      \( \Delta H = 2\Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 - \Delta H_3 \)
      \( \Delta H = 2(-400) + (-300) - (-1300) \)
      \( \Delta H = -800 - 300 + 1300 = +200 \; \text{kJ} \)
    • Shortcut with combustion data gives the same result:
      \( \Delta H = [2(-400) + 1(-300)] - [1(-1300)] = +200 \; \text{kJ} \)
  7. Formation Enthalpy of Ethanol from Mixed Data (40:33)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Required formation reaction of ethanol:
      \( 2C(s,\text{graphite}) + 3H_2(g) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow C_2H_5OH(l) \)
    • Given:
      \( H_2(g) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(l), \; \Delta H_1 = -250 \; \text{kJ} \)
      \( C(s,\text{graphite}) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g), \; \Delta H_2 = -400 \; \text{kJ} \)
      \( C_2H_5OH(l) + 3O_2(g) \rightarrow 2CO_2(g) + 3H_2O(l), \; \Delta H_3 = -100 \; \text{kJ} \)
    • To obtain the required formation equation:
      required equation \( = 3(i) + 2(ii) - (iii) \)
    • Therefore,
      \( \Delta H = 3\Delta H_1 + 2\Delta H_2 - \Delta H_3 \)
      \( \Delta H = 3(-250) + 2(-400) - (-100) \)
      \( \Delta H = -750 - 800 + 100 = -1450 \; \text{kJ} \)
    • This type of question cannot be solved directly by a single shortcut unless the data set is complete in one consistent form.
    • Core approach:
      write the equations represented by the given enthalpy data,
      manipulate them to obtain the required reaction,
      then apply the same algebra to \( \Delta H \).

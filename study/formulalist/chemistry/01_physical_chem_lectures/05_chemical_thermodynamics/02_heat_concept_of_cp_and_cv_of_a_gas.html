  1. Energy exchange: heat vs work (00:25)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Energy exchange between a system and surroundings occurs by two modes:
      1. Heat: energy transfer driven by temperature difference.

      2. Work: energy transfer via mechanical/electrical/etc. “doing work” on surroundings or by surroundings on the system.
    • “System needs energy” can be met by:
      1. Supplying heat to the system.

      2. Doing work on the system (or the system doing work and losing energy).
  2. Heat as energy transfer + Zeroth law link (02:28)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Heat is a mode of energy transfer between system and surroundings that operates only when there is a temperature difference.
    • If two bodies are at the same temperature, heat transfer cannot be the mode of energy exchange between them.
    • Connection to thermal equilibrium (Zeroth law idea): if no heat transfer occurs between two bodies in contact, they are in thermal equilibrium.
  3. Sign convention for heat in chemistry (04:51)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Chemistry sign convention for heat \(q\):
      1. Heat given to the system: \(q > 0\) (system gains energy).

      2. Heat given by the system (system releases heat): \(q < 0\) (system loses energy).
  4. Specific heat capacity: definition + water example (07:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Specific heat capacity (per unit mass):
      \( c = \dfrac{q}{m\,\Delta T} \)
    • Meaning: heat required to raise the temperature of unit mass by one degree.
    • Example interpretation: if water has \(c \approx 4.2\ \text{J g}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\), then 1 g water needs about 4.2 J heat to raise temperature by \(1^\circ\text{C}\).
  5. Gas: why “C” depends on process; zero to infinity (08:51)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For gases, “heat capacity” depends on the process path (how the process is carried out), so it is not a single fixed value like many solids/liquids.
    • Extremes using \( c = \dfrac{q}{m\,\Delta T} \):
      1. Isothermal process: \(\Delta T = 0\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(c \to \infty\) (mathematically).

      2. Adiabatic process: \(q = 0\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(c = 0\) (for that process definition).
    • Conclusion: for gases, the effective heat capacity can vary from 0 to \(\infty\) depending on the process.
  6. Two key molar heat capacities: \(C_p\) and \(C_v\) (11:19)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Two practically important heat capacities for a gas are defined for standard constraint processes:
      1. At constant volume: \(C_v\)

      2. At constant pressure: \(C_p\)
    • Molar definitions:
      1. \( C_v = \dfrac{q}{n\,\Delta T} \) (constant volume)

      2. \( C_p = \dfrac{q}{n\,\Delta T} \) (constant pressure)
    • Corresponding heat expressions (molar form):
      1. At constant volume: \( q_v = n\,C_v\,\Delta T \)

      2. At constant pressure: \( q_p = n\,C_p\,\Delta T \)
  7. Molar vs per-mass: capital vs small \(C_p, C_v\) + molar mass link (15:27)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Distinguish per-mass vs per-mole heat capacities:
      1. Per unit mass: \(c_p, c_v\) (units like \(\text{J g}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\))

      2. Per mole: \(C_p, C_v\) (units like \(\text{J mol}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\))
    • Link between molar and specific (per-mass) values using molar mass \(M\):
      1. \( C_v = M\,c_v \)

      2. \( C_p = M\,c_p \)
    • Example idea: if oxygen has molar mass \(M=32\ \text{g mol}^{-1}\), then \(C_v = 32\,c_v\) for \(\text{O}_2\).
  8. Mayer relation + exam trap with nitrogen (20:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Mayer’s relation for an ideal gas:
      \( C_p - C_v = R \)
    • Typical exam trap: options may be multiples/fractions of \(R\) because the question may be using per-mass values \(c_p, c_v\).
    • Using \(C_p = M c_p\) and \(C_v = M c_v\):
      \( M(c_p - c_v) = R \Rightarrow (c_p - c_v) = \dfrac{R}{M} \)
    • For nitrogen gas \(\text{N}_2\), \(M = 28\ \text{g mol}^{-1}\), so:
      \( c_p - c_v = \dfrac{R}{28} \)
  9. Degrees of freedom \(f\): formulas, \(\gamma\), and summary table (25:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Molar heat capacity at constant volume (ideal-gas result used here):
      \( C_v = \dfrac{f}{2}R \)
    • From Mayer’s relation:
      \( C_p = C_v + R = \left(\dfrac{f}{2} + 1\right)R \)
    • Degree of freedom \(f\) (as used in this lecture, standard exam-level values):
      1. Monatomic gases: \(f=3\)

      2. Diatomic gases: \(f=5\)

      3. Polyatomic gases: \(f=6\)
    • Heat capacity ratio:
      \( \gamma = \dfrac{C_p}{C_v} \)
    • Useful derived relation:
      \( \gamma = 1 + \dfrac{2}{f} \)
    • Summary (ideal-gas, exam-friendly):
      1. Monatomic: \(f=3,\ C_v=\dfrac{3}{2}R,\ C_p=\dfrac{5}{2}R,\ \gamma=\dfrac{5}{3}\approx 1.66\)

      2. Diatomic: \(f=5,\ C_v=\dfrac{5}{2}R,\ C_p=\dfrac{7}{2}R,\ \gamma=\dfrac{7}{5}=1.4\)

      3. Polyatomic: \(f=6,\ C_v=3R,\ C_p=4R,\ \gamma=\dfrac{4}{3}\approx 1.33\)
    • Trend: as atomicity increases, \(\gamma\) decreases (monatomic highest, polyatomic lowest).
    • Quick-use heat formulas (molar form):
      1. At constant pressure: \( q_p = n\,C_p\,\Delta T \)

      2. At constant volume: \( q_v = n\,C_v\,\Delta T \)
    • Sign reminder: \(q > 0\) when heat is absorbed by the system; \(q < 0\) when heat is released by the system.

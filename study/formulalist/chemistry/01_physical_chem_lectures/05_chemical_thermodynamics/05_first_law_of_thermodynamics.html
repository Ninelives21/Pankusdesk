  1. First law idea + sign convention (00:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • First law = conservation of energy for thermodynamic processes.
    • Exam-use equation (this lecture’s sign convention):
      \( \Delta U = \Delta q + \Delta W \)
    • Meaning of symbols (as used here):
      \( \Delta U \): change in internal energy of the system
      \( \Delta q \): heat given to the system
      \( \Delta W \): work done on the system
    • Energy of a system increases by:
      1. Supplying heat to the system (\( \Delta q > 0 \)).

      2. Doing work on the system (\( \Delta W > 0 \)).
    • Differential form (small changes):
      \( dU = dq + dW \)
    • Note: many books use \(\Delta E\) instead of \(\Delta U\) for internal energy change.
  2. Internal energy: what it includes (07:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A system’s total energy can be viewed as:
      1. Macroscopic kinetic energy (due to motion of the whole system).

      2. Macroscopic potential energy (due to external fields such as gravitational/electrostatic).

      3. Internal energy \(U\) (microscopic energy stored within the system).
    • If the whole system is at rest and external fields are absent, total energy is identified with internal energy \(U\).
    • Internal energy arises from molecular-level energies, not from motion of the system as a whole.
    • Molecular contributions to \(U\) include:
      1. Translational kinetic energy of molecules.

      2. Vibrational kinetic energy of molecules.

      3. Rotational kinetic energy of molecules.

      4. Potential energy due to interactions:
        • Intermolecular attraction (molecule–molecule).
        • Electrostatic attraction within atoms/molecules (electron–nucleus, bonding).
    • Practical note: the absolute value of \(U\) for a system cannot be directly determined by summing all microscopic contributions; focus is on \(\Delta U\).
  3. Internal energy: key properties of \(\Delta U\) (17:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Only change in internal energy is needed in calculations:
      \( \Delta U = U_f - U_i \)
    • Internal energy \(U\) is an extensive property: it depends on amount of matter (more moles \(\Rightarrow\) larger \(U\)).
    • \(U\) is a state function:
      • \(\Delta U\) depends only on initial and final states, not on the path taken.
      • For two different paths between the same states \(A \to B\): \(\Delta U_1 = \Delta U_2\).
    • Cyclic process: returning to the initial state implies:
      \( \Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0 \)
    • Contrast:
      • Heat \(q\) and work \(W\) are path-dependent (path functions).
      • In a cycle, total heat exchange and total work done need not be zero, but \(\Delta U\) is zero.
  4. Ideal gas: \(\Delta U = nC_v\Delta T\), \(C_v=\frac{f}{2}R\) (22:04)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General method (always usable with given data):
      \( \Delta U = \Delta q + \Delta W \)
    • For an ideal gas, internal energy change has a direct temperature relation (works for any process):
      \( \Delta U = nC_v\Delta T \)
    • Small change form:
      \( dU = nC_v dT \)
    • Meanings:
      • \(n\): moles of gas
      • \(C_v\): molar heat capacity at constant volume
      • \(\Delta T\): temperature change
    • Usual (constant) \(C_v\) model used in most problems:
      \( C_v = \frac{f}{2}R \)
    • Degrees of freedom \(f\) (as used here):
      1. Monatomic gas: \(f=3\)

      2. Diatomic gas: \(f=5\)

      3. Polyatomic gas: \(f=6\)
    • Gas constant values to use in energy units:
      • \( R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \)
      • \( R \approx 2\ \text{cal mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \)
    • Ideal-gas special note: \(U\) depends only on temperature.
      • If \(\Delta T=0\) for an ideal gas, then \(\Delta U=0\).
    • If a problem gives temperature-dependent \(C_v\) (rare): use integration using the differential relation
      \( dU = nC_v(T)\,dT \) and integrate from \(T_i\) to \(T_f\).
    • Why ideal gas internal energy is treated as kinetic-only:
      • Ideal gas assumes no intermolecular attraction \(\Rightarrow\) no intermolecular potential energy contribution.
      • Hence internal energy is attributed to molecular kinetic energies.
  5. Heat formulas + \(C_p, C_v\) relations (33:26)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Heat is a mode of energy transfer between system and surroundings.
    • Sign convention used here:
      • Heat given to the system: \( \Delta q > 0 \)
      • Heat released by the system: \( \Delta q < 0 \)
    • General heat formula (any substance):
      \( q = m c \Delta T \)
    • For gases (molar form):
      \( q = n C \Delta T \)
    • Choice of \(C\) depends on the process:
      • Constant pressure heating/cooling: use \(C_p\)
      • Constant volume heating/cooling: use \(C_v\)
    • Relation between heat capacities (ideal gas):
      \( C_p = C_v + R \)
    • With \( C_v = \frac{f}{2}R \), obtain \(C_p\) by adding \(R\).
  6. Work for gases: \(-P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V\) + PV-area idea (38:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Sign convention used here:
      • Work done on the system: \( \Delta W > 0 \)
      • Work done by the system: \( \Delta W < 0 \)
    • For gas expansion/compression against external pressure:
      \( W = -P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V \)
    • Volume change definition:
      \( \Delta V = V_f - V_i \)
    • If pressure varies with volume, work is obtained from area under the \(P\)-\(V\) curve.
    • PV-graph interpretation:
      • Expansion (\(\Delta V>0\)) typically gives \(W<0\) (work done by gas).
      • Compression (\(\Delta V<0\)) gives \(W>0\) (work done on gas).
  7. Practice set 1: direct first-law numericals (41:16)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Practice idea: apply \( \Delta U = \Delta q + \Delta W \) with correct signs.
    • Worked example pattern:
      1. If system is given heat \(q\), take \(q\) positive.

      2. If system does work on surroundings, take \(W\) negative.

      3. Compute \(\Delta U\) by direct substitution.
    • Example 1 (calorie units, as stated):
      Given: \( \Delta q = +500\ \text{cal} \), system does work \(350\ \text{cal}\Rightarrow \Delta W = -350\ \text{cal} \)
      Then: \( \Delta U = +150\ \text{cal} \)
    • Interpretation: supplied energy splits into work output and internal energy increase.
  8. Practice set 2: \(\Delta Q/\Delta W\) in isobaric process (56:28)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Isobaric (constant pressure) ideal-gas relations used:
      • \( \Delta q = nC_p\Delta T \)
      • \( \Delta U = nC_v\Delta T \)
      • First law: \( \Delta U = \Delta q + \Delta W \Rightarrow \Delta W = \Delta U - \Delta q \)
    • Ratio setup:
      • \( \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta W} = \frac{nC_p\Delta T}{nC_v\Delta T - nC_p\Delta T} = \frac{C_p}{C_v - C_p} \)
    • Using \(\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v}\):
      • Divide numerator and denominator by \(C_v\):
        \( \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta W} = \frac{\gamma}{1-\gamma} \)
  9. Practice set 3: isothermal process with work given (01:01:16)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Isothermal process: temperature constant (\(\Delta T=0\)).
    • For an ideal gas, \(U\) depends only on temperature \(\Rightarrow\) isothermal implies:
      \( \Delta U = 0 \)
    • If work is done on the gas (given): \( \Delta W > 0 \).
    • Use first law with \(\Delta U=0\):
      \( 0 = \Delta q + \Delta W \Rightarrow \Delta q = -\Delta W \)
    • Interpretation: energy supplied as work leaves as heat so that internal energy does not change.
  10. PV-graph numericals: area + sign + first law (01:04:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • PV-graph method:
      • Compute work from area under the curve (or geometric area if straight lines).
      • Convert units if needed: \(1\ \text{atm·L} = 101.3\ \text{J}\).
      • Decide sign using \( W = -P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V \).
    • Expansion example logic:
      • If \(V\) increases, \(\Delta V>0\Rightarrow W<0\) (work done by gas).
    • Once \(W\) is obtained, find heat using first law:
      \( \Delta q = \Delta U - \Delta W \) (rearranged from \( \Delta U=\Delta q+\Delta W \)).
  11. PV-path comparison: which heat is larger (01:08:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Two different paths from the same initial state to the same final state:
      • \(\Delta U\) is the same for both paths (state function).
      • Heat differs because work differs (path dependence).
    • Use: \( \Delta U = \Delta q + \Delta W \Rightarrow \Delta q = \Delta U - \Delta W \).
    • For expansion paths on a PV diagram:
      • Work magnitude relates to PV-area.
      • A path with larger PV-area has \(|W|\) larger (more negative \(W\) for expansion).
    • Conclusion for the shown case (larger area for path 1): \(\Delta q_1 > \Delta q_2\).
  12. Cyclic process on PV: \(\Delta U=0\) and area trick (01:11:40)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a complete cycle (return to start):
      \( \Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0 \)
    • First law over the cycle gives:
      \( 0 = \Delta q_{\text{cycle}} + \Delta W_{\text{cycle}} \Rightarrow \Delta W_{\text{cycle}} = -\Delta q_{\text{cycle}} \)
    • PV-diagram shortcut for cycle work:
      • Magnitude of work = area enclosed by the cycle.
      • Sign depends on direction (as stated in lecture): clockwise \(\Rightarrow W<0\), anticlockwise \(\Rightarrow W>0\).
    • Use conversion when pressure is in atm and volume in liters:
      \(1\ \text{atm·L} = 101.3\ \text{J}\)
  13. PV-rectangle analysis: signs of \(Q, W, \Delta U\) on each leg (01:15:42)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Goal: determine signs (\(>0\), \(<0\), or \(=0\)) of \(W\), \(\Delta U\), and \(Q\) on each segment using only:
      • \( W = -P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V \)
      • Ideal-gas relation \( PV = nRT \) (to infer temperature trend)
      • Ideal-gas property: \(U\) depends only on \(T\)
      • First law: \( \Delta U = \Delta q + \Delta W \)
    • Segment with constant volume (\(\Delta V=0\)):
      • \(W=0\)
      • If pressure drops at constant \(V\), then \(T\) drops \(\Rightarrow \Delta U<0\Rightarrow \Delta q<0\).
    • Segment with constant pressure and increasing volume:
      • \(\Delta V>0 \Rightarrow W<0\)
      • With \(P\) constant and \(V\) rising, \(T\) rises \(\Rightarrow \Delta U>0\)
      • Then \(\Delta q\) must be \(>0\) to satisfy \(\Delta U=\Delta q+\Delta W\).
    • Segment with constant pressure and decreasing volume:
      • \(\Delta V<0 \Rightarrow W>0\)
      • With \(P\) constant and \(V\) falling, \(T\) falls \(\Rightarrow \Delta U<0\)
      • Then \(\Delta q<0\).
    • Typical conceptual prompts built from this:
      1. Identify on which segment the gas heats/cools (use \(T\) trend).

      2. Identify where work is done by vs on the gas (use \(\Delta V\) sign).

      3. Identify where heat is absorbed/released (use first law with known signs).

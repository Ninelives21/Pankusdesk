  1. Entropy Change from First Law and State Function Idea (01:37)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Entropy is a state function, so a change of state from \( (P_1, V_1, T_1) \) to \( (P_2, V_2, T_2) \) must produce a definite entropy change.
    • For variable temperature processes, the useful differential form is:
      \( dS = \dfrac{dQ}{T} \)
    • On integration between initial and final states:
      \( \Delta S = \int \dfrac{dQ}{T} \)
    • Using the first law of thermodynamics:
      \( dU = dQ + dW \)
    • Therefore:
      \( dQ = dU - dW \)
    • For an ideal gas:
      • \( dU = nC_V\,dT \)
      • \( dW = -P\,dV \)
    • Substitution gives the working form:
      \( \Delta S = \int \dfrac{nC_V\,dT}{T} + \int \dfrac{P\,dV}{T} \)
  2. General Entropy Formula for an Ideal Gas (10:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • From the ideal gas equation:
      \( PV = nRT \Rightarrow P = \dfrac{nRT}{V} \)
    • Substituting \( P \) into the entropy expression gives:
      \( \Delta S = \int \dfrac{nC_V\,dT}{T} + \int \dfrac{nR\,dV}{V} \)
    • Integrating from \( T_1 \) to \( T_2 \) and \( V_1 \) to \( V_2 \):
      \( \Delta S = nC_V \ln \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} + nR \ln \dfrac{V_2}{V_1} \)
    • Using base-10 logarithm:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303\,nC_V \log \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} + 2.303\,nR \log \dfrac{V_2}{V_1} \)
    • This expression is useful, but the process conditions should always be checked first before applying it directly.
  3. Entropy in Standard Thermodynamic Processes (18:26)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Isothermal process: temperature remains constant, so \( dT = 0 \). Therefore:
      \( \Delta S = nR \ln \dfrac{V_2}{V_1} = 2.303\,nR \log \dfrac{V_2}{V_1} \)
    • Using Boyle’s law for an isothermal ideal gas, \( P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 \), so the same result may be written as:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303\,nR \log \dfrac{P_1}{P_2} \)
    • Isochoric process: volume remains constant, so \( dV = 0 \). Therefore:
      \( \Delta S = nC_V \ln \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 2.303\,nC_V \log \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} \)
    • Adiabatic process: no heat exchange, so \( dQ = 0 \). Therefore:
      \( \Delta S = 0 \)
    • Isobaric process: at constant pressure, \( dQ = nC_P\,dT \). Therefore:
      \( \Delta S = nC_P \ln \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 2.303\,nC_P \log \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} \)
    • These formulae describe entropy change of the system.
  4. Closed Container Heating: Entropy Calculation (24:27)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a gas heated in a closed container, volume remains constant, so the process is isochoric.
    • Hence the entropy change is:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303\,nC_V \log \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} \)
    • For the given example:
      • \( n = 2 \)
      • \( C_V = 2.5R \)
      • \( T_1 = 273\,\text{K} \)
      • \( T_2 = 546\,\text{K} \)
    • Substituting values:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303 \times 2 \times 2.5R \times \log \dfrac{546}{273} \)
    • Since \( \dfrac{546}{273} = 2 \), this becomes:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303 \times 2 \times 2.5R \times \log 2 \)
    • The approximate value matches the option near \( 20 \,\text{J K}^{-1} \).
    1. Question 1 Find the entropy change when \( 2 \) moles of \( O_2 \) in a closed container are heated from \( 0^\circ\text{C} \) to \( 273^\circ\text{C} \).

    2. Use constant-volume entropy change:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303\,nC_V \log \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} \)

    3. Final answer is approximately \( 20 \,\text{J K}^{-1} \) for the whole system.
  5. PV Graph Question: Isothermal Entropy Change (30:23)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The given \( PV \) graph represents an isothermal process because \( P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 \).
    • For the shown states, \( 6 \times 1 = 3 \times 2 \), so temperature remains constant.
    • For an isothermal change of an ideal gas:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303\,nR \log \dfrac{V_2}{V_1} \)
    • Substituting the given values:
      • \( n = 2 \)
      • \( V_1 = 1 \)
      • \( V_2 = 3 \)
    • Hence:
      \( \Delta S = 2.303 \times 2 \times R \times \log 3 \)
    • The approximate value comes near \( 20 \,\text{J K}^{-1} \).
    1. Question 2 For the given \( PV \) graph of a monoatomic ideal gas, calculate the approximate entropy change.

    2. Identify the curve as isothermal from \( P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 \), then apply:

    3. Final answer is approximately \( 20 \,\text{J K}^{-1} \).
  6. Entropy Change During Phase Transition (36:29)
    (Back to topic index)
    • During a phase transition, temperature remains constant, so the entropy change may be written as:
      \( \Delta S = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{T} \)
    • For one mole of a solid changing to one mole of liquid at the melting point:
      • Heat absorbed is the enthalpy of fusion, \( \Delta H_f \)
      • Therefore:
        \( \Delta S_{\text{fusion}} = \dfrac{\Delta H_f}{T_m} \)
    • For one mole of a liquid changing to one mole of vapour at the boiling point:
      • Heat absorbed is the enthalpy of vaporization, \( \Delta H_v \)
      • Therefore:
        \( \Delta S_{\text{vap}} = \dfrac{\Delta H_v}{T_b} \)
    • In phase-change problems, the process enthalpy is placed in the numerator and the corresponding constant transition temperature in the denominator.
  7. Melting Point from Enthalpy and Entropy of Fusion (41:21)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For fusion:
      \( \Delta S = \dfrac{\Delta H_f}{T_m} \)
    • Hence the melting point is:
      \( T_m = \dfrac{\Delta H_f}{\Delta S} \)
    • Care must be taken to use consistent units before substitution.
    • For the given data, the computed value is close to \( 273\,\text{K} \), which corresponds to \( 0^\circ\text{C} \).
    1. Question 3 For a liquid, \( \Delta H_f = 1.435\,\text{kcal mol}^{-1} \) and molar entropy change of fusion is \( 5.26\,\text{cal mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \). Find the melting point.

    2. Convert \( \Delta H_f \) into calories per mole and apply:
      \( T_m = \dfrac{\Delta H_f}{\Delta S} \)

    3. Final answer is approximately \( 273\,\text{K} \), or \( 0^\circ\text{C} \).
  8. Entropy Change for Melting 1 g Ice at 273 K (45:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For fusion at \( 273\,\text{K} \):
      \( \Delta S = \dfrac{\Delta H_f}{T} \)
    • If \( \Delta H_f = 6.025\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \), then for one mole of ice:
      \( \Delta S = \dfrac{6025}{273}\,\text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \)
    • Since one mole of ice corresponds to \( 18\,\text{g} \), for \( 1\,\text{g} \):
      \( \Delta S = \dfrac{6025}{273 \times 18}\,\text{J g}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \)
    • The approximate value is close to \( 1.226 \,\text{J g}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \).
    1. Question 4 Find \( \Delta S \) when \( 1\,\text{g} \) of ice melts to water at \( 273\,\text{K} \), given \( \Delta H_f = 6.025\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \).

    2. First calculate entropy change for \( 1 \) mole using:
      \( \Delta S = \dfrac{\Delta H_f}{T} \)

    3. Then convert from \( 18\,\text{g} \) to \( 1\,\text{g} \) by dividing by \( 18 \).

    4. Final answer is approximately \( 1.226 \,\text{J g}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \).

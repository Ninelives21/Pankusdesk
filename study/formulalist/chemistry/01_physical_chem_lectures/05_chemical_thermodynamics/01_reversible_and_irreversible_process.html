  1. Thermodynamics: scope and study plan (00:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Thermodynamics (chemistry) is a concept-heavy chapter; similar ideas appear in physics and chemistry.
    • To avoid confusion, keep a gap between studying thermodynamics in physics vs chemistry (order can be chosen, but do not mix them closely).
    • Main targets in this chapter:
      • Define heat, work, and energy terms.
      • Decide feasibility of a process; analyze heat changes during processes.
      • Use variables such as \(P\), \(V\), \(T\), \(H\), \(S\), \(U\), \(G\) to describe processes.
  2. System, surroundings, universe, boundary (03:59)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Definitions (chemistry convention):
      • System: the part of the universe under investigation (focus of calculation/observation).
      • Surroundings: everything other than the system.
      • Universe: system + surroundings.
    • Relation:
      Surroundings = Universe - System
    • Boundary: the separator between system and surroundings (needed to define a system).
  3. Boundary types: real/imaginary, diathermic/adiabatic (07:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Boundary can be:
      • Real or imaginary (conceptual selection of what is treated as “system”).
      • Rigid or flexible (movable boundary possible).
    • Heat-transfer classification:
      • Diathermic boundary: heat can flow easily across the boundary (conducting).
      • Adiabatic boundary: heat cannot flow across the boundary (insulating).
    • Adiabatic condition (for heat exchange):
      \( \Delta Q = 0 \)
  4. Types of systems: open, closed, isolated (11:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Thermodynamic exchange types:
      • Mass exchange
      • Energy exchange as heat and/or work
    • Open system: exchanges both mass and energy with surroundings.
      • Example: open vessel where evaporation can occur (mass loss), and heat can transfer through a conducting boundary.
    • Closed system: no mass exchange; energy exchange possible.
      • Energy can exchange as heat (if boundary is diathermic) and/or as work (e.g., piston movement).
    • Isolated system: neither mass exchange nor energy exchange (no heat, no work).
      • Conceptual model: sealed system with adiabatic (insulating) boundary and no movable piston arrangement.
  5. Extensive vs intensive properties (19:04)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Two broad classes of properties:
      • Extensive: depends on amount of matter / sample size.
      • Intensive: independent of sample size.
    • Typical examples:
      • Extensive: mass, volume, number of moles, heat capacity.
      • Intensive: density, refractive index, specific heat capacity, viscosity, temperature, pressure, concentration terms (molarity, molality etc).
    • Key contrast: heat capacity is extensive, but specific heat capacity is intensive.
  6. Quick tests for extensive/intensive + examples (27:16)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Fast test:
      • If \( \dfrac{\text{extensive}}{\text{extensive}} \) is formed, the result is intensive.
      • Examples: molarity, molality, mole fraction, normality.
    • Energy-related thermodynamic terms are extensive in general: enthalpy \(H\), entropy \(S\), Gibbs free energy \(G\), internal energy \(U\).
    • “Molar” versions become intensive (per mole):
      • Molar enthalpy, molar entropy, molar internal energy are intensive.
    • pH is intensive because it is based on concentration:
      \( \mathrm{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+] \)
      1. Question 1
        Molarity is intensive or extensive? Give the quickest reason.
        • Answer: Intensive; it is a ratio of extensive quantities \( \dfrac{n}{V} \), so it stays the same for any portion of the same solution.

      2. Question 2
        Among the following, identify which are extensive: specific heat capacity, Gibbs free energy, EMF, dielectric constant.
        • Answer: Gibbs free energy is extensive; the others listed are intensive in this context.
  7. State functions vs path functions; cyclic process idea (35:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • State of a system can be specified by state variables such as:
      • \(P,\,T,\,V,\,n\)
      • \(H,\,S,\,U,\,G\)
    • State function: change depends only on initial and final states:
      \( \Delta X = X_f - X_i \)
    • Path function: value depends on the path taken between states:
      • Heat \(Q\) and work \(W\) are path functions.
    • Cyclic process: system returns to the initial state.
      • For any state function \(X\), in a cycle: \( \Delta X = 0 \).
      • For path functions, \(Q\) and \(W\) over a cycle are generally non-zero.
      1. Question 1
        Two different paths connect the same initial and final states on a \(P\) vs \(V\) plot. Is \(\Delta P\) the same or different for the two paths?
        • Answer: Same; pressure is a state variable, so \(\Delta P = P_f - P_i\) is path-independent.

      2. Question 2
        In a cyclic process, which of the following have zero net change: \(P\), \(V\), \(T\), \(U\), \(Q\), \(W\)?
        • Answer: \(P\), \(V\), \(T\), \(U\) (state variables/functions) have zero net change; \(Q\) and \(W\) are generally non-zero over the cycle.
  8. Types of thermodynamic processes (46:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Process: any change where thermodynamic variables change (e.g., \(P\), \(V\), \(T\), energy exchange).
    • Major named processes:
      • Isothermal: temperature constant, \( \Delta T = 0 \).
      • Isobaric: pressure constant, \( \Delta P = 0 \).
      • Isochoric: volume constant, \( \Delta V = 0 \).
      • Adiabatic: no heat exchange, \( \Delta Q = 0 \).
    • For an ideal gas in an isothermal process:
      • \( \Delta T = 0 \Rightarrow \Delta U = 0 \) (since \(U\) depends only on \(T\) for an ideal gas).
      • Boyle’s law applies: \( P \propto \dfrac{1}{V} \) at constant \(T\).
    • Visual cue (on \(P\)-\(V\) plot): adiabatic curve is steeper than isothermal for the same starting point.
  9. Reversible vs irreversible processes (55:43)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Reversible process (ideal concept):
      • Occurs via infinitely small steps; would require infinite time.
      • System remains in equilibrium at every intermediate step.
    • Irreversible process (practical):
      • Occurs in finite time with a finite driving force.
      • Equilibrium is not maintained throughout; typically only initial and final states are equilibrium states.
    • Piston-with-weights model:
      • Reversible-like: remove very small weight \( \Rightarrow \) tiny pressure difference \( \Rightarrow \) system re-equilibrates stepwise.
      • Irreversible-like: remove many weights suddenly \( \Rightarrow \) large pressure difference \( \Rightarrow \) rapid expansion to a new equilibrium.
    • Driving force vs opposing force:
      • Reversible: driving force is only slightly greater than opposing force.
      • Irreversible: driving force is much greater than opposing force.

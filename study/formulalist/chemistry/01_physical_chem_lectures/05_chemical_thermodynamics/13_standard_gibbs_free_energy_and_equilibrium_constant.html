  1. Gibbs Free Energy and Standard Gibbs Free Energy (01:41)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Gibbs free energy is defined as:
      \( G = H - TS \)
    • For a reaction, the useful quantity is the change in Gibbs free energy:
      \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S \)
    • Meaning of \( \Delta G \):
      • \( \Delta G < 0 \): spontaneous process
      • \( \Delta G > 0 \): non-spontaneous process
      • \( \Delta G = 0 \): equilibrium
    • Standard Gibbs free energy change, \( \Delta G^\circ \), is measured under standard conditions:
      • Temperature \( = 298\,\text{K} \)
      • Pressure \( = 1\,\text{atm} \)
    • Standardisation removes ambiguity caused by different experimental conditions.
    • At standard conditions:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ \)
  2. Two Ways to Calculate \( \Delta G^\circ \) of a Reaction (07:18)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Method 1 uses standard enthalpy and entropy change of the reaction:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \Delta H^\circ_{\text{rxn}} - T\Delta S^\circ_{\text{rxn}} \)
    • Method 2 uses standard Gibbs free energies of formation:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \sum \nu \Delta G^\circ_f(\text{products}) - \sum \nu \Delta G^\circ_f(\text{reactants}) \)
    • In both methods, stoichiometric coefficients must be included.
    • The second method is directly analogous to the standard enthalpy-of-formation method used earlier in thermodynamics.
  3. Standard Gibbs Free Energy of Formation of Elements (10:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For an element in its standard state:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_f = 0 \)
    • Examples:
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(O_2(g)) = 0 \)
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(N_2(g)) = 0 \)
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(H_2(g)) = 0 \)
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(Cl_2(g)) = 0 \)
    • State is important because the standard state must match the naturally stable form under standard conditions.
    • Examples of state sensitivity:
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(Br_2(l)) = 0 \), but \( \Delta G^\circ_f(Br_2(g)) \neq 0 \)
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(I_2(s)) = 0 \), but \( \Delta G^\circ_f(I_2(g)) \neq 0 \)
    1. Question 1 For the reaction \( I_2(s) + H_2S(g) \rightarrow 2HI(g) + S(s) \), given \( \Delta G^\circ_f(HI) = 1.8\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \) and \( \Delta G^\circ_f(H_2S) = 30\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \), find \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} \).

    2. Apply product minus reactant:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = \left[ 2\Delta G^\circ_f(HI) + \Delta G^\circ_f(S) \right] - \left[ \Delta G^\circ_f(I_2) + \Delta G^\circ_f(H_2S) \right] \)

    3. Since \( I_2(s) \) and \( S(s) \) are elements in standard state, their \( \Delta G^\circ_f \) values are zero.

    4. Therefore:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = 2(1.8) - 30 = -26.4\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)

    5. The reaction is spontaneous because \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} < 0 \).
  4. Reaction Quotient \( Q \) and Equilibrium Constant \( K \) (24:21)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a general reaction:
      \( A + 2B \rightarrow 3C + D \)
    • The reaction quotient is:
      \( Q = \dfrac{[C]^3[D]}{[A][B]^2} \)
    • \( Q \) can be defined at any stage of the reaction.
    • If the same expression is evaluated when the system has reached equilibrium, then:
      \( Q = K \)
    • Thus, \( K \) is simply the value of \( Q \) at equilibrium concentration values.
  5. Relation Between \( \Delta G \), \( \Delta G^\circ \), \( Q \), and \( K \) (29:15)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The fundamental relation is:
      \( \Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q \)
    • At equilibrium:
      • \( \Delta G = 0 \)
      • \( Q = K \)
    • Hence:
      \( 0 = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln K \)
    • Therefore:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K \)
    • Using common logarithm:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = -2.303RT\log K \)
    • This is the standard relation between standard Gibbs free energy change and equilibrium constant.
  6. JEE 2007: Finding \( \log K \) from \( \Delta H^\circ \) and \( \Delta S^\circ \) (32:39)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given for \( A \rightleftharpoons B \):
      • \( \Delta H^\circ = -54.07\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)
      • \( \Delta S^\circ = 10\,\text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \)
      • \( T = 298\,\text{K} \)
    • First calculate standard Gibbs free energy change:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ \)
    • Convert enthalpy into joule:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = -54070 - 2980 = -57050\,\text{J mol}^{-1} \)
    • Now use:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = -2.303RT\log K \)
    • Given \( 2.303 \times 8.314 \times 298 = 5705 \), so:
      \( -57050 = -5705\log K \)
    • Hence:
      \( \log K = 10 \)
    1. Question 2 If \( \Delta H^\circ = -54.07\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \) and \( \Delta S^\circ = 10\,\text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \) at \( 298\,\text{K} \), find \( \log K \).

    2. First find:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ \)

    3. Then apply:
      \( \Delta G^\circ = -2.303RT\log K \)

    4. Final answer:
      \( \log K = 10 \)
  7. JEE 2015: Finding \( \Delta G^\circ_f \) of \( NO_2 \) (37:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Reaction:
      \( 2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2NO_2(g) \)
    • Given:
      • \( \Delta G^\circ_f(NO) = 86.6\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)
      • \( K = 1.6 \times 10^{12} \)
      • \( T = 298\,\text{K} \)
    • First calculate standard Gibbs free energy change of the reaction:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = -RT\ln K \)
    • So:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = -R(298)\ln(1.6 \times 10^{12}) \)
    • Now write reaction Gibbs free energy in terms of formation values:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) - \left[ 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO) + \Delta G^\circ_f(O_2) \right] \)
    • Since \( O_2(g) \) is an element in standard state:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_f(O_2) = 0 \)
    • Thus:
      \( 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) - 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO) = -R(298)\ln(1.6 \times 10^{12}) \)
    • Rearranging:
      \( 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) = 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO) - R(298)\ln(1.6 \times 10^{12}) \)
    • Therefore:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) = \dfrac{2(86600) - R(298)\ln(1.6 \times 10^{12})}{2} \)
    1. Question 3 For \( 2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2NO_2(g) \), given \( \Delta G^\circ_f(NO) = 86.6\,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \) and \( K = 1.6 \times 10^{12} \), find \( \Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) \).

    2. First use:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = -RT\ln K \)

    3. Then use:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) - 2\Delta G^\circ_f(NO) \)

    4. Final result:
      \( \Delta G^\circ_f(NO_2) = \dfrac{2(86600) - R(298)\ln(1.6 \times 10^{12})}{2} \)

  1. Heat of reaction and sign convention (03:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Thermochemistry uses enthalpy change (heat of reaction) for chemical equations.
      \( \Delta H = H_p - H_r \)
    • If \( \Delta H < 0 \): heat released (exothermic); typically \( H_r > H_p \).
    • If \( \Delta H > 0 \): heat absorbed (endothermic); typically \( H_p > H_r \).
    • Sign convention is from the system’s viewpoint:
      • Heat given to system \(\Rightarrow\) energy increases \(\Rightarrow\) positive sign.
      • Heat lost by system \(\Rightarrow\) energy decreases \(\Rightarrow\) negative sign.
  2. Energy profile graphs and bond-energy view (05:54)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Exothermic profile: reactants at higher energy, products lower; excess energy leaves as heat.
    • Endothermic profile: products at higher energy; energy must be supplied to the system.
    • Why heat is released/absorbed in reactions (bond explanation):
      • Breaking bonds in reactants requires energy input.
      • Making new bonds in products releases energy.
      • If energy released in bond formation \(>\) energy required for bond breaking \(\Rightarrow\) exothermic.
      • If energy required for bond breaking \(>\) energy released in bond formation \(\Rightarrow\) endothermic.
  3. Writing thermochemical equations: +heat vs \(\Delta H\) sign (10:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Example (neutralization releases heat):
      \( \mathrm{NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) \rightarrow NaCl(aq) + H_2O(l)} \)
    • If heat is written on product side as “\(+\) heat”, it means heat is released (exothermic).
      • So, for the same reaction, \( \Delta H \) is written negative.
    • Example of common sign confusion:
      \( \mathrm{C(s) + H_2O(g) \rightarrow CO(g) + H_2(g)} \)
      If the equation shows “\(-\) heat” on the product side, interpret it as heat being absorbed (endothermic).
    • Quick rule:
      • \(+\) heat on product side \(\Rightarrow\) exothermic \(\Rightarrow\) \( \Delta H < 0 \).
      • \(-\) heat on product side \(\Rightarrow\) move it to reactant side as \(+\) heat \(\Rightarrow\) endothermic \(\Rightarrow\) \( \Delta H > 0 \).
    • Enthalpy values are often reported “per mole” of a specified species; scaling depends on stoichiometric coefficients (enthalpy is extensive).
  4. \(\Delta H\) depends on state, allotropy, solvent, \(T\), \(P\); standard heat \(\Delta H^\circ\) (14:40)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Heat of reaction depends on the physical state of reactants/products.
      • Example idea:
        \( \mathrm{H_2(g) + \tfrac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(g)} \) vs \( \mathrm{H_2(g) + \tfrac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(l)} \)
      • Formation of liquid water includes condensation step, releasing additional heat, so it is typically more negative.
    • Heat of reaction depends on allotropic form (structure) of elements.
      • Example:
        \( \mathrm{C(diamond) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)} \) and \( \mathrm{C(graphite/amorphous) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)} \)
        \( \Delta H \) differs because the starting forms have different enthalpies.
    • Solvent/medium effect: reactions in different solvents (or dry vs moist conditions) can have different \(\Delta H\).
    • Temperature and pressure also affect \(\Delta H\).
    • Standard heat of reaction (standard enthalpy change):
      • Common reference conditions: \(T = 298\,\mathrm{K}\), \(P = 1\,\mathrm{atm}\).
      • For aqueous solutions: concentration \(= 1\,\mathrm{M}\) (i.e., \(1\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}}\)).
      • The value under standard conditions is denoted \( \Delta H^\circ \) (spoken as “delta H naught”).
    • Note: Some reactions require special temperatures; pressure reference is typically kept at \(1\,\mathrm{atm}\) while temperature may be specified as needed.
  5. Standard enthalpy of elements: \(\Delta H^\circ=0\) in natural standard state (22:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Convention: standard enthalpy of formation of an element in its most stable natural form (standard state) is taken as zero at \(298\,\mathrm{K}\) and \(1\,\mathrm{atm}\).
    • Examples (standard state \(\Rightarrow\) \( \Delta H_f^\circ = 0 \)):
      • \( \mathrm{Cl_2(g)} \), \( \mathrm{O_2(g)} \), \( \mathrm{H_2(g)} \), \( \mathrm{N_2(g)} \)
      • \( \mathrm{Br_2(l)} \) (bromine’s standard state is liquid)
      • \( \mathrm{C(graphite)} \) is zero, but \( \mathrm{C(diamond)} \) is not zero.
    • For elements with allotropes, only the chosen standard allotrope is assigned zero.
      • \( \mathrm{S_8} \) (orthorhombic sulfur) taken as standard state \(\Rightarrow\) zero.
      • \( \mathrm{P_4} \) (white phosphorus) taken as standard state \(\Rightarrow\) zero.
    • This is a reference convention (baseline) so other enthalpies can be compared consistently.
  6. Enthalpy of formation \(\Delta H_f^\circ\): definition + examples (25:46)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Definition (standard enthalpy of formation):
      • Heat absorbed or released when \(1\) mole of a compound is formed from its constituent elements in their natural standard states.
      • Reported under standard conditions \(\Rightarrow\) \( \Delta H_f^\circ \).
    • Formation of water vapor as “1 mole product” example:
      \( \mathrm{H_2(g) + \tfrac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow H_2O(g)} \)
      • Using \(\tfrac{1}{2}O_2\) ensures exactly \(1\) mole of \( \mathrm{H_2O} \) is formed.
      • The enthalpy change of this reaction equals \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{H_2O(g)}) \).
    • Key idea under standard-state convention:
      • For a compound, “enthalpy of the compound” is treated as its formation enthalpy because elements in standard state have zero formation enthalpy.
    • Example (carbon dioxide):
      \( \mathrm{C(graphite) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)} \)
      Reaction enthalpy under standard conditions equals \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{CO_2(g)}) \).
    • Extensive property reminder:
      • If the equation forms \(2\) moles of product, the given \(\Delta H\) corresponds to \(2\) moles; divide by \(2\) to get per mole formation enthalpy.
    • Example (hydrogen chloride):
      \( \mathrm{H_2(g) + Cl_2(g) \rightarrow 2HCl(g)} \)
      • If \(\Delta H\) is given for \(2\) moles \( \mathrm{HCl} \), then \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{HCl}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta H \).
    • Stability link:
      • More negative \( \Delta H_f^\circ \Rightarrow\) more heat released on formation \(\Rightarrow\) product at lower energy \(\Rightarrow\) greater stability (relative statement).
  7. Using \(\Delta H_f^\circ\) to compute reaction enthalpy + numericals (37:41)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Core calculation rule:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{rxn}} = \sum \nu\,\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum \nu\,\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{reactants}) \)
    • Use stoichiometric coefficients \(\nu\) because enthalpy is extensive (scales with moles).
    • Example numerical (combustion of methane; values rounded in lecture):
      \( \mathrm{CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l)} \)
      • Given: \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{CH_4}) \approx -80\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}} \), \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{CO_2}) \approx -400\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}} \), \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{H_2O(l)}) \approx -300\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}} \), \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{O_2}) = 0 \).
      • Then \( \Delta H_{\text{rxn}} \approx -400 + 2(-300) - [(-80) + 2(0)] \approx -920\,\mathrm{kJ} \).
      1. Question 1
        Given \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{NH_4NO_3(s)})\approx -400\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}} \), \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{N_2O(g)})\approx +80\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}} \), \( \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{H_2O(l)})\approx -300\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}} \) for the reaction
        \( \mathrm{NH_4NO_3(s) \rightarrow N_2O(g) + 2H_2O(l)} \),
        calculate \( \Delta H \) and \( \Delta U \) at \(25^\circ\mathrm{C}\), \(1\,\mathrm{atm}\).
        • Method:
          \( \Delta H = H_p - H_r = [\Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{N_2O}) + 2\Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{H_2O})] - \Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{NH_4NO_3}) \)
        • Use relation (at constant \(T,P\)) to connect enthalpy and internal energy:
          \( \Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT \)
        • Here \( \Delta n_g = n_g(\text{products}) - n_g(\text{reactants}) \) counting only gaseous moles.

      2. Question 2
        Under normal conditions, estimate the heat released when \(1\,\mathrm{m^3}\) of methane combusts, using formation enthalpies and the combustion equation
        \( \mathrm{CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l)} \).
        • Method:
          • First compute \( \Delta H \) per mole of methane using formation enthalpies.
          • Convert \(1\,\mathrm{m^3}\) to moles using molar volume at NTP/STP as stated in lecture: \(22.4\,\mathrm{L}\) per mole.
          • Use unit conversion: \(1\,\mathrm{L} = 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{m^3}\).
  8. Enthalpy of combustion \(\Delta H_c^\circ\) and “1 mol” rule (46:49)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Definition:
      • Heat released when \(1\) mole of a compound undergoes complete combustion in excess oxygen (air).
      • Symbol: \( \Delta H_c \) (often \( \Delta H_c^\circ \) under standard conditions).
    • Key property: combustion is exothermic, so \( \Delta H_c \) is always negative.
    • Example:
      \( \mathrm{CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l)} \)
      • If the equation shows \(1\) mole \( \mathrm{CH_4} \), then the reaction \(\Delta H\) equals \( \Delta H_c(\mathrm{CH_4}) \).
    • If the combustion equation is written for \(2\) moles of fuel, divide the \(\Delta H\) by \(2\) to get \(\Delta H_c\) per mole.
      1. Question 1
        For the reaction
        \( \mathrm{2C_2H_6(g) + 7O_2(g) \rightarrow 4CO_2(g) + 6H_2O(l)} \),
        if \(\Delta H\) is given, find \( \Delta H_c(\mathrm{C_2H_6}) \) (per mole of ethane).
        • Method: first write the “1 mole fuel” combustion equation:
          \( \mathrm{C_2H_6(g) + \tfrac{7}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow 2CO_2(g) + 3H_2O(l)} \)
        • Then \( \Delta H_c(\mathrm{C_2H_6}) = \tfrac{1}{2}(\Delta H \text{ of the 2-mole equation}) \).
  9. Calorific value: energy per gram \(=\Delta H_c/M\) (51:42)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Calorific value compares fuels by energy released per gram (mass-based comparison).
    • Reason: fuels are purchased/used by mass or volume, not by moles; heavier molecules can show larger \(\Delta H_c\) per mole but not necessarily per gram.
    • Definition:
      • Calorific value = energy released by \(1\) gram of fuel on complete combustion.
    • Calculation rule:
      \( \text{Calorific value} = \dfrac{\Delta H_c}{M} \)
      • \(M\) = molar mass of the fuel (g/mol).
      • \(\Delta H_c\) is per mole; dividing by \(M\) converts to per gram.
    • Interpretation:
      • Higher calorific value \(\Rightarrow\) better fuel in energy sense (ignoring environmental effects).
  10. Enthalpy of phase change: fusion, vaporization, condensation (01:03:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Enthalpy of phase change: heat absorbed/released when \(1\) mole of a substance changes state.
    • Fusion/freezing (liquid \(\leftrightarrow\) solid):
      • Freezing (liquid \(\rightarrow\) solid) releases heat (exothermic).
        \( \mathrm{H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2O(s)} \), \( \Delta H < 0 \) (value in lecture approx).
      • Melting (solid \(\rightarrow\) liquid) absorbs heat (endothermic).
        \( \mathrm{H_2O(s) \rightarrow H_2O(l)} \), \( \Delta H > 0 \) and magnitude equals freezing enthalpy but opposite sign.
    • Vaporization/condensation (liquid \(\leftrightarrow\) gas):
      • Vaporization/boiling (liquid \(\rightarrow\) gas) absorbs heat.
        \( \mathrm{H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2O(g)} \), \( \Delta H > 0 \) (value in lecture approx).
      • Condensation (gas \(\rightarrow\) liquid) releases heat.
        \( \mathrm{H_2O(g) \rightarrow H_2O(l)} \), \( \Delta H < 0 \), same magnitude as vaporization but opposite sign.
    • Sign reversal rule:
      • If a process is reversed, the enthalpy change changes sign.
  11. Enthalpy of sublimation and link to Hess’s law (01:08:33)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Sublimation: solid \(\rightarrow\) gas directly; requires heat, so \( \Delta H_{\text{sub}} > 0 \) (for \(1\) mole).
    • Pathway idea:
      • Solid \(\rightarrow\) liquid (melting / fusion enthalpy, positive)
      • Liquid \(\rightarrow\) gas (vaporization enthalpy, positive)
    • Relation stated:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{sub}} = \Delta H_{\text{fus}} + \Delta H_{\text{vap}} \)
    • Link to Hess’s law:
      • If a reaction/process occurs in steps, the overall enthalpy change equals the sum of enthalpy changes of the steps.

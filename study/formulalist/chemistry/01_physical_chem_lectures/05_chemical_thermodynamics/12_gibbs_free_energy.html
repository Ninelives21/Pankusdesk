  1. Why Gibbs Free Energy Is Needed (00:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Gibbs free energy is introduced as the most reliable criterion for deciding whether a process is spontaneous.
    • Earlier single-factor criteria were incomplete:
      • Enthalpy criterion: exothermic processes with \( \Delta H < 0 \) are often spontaneous because energy release increases stability.
      • Entropy criterion: processes with \( \Delta S > 0 \) are often spontaneous because disorder increases.
    • Each factor alone gives exceptions:
      • Endothermic but spontaneous examples: melting of ice, evaporation of water.
      • Entropy-decreasing yet spontaneous examples: liquefaction of gases under suitable conditions.
    • Therefore, spontaneity must be judged by a combined thermodynamic quantity rather than by \( \Delta H \) or \( \Delta S \) alone.
  2. Definition and Properties of Gibbs Free Energy (11:16)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Gibbs free energy is defined as:
      \( G = H - TS \)
    • Here:
      • \( G \): Gibbs free energy
      • \( H \): enthalpy
      • \( T \): absolute temperature in kelvin
      • \( S \): entropy
    • Since \( H \), \( T \), and \( S \) are state functions, \( G \) is also a state function.
    • Change in Gibbs free energy depends only on initial and final states:
      \( \Delta G = G_{\text{final}} - G_{\text{initial}} \)
    • \( G \) is an extensive property because it depends on the amount of matter.
    • For any cyclic process, change in a state function is zero; therefore, \( \Delta G = 0 \) for a complete cycle.
    • In applications, absolute \( G \) is usually less useful than the change \( \Delta G \).
    • At constant temperature and constant pressure:
      \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S \)
    • This expression is the main working relation for chemical reactions under ordinary laboratory conditions.
  3. Meaning of \( \Delta G \) and Criterion of Spontaneity (17:59)
    (Back to topic index)
    • \( \Delta G \) represents the negative of the useful work obtainable from the system:
      \( \Delta G = -(\text{useful work by system}) \)
    • If \( \Delta G = -100 \,\text{kJ} \), the system can perform \( 100 \,\text{kJ} \) of useful non-expansion work.
    • If \( \Delta G = +50 \,\text{kJ} \), the process requires \( 50 \,\text{kJ} \) of useful work to occur.
    • The work referred to here is not \( P\Delta V \) expansion work; it is useful non-expansion work.
    • Criterion of spontaneity:
      • Spontaneous process: \( \Delta G < 0 \)
      • Non-spontaneous process: \( \Delta G > 0 \)
    • A negative \( \Delta G \) indicates that the system can proceed on its own while still having capacity to do useful work.
  4. Favourable and Unfavourable Sign Combinations (23:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For spontaneity analysis, favourable tendencies are:
      • \( \Delta H < 0 \): exothermic change
      • \( \Delta S > 0 \): increase in entropy
    • Case 1: both favourable
      • \( \Delta H < 0 \) and \( \Delta S > 0 \)
      • Then \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S \) is always negative.
      • The process is spontaneous at all temperatures.
    • Case 2: both unfavourable
      • \( \Delta H > 0 \) and \( \Delta S < 0 \)
      • Then \( \Delta G \) is always positive.
      • The process is non-spontaneous at all temperatures.
    • These two extreme cases are straightforward because both contributing factors push in the same direction.
  5. Temperature Dependence When Signs Oppose (29:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When one factor is favourable and the other is unfavourable, spontaneity depends on temperature.
    • Case 1: \( \Delta H < 0 \), \( \Delta S < 0 \)
      • \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S \)
      • Since \( -T\Delta S \) becomes positive, \( \Delta G \) may be negative or positive.
      • For spontaneity:
        \( \Delta H - T\Delta S < 0 \)
      • This gives:
        \( T < \dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S} \)
      • Because both \( \Delta H \) and \( \Delta S \) are negative, the ratio is positive.
      • Hence, the process is spontaneous at low temperature.
    • Case 2: \( \Delta H > 0 \), \( \Delta S > 0 \)
      • Now the entropy term can overcome the positive enthalpy term only at higher temperature.
      • For spontaneity:
        \( T > \dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S} \)
      • Hence, the process is spontaneous at high temperature.
    • Equilibrium condition:
      • \( \Delta G = 0 \)
      • \( T = \dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S} \)
      • At this temperature, the reaction is at equilibrium.
    • Summary:
      • Spontaneous: \( \Delta G < 0 \)
      • Non-spontaneous: \( \Delta G > 0 \)
      • Equilibrium: \( \Delta G = 0 \)
  6. Numerical on Temperature for Spontaneity (36:19)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given reaction:
      \( A \rightarrow 2B + C \)
    • Given data:
      • \( \Delta H = 30 \,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)
      • \( \Delta S = 60 \,\text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} \)
    • For spontaneity:
      \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S < 0 \)
    • Substituting values in consistent units:
      \( 30 \times 10^3 - T(60) < 0 \)
    • \( 60T > 30 \times 10^3 \)
    • \( T > \dfrac{30 \times 10^3}{60} = 500 \,\text{K} \)
    • Therefore:
      • Spontaneous for \( T > 500 \,\text{K} \)
      • Non-spontaneous for \( T < 500 \,\text{K} \)
      • At equilibrium for \( T = 500 \,\text{K} \)
  7. Exothermic Reactions and Temperature Favourability (39:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For an exothermic reaction, \( \Delta H < 0 \).
    • General practical rule:
      • Exothermic reactions are favoured at low temperature.
      • Endothermic reactions are favoured at high temperature.
    • Reason using Gibbs relation:
      \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S \)
    • For an exothermic reaction:
      • If \( \Delta S > 0 \), the process is strongly favoured.
      • If \( \Delta S < 0 \), the positive term \( -T\Delta S \) opposes spontaneity.
      • Lower temperature reduces the opposing magnitude of \( -T\Delta S \).
    • Hence, low temperature is the safer favourable condition for the forward direction of an exothermic reaction.
  8. Coupling Reactions and Metallurgical Reduction (43:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Coupling reaction concept:
      • If one reaction has \( \Delta G < 0 \), it is spontaneous.
      • If another reaction has \( \Delta G > 0 \), it is non-spontaneous.
      • When both are combined, the overall process becomes spontaneous if total \( \Delta G \) is negative.
    • Illustrative scheme:
      • \( A \rightarrow B \), \( \Delta G = -100 \,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)
      • \( C \rightarrow D \), \( \Delta G = +80 \,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)
      • Combined reaction:
        \( A + C \rightarrow B + D \)
      • Overall:
        \( \Delta G = -20 \,\text{kJ mol}^{-1} \)
    • Thus, a non-spontaneous reaction can be driven by coupling it with a sufficiently spontaneous reaction.
    • Application in metallurgy:
      • Reduction of a metal oxide may be written generically as:
        \( M_xO_y \rightarrow xM + \dfrac{y}{2}O_2 \)
      • If this step has positive \( \Delta G \), it does not occur on its own.
      • Carbon oxidation is written as:
        \( C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 \)
      • By multiplying equations suitably and adding them so that \( O_2 \) cancels, the overall reduction equation is obtained.
    • Because \( \Delta G \) is extensive, multiplying a reaction by a factor multiplies its \( \Delta G \) by the same factor.
    • The metal oxide can be reduced by carbon only if the summed \( \Delta G \) of the coupled overall reaction is negative.
    • If the overall \( \Delta G \) remains positive, carbon cannot reduce that oxide under the given conditions.

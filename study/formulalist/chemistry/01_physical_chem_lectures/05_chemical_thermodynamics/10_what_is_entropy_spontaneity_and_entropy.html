  1. Entropy, Spontaneity, and Randomness (00:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Entropy is introduced through the idea of spontaneous and non-spontaneous processes.
    • A spontaneous process is one that occurs by itself or after a small initiation.
    • Natural spontaneous processes generally proceed toward greater randomness or disorder.
    • Examples illustrating increase of randomness:
      1. random patterns are more probable than one fixed ordered pattern,

      2. two gases kept separately mix spontaneously when allowed to communicate.
    • The origin of entropy lies in this tendency of systems to move toward more probable, disordered states.
  2. Entropy as a Measure of Randomness (04:43)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Entropy, denoted by \( S \), is the measure of randomness or disorder.
    • If a process proceeds with increasing randomness, entropy increases.
    • For a process \( A \rightarrow B \), if entropy increases while moving from \( A \) to \( B \), the process is taken as spontaneous on this basis.
    • Absolute entropy is not directly measurable in simple intuitive terms, but change in entropy is useful and measurable in thermodynamics.
  3. Sign of \( \Delta S \) for Spontaneous, Non-spontaneous, and Reversible Processes (08:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Entropy change is defined as:
      \( \Delta S = S_{\text{final}} - S_{\text{initial}} \)
    • For a spontaneous process:
      randomness increases, so \( \Delta S > 0 \)
    • For a non-spontaneous process:
      randomness decreases, so \( \Delta S < 0 \)
    • For a reversible process:
      both directions are equally favored, so \( \Delta S = 0 \)
    • This sign convention gives a quick criterion when entropy alone is being considered.
  4. Entropy is a State Function (15:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Entropy is a state function.
    • Therefore, entropy change depends only on the initial and final states, not on the path followed.
    • For any process:
      \( \Delta S = S_{\text{final}} - S_{\text{initial}} \)
    • For a chemical reaction, this becomes:
      entropy of products minus entropy of reactants.
  5. State-function Questions on Entropy Change (19:23)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Because entropy is a state function, entropy changes along successive steps can be added algebraically.
    • If a system goes from state \( X \) to \( Z \) through \( Y \), then:
      \( \Delta S_{X \rightarrow Z} = \Delta S_{X \rightarrow Y} + \Delta S_{Y \rightarrow Z} \)
    • This follows directly from dependence on initial and final states only.
      1. Question 1: Given \( \Delta S_{A \rightarrow C} = +50 \; EU \), \( \Delta S_{C \rightarrow D} = +30 \; EU \), and \( \Delta S_{B \rightarrow D} = +20 \; EU \), find \( \Delta S_{A \rightarrow B} \).
        Use \( \Delta S_{D \rightarrow B} = -20 \; EU \), then add:
        \( \Delta S_{A \rightarrow B} = \Delta S_{A \rightarrow C} + \Delta S_{C \rightarrow D} + \Delta S_{D \rightarrow B} \)
        \( = 50 + 30 - 20 = +60 \; EU \).
  6. Mathematical Definition of Entropy Change (23:18)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For constant temperature:
      \( \Delta S = \frac{\Delta Q}{T} \)
    • For variable temperature:
      \( \Delta S = \int \frac{dQ}{T} \)
    • If the system absorbs heat, \( \Delta Q \) is positive and entropy change is positive.
    • If the system releases heat, \( \Delta Q \) is negative and entropy change is negative.
    • Temperature must be taken on the absolute scale.
  7. Units and Extensive Nature of Entropy (26:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • From \( \Delta S = \frac{\Delta Q}{T} \), entropy units are heat unit per kelvin.
    • Common molar units:
      \( J \; mol^{-1} \; K^{-1} \)
    • Entropy is an extensive property.
    • Hence, entropy depends on the amount of substance present.
    • More moles generally imply greater total entropy.
  8. Entropy Change in Isolated and Non-isolated Systems (27:31)
    (Back to topic index)
    • An isolated system exchanges neither heat nor matter with surroundings.
    • Example: two gases enclosed in a thermally insulated, closed arrangement mix after the valve is opened.
    • Even though \( \Delta Q = 0 \), the mixing increases randomness.
    • Therefore, for an isolated system, spontaneity is judged by randomness increase, not merely by direct use of heat exchange.
    • If a spontaneous process occurs in an isolated system:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} > 0 \)
    • For a non-isolated system, both system and surroundings matter.
    • The total entropy criterion is:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} \)
    • For spontaneity in a non-isolated system:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0 \)
  9. Freezing of Water and Total Entropy Criterion (35:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider:
      \( H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2O(s) \)
    • For water to freeze, the system must release heat.
    • Hence, for the system:
      \( \Delta Q_{\text{system}} < 0 \Rightarrow \Delta S_{\text{system}} < 0 \)
    • Yet freezing can still be spontaneous under suitable conditions because the surroundings gain that heat and their entropy increases.
    • Spontaneity is therefore decided by \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} \), not only by \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} \).
  10. Data Interpretation at \( -1^\circ C \), \( 0^\circ C \), and \( +1^\circ C \) (38:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For the process \( H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2O(s) \):
      1. at \( -1^\circ C \), freezing is spontaneous, so \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0 \),

      2. at \( +1^\circ C \), freezing is non-spontaneous, so \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} < 0 \),

      3. at \( 0^\circ C \) and \( 1 \; atm \), the process is reversible, so \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0 \).
    • At the reversible condition, entropy decrease of the system is exactly balanced by entropy increase of the surroundings.
  11. Reversible Vaporization at \( 373 \; K \) and \( 1 \; atm \) (42:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider:
      \( H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2O(g) \) at \( 373 \; K \) and \( 1 \; atm \)
    • At this condition, liquid water and vapor coexist reversibly.
    • The system absorbs heat during vaporization, so:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} > 0 \)
    • Because the process is reversible, total entropy change must be zero:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0 \)
    • Hence, surroundings must have:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} < 0 \)
      1. Question 1: For \( H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2O(g) \) at \( 373 \; K \) and \( 1 \; atm \), determine the signs of \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} \) and \( \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} \).
        Answer: \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} > 0 \) and \( \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} < 0 \).
  12. Entropy Change in Reversible and Irreversible Processes (45:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For any reversible process:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0 \)
    • In an isothermal reversible process, system and surroundings exchange equal heat reversibly, so their entropy changes are equal in magnitude and opposite in sign.
    • In an adiabatic reversible process, no heat is exchanged, so:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} = 0 \),
      \( \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} = 0 \),
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} = 0 \)
    • For an irreversible spontaneous process:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} > 0 \)
    • Example: heat flow from a hotter system at \( T_1 \) to cooler surroundings at \( T_2 \), with \( T_1 > T_2 \).
    • Then:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{system}} = -\frac{dQ}{T_1} \)
      \( \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} = +\frac{dQ}{T_2} \)
    • So:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{total}} = -\frac{dQ}{T_1} + \frac{dQ}{T_2} = dQ \left( \frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1} \right) \)
    • Since \( T_1 > T_2 \), this expression is positive, proving entropy of the universe increases for irreversible spontaneous heat flow.
  13. Entropy of the Universe and Future Scope (52:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The general result is:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{universe}} \geq 0 \)
    • For reversible processes:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0 \)
    • For irreversible spontaneous processes:
      \( \Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0 \)
    • Entropy of the universe therefore tends to increase in natural processes.
    • In problems involving gases and thermodynamic variables, entropy change may need to be evaluated using pressure, volume, and temperature changes rather than direct heat data.
    • This leads to further study of entropy change in:
      1. isothermal processes,

      2. adiabatic processes,

      3. isochoric processes,

      4. isobaric processes,

      5. phase changes.
    • Final spontaneity of processes is later unified more completely through Gibbs free energy.

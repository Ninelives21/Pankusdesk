  1. Need for the Second Law of Thermodynamics (00:24)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The second law of thermodynamics is introduced to address questions that are not answered by the first law.
    • The first law expresses conservation of energy: total energy of the universe remains constant.
    • The second law is required to decide whether a process is feasible in nature and in which direction it can occur.
    • This topic is developed through three connected ideas:
      1. limitation of the first law,
      2. spontaneous and non-spontaneous processes,
      3. criteria used to judge spontaneity.
  2. Limitations of the First Law and Direction of Processes (01:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The first law only ensures conservation of energy. It does not state which process will actually occur.
    • It does not specify the direction of heat flow.
    • Example:
      heat flows naturally from a hot body to a cold body,
      but it does not flow spontaneously from a cold body to a hot body.
    • The first law does not comment on feasibility or spontaneity of a process.
    • Natural spontaneous processes are irreversible in character: they occur in one preferred direction and do not reverse on their own.
    • Representative examples of irreversibility:
      1. water falls downward, but does not rise upward on its own,
      2. salt or sugar dissolves in water, but does not separate spontaneously,
      3. work can be converted completely into heat, but heat cannot be converted completely into work,
      4. gas diffuses into available space, but does not spontaneously re-collect into a smaller region,
      5. evaporation of water occurs naturally, but the same vapor does not return to the lake by itself.
    • A 100% efficient engine is not possible, even though simple energy balance alone does not forbid it.
    • Key conclusion:
      the first law tells what happens to energy if a process occurs,
      but does not tell whether the process itself can occur naturally.
  3. Spontaneous and Non-spontaneous Processes (16:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A spontaneous process is one that has a tendency to occur:
      1. by itself,

      2. or after proper initiation,

      3. under given conditions.
    • Spontaneous does not mean instantaneous.
    • The definition always depends on the specified conditions such as temperature, pressure, and surrounding constraints.
    • A process may be spontaneous under one set of conditions and non-spontaneous under another.
  4. Examples of Spontaneous Processes Under Given Conditions (19:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Common spontaneous processes include:
      1. sugar dissolving in water,

      2. salt dissolving in water,

      3. evaporation of water from a lake,

      4. flow of heat from higher temperature to lower temperature,

      5. diffusion of gas from higher pressure to lower pressure,

      6. melting of ice only under suitable conditions.
    • Condition dependence is essential.
    • Example:
      melting of ice is spontaneous above its melting condition,
      but not spontaneous at very low temperature.
    • Thus, spontaneity must always be judged under the given set of conditions.
  5. Processes Requiring Proper Initiation (22:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Some processes are spontaneous only after a small initial trigger.
    • Example:
      \( 2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O \)
    • This reaction may require an electric spark for initiation, but once started, it proceeds on its own.
    • Combustion of coal also needs ignition only at the beginning.
    • The process continues spontaneously after the required initiation is supplied.
    • A spontaneous process therefore may be:
      1. self-starting,

      2. or start after a small push,

      3. but it does not need continuous external driving throughout the process.
  6. Meaning of Non-spontaneous Processes (23:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A non-spontaneous process has no tendency to occur on its own.
    • It does not proceed by itself and does not continue merely after a small initiation.
    • Such a process can occur only if continuous external energy is supplied.
    • Example 1:
      heat transfer from a colder body to a hotter body is non-spontaneous.
    • Example 2:
      in a refrigerator, heat is continuously removed from the colder interior and transferred to the warmer surroundings,
      which requires external electrical energy.
    • Example 3:
      water does not rise from lower height to higher height on its own,
      but can be pumped upward by continuous energy input.
  7. Need for a Criterion of Spontaneity (29:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A scientific criterion is required to predict whether a process is spontaneous or non-spontaneous.
    • The second law develops this criterion instead of relying only on observation.
    • Three important thermodynamic variables are introduced for this purpose:
      1. enthalpy change, \( \Delta H \),

      2. entropy change, \( \Delta S \),

      3. Gibbs free energy change, \( \Delta G \).
    • The initial discussion begins with enthalpy change, then entropy change, and finally combines them through Gibbs free energy.
  8. Criterion 1: Tendency to Lower Enthalpy (31:27)
    (Back to topic index)
    • One proposed criterion for spontaneity is the tendency of a system to lower its energy.
    • A system with lower energy is generally more stable than one with higher energy.
    • If a process releases energy, it is exothermic and has:
      \( \Delta H < 0 \)
    • This suggests that reactions with negative enthalpy change may be spontaneous.
    • Examples supporting this idea:
      1. \( 2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O \), with \( \Delta H < 0 \),

      2. \( C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) \), with \( \Delta H < 0 \).
    • At first, negative \( \Delta H \) appears to be a useful indicator of spontaneity.
  9. Why Negative Enthalpy Alone Fails (34:50)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Negative \( \Delta H \) is not the only condition for spontaneity.
    • Many endothermic processes are also spontaneous, even though:
      \( \Delta H > 0 \)
    • Examples:
      1. melting of ice to water under suitable conditions,

      2. evaporation of water to water vapor,

      3. decomposition of mercuric oxide on heating:
        \( 2HgO \rightarrow 2Hg + O_2 \)
    • Some spontaneous processes may even have approximately zero enthalpy change.
    • Example:
      \( CH_3COOH + C_2H_5OH \rightarrow CH_3COOC_2H_5 + H_2O \)
    • Therefore, spontaneity cannot be judged only from the sign of \( \Delta H \).
  10. Criterion 2: Tendency to Increase Randomness (38:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A second major tendency is the tendency of a system to acquire maximum randomness or disorder.
    • A disordered arrangement is usually more probable than a highly ordered arrangement.
    • Any system tends to move from low probability states to high probability states.
    • This tendency is described through entropy change:
      if \( \Delta S > 0 \), randomness increases.
    • Increase in entropy can therefore favor spontaneity.
    • Ordered states are less probable because they can be realized in fewer ways, whereas disordered states can be realized in many ways.
  11. Gas Mixing and Increase in Entropy (42:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider two gases separated by a partition.
    • On removing the partition, the gases mix spontaneously.
    • The reverse process, spontaneous separation of the mixed gases, does not occur naturally.
    • Reason:
      the mixed state is more random and therefore more probable than the separated ordered state.
    • Hence, mixing of gases is a spontaneous process because entropy increases:
      \( \Delta S > 0 \)
  12. Competition Between Enthalpy and Entropy Factors (44:26)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Some processes are governed by competing tendencies of enthalpy and entropy.
    • Example:
      ice melting to water.
    • For melting:
      \( \Delta H > 0 \), because heat is absorbed,
      but \( \Delta S > 0 \), because molecular randomness increases.
    • The entropy factor favors spontaneity even though the enthalpy factor does not.
    • If heat exchange is prevented by insulation, then the decision depends only on randomness considerations.
    • Thus, spontaneity can result from a balance between the tendency to lower energy and the tendency to increase disorder.
  13. When Entropy Decreases but Process Can Still Occur (47:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Entropy increase is also not the only condition for spontaneity.
    • Example:
      liquefaction of a gas.
    • In going from gas to liquid, randomness decreases, so:
      \( \Delta S < 0 \)
    • However, the process may still occur because the gas loses heat and:
      \( \Delta H < 0 \)
    • Thus, a decrease in entropy does not always make a process non-spontaneous.
    • Again, the final decision depends on the combined effect of both factors.
  14. Interplay of \( \Delta H \), \( \Delta S \), and \( \Delta G \) (49:21)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Two major tendencies govern spontaneity:
      1. tendency to attain lower energy,

      2. tendency to attain greater randomness.
    • Neither \( \Delta H \) nor \( \Delta S \) alone is universally sufficient.
    • The final thermodynamic criterion is obtained by combining both through Gibbs free energy change, \( \Delta G \).
    • When only one factor is given in a question, that factor alone is used to judge the process.
    • When both factors are relevant, their combined effect must be considered.
    • This motivates the later formal criterion of spontaneity based on Gibbs free energy.

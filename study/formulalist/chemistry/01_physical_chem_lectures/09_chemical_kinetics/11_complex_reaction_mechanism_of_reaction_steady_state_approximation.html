  1. Mechanism overview: elementary vs complex (00:20)
    (Back to topic index)
    • On the basis of mechanism, reactions are classified as:
      1. Simple / elementary (single-step)
      2. Complex (multi-step)
    • Goal: write rate laws for simple vs complex reactions, and learn how mechanisms help derive observable rate laws.
  2. Elementary reactions: transition state, Ea, and rate law signature (02:51)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Elementary (single-step) reaction: reactants \(\to\) products in one step; no intermediate is formed.
    • Transition state (TS): highest-energy arrangement during bond breaking/forming; TS is not an intermediate.
    • Activation energy:
      \(E_a=\) energy needed for reactants to reach the transition state (energy barrier crossing).
    • Energy profile picture is essential for understanding:
    • Example idea (bond changes at TS):
      \(A_2+B_2 \rightarrow 2AB\) proceeds via a TS where \(A\!-\!A\) and \(B\!-\!B\) bonds weaken while \(A\!-\!B\) bonds form.
    • Rate law signature of an elementary step (law of mass action for elementary steps):
      For \(A \rightarrow\) products, rate \(=k[A]^1\).
    • For \(A+B \rightarrow\) products (elementary), rate \(=k[A]^1[B]^1\).
  3. Molecularity, order, and when a reaction is NOT elementary (06:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Molecularity (for an elementary step): number of reactant molecules colliding simultaneously in the rate-determining step (RDS).
      In a single-step reaction, that only step is the RDS.
    • Common cases (elementary steps):
      1. Unimolecular: \(A \rightarrow\) products; molecularity \(=1\); rate \(=k[A]\)
      2. Bimolecular: \(A+B \rightarrow\) products; molecularity \(=2\); rate \(=k[A][B]\)
      3. Termolecular (rare): \(A+2B \rightarrow\) products; molecularity \(=3\); rate \(=k[A][B]^2\)
    • Reason termolecular steps are rare: simultaneous collision + proper orientation + sufficient energy is low-probability.
    • How to identify an elementary reaction:
      1. Do NOT decide from the written equation alone.
      2. Experimentally determine rate law; if powers match stoichiometric coefficients of the step, the step is consistent with being elementary.
    • Order of reaction: sum of exponents in the experimentally observed rate law.
      Example: if rate \(=k[A]^1[B]^2\), order \(=1+2=3\).
    • In elementary reactions (excluding pseudo-order cases), order equals molecularity.
    • Important caution:
      1. If total stoichiometric coefficients \(>3\) in an overall equation (e.g., \(N_2+3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3\)), the reaction is almost surely complex (multi-step).
      2. But if coefficients \(\le 3\), it still may be complex if the observed rate law does not match coefficients.
  4. Complex reactions: key features and rate-determining step idea (19:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Complex (multi-step) reaction: occurs in several elementary steps.
    • Molecularity of an overall complex reaction is not defined.
      Molecularity can be defined only for each elementary step (each \(\le 3\)).
    • Overall rate is determined by the slowest step (rate-determining step, RDS).
    • Complex reactions have intermediates (one or more).
    • Critical rule:
      Intermediates must not appear in the final overall rate law.
    • Rate law may involve reactants, products, and catalysts, but not intermediates.

  6. Solved mechanism: 2A + 3B \(\to\) A2B3 with slow step given (33:04)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Overall reaction:
      \(2A+3B \rightarrow A_2B_3\)
    • Given mechanism (3 elementary steps):
      1. \(A+B \rightarrow AB\) (slow)
      2. \(AB+B \rightarrow AB_2\) (fast)
      3. \(AB_2+A+B \rightarrow A_2B_3\) (fast)
    • Rate law (from slow elementary step):
      rate \(=k_1[A][B]\)
    • Order:
      \(1+1=2\)
    • Molecularity of each step:
      1. Step 1: \(2\)
      2. Step 2: \(2\)
      3. Step 3: \(3\)
    • Intermediates like \(AB\), \(AB_2\) must not appear in the overall rate law; here the slow step uses only reactants, so no issue.
  7. When intermediate appears in RDS: pre-equilibrium elimination method (36:37)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Problem: if the slow step contains an intermediate, a direct rate law contains that intermediate, which is not allowed in the final rate law.
    • Strategy used here (pre-equilibrium approach):
      1. Find a step that relates the intermediate directly to reactants/products.
      2. Assume that step is reversible (often explicitly given in problems).
      3. Write equilibrium constant and express intermediate concentration in terms of measurable species.
      4. Substitute into the slow-step rate expression.
      1. Question 1 Given overall: \(2A+3B \rightarrow A_2B_3\). Mechanism:
        Step 1: \(A+B \rightleftharpoons AB\) (forward \(k_1\), backward \(k_4\)) (fast, assumed equilibrium)
        Step 2: \(AB+B \rightarrow AB_2\) (slow, \(k_2\))
        Step 3: \(AB_2+A+B \rightarrow A_2B_3\) (fast)
        Derive rate law and order.
        • Slow step rate:
          rate \(=k_2[AB][B]\)
        • From step 1 equilibrium:
          \(K=\frac{[AB]}{[A][B]}=\frac{k_1}{k_4}\)
        • So:
          \([AB]=\frac{k_1}{k_4}[A][B]\)
        • Substitute:
          rate \(=k_2\left(\frac{k_1}{k_4}[A][B]\right)[B]=\left(\frac{k_1k_2}{k_4}\right)[A][B]^2\)
        • Effective rate constant:
          \(k_{\text{eff}}=\frac{k_1k_2}{k_4}\)
        • Order:
          \(1+2=3\)
  8. Example: 2O3 \(\to\) 3O2 with intermediate in slow step (45:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Overall reaction:
      \(2O_3 \rightarrow 3O_2\)
    • Given mechanism:
      1. \(O_3 \rightarrow O_2+O\) (fast)
      2. \(O+O_3 \rightarrow 2O_2\) (slow)
    • Direct slow-step rate (contains intermediate \(O\)):
      rate \(=k_2[O][O_3]\)
    • Eliminate \(O\) using reversible first step (pre-equilibrium):
      Step 1 assumed reversible:
      \(O_3 \rightleftharpoons O_2+O\) (forward \(k_1\), backward \(k_2\) in lecture notation; use as given in the problem statement)
    • Equilibrium relation:
      \(K=\frac{[O_2][O]}{[O_3]}=\frac{k_1}{k_{-1}}\)
    • Hence:
      \([O]=\left(\frac{k_1}{k_{-1}}\right)\frac{[O_3]}{[O_2]}\)
    • Substitute into slow-step rate:
      rate \(=k_{\text{slow}}[O_3]\left(\frac{k_1}{k_{-1}}\right)\frac{[O_3]}{[O_2]}=\left(\frac{k_{\text{slow}}k_1}{k_{-1}}\right)\frac{[O_3]^2}{[O_2]}\)
    • Key idea: product \([O_2]\) may appear in rate law; intermediate \([O]\) must not.

      (Backward rate constant symbol for step 1 is inconsistent in transcript; use the one provided in the actual question.)
  9. Steady-state approximation when RDS is not given (50:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Case: mechanism is given but the slowest step (RDS) is not specified.
    • Use steady-state approximation for intermediates:
      Intermediate concentration remains nearly constant during most of the reaction.
    • Mathematical statement for an intermediate \(I\):
      \(\frac{d[I]}{dt}\approx 0\)
    • Graph intuition:
    • Procedure outline:
      1. Write rate expressions for elementary steps (\(R_1, R_2,\dots\)).
      2. Write \(\frac{d[\text{intermediate}]}{dt}\) as (rate of formation) \(-\) (rate of consumption).
      3. Set \(\frac{d[\text{intermediate}]}{dt}=0\) and solve for intermediate concentration.
      4. Use stoichiometric rate definition for overall reaction (e.g., \(-\frac{1}{\nu}\frac{d[\text{reactant}]}{dt}\)) and substitute intermediate value.
    • Syllabus note:
      (Transcript notes steady-state is not part of board syllabus; treat as advanced method used in many reference problems.)
  10. Steady-state example: ozone decomposition gives rate \(=k_1[O_3]\) (56:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Overall reaction:
      \(2O_3 \rightarrow 3O_2\)
    • Mechanism (elementary steps):
      1. \(O_3 \rightarrow O_2+O\) with rate \(R_1=k_1[O_3]\)
      2. \(O+O_3 \rightarrow 2O_2\) with rate \(R_2=k_2[O][O_3]\)
    • Define overall rate by stoichiometry:
      rate \(=-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d[O_3]}{dt}=+\frac{1}{3}\frac{d[O_2]}{dt}\)
    • Steady-state for intermediate \(O\):
      \(\frac{d[O]}{dt}=(\text{formation})-(\text{consumption})=R_1-R_2\approx 0\)
    • Hence:
      \(R_1\approx R_2 \Rightarrow k_1[O_3]\approx k_2[O][O_3]\)
    • Solve for intermediate:
      \([O]\approx \frac{k_1}{k_2}\)
    • Compute \(-\frac{d[O_3]}{dt}\):
      For the overall consumption of \(O_3\), both steps consume \(O_3\) once each per their occurrences in the mechanism, so:
      \(-\frac{d[O_3]}{dt}\approx R_1+R_2\approx k_1[O_3]+k_1[O_3]=2k_1[O_3]\)
    • Therefore:
      rate \(=-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d[O_3]}{dt}\approx -\frac{1}{2}(-2k_1[O_3])=k_1[O_3]\)
    • Final results:
      1. Rate law: rate \(=k_1[O_3]\)
      2. Order: \(1\)
      3. Rate constant (effective): \(k_1\)
    • Cross-check via product formation:
      \(\frac{d[O_2]}{dt}\approx R_1+2R_2\approx k_1[O_3]+2k_1[O_3]=3k_1[O_3]\)
      So rate \(=\frac{1}{3}\frac{d[O_2]}{dt}\approx k_1[O_3]\) (consistent).

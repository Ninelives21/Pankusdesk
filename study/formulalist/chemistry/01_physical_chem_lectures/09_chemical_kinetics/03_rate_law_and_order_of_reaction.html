  1. Rate law: meaning & why experimental (01:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rate law explains how the rate of reaction depends on the concentrations of species involved in the reaction.
      (Species can include reactants, products, and even catalysts in some cases.)
    • This dependence can be complex and can vary as concentrations change, so the practical approach is:
      • Determine the rate law experimentally (there is no reliable “look at the equation and write powers” shortcut for general reactions).
      • Study only the simplest form of dependence (and not the complex form).
  2. Example: determine powers from doubling tests (05:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider: \(2A + B \rightarrow \text{products}\)
    • Experimental observations:
      • If \([A]\) is doubled (keeping \([B]\) constant) and rate doubles, then \( \text{rate} \propto [A]^1\).
      • If \([B]\) is doubled (keeping \([A]\) constant) and rate becomes 4 times, then \( \text{rate} \propto [B]^2\).
    • So the rate law becomes:
      \(\text{ROR} = k[A]^1[B]^2\)
    • \(k\) is the proportionality constant (called rate constant), and the full expression is the rate law expression.
    • The Rate tells the dependence of the rate of reaction on the concentration of the different species involved in the reaction.
    • Powers in a rate law can be:
      • Positive integers (1, 2, 3, ...)
      • Zero
      • Negative
      • Fractional (e.g., \(1/2\))
    • It can ONLY be determined experimentally.
  3. General rate law: orders x, y and overall order n (10:53)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General experimentally-determined rate law expression:
      \(\text{ROR} = k[A]^x[B]^y\)
    • Meanings:
      • \([A]\), \([B]\): concentrations (typically in mol L\(^{-1}\))
      • \(x\): order of reaction w.r.t. A
      • \(y\): order of reaction w.r.t. B
      • \(n = x + y\): overall (total) order of the reaction
    • Important: \(x\), \(y\), and \(n\) can each be negative, zero, positive, or fractional, so they generally cannot be guessed from stoichiometric coefficients.
  4. Rate constant k: meaning & aliases (15:18)
    (Back to topic index)
    • \(k\) is called:
      • rate constant
      • velocity constant (in some books)
      • specific reaction rate
    • Interpretation (specific reaction rate idea):
      • We know from rate law : \( \text{rate} = k\,[A]^x[B]^y \)
      • If concentration of all species in the rate law are unity (i.e., each equals 1), then the rate becomes:
        \(k = \text{rate}\) (because \(1^x = 1\)).
      • Hence k is also known as "specific rate"
  5. Units of k and how unit reveals order (18:27)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Common unit of rate:
      \(\text{rate unit} = \text{mol L}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\)
    • From \(\text{rate} = k[A]^x[B]^y\) and
      \(n = x+y\) (here n is order of reaction)
      \([A]^x[B]^y\) has unit \((\text{mol L}^{-1})^n\)
    • Therefore unit of \(k\) becomes:
      1. Rate law (general form)
        \( \text{Rate} = k\,(A)^x(B)^y \)
      2. Rearrange for \(k\)
        \( k=\frac{\text{Rate}}{(A)^x(B)^y} \)
      3. Write the unit of rate
        (Rate is “concentration per time”)
        \( (\text{Rate}) = \left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)\text{s}^{-1} \)
      4. Write the unit of concentration terms
        \( (A)=(B)=\left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right) \) So,
        \( (A)^x(B)^y=\left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)^x \left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)^y =\left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)^{x+y} \)
      5. Substitute units into the expression for \(k\)
        \( (k) = \frac{\left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)\text{s}^{-1}} {\left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)^{x+y}} \)
      6. Combine powers
        \( (k) = \left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)^{1-(x+y)}\text{s}^{-1} \)
      7. Given overall order \(n=x+y\)
        \( (k) = \left(\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}\right)^{1-n}\text{s}^{-1} \)

        Unit of k is \( (k) =
        \text{mol}^{\,1-n}\,\text{L}^{\,n-1}\,\text{s}^{-1} \).

    • Special cases (useful recognition):
      • Zero order (\(n=0\)):
        \(k\) unit \(= \text{mol L}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\)

      • First order (\(n=1\)):
        \(k\) unit \(= \text{s}^{-1}\)

      • Second order (\(n=2\)):
        \(k\) unit \(= \text{L mol}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\)

    • Example: if \(k\) unit is \(\text{L}^2\text{ mol}^{-2}\text{ s}^{-1}\), then
      \((\text{mol L}^{-1})^{(1-n)} = \text{L}^2\text{ mol}^{-2}\ \Rightarrow\ 1-n = -2\ \Rightarrow\ n=3\)
  6. Solved-type questions on rate change (27:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given: \(2a + 3b \rightarrow \text{products}\), and experimentally:
      Order w.r.t. \(a = 2\), order w.r.t. \(b = -1\)

    • Question : Write Rate law.
      Answer : \(\text{rate} = k[a]^2[b]^{-1}\)

    • Question : Write overall order:
      Answer : \(n = 2 + (-1) = 1\)

    • Question : What is the unit of \(k\) (since \(n=1\)):
      Answer : \(k\) unit \(= \text{s}^{-1}\)

      Based on the above scenario, answer the following questions.

    1. Question 1

      What happens to the rate when \([a]\) is doubled alone?

      Answer :

      Rate increases 4 times.

      • \(\text{rate} \propto [a]^2\)
      • Doubling \([a]\): factor \(= 2^2 = 4\)

    2. Question 2

      What happens to the rate when \([b]\) is halved alone?

      Answer :

      Rate increases 2 times.

      • \(\text{rate} \propto [b]^{-1}\)
      • Halving \([b]\): factor \(= (1/2)^{-1} = 2\)

    3. Question 3

      What happens to the rate when both \([a]\) and \([b]\) are doubled?

      Answer :

      Rate increases 2 times.

      • Net factor \(= 2^2 \times 2^{-1}\)
      • \(= 4 \times 1/2 = 2\)

    4. Question 4

      If container volume becomes 3 times (moles unchanged), what happens to the rate?

      Answer :

      Rate decreases 3 times.

      • Concentration \(\propto 1/V\), so each concentration becomes \(1/3\) of original
      • Net factor \(= (1/3)^2 \times (1/3)^{-1} = 1/3\)
  7. Finding x, y from “A doubled” and “A,B doubled” data (38:20)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For \(2A + B \rightarrow \text{products}\), assume:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^x[B]^y\)
    • Given experimentally:
      • If \([A]\) doubled alone → rate becomes 2 times:
        \(2 = 2^x \Rightarrow x = 1\)
      • If \([A]\) and \([B]\) both doubled → rate becomes 8 times:
        \(8 = 2^{x+y} = 2^3 \Rightarrow x+y=3\)
      • With \(x=1\), \(y=2\)
    • Final:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^1[B]^2\), overall order \(n = 3\)
  8. 3-species question: volume change + A/B/C changes (44:33)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Question
      • Reaction:
        \( A + 2B + 4C \rightarrow \text{Products} \)
      • When volume of container is doubled, rate of reaction (ROR) decreases 8 times.
      • When (A) is doubled and (B) is halved, ROR increases 4 times.
      • When (C) alone is increased 4 times, ROR increases 64 times.
      • Write the rate law expression and find the order of reaction.
    • Answer
      • \( r = \text{ROR} = k(A)^x(B)^y(C)^z \)   (i)
      • \( \frac{r}{8} = k\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)^x\left(\frac{B}{2}\right)^y\left(\frac{C}{2}\right)^z \)   (ii)
      • \( 4r = k(2A)^x\left(\frac{B}{2}\right)^y(C)^z \)   (iii)
      • \( 64r = k(A)^x(B)^y(4C)^z \)   (iv)

      • (i) ÷ (ii) :
        \( 8 = 2^{x+y+z} \)
        \( x+y+z = 3 \)

      • (iv) ÷ (i) :
        \( 64 = 4^{z} \)
        \( z = 3 \)

      • (iii) ÷ (i) :
        \( 4 = 2^{x-y} \)
        \( x-y = 2 \)

      • Substitute \( z = 3 \) into \( x+y+z = 3 \)
        \( x+y+3 = 3 \)
        \( x+y = 0 \)

      • Solve simultaneously:
        \( x+y = 0 \)
        \( x-y = 2 \)

      • Adding both equations:
        \( 2x = 2 \)
        \( x = 1 \)
        \( y = -1 \)

      • Final rate law:
        \(\text{rate} = k[A]^1[B]^{-1}[C]^3\)
      • Overall order:
        \(n = x+y+z = 1 + (-1) + 3 = 3\)
  9. Key facts: stoichiometry ≠ order; products/catalyst/zero-order terms (53:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • (i) Stoichiometric coefficients have nothing to do with order.
      \( 2N_2O_5 \rightarrow 4NO_2 + O_2 \)
      \( \text{ROR} = k(N_2O_5)^1 \) (from experiments)

    • (ii) In rate law, concentration terms of products can be present.
      \( 2O_3 \rightarrow 3O_2 \)
      \( \text{ROR} = k(O_3)^2(O_2)^{-1} \)

    • (iii) In rate law, concentration term of some reactant may be absent.
      \( NO_2 + CO \rightarrow NO + CO_2 \)
      \( \text{ROR} = k(NO_2)^2 \)
      \( \text{ROR} = k(NO_2)^2(CO)^0 \)

    • (iv) In rate law, concentration term of catalyst may be present.
      \( 2SO_2 + O_2 \xrightarrow{NO} 2SO_3 \)
      \( \text{ROR} = k(O_2)^2(NO)^1 \)
  10. Law of mass action validity: elementary step & RDS idea (58:50)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The “law of mass action” idea (using stoichiometric coefficients as powers) is not generally valid.
    • It is valid for elementary (single-step) reactions, where the written step itself is the rate-determining step.
    • For complex (multi-step) reactions, we will need to know the intermediate steps and find the slowest (rate determining) step.
      • One step is the slowest step (rate-determining step, RDS).
      • Rate law can often be related to the stoichiometric coefficients of the RDS (once the mechanism is known).
    • Mechanism illustration (conceptual):
      • Overall complex reaction: \(3A + 2B \rightarrow C\)
      • Possible steps:
        • \(2A + B \rightarrow P\) (RDS)
        • \(P + A \rightarrow Q\)
        • \(Q + B \rightarrow C\)
        • \(3A + 2B \rightarrow C\)
      • If the slowest step is \(2A + B \rightarrow P\), then rate may follow:
        \(\text{rate} = k[A]^2[B]^1\)
    • Examples of simple, single-step reaction:
      • Elementary: \(H_2 + I_2 \rightarrow 2HI\) → possible \(\text{rate} = k[H_2]^1[I_2]^1\)
      • Not necessarily elementary (experimental surprise possible): \(H_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2HCl\) may show orders like 0 and 0 experimentally, indicating a complex mechanism.

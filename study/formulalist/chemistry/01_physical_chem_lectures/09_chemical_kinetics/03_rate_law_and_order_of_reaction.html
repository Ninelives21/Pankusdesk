  1. Rate law: meaning & why experimental (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rate law explains how the rate of reaction depends on the concentrations of species involved in the reaction.
      (Species can include reactants, products, and even catalysts in some cases.)
    • This dependence can be complex and can vary as concentrations change, so the practical approach is:
      • Determine the rate law experimentally (there is no reliable “look at the equation and write powers” shortcut for general reactions).
  2. Example: determine powers from doubling tests (05:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider: \(2A + B \rightarrow \text{products}\)
    • Experimental observations:
      • If \([A]\) is doubled (keeping \([B]\) constant) and rate doubles, then \( \text{rate} \propto [A]^1\).
      • If \([B]\) is doubled (keeping \([A]\) constant) and rate becomes 4 times, then \( \text{rate} \propto [B]^2\).
    • So the rate law becomes:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^1[B]^2\)
    • \(k\) is the proportionality constant (called rate constant), and the full expression is the rate law expression.
    • Powers in a rate law can be:
      • Positive integers (1, 2, 3, ...)
      • Zero
      • Negative
      • Fractional (e.g., \(1/2\))
  3. General rate law: orders x, y and overall order n (11:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General experimentally-determined form:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^x[B]^y\)
    • Meanings:
      • \([A]\), \([B]\): concentrations (typically in mol L\(^{-1}\))
      • \(x\): order w.r.t. A
      • \(y\): order w.r.t. B
      • \(n = x + y\): overall (total) order of the reaction
    • Important: \(x\), \(y\), and \(n\) can each be negative, zero, positive, or fractional, so they generally cannot be guessed from stoichiometric coefficients.
  4. Rate constant k: meaning & aliases (15:18)
    (Back to topic index)
    • \(k\) is called:
      • rate constant
      • velocity constant (in some books)
      • specific reaction rate
    • Interpretation (specific reaction rate idea):
      • If all concentration terms in the rate law are unity (i.e., each equals 1), then the rate becomes:
        \(k = \text{rate}\) (because \(1^x = 1\)).
    • \(k\) depends on conditions like temperature (details studied later).
  5. Units of k and how unit reveals order (18:27)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Common unit of rate:
      \(\text{rate unit} = \text{mol L}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\)
    • From \(\text{rate} = k[A]^x[B]^y\) and \(n = x+y\):
      \([A]^x[B]^y\) has unit \((\text{mol L}^{-1})^n\)
    • Therefore unit of \(k\) becomes:
      \(k\ \text{unit} = (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{(1-n)} \text{ s}^{-1}\)
    • Special cases (useful recognition):
      • Zero order (\(n=0\)):
        \(k\) unit \(= \text{mol L}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\)
      • First order (\(n=1\)):
        \(k\) unit \(= \text{s}^{-1}\)
      • Second order (\(n=2\)):
        \(k\) unit \(= \text{L mol}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\)
    • Example: if \(k\) unit is \(\text{L}^2\text{ mol}^{-2}\text{ s}^{-1}\), then
      \((\text{mol L}^{-1})^{(1-n)} = \text{L}^2\text{ mol}^{-2}\ \Rightarrow\ 1-n = -2\ \Rightarrow\ n=3\)
  6. Solved-type questions on rate change (27:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given: \(2a + 3b \rightarrow \text{products}\), and experimentally:
      Order w.r.t. \(a = 2\), order w.r.t. \(b = -1\)
    • Rate law:
      \(\text{rate} = k[a]^2[b]^{-1}\)
    • Overall order:
      \(n = 2 + (-1) = 1\)
    • Unit of \(k\) (since \(n=1\)):
      \(k\) unit \(= \text{s}^{-1}\)
    1. Question 1

      What happens to the rate when \([a]\) is doubled alone?

      Rate increases 4 times.

      • \(\text{rate} \propto [a]^2\)
      • Doubling \([a]\): factor \(= 2^2 = 4\)

    2. Question 2

      What happens to the rate when \([b]\) is halved alone?

      Rate increases 2 times.

      • \(\text{rate} \propto [b]^{-1}\)
      • Halving \([b]\): factor \(= (1/2)^{-1} = 2\)

    3. Question 3

      What happens to the rate when both \([a]\) and \([b]\) are doubled?

      Rate increases 2 times.

      • Net factor \(= 2^2 \times 2^{-1}\)
      • \(= 4 \times 1/2 = 2\)

    4. Question 4

      If container volume becomes 3 times (moles unchanged), what happens to the rate?

      Rate decreases 3 times.

      • Concentration \(\propto 1/V\), so each concentration becomes \(1/3\) of original
      • Net factor \(= (1/3)^2 \times (1/3)^{-1} = 1/3\)
  7. Finding x, y from “A doubled” and “A,B doubled” data (38:20)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For \(2A + B \rightarrow \text{products}\), assume:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^x[B]^y\)
    • Given experimentally:
      • If \([A]\) doubled alone → rate becomes 2 times:
        \(2 = 2^x \Rightarrow x = 1\)
      • If \([A]\) and \([B]\) both doubled → rate becomes 8 times:
        \(8 = 2^{x+y} = 2^3 \Rightarrow x+y=3\)
      • With \(x=1\), \(y=2\)
    • Final:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^1[B]^2\), overall order \(n = 3\)
  8. 3-species question: volume change + A/B/C changes (44:33)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a reaction involving \(A, B, C\), assume:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^x[B]^y[C]^z\)
    • If volume is doubled (moles constant), each concentration becomes half:
      \(\text{rate changes by factor} = (1/2)^{x+y+z}\)
    • If \(C\) is increased 4 times alone and rate increases 64 times:
      \(64 = 4^z \Rightarrow z = 3\)
    • If \(A\) doubled and \(B\) halved (with \(C\) unchanged) and rate becomes 4 times:
      \(4 = 2^x \times (1/2)^y = 2^{x-y} \Rightarrow x-y=2\)
    • With the volume-doubling relation giving \(x+y+z = 3\) and \(z=3\):
      \(x+y = 0\)
    • Solve:
      • \(x+y=0\)
      • \(x-y=2\)
      • \(\Rightarrow x=1,\ y=-1\)
    • Final rate law:
      \(\text{rate} = k[A]^1[B]^{-1}[C]^3\)
    • Overall order:
      \(n = x+y+z = 1 + (-1) + 3 = 3\)
  9. Key facts: stoichiometry ≠ order; products/catalyst/zero-order terms (53:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Stoichiometric coefficients have no direct relation with order (in general reactions).
      Order is determined experimentally, not by balancing numbers.
    • In a rate law, concentration terms of products can appear (sometimes with negative powers).
      Example form: \(\text{rate} = k[O_3]^2[O_2]^{-1}\)
    • Some reactants can be absent from the written rate law because their order is zero.
      Example: \(\text{rate} = k[NO_2]^2[CO]^0 \Rightarrow \text{rate} = k[NO_2]^2\)
    • A catalyst concentration term can also appear if the rate depends on it.
      Example (contact process idea): \(2SO_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2SO_3\), catalyst \(NO\)
      Possible experimental form: \(\text{rate} = k[O_2]^2[NO]^1\)
  10. Law of mass action validity: elementary step & RDS idea (58:45)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The “law of mass action” idea (using stoichiometric coefficients as powers) is not generally valid.
    • It is valid for elementary (single-step) reactions, where the written step itself is the rate-determining step.
    • For complex (multi-step) reactions:
      • One step is the slowest step (rate-determining step, RDS).
      • Rate law can often be related to the stoichiometric coefficients of the RDS (once the mechanism is known).
    • Mechanism illustration (conceptual):
      • Overall: \(3A + 2B \rightarrow C\)
      • Possible steps:
        • \(2A + B \rightarrow P\)
        • \(P + A \rightarrow Q\)
        • \(Q + B \rightarrow C\)
      • If the slowest step is \(2A + B \rightarrow P\), then rate may follow:
        \(\text{rate} = k[A]^2[B]^1\)
    • Examples mentioned:
      • Elementary: \(H_2 + I_2 \rightarrow 2HI\) → possible \(\text{rate} = k[H_2]^1[I_2]^1\)
      • Not necessarily elementary (experimental surprise possible): \(H_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2HCl\) may show orders like 0 and 0 experimentally, indicating a complex mechanism.

  1. Chemical Kinetics: scope + roadmap (00:14)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Chemical Kinetics (Physical Chemistry) studies three connected areas:
      • Rate of reaction (how fast a reaction proceeds)
      • Factors affecting rate (e.g., temperature, concentration, pressure)
      • Reaction mechanism (stepwise pathway)
    • Chapter flow (high-level): Rate of reaction → factors (e.g., Arrhenius, order) → rate law → molecularity → mechanism.
  2. Rate of reaction: meaning + how measured (05:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rate of reaction = change in concentration of reactant or product with respect to time.
    • Practical idea:
      If reactant concentration decreases faster OR product concentration increases faster → reaction is faster.
    • Rate is meaningful mainly for reactions where concentration change can be measured over a reasonable time scale.
  3. Very fast vs very slow vs moderate reactions (06:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Very fast reactions:
      So rapid that rate is practically not measurable (e.g., many ionic precipitation-type processes).
    • Very slow reactions:
      May be measurable but not very useful for kinetics applications (take months/years; e.g., rusting).
    • Moderate reactions:
      Rate can be measured and is useful (often molecular reactions where bonds break/form).
    • Key reason moderate molecular reactions take finite time:
      bond breaking + bond formation requires time; unlike ionic solutions where ions can combine quickly.
  4. Average rate of reaction: stoichiometric form (12:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a general reaction:
      \( aA + bB \rightarrow cC + dD \)
    • Average rate (over a finite time interval):
      \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta [A]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{\Delta [B]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\Delta [C]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{d}\frac{\Delta [D]}{\Delta t} \)
    • Sign convention:
      reactants decrease → negative \(\Delta\), so a minus sign makes overall rate positive.
    • Always divide by stoichiometric coefficient when writing rate-of-reaction in terms of any species.
  5. Rate of appearance / disappearance + units (16:43)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rate of appearance (product):
      \( \text{ROA of product} = \frac{\Delta[\text{product}]}{\Delta t} \) (positive by meaning)
    • Rate of disappearance (reactant):
      \( \text{ROD of reactant} = \left|\frac{\Delta[\text{reactant}]}{\Delta t}\right| \) (use magnitude for a positive rate)
    • Important distinction:
      ROA/ROD are for a specific species and do not require dividing by stoichiometric coefficient by definition, but when converting between ROA/ROD and “rate of reaction”, coefficients matter.
    • Units of rate (common):
      concentration/time = \( \text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)
    • For gases sometimes concentration is expressed via partial pressure:
      unit becomes pressure/time (depending on how the problem defines “concentration”).
  6. Practice questions (21:21)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Question 1

      A reaction has stoichiometric coefficients 4, 5, 4, 6 for four species (two reactants → two products). Write the average rate of reaction expression in terms of each species’ concentration change.

      Answer

      • For reactants (decreasing concentrations):
        \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{4}\frac{\Delta[\text{Reactant 1}]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{5}\frac{\Delta[\text{Reactant 2}]}{\Delta t} \)
      • For products (increasing concentrations):
        \( \text{Rate} = \frac{1}{4}\frac{\Delta[\text{Product 1}]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{6}\frac{\Delta[\text{Product 2}]}{\Delta t} \)
      • Rule used: divide by stoichiometric coefficient; reactants carry a minus sign so the rate stays positive.

    2. Question 2

      During formation of ammonia:
      \( N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3 \)
      In a 1 L vessel, 2 moles of \(N_2\) disappear in 1 minute. Find:
      (i) rate of reaction, (ii) rate of disappearance of \(H_2\), (iii) rate of appearance of \(NH_3\).

      Answer

      • Convert to concentration change:
        \( \Delta[N_2] = -2~\text{mol L}^{-1} \) in \(60~\text{s}\)
        \( \frac{\Delta[N_2]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{2}{60} = -\frac{1}{30}~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)
      • Rate of reaction:
        \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{1}\frac{\Delta[N_2]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{30}~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)
      • Rate of disappearance of \(H_2\):
        From \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{3}\frac{\Delta[H_2]}{\Delta t} \Rightarrow \left|\frac{\Delta[H_2]}{\Delta t}\right| = 3\times \text{Rate} \)
        \( \text{ROD}(H_2) = 3 \times \frac{1}{30} = \frac{1}{10}~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)
      • Rate of appearance of \(NH_3\):
        From \( \text{Rate} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[NH_3]}{\Delta t} \Rightarrow \frac{\Delta[NH_3]}{\Delta t} = 2\times \text{Rate} \)
        \( \text{ROA}(NH_3) = 2 \times \frac{1}{30} = \frac{1}{15}~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)

    3. Question 3

      For:
      \( 2N_2O_5 \rightarrow 4NO_2 + O_2 \)
      If rate of appearance of \(NO_2\) is \(2~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}\), find:
      (i) rate of reaction, (ii) rate of disappearance of \(N_2O_5\).

      Answer

      • Rate of reaction in terms of \(NO_2\):
        \( \text{Rate} = \frac{1}{4}\frac{\Delta[NO_2]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{4}\times 2 = \frac{1}{2}~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)
      • For \(N_2O_5\):
        \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[N_2O_5]}{\Delta t} \Rightarrow \text{ROD}(N_2O_5)=\left|\frac{\Delta[N_2O_5]}{\Delta t}\right| = 2\times \text{Rate} \)
        \( \text{ROD}(N_2O_5)= 2 \times \frac{1}{2} = 1~\text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \)
  7. Instantaneous rate: differential form + slope (33:58)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Instantaneous rate refers to rate at a particular instant (very small \(\Delta t\); conceptually \(\Delta t \to 0\)).
    • Replace \(\Delta\) with differential \(d\):
      \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{d[B]}{dt} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{d[C]}{dt} \)
    • Graph meaning:
      \( \frac{d[\text{species}]}{dt} \) equals the slope of concentration vs time curve at that point (tangent slope).
    • Reactant curve typically slopes downward; product curve slopes upward; slopes approach 0 near equilibrium.
  8. Graph-based questions (angle/slope → rate) (39:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • From a graph, slope can be computed as \( \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} \) (units = concentration/time).
    • If an angle \(\theta\) is given for the tangent, slope = \( \tan\theta \) (angle measured from positive x-axis direction).
    • Once \( \frac{d[\text{species}]}{dt} \) is obtained, convert to rate of reaction using:
      \( \text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{\nu_\text{reactant}}\frac{d[\text{reactant}]}{dt} \) or \( \text{Rate} = \frac{1}{\nu_\text{product}}\frac{d[\text{product}]}{dt} \)

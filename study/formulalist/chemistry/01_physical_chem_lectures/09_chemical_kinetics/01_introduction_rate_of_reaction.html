  2. Rate of reaction: meaning (05:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    • “Rate” means how fast a change is happening.
    • Rate of reaction is measured via concentration change with time:
      \( \text{ROR} \propto \dfrac{\Delta[\text{reactant}]}{\Delta t} \) or \( \dfrac{\Delta[\text{product}]}{\Delta t} \)
    • Intuition:
      • Reactant concentration decreases with time → faster decrease ⇒ faster reaction
      • Product concentration increases with time → faster increase ⇒ faster reaction
  3. Which reactions matter (06:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    • ROR is practically useful mainly for moderate-speed reactions (neither too fast nor too slow).
    • Very fast (typically ionic) reactions: too quick to measure reliably.
      Example (precipitation):
      \( \mathrm{NaCl + AgNO_3 \rightarrow AgCl\,(s) + NaNO_3} \)
      • Ions already present in solution → interaction happens in microseconds (order of \(10^{-6}\,\mathrm{s}\))
      • So ROR cannot be determined practically for such ultra-fast cases
    • Very slow reactions: can be measured, but not very useful for kinetics-driven applications.
      • Example: rusting of iron (takes months/years)
    • Moderate reactions (often molecular): bond breaking + bond making takes finite time → measurable.
      Example (Haber process overall):
      \( \mathrm{N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3} \)
  4. Average rate formula (12:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a general reaction:
      \( \mathrm{2A + 3B \rightarrow 4C} \)
    • Average rate of reaction is written by dividing by stoichiometric coefficient:
      • Using a reactant (negative sign to keep rate positive):
        \( \text{Average ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{\Delta[B]}{\Delta t} \)
      • Using a product (positive sign):
        \( \text{Average ROR} = +\dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{\Delta[C]}{\Delta t} \)
    • Key rules (for ROR written from concentration-change data):
      • Reactant term: use a minus sign (since \(\Delta[\text{reactant}]\) is negative)
      • Product term: use a plus sign (since \(\Delta[\text{product}]\) is positive)
      • Always divide by the stoichiometric coefficient of that species
    • Another example template:
      \( \mathrm{3X + Y \rightarrow 2Z} \)
      \( \text{Average ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{\Delta[X]}{\Delta t} = -\dfrac{1}{1}\dfrac{\Delta[Y]}{\Delta t} = +\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{\Delta[Z]}{\Delta t} \)
  5. ROA/ROD and units (16:40)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Two related terms:
      • Rate of appearance (ROA): how fast a product forms.
        Example for product \(Z\):
        \( \text{ROA of }Z = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[Z]}{\Delta t}\right| \)
      • Rate of disappearance (ROD): how fast a reactant is consumed.
        Example for reactant \(A\):
        \( \text{ROD of }A = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t}\right| \)
    • Important distinction (as stated in lecture):
      • For ROA/ROD, use magnitude (mod) so the reported rate is positive
      • Stoichiometric coefficient division is a rule for ROR expressions, not a mandatory rule for directly stating ROA/ROD as \(\left|\Delta[\cdot]/\Delta t\right|\)
    • Units of rate:
      • If concentration is in \(\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}}\) and time in \(\mathrm{s}\):
        Unit \(= \mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}}\)
      • If gases are expressed using partial pressure, then rate unit becomes:
        \(\mathrm{pressure\,time^{-1}}\) (e.g., \(\mathrm{atm\,s^{-1}}\) or \(\mathrm{Pa\,s^{-1}}\))
  6. Practice: average rate & ROA/ROD (21:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Practice questions (average rate + ROA/ROD), with quick answers.
      1. Question 1
        • Given (from coefficients used in the expression): \( \mathrm{4NH_3 + 5O_2 \rightarrow 4NO + 6H_2O} \)
        • Average ROR:
          \( \text{Average ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{\Delta t} = -\dfrac{1}{5}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{O_2}]}{\Delta t} = +\dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NO}]}{\Delta t} = +\dfrac{1}{6}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2O}]}{\Delta t} \)
        • ROA of \(\mathrm{NO}\):
          \( \text{ROA} = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NO}]}{\Delta t}\right| \)
        • ROD of \(\mathrm{O_2}\):
          \( \text{ROD} = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{O_2}]}{\Delta t}\right| \)

      2. Question 2
        • Reaction: \( \mathrm{N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3} \)
        • Given: \(2\) moles of \(\mathrm{N_2}\) disappear in \(1\) minute, vessel volume \(= 1\,\mathrm{L}\).
        • Convert to concentration change rate:
          \( \dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2}]}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{-2\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}}}{60\,\mathrm{s}} = -\dfrac{1}{30}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
        • Rate of reaction (from \(\mathrm{N_2}\) term):
          \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{1}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2}]}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{1}{30}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
        • ROD of \(\mathrm{H_2}\) (as magnitude):
          \( \text{ROD of }\mathrm{H_2} = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2}]}{\Delta t}\right| = 3 \times \text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{10}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
        • ROA of \(\mathrm{NH_3}\) (as magnitude):
          \( \text{ROA of }\mathrm{NH_3} = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{\Delta t}\right| = 2 \times \text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{15}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)

      3. Question 3
        • Reaction: \( \mathrm{2N_2O_5 \rightarrow 4NO_2 + O_2} \)
        • Given: ROA of \(\mathrm{NO_2}\) \(= 2\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}}\).
        • Rate of reaction:
          \( \text{ROR} = +\dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NO_2}]}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{1}{4}\times 2 = \dfrac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
        • ROD of \(\mathrm{N_2O_5}\) (magnitude):
          \( \text{ROD of }\mathrm{N_2O_5} = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2O_5}]}{\Delta t}\right| = 2 \times \text{ROR} = 1\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
  7. Instantaneous rate & graphs (33:46)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Instantaneous rate = rate at a specific instant (very small time interval, \(\Delta t \to 0\)).
    • Replace \(\Delta\) with \(d\) (derivative form).
      For \( \mathrm{2A + 3B \rightarrow 4C} \):
      \( \text{Instantaneous ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[B]}{dt} = +\dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{d[C]}{dt} \)
    • Graph link: \( \dfrac{d[\cdot]}{dt} \) is the slope of concentration–time graph at that point (slope of tangent).
    • Graph reminders used in the lecture:
      • Reactant concentration vs time: slope is negative (decreasing)
      • Product concentration vs time: slope is positive initially (increasing), then becomes zero at equilibrium (flat)
      • Slope is measured with the angle made by the tangent with the positive \(x\)-axis direction
  8. Practice: slope/angle/tangent method (40:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When a concentration–time graph is given, compute slope using two points (or using \(\tan\theta\) if angle is given), then convert to ROR using stoichiometric relations.
    • Quick worked cases from the lecture:
      • For \( \mathrm{N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3} \), if the graph gives two points: \((t_1, [\mathrm{H_2}]_1) = (2\,\mathrm{s}, 10)\) and \((t_2, [\mathrm{H_2}]_2) = (4\,\mathrm{s}, 6)\)
        \( \dfrac{d[\mathrm{H_2}]}{dt} \approx \dfrac{6 - 10}{4 - 2} = -2\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
        Then \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{H_2}]}{dt} = \dfrac{2}{3}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • If a straight-line decrease is shown and the tangent angle (with +\(x\)-axis) is given, use:
        \( \text{slope} = \tan\theta \)
    • If \(\theta = 135^\circ\), then \(\tan 135^\circ = -1\), so \( \dfrac{d[Q]}{dt} = -1 \) (units follow the axes).
      • For a reaction where \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[Q]}{dt} \), this gives \( \text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{3} \).
      • Rate of appearance of product \(R\) equals \( \dfrac{d[R]}{dt} \) and is linked to ROR by the coefficient in the ROR definition for that species.
    • At a specific time (instantaneous):
      • If \(\theta = 150^\circ\), then \(\tan 150^\circ = -\tan 30^\circ = -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\), so \( \dfrac{d[B]}{dt} = -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \).
      • For \( \mathrm{2A + B \rightarrow 3C} \):
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{1}\dfrac{d[B]}{dt} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • Rate of disappearance of \(A\):
        \( \text{ROD of }A = \left|\dfrac{d[A]}{dt}\right| = 2 \times \text{ROR} = \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • Rate of appearance of \(C\):
        \( \text{ROA of }C = \left|\dfrac{d[C]}{dt}\right| = 3 \times \text{ROR} = \sqrt{3}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)

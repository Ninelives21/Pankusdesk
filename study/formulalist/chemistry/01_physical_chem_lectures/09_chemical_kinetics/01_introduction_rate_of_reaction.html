  2. Rate of Reaction: Meaning and Definition (05:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Rate of reaction refers to the speed at which a chemical reaction occurs.
    2. It is measured as the change in concentration of reactants or products with respect to time.
  3. Which Reactions Have a Useful Measurable Rate? (06:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Very fast reactions:
      • Example: Ionic reactions in aqueous solution
      • Example reaction:
        \( \mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{AgNO_3} \rightarrow \mathrm{AgCl} + \mathrm{NaNO_3} \)
      • Occur in microseconds, so rate cannot be practically measured
    2. Very slow reactions:
      • Example: Rusting of iron
      • Rate is extremely small and not useful for practical applications
    3. Moderate-speed reactions:
      • Rate is measurable and useful
      • Example molecular reaction:
        \( \mathrm{N_2} + 3\mathrm{H_2} \rightarrow 2\mathrm{NH_3} \)
      • Involves bond breaking and bond formation
  4. Average Rate of Reaction (12:47)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. For a reaction:
      \( 2\mathrm{A} + 3\mathrm{B} \rightarrow 4\mathrm{C} \)
    2. Average rate of reaction is defined as:
      \( \text{ROR} = -\frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{A}]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{3}\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{B}]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{4}\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{C}]}{\Delta t} \)
    3. Negative sign is used for reactants because their concentration decreases with time.
    4. Each term is divided by the stoichiometric coefficient to obtain a single reaction rate.
  5. Rate of Appearance and Rate of Disappearance (16:43)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Rate of appearance (for products) is defined as the rate at which product concentration increases:
      • Example for product \( \mathrm{Z} \):
        \( \text{ROA of } \mathrm{Z} = \dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{Z}]}{\Delta t} \)
      • Reported value should be positive because product concentration increases.
    2. Rate of disappearance (for reactants) is defined as the rate at which reactant concentration decreases:
      • Example for reactant \( \mathrm{O_2} \):
        \( \text{ROD of } \mathrm{O_2} = \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{O_2}]}{\Delta t}\right| \)
      • Reported value should be positive (use magnitude).
    3. Stoichiometric coefficient division is NOT applied to ROA/ROD (direct concentration change per time).
  6. Units of Rate of Reaction (19:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Rate of reaction has the unit “concentration per time”.
      • If concentration is in \( \mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}} \) and time is in \( \mathrm{s} \), then:
        \( \text{Unit of rate} = \mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • For gases, concentration may be expressed in terms of partial pressure, so the unit can be “pressure per time”.

  7. Instantaneous Rate and Graph Interpretation (33:58)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Instantaneous rate is defined for a very small time interval (\( \Delta t \to 0 \)).
    2. For a reaction:
      \( 2\mathrm{A} + 3\mathrm{B} \rightarrow 4\mathrm{C} \)
      • Instantaneous rate form:
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{A}]}{dt} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{B}]}{dt} = \dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{C}]}{dt} \)
      • \( \dfrac{d[\ ]}{dt} \) represents a very small change of concentration with time.
    3. Graph meaning:
      • Reactant concentration decreases with time; product concentration increases with time.
      • \( \dfrac{d[\mathrm{A}]}{dt}, \dfrac{d[\mathrm{B}]}{dt}, \dfrac{d[\mathrm{C}]}{dt} \) correspond to slopes of their concentration–time graphs.
      • Slope is taken as the angle made by the tangent with the positive direction of the \(x\)-axis.
  8. Practice Problems Covered (21:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    1. Answer Video
      • For a general reaction:
        \( a\mathrm{A} + b\mathrm{B} \rightarrow c\mathrm{C} + d\mathrm{D} \)
      • Average rate of reaction (single consistent rate):
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{a}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{A}]}{\Delta t} = -\dfrac{1}{b}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{B}]}{\Delta t} = +\dfrac{1}{c}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{C}]}{\Delta t} = +\dfrac{1}{d}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{D}]}{\Delta t} \)
      • Reactant concentration decreases, so \(\Delta[\text{reactant}]<0\); minus sign keeps ROR positive.
      • Product concentration increases, so product term is positive.
      • Division by stoichiometric coefficient is mandatory for ROR (not for ROA/ROD).
    2. Answer Video
      • Convert moles to concentration and minutes to seconds:
        \( \Delta[\mathrm{N_2}] = \dfrac{2\,\mathrm{mol}}{1\,\mathrm{L}} = 2\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}} \), \( \Delta t = 60\,\mathrm{s} \)
      • \( \dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2}]}{\Delta t} = -\dfrac{2}{60} = -\dfrac{1}{30}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • ROR using \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{1}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2}]}{\Delta t} \):
        \( \text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{30}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • From \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2}]}{\Delta t} \):
        \( \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2}]}{\Delta t}\right| = 3\,\text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{10}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • From \( \text{ROR} = +\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{\Delta t} \):
        \( \dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{\Delta t} = 2\,\text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{15}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • ROD/ROA values are reported positive as magnitudes; sign is carried in \(\Delta[\ ]\) when writing ROR.
    3. Answer Video
      • Given \( \dfrac{d[\mathrm{NO_2}]}{dt} = 2\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \) (appearance of product).
      • ROR from product term (divide by coefficient \(4\)):
        \( \text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{4}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{NO_2}]}{dt} = \dfrac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
      • ROD of \( \mathrm{N_2O_5} \) using reactant term (coefficient \(2\)):
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{N_2O_5}]}{dt} \Rightarrow \left|\dfrac{d[\mathrm{N_2O_5}]}{dt}\right| = 2\,\text{ROR} = 1\,\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)
    4. Answer Video
      • From a concentration–time graph, estimate the rate using slope between two points:
        \( \dfrac{d[\mathrm{H_2}]}{dt} \approx \dfrac{[\mathrm{H_2}]_2 - [\mathrm{H_2}]_1}{t_2 - t_1} \)
      • For \( \mathrm{N_2} + 3\mathrm{H_2} \rightarrow 2\mathrm{NH_3} \):
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[\mathrm{H_2}]}{dt} \)
      • Rate of disappearance of \( \mathrm{N_2} \):
        \( \text{ROD}(\mathrm{N_2}) = \left|\dfrac{d[\mathrm{N_2}]}{dt}\right| = \text{ROR} \)
      • Rate of appearance of \( \mathrm{NH_3} \):
        \( \text{ROA}(\mathrm{NH_3}) = \left|\dfrac{d[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{dt}\right| = 2\,\text{ROR} \)
      • Units are taken from axes (typically \( \mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}} \)).
    5. Answer Video
      • If a tangent makes angle \( \theta \) with the positive \(x\)-axis, then slope is:
        \( \dfrac{d[\mathrm{Q}]}{dt} = \tan\theta \) (units: concentration per time)
      • Example used: \( \theta = 135^\circ \Rightarrow \tan(135^\circ) = -1 \), so concentration is decreasing.
      • Convert slope to ROR using the stoichiometric form shown in the question statement:
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{\nu}\dfrac{d[\text{reactant}]}{dt} \) (divide by the coefficient \(\nu\)).
      • After ROR is found, convert to ROA/ROD by multiplying with stoichiometric coefficients (as required).
    6. Answer Video
      • Instantaneous rate uses derivative at a point:
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{1}{\nu}\dfrac{d[\text{reactant}]}{dt} = +\dfrac{1}{\nu}\dfrac{d[\text{product}]}{dt} \)
      • Tangent slope from angle \( \theta \):
        \( \dfrac{d[\mathrm{B}]}{dt} = \tan\theta \)
      • Example used: \( \theta = 150^\circ \Rightarrow \tan(150^\circ) = -\tan(30^\circ) = -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \)
      • If the coefficient of \( \mathrm{B} \) is \(1\), then:
        \( \text{ROR} = -\dfrac{d[\mathrm{B}]}{dt} \Rightarrow \text{ROR} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \) (with proper units)
      • ROD/ROA conversion uses stoichiometric coefficients:
        • \( \text{ROD}(\mathrm{A}) = \nu_{\mathrm{A}} \times \text{ROR} \)
        • \( \text{ROA}(\mathrm{C}) = \nu_{\mathrm{C}} \times \text{ROR} \)
    • JEE Quick Traps / Quick Recall:
      • ROR definition: divide by stoichiometric coefficient; reactants negative, products positive.
      • ROA/ROD are directly \( \left|\dfrac{\Delta[\ ]}{\Delta t}\right| \) or \( \left|\dfrac{d[\ ]}{dt}\right| \) (reported positive).
      • Average rate uses \( \dfrac{\Delta[\ ]}{\Delta t} \); instantaneous rate uses \( \dfrac{d[\ ]}{dt} \).
      • Graph slope gives \( \dfrac{d[\ ]}{dt} \); use units from axes.
      • Angle-based slope: angle must be measured from positive \(x\)-axis (avoid using the wrong angle).

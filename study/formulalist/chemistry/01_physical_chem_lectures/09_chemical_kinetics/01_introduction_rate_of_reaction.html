  2. Rate Of Reaction: Meaning (05:47)
    1. Core idea (06:03)
      • Rate of reaction measures how fast a reaction proceeds through concentration change with time:
        • Reactant concentration decreases with time
        • Product concentration increases with time
        • Rate is the “speed” of this decrease/increase
      • In short: \(ROR\) is based on “change in concentration per unit time”.
    2. Practical note (06:44)
      • In this chapter, we focus on reactions where the rate is meaningful and practically discussable (moderate rates).
  3. Which Reactions Have Measurable Rate (06:56)
    1. Very fast reactions (07:05)
      • Ionic reactions in aqueous solution can be extremely fast (microseconds), so their rate is not practically measurable in typical problems.
      • Illustrative example referenced: \(NaCl + AgNO_3 \rightarrow AgCl + NaNO_3\) (very fast precipitation in solution).
    2. Very slow reactions (08:19)
      • Some reactions are so slow (months/years) that rate discussion is not useful for typical kinetics applications.
      • Illustrative example referenced: rusting of iron (takes very long time).
    3. Moderate reactions (09:41)
      • The chapter mainly deals with reactions that occur at a moderate rate where concentration changes can be tracked meaningfully.
      • Illustrative example: molecular reactions involving bond breaking/forming, e.g. \(N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3\) (finite time).
  4. Average Rate Of Reaction (12:47)
    1. General form using a stoichiometric reaction (12:48)
      • For a reaction like \(2A + 3B \rightarrow 4C\), average rate is written using any species:
        • \(ROR = -\frac{1}{2}\,\frac{\Delta[A]}{\Delta t}\)
        • \(ROR = -\frac{1}{3}\,\frac{\Delta[B]}{\Delta t}\)
        • \(ROR = +\frac{1}{4}\,\frac{\Delta[C]}{\Delta t}\)
    2. Sign convention and “divide by coefficient” rule (13:40)
      • Reactants decrease \(\Rightarrow \Delta[\text{reactant}] < 0\), so a negative sign is used to keep \(ROR\) positive.
      • Products increase \(\Rightarrow \Delta[\text{product}] > 0\), so the sign is positive.
      • Always divide by the stoichiometric coefficient to define a single consistent \(ROR\).
    3. Example pattern (15:04)
      • For \(3X + Y \rightarrow 2Z\):
        • \(ROR = -\frac{1}{3}\,\frac{\Delta[X]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{1}\,\frac{\Delta[Y]}{\Delta t} = +\frac{1}{2}\,\frac{\Delta[Z]}{\Delta t}\)
  5. Rate Of Appearance And Disappearance (16:40)
    • Distinction from \(ROR\) (16:40)
      • Rate of appearance (usually for products): \(\frac{\Delta[\text{product}]}{\Delta t}\) (kept positive as it forms).
      • Rate of disappearance (for reactants): \(\left|\frac{\Delta[\text{reactant}]}{\Delta t}\right|\) (reported as a positive magnitude).
      • Important rule: for “appearance/disappearance” rates, you typically do not apply the stoichiometric division rule; it is simply the magnitude of concentration change per time for that species.
  6. Units Of Rate (19:22)
    1. Common unit in solutions (19:47)
      • Concentration is commonly in \(mol\,L^{-1}\) and time in \(s\), so:
        • \(ROR\) unit: \(mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}\)
    2. Gas-phase (partial pressure) description (20:14)
      • If concentration is expressed using partial pressure, unit becomes:
        • \(\frac{\text{pressure}}{\text{time}}\) (e.g., \(atm\,s^{-1}\) or \(Pa\,s^{-1}\), depending on how pressure is given)
  7. Numerical Practice: Average Rate (21:28)
    1. Converting statements into \(\Delta[\ ]/\Delta t\) (23:07)
      • If given “moles disappear in a given time” and vessel volume, convert to concentration change:
        • Use \([\,] = \frac{\text{moles}}{\text{volume}}\)
        • Convert time to \(s\) if needed (e.g., \(1\,min = 60\,s\))
    2. Worked-type pattern: formation of ammonia (23:33)
      • For \(N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3\), typical asks:
        • Find \(ROR\)
        • Find \(ROD\) of \(H_2\)
        • Find \(ROA\) of \(NH_3\)
      • Key method: compute the given species’ \(\left|\frac{\Delta[\,]}{\Delta t}\right|\), then use stoichiometric relationships to scale where required.
    3. Worked-type pattern: decomposition relation (30:09)
      • For \(2N_2O_5 \rightarrow 4NO_2 + O_2\):
        • If \(ROA\) of \(NO_2\) is given, then \(ROR = \frac{1}{4}\,\frac{\Delta[NO_2]}{\Delta t}\).
        • Then \(ROD\) of \(N_2O_5\) relates via its coefficient (magnitude scaling).
  8. Instantaneous Rate And Differential Form (33:58)
    1. Differential form (34:07)
      • Instantaneous rate deals with an extremely small time interval (\(\Delta t \to 0\)).
      • Replace \(\Delta\) by \(d\):
        • \(ROR = -\frac{1}{2}\,\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -\frac{1}{3}\,\frac{d[B]}{dt} = +\frac{1}{4}\,\frac{d[C]}{dt}\)
      • Meaning: rate at a specific instant of time.
  9. Graphs And Slope Interpretation (35:37)
    1. Reactant vs time and product vs time shapes (35:53)
      • Reactant concentration typically decreases with time.
      • Product concentration typically increases with time, then can become nearly constant as equilibrium is approached (reaction not \(100\%\) complete in many cases).
    2. Linking slope to \(d[\,]/dt\) (36:41)
      • At any point, the slope of the tangent gives instantaneous rate of change:
        • Slope for reactant curve \(\rightarrow \frac{d[\text{reactant}]}{dt}\) (negative)
        • Slope for product curve \(\rightarrow \frac{d[\text{product}]}{dt}\) (positive)
      • Slope is the angle a tangent makes with the positive direction of the \(x\)-axis (standard convention).
  10. Graph-Based Numericals (39:58)
    1. Reading slope using two points (41:19)
      • From a straight-line segment, slope can be found as:
        • \(\text{slope} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\)
      • Units follow axes (e.g., \(mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}\)).
      • Then convert slope of one species (e.g., \(\frac{d[H_2]}{dt}\)) into \(ROR\) using stoichiometric relationships.
    2. Angle-based slope questions (44:07)
      • If the tangent makes an angle \(\theta\) with the positive \(x\)-axis, slope \(= \tan\theta\).
      • Example-style idea: \(\tan(135^\circ) = -1\) used for decreasing line.
    3. Instantaneous slope at a given time (48:17)
      • If a tangent at \(t = 5\,s\) is shown, use its angle \(\theta\) to compute slope:
        • Example-style angle: \(\tan(150^\circ) = -\tan(30^\circ) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\)
      • Then use:
        • \(ROR = -\frac{1}{\nu}\,\frac{d[\text{reactant}]}{dt}\)
        • and scale to get \(ROD\) or \(ROA\) for other species using coefficients when asked.

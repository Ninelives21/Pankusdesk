  1. Zero-order reaction: meaning (00:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General idea: reactant \(A\) converts to products.
      Rate law for zero order:
      \( r = k[A]^0 = k \)
    • Rate is independent of reactant concentration \([A]\).
    • \([A]\) decreases with time, but rate remains constant (for fixed temperature/conditions).
    • Common misconception: “\([A]\) constant” is false; only the rate stays constant.
  2. Integrated rate law derivation (05:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For \(A \rightarrow\) products and zero order:
      \( r = k[A]^0 = k \)
    • Rate in terms of reactant consumption (stoichiometric coefficient \(=1\)):
      \( r = -\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} \)
    • Equate:
      \(-\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = k\)
    • Rearrange and integrate with limits: at \(t=0,\ [A]=[A]_0\); at \(t=t,\ [A]=[A]_t\).
      \(\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} d[A] = -k \int_{0}^{t} dt\)
    • Result (integrated rate law):
      \([A]_t - [A]_0 = -kt\)
      \([A]_t = [A]_0 - kt\)
    • Meaning of symbols:
      • \([A]_0\): initial concentration
      • \([A]_t\): concentration remaining at time \(t\)
      • \(k\): rate constant (zero order)
  3. Graph: \([A]\) vs time, slope = \(-k\) (13:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • From \([A]_t = [A]_0 - kt\), compare with \(y = mx + c\):
      • \(y \equiv [A]_t\)
      • \(x \equiv t\)
      • Slope \(m = -k\)
      • Intercept \(c = [A]_0\)
    • Interpretation:
      • \([A]\) decreases linearly with time.
      • At the time when the line hits \([A]=0\), reaction is complete (for this simple case).
    • Exam cue: if \([A]\) vs \(t\) is a straight line with negative slope \(\Rightarrow\) zero-order reaction.
    • Using angle form (if graph gives an angle \(\theta\)):
      \(\tan\theta = \text{slope} = -k\)
  4. Half-life and life time (18:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Half-life \(t_{1/2}\): time for 50% completion.
      At \(t=t_{1/2}\), remaining concentration:
      \([A] = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2}\)
    • Use \([A]_t = [A]_0 - kt\):
      \(\dfrac{[A]_0}{2} = [A]_0 - k t_{1/2}\)
      \(k t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2}\)
      \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}\)
    • Life time \(t_{\text{lf}}\): time for 100% completion (in this simple \(A \to\) products case).
      At completion, \([A]=0\):
      \(0 = [A]_0 - k t_{\text{lf}}\)
      \(t_{\text{lf}} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{k}\)
    • Key dependence (zero order):
      • \(t_{1/2}\) depends on initial concentration \([A]_0\).
      • This is unlike first order, where half-life is independent of \([A]_0\).
  5. Equal concentration intervals take equal time (23:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For zero order, \([A]\) decreases linearly with time.
      So equal decreases in \([A]\) take equal time.
    • Example pattern (conceptual):
      • If \([A]\) goes \(100 \to 90\) in time \(t\), then \(90 \to 80\) also takes time \(t\), and so on.
      • If 10% completion takes time \(t\), then 80% completion takes \(8t\).
    • Quick rule: completion percentage converts to remaining \([A]\), then use linearity or \([A]_t = [A]_0 - kt\).
  6. Practice: 50% in 2 min, find 75% time (29:20)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given: zero-order \(A \to\) products, time for 50% completion \(=2\) min.
    • Set \([A]_0 = 100\) (any convenient value).
      • At 50% completion: \([A]=50\), \(t=2\) min.
      • Use \(t = \dfrac{[A]_0 - [A]}{k}\): \(2 = \dfrac{100-50}{k} = \dfrac{50}{k}\).
    • For 75% completion: remaining \([A]=25\).
      • \(t = \dfrac{100-25}{k} = \dfrac{75}{k}\).
      • Divide: \(\dfrac{t}{2} = \dfrac{75/k}{50/k} = \dfrac{75}{50} = \dfrac{3}{2}\).
      • \(t = 3\) min.
    • Unitary shortcut (concept): 50 drop (100 to 50) takes 2 min, so 25 drop (50 to 25) takes 1 min; total \(=3\) min.
      1. Question 1
        For a zero-order reaction \(A \to\) products, time for 50% completion is 2 min. Find time for 75% completion.
        Answer: 3 min.
  7. Practice: 33.33% completion (35:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given: time for 50% completion \(=2\) min.
      Find time for 33.33% completion.
    • Use \([A]_0=100\):
      • 50% completion \(\Rightarrow [A]=50\): \(2 = \dfrac{100-50}{k}=\dfrac{50}{k}\).
      • 33.33% completion \(\Rightarrow [A]=66.66\): \(t = \dfrac{100-66.66}{k}=\dfrac{33.33}{k}\).
      • Divide: \(\dfrac{t}{2}=\dfrac{33.33}{50}\Rightarrow t \approx 1.333\) min.
    • Unitary idea (approx): 50 drop takes 2 min, so 10 drop takes \(2/50 \times 10 = 0.4\) min; then scale linearly for required drop.
      1. Question 2
        For a zero-order reaction, time for 50% completion is 2 min. Find time for 33.33% completion.
        Answer: \(\approx 1.333\) min.
  8. Order from multiple time data (42:36)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Some problems give times for two different % completions and ask order.
    • Practical “hit-and-trial” method:
      • Assume an order (often start with first order in many exams), compute \(k\) from each data point.
      • If \(k\) values are same (or nearly same within experimental error), the assumed order is consistent.
      • If not, try another order (zero order, then second order, etc.).
    • In zero order, proportional time pattern may appear: if 20% takes 40 min and 40% takes 80 min, it strongly suggests zero order.
    • Using zero-order equation for each data point:
      \(k = \dfrac{[A]_0 - [A]_t}{t}\)
    • Unit of \(k\) for zero order:
      \( [k] = \text{concentration} \times \text{time}^{-1} \)
      Example forms: \(\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,s^{-1}}\) or \(\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}\,min^{-1}}\) depending on time unit used.
  9. Summary of important graphs (49:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Core graphs for zero order (for \(A \to\) products type):
      • Rate vs \([A]\): horizontal line (rate constant, independent of \([A]\)).
      • Rate vs \(t\): horizontal line (rate constant with time, under fixed conditions).
      • \([A]\) vs \(t\): straight line with slope \(-k\), intercept \([A]_0\).
    • Half-life dependence:
      \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}\)
      So plot \(t_{1/2}\) vs \([A]_0\) is a straight line through origin with slope \(\dfrac{1}{2k}\).
    • If graph gives an angle \(\theta\) for \(t_{1/2}\) vs \([A]_0\):
      \(\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{2k}\)
  10. Product formation in \(A \to B\) (52:54)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider \(A \rightarrow B\) (product \(B\)).
      Initial: \([A]_0\), \([B]_0 = 0\).
    • Let after time \(t\), \([A] = [A]_0 - x\), then \([B] = x\) (stoichiometric 1:1).
    • Use zero-order integrated law for \(A\):
      \([A] = [A]_0 - kt\)
    • Substitute \([A] = [A]_0 - x\):
      \([A]_0 - x = [A]_0 - kt \Rightarrow x = kt\)
    • Therefore, product concentration:
      \([B] = kt\)
    • Graph: \([B]\) vs \(t\) is a straight line through origin with slope \(k\).
  11. Examples of zero-order reactions (56:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Photochemical example (experimentally shows zero order in reactants):
      \( \mathrm{H_2 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{h\nu} 2HCl} \)
    • Observed form discussed: \( r \propto [\mathrm{H_2}]^0[\mathrm{Cl_2}]^0 \) (under the described photochemical conditions).
    • Rate can depend on light intensity (photon flux) rather than reactant concentrations in such cases.
    • Other contexts mentioned:
      • Enzyme-catalyzed reactions (often show saturation-type behavior leading to zero-order region).
      • Gas dissociation on solid surfaces at high pressure (surface saturation).
    • Example cited for surface process:
      \( \mathrm{2NH_3 \rightarrow N_2 + 3H_2} \)

  12. Special case: \(3A \to\) products (58:53)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Special case: reaction is \(3A \rightarrow\) products, but rate law is still zero order:
      \( r = k[A]^0 = k \)
    • Rate in terms of \(A\) with stoichiometric coefficient 3:
      \( r = -\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} \)
    • Equate:
      \(-\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = k \Rightarrow \dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -3k\)
    • Integrate with \([A]=[A]_0\) at \(t=0\):
      \([A]_t = [A]_0 - 3kt\)
    • Shortcut idea:
      • For reactant \(A\), effective constant becomes \(k_A = 3k\) (multiply by stoichiometric coefficient of \(A\)).
      • Then use the same zero-order forms with \(k \to k_A\).
    • Half-life for \(3A \to\) products:
      \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2(3k)} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{6k}\)
  13. Special case: \(\frac{5}{2}A \to\) products (01:06:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Reaction: \(\dfrac{5}{2}A \rightarrow\) products, with zero-order rate:
      \(r = k[A]^0 = k\)
    • Rate relation:
      \(r = -\dfrac{1}{(5/2)}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -\dfrac{2}{5}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt}\)
      \(-\dfrac{2}{5}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = k \Rightarrow \dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -\dfrac{5}{2}k\)
    • Integrated form:
      \([A]_t = [A]_0 - \dfrac{5}{2}kt\)
    • Half-life:
      \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2\left(\frac{5}{2}k\right)} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{5k}\)
  14. Setup: \(A + 2B \to\) products (01:08:16)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Reaction: \(A + 2B \rightarrow\) products with zero-order rate:
      \(r = k[A]^0[B]^0 = k\)
    • Reactant-specific consumption relations:
      • \(r = -\dfrac{1}{1}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -\dfrac{d[A]}{dt}\Rightarrow \dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = -k\)
      • \(r = -\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{d[B]}{dt}\Rightarrow \dfrac{d[B]}{dt} = -2k\)
    • Integrated (with \([A]=[A]_0,\ [B]=[B]_0\) at \(t=0\)):
      • \([A]_t = [A]_0 - kt\)
      • \([B]_t = [B]_0 - 2kt\)
    • Corresponding half-lives (based on each reactant halving):
      • For \(A\): \(t_{1/2}(A) = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}\)
      • For \(B\): \(t_{1/2}(B) = \dfrac{[B]_0}{2(2k)} = \dfrac{[B]_0}{4k}\)
    • Note: these are “reactant half-lives” defined by each reactant reaching half of its own initial concentration.

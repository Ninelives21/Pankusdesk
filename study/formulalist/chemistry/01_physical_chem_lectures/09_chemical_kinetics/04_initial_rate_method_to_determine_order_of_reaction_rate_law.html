  1. Initial Rate Method: objective & idea (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Use experimental initial-rate data to:
      (i) write the correct rate law, (ii) find order of reaction, (iii) find rate constant \(k\).
    • Key principle:
      Keep one concentration constant, change the other, and observe how the rate changes.
    • Initial-rate method is purely experimental:
      powers in the rate law are NOT taken from stoichiometric coefficients.
  2. Rate law recap: meaning of \(x,y,k,n\) (01:51)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a reaction like \(2A + B \to\) products, write rate law as:
      \( r = k[A]^x[B]^y \)
    • Definitions:
      • \(k\): rate constant.
      • \(x\): order w.r.t. \(A\); \(y\): order w.r.t. \(B\).
      • \(n = x+y\): overall order of reaction.
    • Orders \(x,y\) can be:
      zero, fractional, negative, or positive (not restricted to integers).
  3. Method 1 (Board): make equations & divide (04:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Assume a general rate law:
      \( r = k[A]^x[B]^y \)
    • Substitute each experiment’s initial rate and initial concentrations to create equations.
    • Divide suitable pairs of equations so that \(k\) cancels and one variable (\(x\) or \(y\)) can be isolated.
    • After finding \(x\) and \(y\), substitute back into any one equation to get \(k\).
  4. Shortcut (JEE): isolate dependency by comparison (12:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Compare two experiments where \([A]\) is constant:
      Any rate change is due only to \([B]\).
    • Compare two experiments where \([B]\) is constant:
      Any rate change is due only to \([A]\).
    • Pattern rules:
      • If doubling \([X]\) makes rate \(\times 2\), then order in \(X\) is \(1\).
      • If doubling \([X]\) makes rate \(\times 4\), then order in \(X\) is \(2\).
      • If doubling \([X]\) makes rate unchanged, then order in \(X\) is \(0\).
      • If doubling \([X]\) makes rate \(\times \frac{1}{2}\), then order in \(X\) is \(-1\).
    • After identifying powers, compute \(k\) by substituting any one experiment into:
      \( r = k[A]^x[B]^y \)
  5. Example 1: \(y=-1\), \(x=1\), overall order \(0\) (04:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given (initial data):
      • \([A]=0.1,\ [B]=0.1 \Rightarrow r = 2\times 10^{-3}\)
      • \([A]=0.1,\ [B]=0.2 \Rightarrow r = 1\times 10^{-3}\)
      • \([A]=0.2,\ [B]=0.1 \Rightarrow r = 4\times 10^{-3}\)
    • With \([A]\) constant (first vs second):
      \([B]\) doubles but rate halves \(\Rightarrow r \propto [B]^{-1}\) so \(y=-1\).
    • With \([B]\) constant (first vs third):
      \([A]\) doubles and rate doubles \(\Rightarrow r \propto [A]^1\) so \(x=1\).
    • Final rate law:
      \( r = k[A]^1[B]^{-1} \)
    • Overall order:
      \( n=x+y=1+(-1)=0 \) (zero-order overall, possible in experimental kinetics).
    • Rate constant from any row (e.g., first):
      \( 2\times 10^{-3} = k(0.1)^1(0.1)^{-1} = k \Rightarrow k = 2\times 10^{-3} \)
    • Unit of \(k\) using \( [k]=(\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1-n}\ \text{s}^{-1} \):
      since \(n=0\), \( [k] = (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1}\text{s}^{-1} = \text{mol L}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1} \).
  6. Example 2: \(x=-4\), \(y=2\) from data (16:45)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given (initial data):
      • \([A]=0.2,\ [B]=0.1 \Rightarrow r = 1\times 10^{-3}\)
      • \([A]=0.2,\ [B]=0.2 \Rightarrow r = 4\times 10^{-3}\)
      • \([A]=0.4,\ [B]=0.2 \Rightarrow r = 0.25\times 10^{-3}\)
    • With \([A]\) constant (1st vs 2nd):
      \([B]\) doubles, rate becomes \(\times 4 \Rightarrow y=2\).
    • With \([B]\) constant (2nd vs 3rd):
      \([A]\) doubles, rate becomes \(\times \frac{1}{16} \Rightarrow x=-4\) (inverse fourth power).
    • Final rate law:
      \( r = k[A]^{-4}[B]^2 \)
    • Overall order:
      \( n = -4 + 2 = -2 \) (negative overall order can occur from complex mechanisms).
    • Rate constant (using row 2):
      \( 4\times 10^{-3} = k(0.2)^{-4}(0.2)^2 = k(0.2)^{-2} \Rightarrow k = 4\times 10^{-3}(0.2)^2 = 16\times 10^{-5} \)
    • Unit of \(k\):
      \( [k]=(\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1-n}\ \text{s}^{-1} = (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1-(-2)}\text{s}^{-1} = (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{3}\text{s}^{-1} \)
  7. IIT 2004-type: order zero in \(B\) (29:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Identify cases where one reactant doubles but rate is unchanged:
      that reactant has power \(0\) in the rate law.
    • From the discussed pattern:
      • \([A]\) doubles with \([B]\) constant \(\Rightarrow\) rate doubles \(\Rightarrow\) power of \(A\) is \(1\).
      • \([B]\) doubles with \([A]\) constant \(\Rightarrow\) rate unchanged \(\Rightarrow\) power of \(B\) is \(0\).
    • Rate law form:
      \( r = k[A]^1[B]^0 = k[A] \)
    • If overall order \(n=1\), then unit of \(k\) is:
      \( [k]=(\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1-1}\text{s}^{-1} = \text{s}^{-1} \)
  8. IIT 2014 Mains: \( \frac{d[C]}{dt} \) vs ROR (35:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Reaction given:
      \( 2A + B \rightarrow C + D \)
    • Rate-of-reaction relation:
      \( \text{ROR} = -\frac{1}{2}\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -\frac{1}{1}\frac{d[B]}{dt} = +\frac{1}{1}\frac{d[C]}{dt} = +\frac{1}{1}\frac{d[D]}{dt} \)
    • For this stoichiometry, \( \text{ROR} = \frac{d[C]}{dt} \) (since coefficient of \(C\) is \(1\)).
    • Using initial-rate comparisons:
      • Holding \(A\) constant, doubling \(B\) gives no change in rate \(\Rightarrow\) order in \(B\) is \(0\).
      • Holding \(B\) constant, doubling \(A\) doubles rate \(\Rightarrow\) order in \(A\) is \(1\).
    • Therefore:
      \( \frac{d[C]}{dt} = k[A]^1[B]^0 = k[A] \)
  9. IIT 2014 Advanced: factor-of-8 on doubling (41:46)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Statement:
      Rate of disappearance of \(M\) increases by a factor of \(8\) when \([M]\) is doubled.
    • Let rate \(\propto [M]^n\). On doubling \([M]\):
      rate multiplies by \(2^n\).
    • Given factor \(8\):
      \( 2^n = 8 = 2^3 \Rightarrow n = 3 \)
    • Order with respect to \(M\) is \(3\).
  10. Wrap-up: summary + next lecture preview (46:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Initial-rate method workflow:
      1. Compare experiments to get powers (orders) \(x,y\).

      2. Write \( r = k[A]^x[B]^y \) and compute \(k\) from any row.

      3. Overall order \(n=x+y\); unit of \(k\) from \( (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1-n}\text{s}^{-1} \).

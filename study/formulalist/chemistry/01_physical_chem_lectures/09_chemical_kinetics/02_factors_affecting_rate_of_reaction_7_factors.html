  1. Overview: Factors Affecting Rate Of Reaction (00:14)
    • Two variables form the backbone of the chapter (and dominate exams):
      • Concentration
      • Temperature
    • Other factors are included for conceptual completeness and special cases.
  2. Factor 1: Nature Of Reactants (02:40)
    1. Ionic vs Covalent Reactants (03:04)
      • Ionic reactions are generally faster because ions are already present; the process is mainly mixing/association.
        • Illustrative idea: mixing \(NaCl\) and \(AgNO_3\) rapidly forms \(AgCl\) (white ppt) and \(NaNO_3\).
      • Covalent reactions tend to be slower because bonds must first break and then new bonds form.
        • Illustrative idea: \(N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3\) requires breaking existing covalent bonds before forming new ones.
    2. Homogeneous vs Heterogeneous Reactants (05:20)
      • Reactions between similar-phase/similar-type reactants are typically faster due to better interaction/collision suitability.
        • Trend emphasized: gas–gas reactions are faster than gas–liquid (heterogeneous) cases.
    3. Stability Considerations (06:33)
      • Rate of reaction is directly proportional to stability of products and inversely proportional to stability of reactants.
        • More stable products / more unstable reactants → faster conversion.
        • Highly unstable intermediates (e.g., very unstable carbocations) react rapidly to form more stable species.
      • Example : Comparing dissociation tendencies using product stability.
        • \(CH_3COOH \rightleftharpoons CH_3COO^- + H^+\) = Unstable
        • \(HCOOH \rightleftharpoons HCOO^- + H^+\) = Stable
        • The above can be explained using +I effect that is more in acetic acid , thus making it more unstable.
    4. Acid–Base Nature (10:30)
      • Strong acid–strong base reactions proceed faster than weak acid–weak base reactions.
        • Idea used: stronger electrolytes provide ions readily, enabling faster reaction.
  3. Factor 2: Physical State Of Reactants (11:35)
    • Physical state affects molecular motion and collision frequency:
      • \(\text{Gas} > \text{Liquid} > \text{Solid}\) in general reaction rate.
      • Gases have higher mobility → more frequent collisions → higher chance of reaction (collision theory basis).
  4. Factor 3: Surface Area Of Solid Reactants (13:24)
    • For solid reactants, rate increases with exposed surface:
      • \(\text{Rate of reaction} \propto \text{surface area (solid)}\)
      • Powdered form exposes more reacting sites than lumps → faster reaction/dissolution.
  5. Factor 4: Intensity Of Light (16:30)
    • Applies only to photochemical (light-sensitive) reactions:
      • Increasing light intensity increases the number of photons (not the energy per photon).
      • More photons can increase bond-breaking initiation events → faster reaction (where applicable).
      • Such reactions are often indicated by \(h\nu\) (energy of photon) written above the reaction arrow.
  6. Factor 5: Catalyst (19:55)
    • Catalyst
      • A catalyst increases the rate of reaction by providing an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy.
      • Lower activation energy increases the number of effective collisions per unit time, thereby increasing the rate of reaction.
      • The catalyst does not change the initial or final energy of reactants and products; it only lowers the energy barrier.
      • Example: In the oxidation of \(SO_2\) to \(SO_3\), the presence of a catalyst (e.g., \(NO\)) increases the reaction rate; higher catalyst concentration can lead to a higher rate.
  7. Factor 6: Temperature (23:53)
    • Temperature generally increases reaction rate:
      • Often, increasing temperature by \(10^\circ C\) can approximately double or triple the rate (temperature coefficient).
      • More detailed and accurate treatment is via Arrhenius equation (discussed later): \(k = A e^{-E_a/(RT)}\).
  8. Factor 7: Concentration Of Reactants (26:25)
    • Concentration can affect rate in multiple ways (depends on reaction/rate law):
      • Generally, increasing reactant concentration increases rate, but not always.
      • Possible dependencies include direct, square, inverse, or no dependence at all.
      • In some reactions, rate may also depend on product concentration.
      • The exact mathematical dependence is defined by the rate law (next lecture topic).

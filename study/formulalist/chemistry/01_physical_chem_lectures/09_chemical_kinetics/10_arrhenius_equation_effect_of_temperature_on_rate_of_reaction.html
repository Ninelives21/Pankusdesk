  1. Temperature effect on rate constant: overview (00:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Most reactions show higher rate when temperature increases; focus is on how the rate constant \(k\) changes with \(T\).
      \( \text{Rate} \propto k \)
    • If \(k\) increases with \(T\), then rate also increases (for fixed concentrations/conditions).
      \( k \uparrow \Rightarrow \text{Rate} \uparrow \)
  2. Approximate rule: temperature coefficient (05:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Approximate dependence used for quick estimates: for many reactions, increasing temperature by \(10^\circ\text{C}\) roughly multiplies rate (and \(k\)) by a constant factor (often about 2).
      \( \dfrac{k_{t+10^\circ C}}{k_{t^\circ C}} \approx \gamma \)
    • Temperature coefficient \(\gamma\) is defined as the ratio of rate constants at temperatures differing by \(10^\circ\text{C}\).
      \( \gamma = \dfrac{k_{t+10^\circ C}}{k_{t^\circ C}} \)
    • If \(\gamma = 3\), then \(k\) (and rate) triples every \(10^\circ\text{C}\): \(K, 3K, 9K, 27K,\dots\).
      \( k(T+10n) = k(T)\,\gamma^{n} \)
  3. Temperature coefficient example: rate at 80°C (09:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given \(\gamma = 2\) and rate at \(20^\circ\text{C}\) is \(x\); find rate at \(80^\circ\text{C}\).
      \( \Delta T = 60^\circ\text{C} \Rightarrow n = 6 \)
    • Rate multiplies by \(2\) for each \(10^\circ\text{C}\) step, so total factor \(2^6\).
      \( \text{Rate at }80^\circ\text{C} = x \cdot 2^{6} = 64x \)
  4. Arrhenius equation: exponential form and terms (12:24)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Exact temperature dependence of rate constant is given by Arrhenius equation.
      \( k = A\,e^{-E_a/(RT)} \)
    • Meaning of symbols (typical units):
      • \(k\): rate constant
      • \(A\): frequency factor / pre-exponential (Arrhenius factor)
      • \(E_a\): activation energy (J mol\(^{-1}\))
      • \(R\): gas constant
      • \(T\): absolute temperature (K)
    • Qualitative graph: \(k\) increases with \(T\) but not linearly; the rise is exponential-like.
      \( k(T) \text{ increases rapidly as } T \text{ increases} \)

  5. Collision theory link: effective collisions and activation energy (17:10)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Collision theory: reaction occurs via collisions, but not every collision gives products (not all are effective).
      \( \text{Collisions} \not\Rightarrow \text{Products (always)} \)
    • Two key requirements for an effective collision:
      1. Proper orientation (steric/orientation factor): molecules must collide in a favorable geometry for bond breaking/forming.
        \( \text{Correct orientation} \Rightarrow \text{chance of reaction increases} \)

      2. Sufficient energy to cross the energy barrier (activation energy requirement).
        \( E_{\text{collision}} \ge E_a \)
    • Activation energy \(E_a\): minimum extra energy needed (beyond reactants’ existing energy) for molecules to reach the transition state and react.
      \( E_a = E_{\text{threshold}} - E_{\text{reactants}} \)
    • Energy profile view: reactants must reach a peak (transition state) before forming products; products may be higher (endothermic) or lower (exothermic) than reactants, but barrier exists in both.
      \( \text{Barrier exists regardless of } \Delta H \text{ sign} \)

  6. Boltzmann fraction and origin of \(e^{-E_a/RT}\) (27:32)
    (Back to topic index)
    • At a given temperature, molecules have a distribution of kinetic energies; only a fraction have energy \(\ge E_a\) and can potentially react (if orientation is also favorable).
      \( \text{Fraction} = \text{molecules with } E \ge E_a \)
    • Boltzmann result (fraction crossing energy barrier):
      \( \text{Fraction} \propto e^{-E_a/(RT)} \)
    • Increasing \(T\) increases the fraction of molecules with \(E \ge E_a\), so effective collisions become more frequent, increasing \(k\) and rate.
      \( T \uparrow \Rightarrow e^{-E_a/(RT)} \uparrow \Rightarrow k \uparrow \)
    • Arrhenius equation can be seen as proportionality turned into equality by introducing \(A\):
      \( k = A\,e^{-E_a/(RT)} \)

  7. Log form and Arrhenius plot: \(\ln k\) vs \(1/T\) (33:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Taking natural log on Arrhenius equation gives a linear form in \(1/T\).
      \( \ln k = \ln A - \dfrac{E_a}{RT} \)
    • Compare with straight-line form \(y = mx + c\) using \(y = \ln k\) and \(x = 1/T\).
      \( \ln k = \left(-\dfrac{E_a}{R}\right)\left(\dfrac{1}{T}\right) + \ln A \)
    • From \(\ln k\) vs \(1/T\) plot:
      slope \(m = -E_a/R\), intercept \(c = \ln A\)

  8. Base-10 log form: slope and intercept (38:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Converting to common logarithm (base 10) form:
      \( \log k = \log A - \dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,RT} \)
    • For plot of \(\log k\) vs \(1/T\):
      \( \log k = \left(-\dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,R}\right)\left(\dfrac{1}{T}\right) + \log A \)
    • From \(\log k\) vs \(1/T\) plot:
      slope \(m = -E_a/(2.303\,R)\), intercept \(c = \log A\)
  9. Graph-based \(E_a\) and frequency factor from angle/intercept (41:48)
    (Back to topic index)
    • In a \(\log k\) vs \(1/T\) straight-line graph, slope must be taken using angle with positive \(x\)-axis; for a descending line, the relevant angle may be \(>90^\circ\).
      \( m = \tan\theta \)
    • If the angle with positive \(x\)-axis is \(120^\circ\):
      \( m = \tan 120^\circ = -\sqrt{3} \)
    • Equate slope to Arrhenius slope (base 10):
      \( -\sqrt{3} = -\dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,R} \)
    • Hence activation energy:
      \( E_a = \sqrt{3}\,(2.303)\,R \)
    • Frequency factor from intercept: if \(y\)-intercept is \(\log A\), then
      \( A = 10^{(\log A)} \)
  10. Numerical practice: find \(k\) at given \(T\) (43:39)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Use log form (base 10) with \(A\) and \(E_a\) treated as temperature-independent unless stated otherwise.
      \( \log k = \log A - \dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,RT} \)
    • Convert temperature to Kelvin before substitution.
      \( T(\text{K}) = T(^\circ\text{C}) + 273 \)
    • Example setup (values from question statement):
      \( \log k = \log(100) - \dfrac{19137}{2.303\times 8.314 \times 1000} \)
    • If needed, final step is anti-log to get \(k\).
      \( k = 10^{(\log k)} \)
  11. Mixed concepts: half-life \(\to k\) \(\to T\) (47:11)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For first-order reaction, connect half-life and rate constant.
      \( t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{k} \)
    • If \(t_{1/2}\) is given, first compute \(k\), then substitute into given \(\log k\) vs \(T\) expression to solve for \(T\).
      \( k = \dfrac{0.693}{t_{1/2}} \)
    • Example idea: if \(t_{1/2}=70\) min, then \(k\) is of order \(10^{-2}\) min\(^{-1}\) (approx), so \(\log k\) becomes a known value for solving \(T\).
      \( \log(10^{-2}) = -2 \)
    • Identify \(\log A\) from constant term in the given equation; then infer \(A\).
      \( A = 10^{(\log A)} \)
  12. Two-temperature relation: \(k_2/k_1\) formula (54:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Write Arrhenius log form at two temperatures \(T_1, T_2\) with rate constants \(k_1, k_2\) (in Kelvin).
      \( \log k_1 = \log A - \dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,R\,T_1} \)
    • \( \log k_2 = \log A - \dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,R\,T_2} \)
    • Subtract to eliminate \(\log A\) and obtain ratio form.
      \( \log\left(\dfrac{k_2}{k_1}\right) = \dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,R}\left(\dfrac{1}{T_1}-\dfrac{1}{T_2}\right) \)
    • Equivalent rearrangement (often used):
      \( \log\left(\dfrac{k_2}{k_1}\right) = \dfrac{E_a}{2.303\,R}\left(\dfrac{T_2-T_1}{T_1T_2}\right) \)
    • Use consistent ordering: if \(T_2>T_1\), then typically \(k_2>k_1\), giving a positive \(\log(k_2/k_1)\).
      \( T_2>T_1 \Rightarrow k_2>k_1 \)

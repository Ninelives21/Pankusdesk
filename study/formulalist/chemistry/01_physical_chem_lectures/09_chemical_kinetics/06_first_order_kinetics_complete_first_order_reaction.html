  1. First-order basics (00:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Consider a simple reaction: \(A \rightarrow \text{products}\).
      For a first-order reaction:
      \(r = k[A]\)
    • Rate depends directly on \([A]\). As time increases, \([A]\) decreases, so \(r\) also decreases with time.
    • Contrast (for quick identification):
      • Zero order: \(r = k\) (independent of \([A]\)).
      • First order: \(r = k[A]\) (directly proportional to \([A]\)).
  2. Differential to integrated rate law (04:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For \(A \rightarrow \text{products}\), rate can be written as:
      \(r = -\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = k[A]\)
    • Separate variables and integrate:
      \(\dfrac{d[A]}{[A]} = -k\,dt\)
    • Define concentrations:
      • At \(t=0\): \([A]=[A]_0\) (initial concentration)
      • At time \(t\): \([A]=[A]\) (remaining concentration)
    • Integrated (base-10 log) form:
      \(\log_{10}[A] = \log_{10}[A]_0 - \dfrac{k t}{2.303}\)
    • Equivalent rearranged form:
      \(\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]}\right) = \dfrac{k t}{2.303}\)
  3. Most-used numerical form for \(k\) and \(t\) (15:39)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For numericals, use:
      \(t = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]}\right)\)
    • Solve for rate constant:
      \(k = \dfrac{2.303}{t}\,\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]}\right)\)
    • Meaning of symbols:
      • \([A]_0\): initial concentration
      • \([A]\): remaining concentration at time \(t\)
      • \(k\): rate constant
    • Units of \(k\) for first order:
      \([k] = \text{time}^{-1}\) (e.g., \(\text{s}^{-1}\), \(\text{min}^{-1}\))
  4. Exponential form and concentration–time behavior (18:15)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Exponential (natural base) form:
      \([A] = [A]_0\,e^{-kt}\)
    • Physical meaning:
      • At \(t=0\): \([A]=[A]_0\)
      • As \(t \rightarrow \infty\): \([A] \rightarrow 0\) (approaches zero asymptotically)
    • Rate vs time:
      \(r = k[A] = k[A]_0 e^{-kt}\)
  5. Key plots and what they prove (11:53)
    (Back to topic index)

    • Key diagnostic graphs for first-order kinetics (for \(A \rightarrow \text{products}\)):
    • Rate vs concentration:
      \(r = k[A]\)
      Straight line through origin; slope \(=k\).
    • \(\log_{10}[A]\) vs time:
      \(\log_{10}[A] = \log_{10}[A]_0 - \dfrac{k}{2.303}t\)
      Straight line with slope \(= -\dfrac{k}{2.303}\) and intercept \(=\log_{10}[A]_0\).
    • \(\ln[A]\) vs time:
      \(\ln[A] = \ln[A]_0 - kt\)
      Straight line with slope \(=-k\) and intercept \(=\ln[A]_0\).
    • Concentration vs time:
      \([A] = [A]_0 e^{-kt}\)
      Exponential decay (not a straight line); meets the time-axis only as \(t \rightarrow \infty\).
    • Rate vs time:
      \(r = k[A]_0 e^{-kt}\)
      Exponential decrease from initial \(r_0 = k[A]_0\).
    • Identification rule (high-frequency in exams):
      • If \([A]\) vs \(t\) is a straight line \(\Rightarrow\) zero order.
      • If \(\log[A]\) vs \(t\) is a straight line \(\Rightarrow\) first order.
    • Using slope to find \(k\):
      • From \(\log_{10}[A]\) vs \(t\): \(k = -2.303 \times (\text{slope})\).
      • From \(\ln[A]\) vs \(t\): \(k = -(\text{slope})\).
  6. Half-life and lifetime (23:42)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Definition of half-life \(t_{1/2}\):
      Time in which \([A]\) becomes half of \([A]_0\), i.e., \([A]=\dfrac{[A]_0}{2}\).
    • For first order:
      \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\log_{10}2 = \dfrac{0.693}{k} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{k}\)
    • Key contrast:
      • First order: \(t_{1/2}\) is independent of \([A]_0\).
      • Zero order: \(t_{1/2}\) depends on \([A]_0\).

    • Lifetime (time for 100% completion):
      • In the ideal first-order model, \([A]=0\) occurs only at \(t \rightarrow \infty\).
      • So, theoretical lifetime is not defined (infinite); reactions approach completion asymptotically.
    • Plot idea:
      • First order: \(t_{1/2}\) vs \([A]_0\) is a horizontal line (constant \(0.693/k\)).
      • Zero order: \(t_{1/2}\) vs \([A]_0\) increases linearly.
  7. Product formation vs time (42:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Assume a 1:1 conversion:
      \(A \rightarrow B\)
    • Let initial \([A]_0\), and at time \(t\), remaining \([A]=[A]_0 - x\), so product formed \([B]=x\).
    • Using \([A] = [A]_0 e^{-kt}\):
      \([A]_0 - x = [A]_0 e^{-kt}\)
      \([B]=x = [A]_0(1 - e^{-kt})\)
    • Graph features:
      • At \(t=0\): \([B]=0\)
      • As \(t \rightarrow \infty\): \([B] \rightarrow [A]_0\)
  8. Practice questions (45:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Use (core numerical formula):
      \(k = \dfrac{2.303}{t}\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]}\right)\)
    • Practice set:
      1. Question 1
        In a first-order reaction, concentration decreases from \(800\) to \(50\) (same units) in \(2\times 10^{4}\,\text{s}\). Find \(k\). Unit of \(k\) for first order is \(\text{s}^{-1}\).

      2. Question 2
        A first-order reaction is 20% complete in 5 minutes. Find the time for 60% completion.

      3. Question 3
        \([A]\) changes from \(0.10\,\text{M}\) to \(0.025\,\text{M}\) in 40 minutes. Find the rate when \([A]=0.010\,\text{M}\).
        Hint: first find \(k\) using the given change, then use \(r=k[A]\).
  9. Time for any fraction; 75%, 99.9% relations (01:01:15)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If fraction \(f\) is completed, remaining reactant is:
      \([A] = (1-f)[A]_0\)
    • Time for fraction \(f\) completion:
      \(t_f = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{[A]_0}{(1-f)[A]_0}\right) = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{1}{1-f}\right)\)
    • Common special cases:
      • \(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{k}\)
      • \(t_{1/3} = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)\)
      • \(t_{2/3} = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\log_{10}(3)\)
      • \(t_{3/4} = \dfrac{2.303}{k}\log_{10}(4)\)
    • High-frequency results:
      • \(t_{75\%} = 2\,t_{50\%}\)
      • \(t_{99.9\%} \approx 10\,t_{50\%}\)
      • \(t_{25\%} \approx 0.415\,t_{1/2}\) (value stated; derivation not fully shown in transcript)
  10. Common examples of first-order reactions (01:05:37)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Standard examples (often asked as direct facts):
      1. Radioactive decay (nuclear chemistry/modern physics):
        Follows first-order kinetics.

      2. Growth and decay of bacteria (modeled as first order in many problems).

      3. Inversion (acid-catalyzed hydrolysis) of cane sugar (sucrose) in acidic medium:
        \( \mathrm{C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}} + \mathrm{H_2O} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{H^+}} \mathrm{C_{6}H_{12}O_{6}}(\text{glucose}) + \mathrm{C_{6}H_{12}O_{6}}(\text{fructose}) \)

      4. Acidic hydrolysis of ester (example: ethyl acetate):
        \( \mathrm{CH_3COOC_2H_5} + \mathrm{H_2O} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{H^+}} \mathrm{CH_3COOH} + \mathrm{C_2H_5OH} \)

      5. Dissociation (decomposition) of hydrogen peroxide:
        \( 2\,\mathrm{H_2O_2} \rightarrow 2\,\mathrm{H_2O} + \mathrm{O_2} \)

      6. Dissociation (decomposition) of \( \mathrm{N_2O_5} \) (commonly used as a first-order example):
        \( 2\,\mathrm{N_2O_5} \rightarrow 4\,\mathrm{NO_2} + \mathrm{O_2} \)
  11. Two quick identifying facts (01:12:37)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If a problem states:
      • Time for 75% completion is double the time for 50% completion \(\Rightarrow\) first-order behavior.
    • Useful numerical mental-log values (approx):
      • \(\log_{10}2 \approx 0.30\)
      • \(\log_{10}3 \approx 0.48\)
      • \(\log_{10}5 \approx 0.70\)
      • \(\log_{10}8 = \log_{10}(2^3) = 3\log_{10}2 \approx 0.90\) (approximation may vary)

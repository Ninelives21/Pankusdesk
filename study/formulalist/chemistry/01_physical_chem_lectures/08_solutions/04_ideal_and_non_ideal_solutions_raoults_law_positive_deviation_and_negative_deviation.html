  1. Basis of Ideal and Non-Ideal Solutions (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • An ideal solution obeys Raoult’s law at all concentrations and temperatures.
    • A non-ideal solution does not obey Raoult’s law over the full range of composition and temperature.
    • For two pure liquids of volumes \(V_1\) and \(V_2\), if the final solution volume \(V_f = V_1 + V_2\), the behaviour is ideal.
    • If \(V_f \ne V_1 + V_2\), the behaviour is non-ideal.
  2. Key Mixing Quantities and Interaction Logic (01:59)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Change in volume on mixing:
      \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} = V_f - (V_1 + V_2) \)
    • If \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} = 0 \), there is no contraction or expansion on mixing.
    • Change in enthalpy on mixing depends on:
      breaking \(A\!-\!A\) and \(B\!-\!B\) interactions, and forming \(A\!-\!B\) interactions.
    • If heat absorbed in breaking old interactions equals heat released in forming new interactions, then:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}} = 0 \)
    • Relative interaction strengths \(A\!-\!A\), \(B\!-\!B\), and \(A\!-\!B\) decide both \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} \) and \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}} \).
    • For all solutions:
      1. \( \Delta S_{\text{mix}} > 0 \)

      2. \( \Delta G_{\text{mix}} < 0 \)
    • Mixing increases randomness, so entropy increases.
    • Solution formation is spontaneous, so Gibbs free energy decreases.
  3. Ideal Solution: Conditions, Graph and Examples (14:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Conditions for an ideal solution:
      1. Raoult’s law is obeyed at all concentrations and temperatures.

      2. \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} = 0 \)

      3. \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}} = 0 \)

      4. \( A\!-\!A \approx B\!-\!B \approx A\!-\!B \)
    • For each component:
      \( P_A = P_A^0 x_A \), \( P_B = P_B^0 x_B \)
    • Total vapour pressure:
      \( P_{\text{total}} = P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B \)
    • Observed vapour pressure and Raoult-law calculation give the same value for both partial pressures and total pressure.
    • Because intermolecular attractions remain effectively unchanged on mixing, molecules neither come significantly closer nor move farther apart.
    • Hence no volume change occurs and no net heat effect appears on mixing.
    • Common examples:
      1. methanol + ethanol

      2. \(\mathrm{C_2H_5Cl + C_2H_5Br}\)

      3. \(n\)-hexane + \(n\)-heptane

      4. \(\mathrm{C_2H_4Br_2 + C_2H_4Cl_2}\)

      5. benzene + toluene
    • Typical requirement: similar polarity and nearly similar molecular size.
    • A large size mismatch can destroy ideal behaviour even when functional type appears similar.
  4. Non-Ideal Solution: General Features (27:57)
    (Back to topic index)
    • General features of a non-ideal solution:
      1. Raoult’s law is not followed over the full range.

      2. \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} \ne 0 \)

      3. \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}} \ne 0 \)

      4. \( A\!-\!A \), \( B\!-\!B \), and \( A\!-\!B \) interactions are unequal.
    • If \(A\!-\!B\) attraction becomes stronger, molecules come closer and volume decreases.
    • If \(A\!-\!B\) attraction becomes weaker, molecules move farther apart and volume increases.
    • Non-ideal solutions are of two types:
      1. negative deviation from Raoult’s law

      2. positive deviation from Raoult’s law
  5. Negative Deviation from Raoult’s Law (33:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For negative deviation:
      1. \( P_{\text{total, obs}} < P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B \)

      2. \( P_{A,\text{obs}} < P_A^0 x_A \)

      3. \( P_{B,\text{obs}} < P_B^0 x_B \)
    • Unlike interactions are stronger:
      \( A\!-\!B > A\!-\!A \) and \( A\!-\!B > B\!-\!B \)
    • Stronger attraction holds molecules more tightly in the liquid phase, so fewer molecules escape into vapour.
    • Therefore vapour pressure decreases.
    • Because molecules come closer:
      \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} < 0 \)
    • Because formation of strong \(A\!-\!B\) interactions releases more heat than is absorbed in breaking old interactions:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}} < 0 \)
    • Important examples:
      1. chloroform + acetone

      2. strong acid + water
    • Common strong acid examples:
      1. \(\mathrm{HCl + H_2O}\)

      2. \(\mathrm{HNO_3 + H_2O}\)

      3. \(\mathrm{H_2SO_4 + H_2O}\)

      4. \(\mathrm{HI + H_2O}\)
    • Rule of thumb: if a stronger specific interaction appears after mixing, negative deviation is likely.
  6. Positive Deviation from Raoult’s Law (46:11)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For positive deviation:
      1. \( P_{\text{total, obs}} > P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B \)

      2. \( P_{A,\text{obs}} > P_A^0 x_A \)

      3. \( P_{B,\text{obs}} > P_B^0 x_B \)
    • Unlike interactions are weaker:
      \( A\!-\!B < A\!-\!A \) and \( A\!-\!B < B\!-\!B \)
    • Weaker attraction makes escape into vapour easier, so vapour pressure increases.
    • Because molecules move farther apart:
      \( \Delta V_{\text{mix}} > 0 \)
    • Because heat absorbed in breaking strong old interactions exceeds heat released on forming weaker new interactions:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{mix}} > 0 \)
    • Typical examples:
      1. \(\mathrm{H_2O + C_6H_6}\) (water + benzene)

      2. \(\mathrm{H_2O + C_6H_5CH_3}\) (water + toluene)

      3. methanol + benzene

      4. ethanol + toluene
    • Rule of thumb: if a strong pre-existing interaction is weakened or disrupted on mixing, positive deviation is likely.
  7. Positive Deviation: Type 1 and Type 2 Curves (55:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Positive-deviation systems can show two graph patterns.
    • In both types:
      \( P_{\text{total}} \) remains greater than the Raoult-law prediction.
    • Type 1:
      \( P_{\text{total}} \) stays above the ideal line but does not exceed the vapour pressure of the more volatile pure component.
    • Type 2:
      \( P_{\text{total}} \) exceeds even the vapour pressure of one pure component at some intermediate composition.
    • This distinction is important for later discussion of azeotropic mixtures.

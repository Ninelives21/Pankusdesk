  1. Introduction to Non-volatile Solute Solutions (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The topic is the vapour pressure of a solution containing a non-volatile solute.
    • In such a solution, the solute does not evaporate, so vapour is produced only by the solvent.
    • This case differs from a solution containing two volatile components, where both components contribute to vapour pressure.
    • The main objective is to determine how the presence of a non-volatile solute affects the vapour pressure of the solvent.
  2. Nature of Solute and Solvent Components (01:11)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A solution has two components: solute and solvent.
    • The component present in smaller amount is called the solute, and the component present in larger amount is called the solvent.
    • Here, the solute is taken as non-volatile, so it is treated as a solid that does not form vapour under the given conditions.
    • Common non-volatile solutes used in problems include:
      • Urea: \( \mathrm{NH_2CONH_2} \), molar mass \( 60 \, \mathrm{g \, mol^{-1}} \)
      • Glucose: \( \mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6} \), molar mass \( 180 \, \mathrm{g \, mol^{-1}} \)
      • Sucrose: \( \mathrm{C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}} \), molar mass \( 342 \, \mathrm{g \, mol^{-1}} \)
    • The solvent is volatile and is generally a liquid such as water, methanol, ethanol, benzene, or toluene.
    • For notation throughout the discussion:
      • Solvent is represented by \( A \)
      • Solute is represented by \( B \)
    • Since \( B \) is non-volatile, its contribution to vapour pressure is zero.
  3. Why Vapour Pressure Decreases (04:11)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For pure volatile solvent \( A \), molecules escape into the vapour phase and establish the vapour pressure of pure solvent, written as \( P_A^0 \).
    • When a non-volatile solute \( B \) is added, some part of the liquid surface becomes occupied by solute particles.
    • Evaporation is a surface phenomenon, so fewer solvent molecules are now available at the surface for escape into the vapour phase.
    • As a result, the number of solvent vapour molecules above the solution decreases.
    • The vapour pressure of the solution is therefore less than the vapour pressure of pure solvent:
      \( P_A < P_A^0 \)
    • Because the solute is non-volatile, it does not contribute directly to the vapour pressure of the solution.
    • Hence, addition of a non-volatile solute lowers the vapour pressure of the solvent.
  4. Raoult's Law for a Non-volatile Solute (09:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a solution containing volatile solvent \( A \) and non-volatile solute \( B \), the vapour pressure of the solution depends only on the solvent.
    • According to Raoult's law, the partial vapour pressure due to a volatile component in a solution is directly proportional to the mole fraction of that component.
    • For solvent \( A \):
      \( P_A \propto \chi_A \)
    • Introducing the proportionality constant and evaluating it for pure solvent gives:
      \( P_A = P_A^0 \chi_A \)
    • Here:
      • \( P_A \) = vapour pressure of the solution
      • \( P_A^0 \) = vapour pressure of pure solvent \( A \)
      • \( \chi_A \) = mole fraction of solvent \( A \)
    • Mole fraction of solvent is:
      \( \chi_A = \dfrac{n_A}{n_A + n_B} \)
    • This expression is one of the main working formulas for problems involving non-volatile solutes.
  5. Relative Lowering in Vapour Pressure (15:13)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Since the solution contains only two components, the mole fractions satisfy:
      \( \chi_A + \chi_B = 1 \)
    • Therefore:
      \( \chi_A = 1 - \chi_B \)
    • Substituting this into Raoult's law:
      \( P_A = P_A^0 (1 - \chi_B) \)
    • Rearranging:
      \( P_A^0 - P_A = P_A^0 \chi_B \)
    • Dividing by \( P_A^0 \):
      \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B \)
    • \( P_A^0 - P_A \) is called the lowering in vapour pressure.
    • \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} \) is called the relative lowering in vapour pressure.
    • Thus, for a solution containing a non-volatile solute:
      \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B \)
    • Equivalent working formulas are:
      • \( P_A = P_A^0 \chi_A \)
      • \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B \)
  6. Colligative Property and Its Meaning (20:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Relative lowering in vapour pressure is a colligative property.
    • A colligative property depends on the number of solute particles present in the solution.
    • It does not depend on the chemical nature of the solute particles.
    • Since
      \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B = \dfrac{n_B}{n_A + n_B} \),
      this property depends on the amount of solute particles.
    • For the present discussion, only non-electrolytes are considered.
    • Electrolyte effects are not included here, because dissociation and van't Hoff factor are treated separately.
    • A common distinction to remember:
      • Vapour pressure itself is not a colligative property.
      • Lowering in vapour pressure is not taken as the colligative property in this context.
      • Relative lowering in vapour pressure is the colligative property.
    • Other standard colligative properties studied later are elevation in boiling point, depression in freezing point, and osmotic pressure.
  7. Numerical Applications of Vapour Pressure Lowering (26:38)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The following examples apply the two standard relations:
      • \( P_A = P_A^0 \chi_A \)
      • \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B \)
    • Useful mole relation:
      \( n = \dfrac{\text{mass}}{\text{molar mass}} \)
    • For a one molal solution:
      \( 1 \, \text{molal} = 1 \, \text{mole solute in} \, 1000 \, \mathrm{g} \, \text{solvent} \)
      1. Question 1
        • Given:
          \( P_A^0 = 60 \, \mathrm{mmHg} \)
        • Solution contains \( 30 \, \mathrm{g} \) urea in \( 72 \, \mathrm{g} \) water.
        • Moles of urea:
          \( n_B = \dfrac{30}{60} = 0.5 \)
        • Moles of water:
          \( n_A = \dfrac{72}{18} = 4 \)
        • Mole fraction of solute:
          \( \chi_B = \dfrac{0.5}{4.5} = \dfrac{1}{9} \)
        • Using
          \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B \),
          \( \dfrac{60 - P_A}{60} = \dfrac{1}{9} \)
        • Therefore:
          \( P_A = \dfrac{160}{3} = 53.33 \, \mathrm{mmHg} \)
        • The same result is obtained using \( P_A = P_A^0 \chi_A \).

      2. Question 2
        • Find the mass of a non-volatile solute of molar mass \( 40 \, \mathrm{g \, mol^{-1}} \) to be dissolved in \( 114 \, \mathrm{g} \) octane so that vapour pressure becomes \( 80\% \) of the original value.
        • Take original vapour pressure as \( 100 \) units and final vapour pressure as \( 80 \) units.
        • Then:
          \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \dfrac{100 - 80}{100} = \dfrac{1}{5} \)
        • Hence:
          \( \chi_B = \dfrac{1}{5} \)
        • Octane is \( \mathrm{C_8H_{18}} \) with molar mass \( 114 \, \mathrm{g \, mol^{-1}} \), so moles of octane:
          \( n_A = \dfrac{114}{114} = 1 \)
        • Using
          \( \chi_B = \dfrac{n_B}{n_A + n_B} \),
          \( \dfrac{1}{5} = \dfrac{n_B}{1 + n_B} \)
        • Solving gives:
          \( n_B = \dfrac{1}{4} \)
        • Mass of solute:
          \( \text{mass} = n_B \times M = \dfrac{1}{4} \times 40 = 10 \, \mathrm{g} \)
        • Required mass of solute \( = 10 \, \mathrm{g} \).

      3. Question 3
        • Given:
          \( P_A^0 = 12.3 \, \mathrm{kPa} \) at \( 300 \, \mathrm{K} \)
        • The solution contains a one molal non-volatile solute.
        • One molal means \( 1 \) mole solute in \( 1000 \, \mathrm{g} \) solvent.
        • Here, solvent is water, so:
          \( n_B = 1 \)
        • Moles of water:
          \( n_A = \dfrac{1000}{18} \)
        • Using the solvent mole fraction:
          \( \chi_A = \dfrac{1000/18}{1000/18 + 1} \)
        • Then:
          \( P_A = P_A^0 \chi_A = 12.3 \times \dfrac{1000/18}{1000/18 + 1} \)
        • Therefore:
          \( P_A \approx 12.08 \, \mathrm{kPa} \)
    • Final takeaway:
      • Addition of a non-volatile solute lowers vapour pressure.
      • \( P_A = P_A^0 \chi_A \)
      • \( \dfrac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = \chi_B \)
      • Relative lowering in vapour pressure is a colligative property.
      • These results are applied here only to non-electrolyte solutions.

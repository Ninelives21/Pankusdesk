  1. Colligative Properties and Particle Count (01:55)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For dilute solutions, colligative properties depend only on the number of solute particles present, not on the chemical nature of the solute.
    • The four colligative properties recalled here are:
      \( \frac{P_A^\circ - P_A}{P_A^\circ} = x_{\text{solute}} \), \( \Delta T_b = K_b m \), \( \Delta T_f = K_f m \), \( \pi = MRT \).
    • In a dilute solution, increasing the number of solute particles increases:
      \( \frac{P_A^\circ - P_A}{P_A^\circ} \), \( \Delta T_b \), \( \Delta T_f \), and \( \pi \).
    • For relative lowering of vapour pressure in a dilute solution:
      \( x_{\text{solute}} = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{n_{\text{solute}} + n_{\text{solvent}}} \approx \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{n_{\text{solvent}}} \), because \( n_{\text{solute}} \ll n_{\text{solvent}} \).
    • The central idea of the lecture is that any process that changes the actual number of particles in solution will also change the observed colligative property.
  2. Why Electrolytes and Association Change Results (05:34)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Earlier examples such as urea and glucose behave as non-electrolytes; they neither dissociate nor associate in solution.
    • Electrolytes dissociate into ions in solution. Example:
      \( \mathrm{NaCl \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^-} \)
    • If \( 1 \) mole of \( \mathrm{NaCl} \) is treated as \( 1 \) solute particle during calculation, but actually dissociates into \( 2 \) moles of particles in solution, the observed colligative property becomes larger than the calculated value.
    • Association gives the opposite effect. If solute particles combine in solution, the actual number of particles decreases, so the observed colligative property becomes smaller than the calculated value.
    • Therefore, mismatch between observed and calculated colligative properties arises because the actual particle count in solution is different from the count assumed during calculation.
  3. van't Hoff Factor \( i \): Meaning and Definition (13:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The van't Hoff factor \( i \) tells how many times the number of solute particles increases or decreases in solution.
    • It also tells how many times the observed colligative property increases or decreases relative to the calculated value.
    • Definition:
      \( i = \frac{\text{total moles of solute after dissociation/association}}{\text{total moles of solute before dissociation/association}} \)
    • If particles increase after entering solution, then \( i > 1 \).
    • If particles decrease due to association, then \( i < 1 \).
    • If the solute neither dissociates nor associates, then \( i = 1 \).
  4. Dissociation: Complete and Partial Cases (15:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For complete dissociation, \( i \) is found by simple particle counting.
    • Examples for \( 100\% \) dissociation:
      \( \mathrm{NaCl \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^-} \Rightarrow i = 2 \)
      \( \mathrm{MgCl_2 \rightarrow Mg^{2+} + 2Cl^-} \Rightarrow i = 3 \)
      \( \mathrm{Al_2(SO_4)_3 \rightarrow 2Al^{3+} + 3SO_4^{2-}} \Rightarrow i = 5 \)
      \( \mathrm{K_4[Fe(CN)_6] \rightarrow 4K^+ + [Fe(CN)_6]^{4-}} \Rightarrow i = 5 \)
    • Hence, in dissociation cases, \( i \) is always greater than \( 1 \), and the observed colligative property is greater than the calculated value.
    • For partial dissociation, let \( \alpha \) be the degree of dissociation, where \( \alpha \) is the fraction of initial solute that dissociates.
    • For \( \mathrm{NaCl} \):
      initial moles \( = 1, 0, 0 \)
      equilibrium moles \( = 1 - \alpha, \alpha, \alpha \)
      so \( i = (1 - \alpha) + \alpha + \alpha = 1 + \alpha \)
    • For \( \mathrm{MgCl_2} \):
      \( i = (1 - \alpha) + \alpha + 2\alpha = 1 + 2\alpha \)
    • For \( \mathrm{K_4[Fe(CN)_6]} \):
      \( i = (1 - \alpha) + 4\alpha + \alpha = 1 + 4\alpha \)
    • General dissociation pattern:
      if one formula unit gives \( n \) particles, then
      \( i = 1 + \alpha (n - 1) \)
      and \( \alpha = \frac{i - 1}{n - 1} \)
  5. Association: Dimerization, Trimerization, and \( \alpha \) (29:22)
    (Back to topic index)
    • In association, separate solute molecules combine to form larger units, so the number of particles decreases and \( i < 1 \).
    • A common example is dimerization of carboxylic acids such as acetic acid or benzoic acid in benzene due to hydrogen bonding.
    • For \( 100\% \) dimerization:
      \( 2 \) moles become \( 1 \) mole, so \( i = \frac{1}{2} \)
    • For \( 100\% \) trimerization:
      \( 3 \) moles become \( 1 \) mole, so \( i = \frac{1}{3} \)
    • For \( 100\% \) tetramerization:
      \( 4 \) moles become \( 1 \) mole, so \( i = \frac{1}{4} \)
    • For partial association into an \( n \)-mer, if \( \alpha \) is the degree of association, then total moles after association are:
      \( 1 - \alpha + \frac{\alpha}{n} \)
    • Hence:
      \( i = 1 - \alpha + \frac{\alpha}{n} \)
      \( i - 1 = \alpha \left( \frac{1}{n} - 1 \right) \)
      \( \alpha = \frac{i - 1}{\frac{1}{n} - 1} \)
    • In association cases, the observed colligative property is less than the calculated value because the actual number of particles is lower.
  6. Modified Colligative Property Formulae Using \( i \) (40:38)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The van't Hoff factor can be written as:
      \( i = \frac{\text{Observed colligative property}}{\text{Calculated colligative property}} \)
    • This follows because observed colligative properties depend on the actual number of solute particles present after dissociation or association.
    • The corrected formulae become:
      \( \frac{P_A^\circ - P_A}{P_A^\circ} = i x_{\text{solute}} \)
      \( \Delta T_b = i K_b m \)
      \( \Delta T_f = i K_f m \)
      \( \pi = iMRT \)
    • These formulae are valid for electrolytes, associating solutes, and non-electrolytes.
    • For solutes such as urea or sucrose that neither dissociate nor associate, take \( i = 1 \).
  7. Practice Set: Comparing Osmotic Pressure (47:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When temperature, gas constant, and molarity are all the same, osmotic pressure depends directly on \( i \) because
      \( \pi = iMRT \).
    • If concentration is not given as molarity, \( i \) alone is not sufficient; the full expression must be used.
      1. Question 1 : At a fixed temperature, compare osmotic pressure of \( 1 \, \mathrm{M} \) \( \mathrm{NaCl} \), \( 1 \, \mathrm{M} \) \( \mathrm{Na_2SO_4} \), and \( 1 \, \mathrm{M} \) \( \mathrm{Al_2(SO_4)_3} \).
        • Assuming complete dissociation:
          \( i(\mathrm{NaCl}) = 2 \), \( i(\mathrm{Na_2SO_4}) = 3 \), \( i(\mathrm{Al_2(SO_4)_3}) = 5 \)
        • Order of osmotic pressure:
          \( \mathrm{Al_2(SO_4)_3 > Na_2SO_4 > NaCl} \)

      2. Question 2 : Compare osmotic pressure of \( 1 \, \mathrm{M} \) \( \mathrm{NaCl} \), \( 1 \, \mathrm{M} \) urea, and \( 1 \, \mathrm{M} \) benzoic acid in water.
        • Using complete dissociation or association:
          \( i(\mathrm{NaCl}) = 2 \), \( i(\text{urea}) = 1 \), \( i(\text{benzoic acid}) = \frac{1}{2} \)
        • Order:
          \( \mathrm{NaCl > urea > benzoic\ acid} \)

      3. Question 3 : Compare osmotic pressure of \( 1\% \) \( \text{w/v} \) urea, \( 1\% \) \( \text{w/v} \) \( \mathrm{NaCl} \), and \( 1\% \) \( \text{w/v} \) \( \mathrm{Na_2SO_4} \) at the same temperature.
        • Here \( \pi \propto Mi \), not merely \( i \).
        • For urea:
          \( M = \frac{1/60}{0.1} = \frac{1}{6} \), so \( Mi = \frac{1}{6} \)
        • For \( \mathrm{NaCl} \):
          \( M = \frac{1/58.5}{0.1} = \frac{1}{5.85} \), so \( Mi = \frac{2}{5.85} \)
        • For \( \mathrm{Na_2SO_4} \):
          \( M = \frac{1/142}{0.1} = \frac{1}{14.2} \), so \( Mi = \frac{3}{14.2} \)
        • Order:
          \( \mathrm{NaCl > Na_2SO_4 > urea} \)
  8. Practice Set: Finding \( i \), \( \alpha \), and \( \pi \) (56:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When a colligative property is given experimentally, \( i \) is first obtained from the corrected formula, and then \( \alpha \) is determined from the relevant relation between \( i \) and \( \alpha \).
      1. Question 1 : A \( 2 \, \mathrm{m} \) \( \mathrm{NaCl} \) solution shows \( \Delta T_b = 1.88 \, \mathrm{K} \). For water, \( K_b = 0.52 \, \mathrm{K \, kg \, mol^{-1}} \). Find \( i \) and \( \alpha \).
        • Use:
          \( \Delta T_b = iK_bm \)
        • So:
          \( i = \frac{1.88}{0.52 \times 2} \approx 1.8 \)
        • For \( \mathrm{NaCl} \), \( i = 1 + \alpha \).
        • Hence:
          \( \alpha = i - 1 \approx 0.8 \)
        • Degree of dissociation \( \approx 80\% \).

      2. Question 2 : \( \mathrm{K_4[Fe(CN)_6]} \) solution of concentration \( \mathrm{M/10} \) is \( 50\% \) dissociated at \( 27^\circ\mathrm{C} \). Find osmotic pressure.
        • For \( \mathrm{K_4[Fe(CN)_6]} \):
          \( i = 1 + 4\alpha \)
        • With \( \alpha = 0.5 \):
          \( i = 1 + 4(0.5) = 3 \)
        • Now use:
          \( \pi = iMRT \)
        • Substituting:
          \( \pi = 3 \times \frac{1}{10} \times 0.0821 \times 300 \approx 7.389 \, \mathrm{atm} \)
        • Nearest option: \( 7.5 \, \mathrm{atm} \).
  9. Abnormal Molar Mass and Its Relation with \( i \) (76:08)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If the van't Hoff factor is ignored while using a colligative-property formula to calculate molar mass, the molar mass obtained is abnormal.
    • This happens because dissociation or association changes the actual number of particles in solution, so the particle count assumed in the simple formula becomes incorrect.
    • Any colligative property is inversely proportional to the molar mass of the solute, because the number of moles of solute is:
      \( n = \frac{w}{M} \)
    • Therefore:
      \( \text{Colligative property} \propto \frac{1}{M} \)
    • Since \( i = \frac{\text{Observed colligative property}}{\text{Calculated colligative property}} \), and molar mass varies inversely with colligative property:
      \( i = \frac{M_{\text{calculated}}}{M_{\text{observed}}} \)
    • Equivalently:
      \( M_{\text{observed}} = \frac{M_{\text{calculated}}}{i} \)
    • For dissociation, \( i > 1 \), so the observed molar mass is lower than the normal molar mass.
    • For association, \( i < 1 \), so the observed molar mass is higher than the normal molar mass.

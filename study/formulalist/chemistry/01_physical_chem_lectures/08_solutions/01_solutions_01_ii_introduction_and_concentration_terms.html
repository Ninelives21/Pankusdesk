  1. Chapter Scope and Important Areas (00:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The chapter begins with basic concentration terms, then moves to vapor pressure and colligative properties.
    • The four colligative properties discussed are:
      1. Relative lowering of vapor pressure

      2. Elevation in boiling point

      3. Depression in freezing point

      4. Osmotic pressure
    • These properties depend on the number of solute particles, not on the chemical nature of the solute.
    • Van't Hoff factor is used later with colligative properties.
    • Solubility of gases is treated at the end of the chapter through Henry's law.
  2. Meaning of Solution and Binary Mixtures (05:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.
    • Homogeneous means the composition and properties are uniform throughout the sample.
    • In a true solution, the components lose their separate identities and exhibit common properties of the mixture.
    • Components of a solution cannot be separated by simple mechanical methods such as filtration.
    • The chapter mainly focuses on binary solutions, that is, solutions containing two components.
  3. Solute, Solvent and State-Based Identification (09:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • In a binary solution, the component present in smaller amount is usually treated as the solute, while the component present in larger amount is treated as the solvent.
    • The roles of water and another component can interchange depending on composition. For example, in 20% ethanol in water, water is the solvent; in 80% ethanol in water, ethanol is the solvent.
    • If both components are present in equal amount, the designation may be stated explicitly in the question.
    • Another identifying rule is based on physical state: the component whose physical state is adopted by the final solution is treated as the solvent.
    • Example: sugar is solid and water is liquid, but sugar solution is liquid; therefore water is treated as the solvent.
  4. Classification of Concentration Terms (13:14)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Concentration terms can be grouped into:
      1. Volume-related terms

      2. Mass-related terms
    • Volume-related concentration terms change with temperature because volume changes with temperature.
    • Mass-related concentration terms are temperature independent because mass remains unchanged with ordinary temperature variation.
    • Volume-related terms include:
      1. Percentage by volume, or \(v/v\)

      2. Weight by volume percentage, or \(w/v\)

      3. Molarity

      4. Normality
    • Mass-related terms include:
      1. Percentage by mass, or \(w/w\)

      2. Mole fraction

      3. Molality

      4. Parts per million / parts per billion
  5. Percentage Expressions: \(w/w\), \(v/v\), \(w/v\) (17:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Weight by weight percentage:
      \( \%\,w/w = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} \times 100 \)
    • Interpretation: 5% \(w/w\) NaCl means 5 g NaCl in 100 g of solution.
    • Volume by volume percentage:
      \( \%\,v/v = \dfrac{\text{volume of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times 100 \)
    • Interpretation: 7% \(v/v\) HCl means 7 mL solute in 100 mL of solution.
    • Weight by volume percentage:
      \( \%\,w/v = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times 100 \)
    • Interpretation: 3% \(w/v\) sugar solution means 3 g sugar in 100 mL of solution.
    • Whenever density is given, conversion between mass and volume is done using:
      \( \rho = \dfrac{m}{V} \), so \( m = \rho V \) and \( V = \dfrac{m}{\rho} \)
    • For any solution:
      \( \text{mass of solution} = \text{mass of solute} + \text{mass of solvent} \)
      1. Question 1 10 g sucrose is dissolved in 100 g water. Find \(w/w\)%.
        • Mass of solution \(= 110\) g
        • \( \%\,w/w = \dfrac{10}{110}\times 100 = 9.09\% \)

      2. Question 2 10 g NaCl is present in 100 mL solution; density \(=1.2\) g mL\(^{-1}\). Find \(w/w\)%.
        • Mass of solution \(= 1.2 \times 100 = 120\) g
        • \( \%\,w/w = \dfrac{10}{120}\times 100 = 8.33\% \)

      3. Question 3 A sugar solution is 10% \(w/v\). If density \(=1.25\) g mL\(^{-1}\), find \(w/w\)%.
        • 10% \(w/v\) means 10 g solute in 100 mL solution
        • Mass of solution \(= 1.25 \times 100 = 125\) g
        • \( \%\,w/w = \dfrac{10}{125}\times 100 = 8\% \)

      4. Question 4 HNO\(_3\) solution is 5% \(w/w\). Find mass of HNO\(_3\) in 100 mL solution if density \(=1.4\) g mL\(^{-1}\).
        • Mass of solution \(= 1.4 \times 100 = 140\) g
        • \( \text{mass of solute} = \dfrac{5}{100}\times 140 = 7 \) g
  6. Mole Fraction and Trace Concentration Terms (33:46)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a component \(A\) in a mixture:
      \( x_A = \dfrac{n_A}{n_A+n_B+\cdots} \)
    • Similarly, for component \(B\):
      \( x_B = \dfrac{n_B}{n_A+n_B+\cdots} \)
    • The sum of mole fractions of all components in a mixture is always 1:
      \( x_A + x_B + x_C + \cdots = 1 \)
    • Mass data must first be converted to moles using:
      \( n = \dfrac{m}{M} \)
    • Parts per million:
      \( \text{ppm} = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} \times 10^6 \)
    • Parts per billion is the analogous much smaller concentration scale using \(10^9\).
    • ppm is useful for very dilute solutions.
      1. Question 1 40 g NaOH and 54 g water are mixed. Find mole fractions.
        • \(n(\text{NaOH}) = \dfrac{40}{40} = 1\)
        • \(n(\text{H}_2\text{O}) = \dfrac{54}{18} = 3\)
        • \(x_{\text{NaOH}}=\dfrac{1}{4}=0.25\), \(x_{\text{H}_2\text{O}}=\dfrac{3}{4}=0.75\)

      2. Question 2 A solution contains 46% \(w/w\) ethyl alcohol in water. Find mole fraction of ethyl alcohol.
        • Take 100 g solution: 46 g ethanol and 54 g water
        • \(n(\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH})=\dfrac{46}{46}=1\)
        • \(n(\text{H}_2\text{O})=\dfrac{54}{18}=3\)
        • \(x_{\text{ethanol}}=\dfrac{1}{4}=0.25\)
  7. Molarity and Its Applications (47:06)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Molarity is the number of moles of solute present in 1 litre of solution.
    • Formula:
      \( M = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution in litres}} \)
    • Using mass data:
      \( M = \dfrac{m/M_{\text{molar}}}{V(\text{L})} \)
    • Unit of molarity is mol L\(^{-1}\), commonly written as \(M\).
    • For ions formed by complete dissociation, ionic molarity is obtained by multiplying the molarity of the solute by the stoichiometric coefficient of that ion.
    • For pure water at density \(1\) g mL\(^{-1}\):
      \( M = \dfrac{1000}{18} \approx 55.55 \)
    • Terms based on molarity:
      1. Molar solution \(\Rightarrow 1\,M\)

      2. Bimolar solution \(\Rightarrow 2\,M\)

      3. Semimolar solution \(\Rightarrow \dfrac{1}{2}M\)

      4. Decimolar solution \(\Rightarrow \dfrac{1}{10}M\)

      5. Centimolar solution \(\Rightarrow \dfrac{1}{100}M\)
      1. Question 1 40 g NaOH is dissolved to make 250 mL solution. Find molarity.
        • \(n=\dfrac{40}{40}=1\)
        • \(V=0.250\) L
        • \(M=\dfrac{1}{0.250}=4\,M\)

      2. Question 2 4.9 g H\(_2\)SO\(_4\) is present in 500 cm\(^3\) solution. Find molarity.
        • \(n=\dfrac{4.9}{98}=0.05\)
        • \(V=0.5\) L
        • \(M=\dfrac{0.05}{0.5}=0.1\,M\)

      3. Question 3 Find mass of Na\(_2\)CO\(_3\) present in 100 mL of 3 M solution.
        • \(n = MV = 3 \times 0.1 = 0.3\)
        • Molar mass of Na\(_2\)CO\(_3\) \(=106\)
        • \(m = 0.3 \times 106 = 31.8\) g

      4. Question 4 Find molarity of 10% \(w/w\) aqueous H\(_2\)SO\(_4\), density \(=1.1\) g mL\(^{-1}\).
        • Take 100 g solution \(\Rightarrow\) 10 g H\(_2\)SO\(_4\)
        • \(n=\dfrac{10}{98}\)
        • \(V=\dfrac{100}{1.1}\) mL \(=\dfrac{100}{1.1\times1000}\) L
        • \(M=\dfrac{10/98}{100/(1.1\times1000)}\)

      5. Question 5 120 g urea is dissolved in 1000 g water; density of solution \(=1.12\) g mL\(^{-1}\). Find molarity.
        • Molar mass of urea \(=60\)
        • \(n=\dfrac{120}{60}=2\)
        • Mass of solution \(=120+1000=1120\) g
        • \(V=\dfrac{1120}{1.12}=1000\) mL \(=1\) L
        • \(M=2\,M\)

      6. Question 6 53 g Na\(_2\)CO\(_3\) is dissolved in 500 mL solution. Find molarity of Na\(_2\)CO\(_3\), Na\(^+\) and CO\(_3^{2-}\).
        • \(M(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3)=1\,M\)
        • \(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow 2\text{Na}^+ + \text{CO}_3^{2-}\)
        • \(M(\text{Na}^+) = 2\,M\)
        • \(M(\text{CO}_3^{2-}) = 1\,M\)
  8. Dilution and Mixing of Solutions (01:15:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • On dilution with water, the number of moles of solute remains unchanged; only volume changes.
    • Therefore, for dilution:
      \( M_1V_1 = M_2V_2 \)
    • The relation follows from \(n=MV\), with the same solute amount before and after dilution.
    • For mixing two solutions of the same solute:
      \( M = \dfrac{M_1V_1 + M_2V_2}{V_1+V_2} \)
    • This formula is obtained by adding the moles contributed by each solution.
      1. Question 1 0.2 M, 100 mL HNO\(_3\) solution is diluted with 100 mL water. Find new molarity.
        • \(M_1V_1=M_2V_2\)
        • \(0.2\times100=M_2\times200\)
        • \(M_2=0.1\,M\)

      2. Question 2 Mix 0.2 M, 100 mL HCl with 0.1 M, 300 mL HCl. Find molarity of mixture.
        • \( M = \dfrac{0.2\times100 + 0.1\times300}{100+300} \)
  9. Molality and Molarity vs Molality (01:23:58)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Molality is the number of moles of solute present in 1 kg of solvent.
    • Formula:
      \( m = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}} \)
    • Molality depends on mass of solvent, not on volume of solution.
    • Main distinctions:
      1. Molarity uses volume of solution in litres

      2. Molality uses mass of solvent in kilograms

      3. Molarity changes with temperature, molality does not
    • While solving molality problems, first isolate the solvent mass:
      \( \text{mass of solvent} = \text{mass of solution} - \text{mass of solute} \)
      1. Question 1 20 g NaOH is present in 100 g solution. Find molality.
        • \(n(\text{NaOH})=\dfrac{20}{40}=0.5\)
        • Mass of solvent \(=100-20=80\) g \(=0.08\) kg
        • \(m=\dfrac{0.5}{0.08}=6.25\)

      2. Question 2 0.85 g NH\(_3\) is dissolved in 100 mL solution; density \(=1.2\) g mL\(^{-1}\). Find molality.
        • \(n(\text{NH}_3)=\dfrac{0.85}{17}=0.05\)
        • Mass of solution \(=1.2\times100=120\) g
        • Mass of solvent \(=120-0.85=119.15\) g \(=0.11915\) kg
        • \(m=\dfrac{0.05}{0.11915}\)

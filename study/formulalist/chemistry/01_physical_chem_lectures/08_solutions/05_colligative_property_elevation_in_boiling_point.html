  1. Meaning of Colligative Property (00:45)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Colligative properties are properties of dilute solutions that depend only on the number of solute particles and not on the nature of the solute.
    • If two different non-electrolyte solutes are present in the same number of moles, their colligative effect is the same under similar conditions.
    • Hence these properties depend on concentration terms such as mole fraction or molality, not on chemical identity.
  2. Types of Colligative Properties (02:03)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The four standard colligative properties are:
      1. Relative lowering of vapour pressure
      2. Elevation in boiling point
      3. Depression in freezing point
      4. Osmotic pressure
    • For relative lowering of vapour pressure:
      \( \dfrac{P_A^{0} - P_A}{P_A^{0}} = X_{\text{solute}} \)
    • This also shows dependence on the amount of solute present.
  3. Why Boiling Point Increases (04:14)
    (Back to topic index)
    • When a non-volatile solute is added to a solvent, the solute does not contribute to vapour formation.
    • As a result, the vapour pressure of the solution becomes lower than that of the pure solvent at the same temperature.
    • Because the solution now has lower vapour pressure, a higher temperature is required for it to boil.
    • Therefore, addition of a non-volatile solute causes:
      1. Lowering of vapour pressure
      2. Increase in boiling point
  4. Boiling Point from Vapour Pressure Graph (06:30)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For both pure solvent and solution, vapour pressure increases with increase in temperature.
    • The vapour pressure curve of the solution lies below that of the pure solvent because the solution has lower vapour pressure at every temperature.
    • Boiling point is defined as the temperature at which vapour pressure becomes equal to atmospheric pressure.
    • If \(T_b^{0}\) is the boiling point of the pure solvent and \(T_b\) is the boiling point of the solution, then \(T_b > T_b^{0}\).
    • The elevation in boiling point is:
      \( \Delta T_b = T_b - T_b^{0} \)
  5. Elevation in Boiling Point and Main Formula (13:17)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Experimentally, elevation in boiling point is directly proportional to molality of the solution.
    • \( \Delta T_b \propto m \)
    • Hence:
      \( \Delta T_b = K_b m \)
    • Molality is:
      \( m = \dfrac{\text{moles of non-volatile solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}} \)
    • Since \( \Delta T_b \) depends on the number of solute particles through molality, it is a colligative property.
  6. Molal Elevation Constant \(K_b\) (15:05)
    (Back to topic index)
    • \(K_b\) is called the molal elevation constant or ebullioscopic constant.
    • \(K_b\) is a constant for a given solvent and does not depend on the solute.
    • From \( \Delta T_b = K_b m \), the unit of \(K_b\) is:
      \( \text{K kg mol}^{-1} \)
    • Definition of \(K_b\):
      It is the elevation in boiling point when the molality of the solution is \(1\), that is, when \(1\) mole of a non-volatile solute is dissolved in \(1\) kg of solvent.
  7. Example: Glucose in Water (18:40)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given:
      1. \(18\) g glucose dissolved in \(1\) kg water
      2. \(T_b^{0} = 373\) K
      3. \(K_b\) for water \(= 0.52\ \text{K kg mol}^{-1}\)
    • Molar mass of glucose \( \left( \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 \right) = 180\ \text{g mol}^{-1} \).
    • Moles of glucose:
      \( \dfrac{18}{180} = 0.1 \)
    • Molality:
      \( m = \dfrac{0.1}{1} = 0.1 \)
    • Elevation in boiling point:
      \( \Delta T_b = K_b m = 0.52 \times 0.1 = 0.052\ \text{K} \)
    • Boiling point of solution:
      \( T_b = 373 + 0.052 = 373.052\ \text{K} \)
  8. Example: Urea and Glucose Together (22:07)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given:
      1. \(6\) g urea + \(9\) g glucose dissolved in \(300\) g water
      2. \(T_b^{0} = 100^\circ \text{C}\)
      3. \(K_b\) for water \(= 0.52\ \text{K kg mol}^{-1}\)
    • For colligative properties, total number of solute particles is used. Therefore total moles of solute are added.
    • Moles of urea \( \left( \text{NH}_2\text{CONH}_2 \right) \):
      \( \dfrac{6}{60} = 0.1 \)
    • Moles of glucose:
      \( \dfrac{9}{180} = 0.05 \)
    • Total moles of solute:
      \( 0.1 + 0.05 = 0.15 \)
    • Mass of solvent \(= 300\) g \(= 0.3\) kg.
    • Molality:
      \( m = \dfrac{0.15}{0.3} = 0.5 \)
    • Elevation in boiling point:
      \( \Delta T_b = 0.52 \times 0.5 = 0.26 \)
    • Boiling point of solution:
      \( T_b = 100 + 0.26 = 100.26^\circ \text{C} \)
    • Change in temperature has the same numerical value in Kelvin and Celsius.
  9. Example: Molar Mass of Solute (28:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Given:
      1. \(3.3\) g non-volatile solute dissolved in \(125\) g benzene
      2. \(T_b^{0} = 80^\circ \text{C}\)
      3. \(T_b = 80.66^\circ \text{C}\)
      4. \(K_b\) for benzene \(= 3.28\ \text{K kg mol}^{-1}\)
    • Elevation in boiling point:
      \( \Delta T_b = 80.66 - 80 = 0.66 \)
    • Use:
      \( \Delta T_b = K_b m \)
    • Also:
      \( m = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}} = \dfrac{3.3/M}{0.125} \)
    • Substituting:
      \( 0.66 = 3.28 \times \dfrac{3.3/M}{0.125} \)
    • On solving:
      \( M \approx 131.2\ \text{g mol}^{-1} \)
    • Approximate molar mass of the solute:
      \( 130\ \text{g mol}^{-1} \)
  10. Formula for \(K_b\) When Not Given (33:21)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If \(K_b\) is not given, it may be calculated using:
      \( K_b = \dfrac{R {T_b^{0}}^2 M}{1000\,\Delta H_{\text{vap}}} \)
    • Here:
      1. \(R\) = universal gas constant
      2. \(T_b^{0}\) = boiling point of pure solvent in Kelvin
      3. \(M\) = molar mass of solvent
      4. \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\) = enthalpy of vaporization of the solvent
    • If latent heat of vaporization \(L_v\) is given per gram, then:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{vap}} = L_v \times M \)
    • For water:
      1. \(T_b^{0} = 373\ \text{K}\)
      2. \(M = 18\)
      3. If \(L_v = 540\ \text{cal g}^{-1}\), then \( \Delta H_{\text{vap}} = 540 \times 18 \)
    • With appropriate unit choice for \(R\), calculation gives approximately:
      \( K_b \approx 0.52\ \text{K kg mol}^{-1} \) for water.

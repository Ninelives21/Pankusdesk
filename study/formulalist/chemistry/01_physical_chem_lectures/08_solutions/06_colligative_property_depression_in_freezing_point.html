  1. Depression in Freezing Point: Basic Idea (00:00)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Depression in freezing point means lowering of the freezing point after adding a non-volatile solute to a solvent.
    • For a non-volatile solute:
      \( \text{boiling point increases} \)
      \( \text{freezing point decreases} \)
    • This is a colligative property, so it depends on the number of solute particles, not on the chemical nature of the solute.
    • A simple structural interpretation is that freezing requires formation of an ordered arrangement. Solute particles disturb this arrangement, so a lower temperature is needed for freezing.
    • Freezing does not always imply decrease in volume. Water is a standard counterexample because it expands on freezing.
  2. Vapour Pressure Graph and Freezing Point Definition (01:21)
    (Back to topic index)
    • At any given temperature, the vapour pressure of the solution is lower than that of the pure solvent because the non-volatile solute does not contribute to vapour formation.
    • Freezing point is defined as the temperature at which the vapour pressure of the liquid state becomes equal to the vapour pressure of the solid state.
    • For the pure solvent, the freezing point is where the liquid-solvent curve intersects the solid-solvent curve.
    • After adding a non-volatile solute, the liquid-phase vapour pressure curve shifts downward, so its intersection with the solid-solvent curve occurs at a lower temperature.
    • If \(T_f^0\) is the freezing point of the pure solvent and \(T_f\) is the freezing point of the solution, then:
      \( \Delta T_f = T_f^0 - T_f \)
    • Since \(T_f < T_f^0\), the quantity \( \Delta T_f \) is positive and represents depression in freezing point.
  3. Formula, Molality, and Cryoscopic Constant (11:02)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Experimentally,
      \( \Delta T_f \propto m \)
    • Hence,
      \( \Delta T_f = K_f m \)
    • Molality is:
      \( m = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}} \)
    • This confirms that depression in freezing point is a colligative property because it depends on the number of dissolved solute particles.
    • \(K_f\) is the molal depression constant or cryoscopic constant.
    • \(K_f\) depends only on the solvent, not on the solute.
    • Unit of \(K_f\):
      \( \text{K kg mol}^{-1} \)
    • Definition of \(K_f\): it is equal to the depression in freezing point produced when 1 mole of a non-volatile solute is dissolved in 1 kg of the solvent.
  4. Solved Questions on Depression in Freezing Point (16:41)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Standard working formula:
      \( \Delta T_f = K_f m \)
    • When required, use:
      \( m = \dfrac{w_2/M_2}{w_1(\text{kg})} \)
      where \(w_2\) is mass of solute, \(M_2\) is molar mass of solute, and \(w_1\) is mass of solvent.
    • For percentage by mass, \(5\%\) by mass means \(5\text{ g}\) solute in \(100\text{ g}\) solution.
      1. Question 1
        • Find the mass of ethanol, assumed non-volatile, to be dissolved in \(1\text{ kg}\) water so that the solution freezes at \(-2^\circ\text{C}\).
        • Given: \(T_f^0 = 0^\circ\text{C}\), \(T_f = -2^\circ\text{C}\), so \( \Delta T_f = 2 \).
        • Also, \(K_f(\text{water}) = 1.86\text{ K kg mol}^{-1}\).
        • Using \( \Delta T_f = K_f m \):
          \( 2 = 1.86 \times \dfrac{x/46}{1} \)
        • \( x \approx 49.5\text{ g} \), so about \(50\text{ g}\) ethanol is required.

      2. Question 2
        • \(50\text{ cm}^3\) ethylene glycol is dissolved in \(50\text{ g}\) water. The freezing point becomes \(-34^\circ\text{C}\). Find the density of ethylene glycol.
        • Given: \( \Delta T_f = 34 \), \(K_f = 1.86\text{ K kg mol}^{-1}\), molar mass of ethylene glycol \(= 62\text{ g mol}^{-1}\).
        • Using
          \( 34 = 1.86 \times \dfrac{x/62}{50/1000} \)
        • \( x \approx 56.66\text{ g} \)
        • Density:
          \( \rho = \dfrac{56.66}{50} \approx 1.13\text{ g cm}^{-3} \)

      3. Question 3
        • \(5\%\) cane sugar by mass has freezing point \(271\text{ K}\). Find the freezing point of \(5\%\) glucose by mass. Take freezing point of pure water as \(273.15\text{ K}\).
        • For cane sugar solution:
          \( \Delta T_f = 273.15 - 271 = 2.15\text{ K} \)
        • \(5\%\) by mass means \(5\text{ g}\) solute in \(100\text{ g}\) solution, so solvent mass \(=95\text{ g}\).
        • For equal solvent mass and same solvent, \(K_f\) cancels out. Therefore,
          \( \dfrac{\Delta T_f(\text{glucose})}{2.15} = \dfrac{5/180}{5/342} = \dfrac{342}{180} = 1.9 \)
        • \( \Delta T_f(\text{glucose}) = 2.15 \times 1.9 = 4.085\text{ K} \)
        • Freezing point of glucose solution:
          \( 273.15 - 4.085 = 269.065\text{ K} \approx 269.06\text{ K} \)
  5. When \(K_f\) Is Not Given (36:09)
    (Back to topic index)
    • If \(K_f\) is not given, it can be calculated from:
      \( K_f = \dfrac{R(T_f^0)^2 M}{1000\,\Delta H_{\text{fusion}}} \)
    • Here:
      \(R\) = universal gas constant,
      \(T_f^0\) = freezing point of pure solvent in Kelvin,
      \(M\) = molar mass of solvent,
      \( \Delta H_{\text{fusion}} \) = enthalpy of fusion of the solvent.
    • Use \(T_f^0\) only in Kelvin in this formula.
    • If latent heat of fusion is given per gram, then:
      \( \Delta H_{\text{fusion}} = (\text{latent heat of fusion}) \times (\text{molar mass}) \)
    • The energy form of \(R\) should be used here, not the gas-law value in litre-atmosphere units.

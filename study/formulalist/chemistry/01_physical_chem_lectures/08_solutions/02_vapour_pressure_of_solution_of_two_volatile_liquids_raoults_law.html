  1. Lecture Scope and Vapour Pressure Basics (00:01)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The lecture introduces vapour pressure and the vapour pressure of a solution containing two volatile liquids.
    • This topic forms the basis for later discussion of colligative properties.
    • The starting idea is that vapour originates due to evaporation of a liquid.
  2. Evaporation in Open and Closed Containers (01:12)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Evaporation occurs from the surface of a liquid, not from the bulk.
    • Surface molecules experience weaker net intermolecular attraction than bulk molecules.
    • Surface molecules are treated as having relatively higher energy, so they can escape into the vapour phase.
    • In an open container, vapours escape continuously; therefore vapour pressure is not discussed for an open vessel.
    • In a closed container, evaporation continues but the vapours cannot escape.
    • In a closed container, both evaporation and condensation occur simultaneously.
  3. Definition of Vapour Pressure and Equilibrium (08:53)
    (Back to topic index)
    • After some time in a closed container, equilibrium is established between liquid and vapour.
    • At equilibrium, the rate of evaporation becomes equal to the rate of condensation.
    • The pressure exerted by vapours above the liquid surface at equilibrium is called vapour pressure.
    • For a pure liquid \(A\), vapour pressure is denoted by \(P_A^0\).
    • Thus, \(P_A^0\) means the vapour pressure of pure liquid \(A\).
    • Vapour pressure is defined only for a closed system at liquid-vapour equilibrium.
  4. Factors Affecting Vapour Pressure (11:57)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Vapour pressure does not depend on the shape or size of the container.
    • Vapour pressure depends on the nature of the liquid.
    • Stronger intermolecular attraction leads to lower vapour pressure.
    • Hence, vapour pressure is inversely related to the strength of intermolecular forces.
    • At the same temperature, ethanol has higher vapour pressure than water because water has stronger hydrogen bonding.
    • For alcohols with increasing number of \(\text{OH}\) groups, intermolecular hydrogen bonding increases and vapour pressure decreases.
    • The trend discussed is:
      \( P_{\text{ethanol}}^0 > P_{\text{glycol}}^0 > P_{\text{glycerol}}^0 \)
    • Vapour pressure also increases with rise in temperature because evaporation becomes more extensive.
  5. Volatile and Non-Volatile Substances (18:44)
    (Back to topic index)
    • On the basis of tendency to evaporate, substances are classified as volatile and non-volatile.
    • Volatile substances have non-zero vapour pressure.
    • Non-volatile substances have negligible tendency to evaporate; in this treatment, their pure vapour pressure is taken as zero.
    • Common non-volatile examples used in numericals are urea, glucose and sucrose.
    • Common volatile examples used in the chapter are water, methanol, ethanol, benzene and toluene.
    • This classification is important for later cases: volatile + volatile and volatile + non-volatile solutions.
  6. Solutions Containing Two Volatile Liquids (24:11)
    (Back to topic index)
    • The solution considered here contains two volatile liquid components.
    • One component is treated as solvent \(A\) and the other as solute \(B\).
    • Both components are liquids; the term solute here only means the component present in smaller amount.
    • For the pure liquids, vapour pressures are denoted by \(P_A^0\) and \(P_B^0\).
    • When pure \(A\) and pure \(B\) are mixed, both components contribute to the vapour phase.
    • After mixing, the vapour pressure of each component becomes less than its vapour pressure in pure state.
  7. Raoult's Law and Partial Vapour Pressures (29:11)
    (Back to topic index)
    • For a solution of volatile liquids, the vapour pressure contributed by each component is called its partial vapour pressure.
    • Raoult's law states that the partial vapour pressure of each component is directly proportional to the mole fraction of that component in the solution.
    • Thus,
      \( P_A \propto X_A \)
    • Similarly,
      \( P_B \propto X_B \)
    • Mole fractions are:
      \( X_A = \dfrac{n_A}{n_A+n_B} \)
    • \( X_B = \dfrac{n_B}{n_A+n_B} \)
    • Also,
      \( X_A + X_B = 1 \)
    • Using the pure-liquid condition, the proportionality constants become \(P_A^0\) and \(P_B^0\).
    • Hence, Raoult's law gives:
      \( P_A = P_A^0 X_A \)
    • \( P_B = P_B^0 X_B \)
  8. Total Vapour Pressure and Core Formulae (37:56)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Total vapour pressure of the solution is the sum of the partial vapour pressures of all volatile components.
    • This follows the Dalton-type addition of partial pressures in the vapour phase.
    • Therefore,
      \( P_T = P_A + P_B \)
    • Substituting Raoult's law:
      \( P_T = P_A^0 X_A + P_B^0 X_B \)
    • At a fixed temperature, \(P_A^0\) and \(P_B^0\) are experimentally known constants for the pure liquids.
  9. Solved Examples on Partial and Total Vapour Pressure (42:47)
    (Back to topic index)
      1. Question 1 0.78 g benzene and 0.92 g toluene are mixed. Given \(P_{\text{benzene}}^0 = 400\) mmHg and \(P_{\text{toluene}}^0 = 200\) mmHg, find partial pressures and total vapour pressure.
        • Moles of benzene:
          \( n_{\text{benzene}} = \dfrac{0.78}{78} = 0.01 \)
        • Moles of toluene:
          \( n_{\text{toluene}} = \dfrac{0.92}{92} = 0.01 \)
        • Mole fractions:
          \( X_{\text{benzene}} = \dfrac{1}{2}, \quad X_{\text{toluene}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \)
        • Partial pressure of benzene:
          \( P_{\text{benzene}} = 400 \times \dfrac{1}{2} = 200 \text{ mmHg} \)
        • Partial pressure of toluene:
          \( P_{\text{toluene}} = 200 \times \dfrac{1}{2} = 100 \text{ mmHg} \)
        • Total vapour pressure:
          \( P_T = 200 + 100 = 300 \text{ mmHg} \)

      2. Question 2 Ethanol and methanol are mixed in mole ratio \(3:1\). Given \(P_{\text{ethanol}}^0 = 55\) mmHg and \(P_{\text{methanol}}^0 = 50\) mmHg, find total vapour pressure.
        • Mole fractions:
          \( X_{\text{ethanol}} = \dfrac{3}{4}, \quad X_{\text{methanol}} = \dfrac{1}{4} \)
        • Total vapour pressure:
          \( P_T = 55 \times \dfrac{3}{4} + 50 \times \dfrac{1}{4} \)
        • \( P_T = \dfrac{165 + 50}{4} = \dfrac{215}{4} = 53.75 \text{ mmHg} \)

      3. Question 3 Two data sets are given for a binary solution:
        • When moles of \(X:Y = 2:3\), total vapour pressure is 500 mmHg.
        • When moles of \(X:Y = 3:2\), total vapour pressure is 400 mmHg.
        • Use
          \( P_T = P_X^0 X_X + P_Y^0 X_Y \)
        • First case:
          \( 500 = P_X^0 \times \dfrac{2}{5} + P_Y^0 \times \dfrac{3}{5} \)
        • Second case:
          \( 400 = P_X^0 \times \dfrac{3}{5} + P_Y^0 \times \dfrac{2}{5} \)
        • Solving gives:
          \( P_X^0 = 200 \text{ mmHg}, \quad P_Y^0 = 700 \text{ mmHg} \)
  10. Mole Fraction in Vapour Phase and Graphs (56:54)
    (Back to topic index)
    • Mole fraction in liquid phase is represented by \(X\), whereas mole fraction in vapour phase is represented by \(Y\).
    • For any component in the vapour phase:
      \( Y_A = \dfrac{P_A}{P_T} \)
    • Similarly,
      \( Y_B = \dfrac{P_B}{P_T} \)
      1. Question 1 0.92 g ethanol and 0.32 g methanol are mixed. Given \(P_{\text{ethanol}}^0 = 55\) mmHg and \(P_{\text{methanol}}^0 = 50\) mmHg, find mole fractions in vapour phase.
        • Moles:
          \( n_{\text{ethanol}} = \dfrac{0.92}{46} = 0.02,\quad n_{\text{methanol}} = \dfrac{0.32}{32} = 0.01 \)
        • Liquid-phase mole fractions:
          \( X_{\text{ethanol}} = \dfrac{2}{3},\quad X_{\text{methanol}} = \dfrac{1}{3} \)
        • Partial pressures:
          \( P_{\text{ethanol}} = 55 \times \dfrac{2}{3} = 36.66 \text{ mmHg} \)
        • \( P_{\text{methanol}} = 50 \times \dfrac{1}{3} = 16.66 \text{ mmHg} \)
        • Total pressure:
          \( P_T = 36.66 + 16.66 = 53.33 \text{ mmHg} \)
        • Vapour-phase mole fractions:
          \( Y_{\text{ethanol}} = \dfrac{36.66}{53.33} \approx 0.68 \)
        • \( Y_{\text{methanol}} = \dfrac{16.66}{53.33} \approx 0.32 \)

      2. Question 2 Sketch the variation of \(P_A\), \(P_B\) and total vapour pressure with composition.
        • From Raoult's law:
          \( P_A = P_A^0 X_A \)
        • \( P_B = P_B^0 X_B \)
        • Each partial pressure varies linearly with the corresponding mole fraction.
        • Total vapour pressure is obtained by adding the two partial-pressure lines:
          \( P_T = P_A^0 X_A + P_B^0 X_B \)

      3. Question 3 If total vapour pressure is given as \( P_T = 100 + 60X_{\text{benzene}} \), identify vapour pressures of pure benzene and pure toluene.
        • Use
          \( P_T = P_{\text{benzene}}^0 X_{\text{benzene}} + P_{\text{toluene}}^0 X_{\text{toluene}} \)
        • Since
          \( X_{\text{toluene}} = 1 - X_{\text{benzene}} \)
        • Rewrite:
          \( P_T = P_{\text{benzene}}^0 X_{\text{benzene}} + P_{\text{toluene}}^0 (1 - X_{\text{benzene}}) \)
        • \( P_T = P_{\text{toluene}}^0 + (P_{\text{benzene}}^0 - P_{\text{toluene}}^0)X_{\text{benzene}} \)
        • Comparing with \( P_T = 100 + 60X_{\text{benzene}} \):
          \( P_{\text{toluene}}^0 = 100 \text{ mmHg} \)
        • \( P_{\text{benzene}}^0 - P_{\text{toluene}}^0 = 60 \Rightarrow P_{\text{benzene}}^0 = 160 \text{ mmHg} \)

    2. Sum of mass of reactants = sum of mass of products.

      A given chemical compound always contains the same elements in the exact same proportions by mass.

      When two elements combine with each other to form more than one compound, the weights of one element that combine with a fixed weight of the other are in a ratio of small whole numbers.

      When gases react, they do so in volume which bears a simple whole number ratio to one another and to the volume of products formed, if gaseous (under the same temperature and pressure conditions).

      Equal volumes of all gases, at the same temperature and pressure, have the same number of molecules.

      When two elements combine with a fixed amount of a third element, the ratio of their masses is either the same or a whole number multiple.

      \(\dfrac{\text{Molecular Weight}}{\text{Valency Factor}}\)

      Valency Factor :

      • For acids, valency Factor = basicity
        = how many \(\text{H}^+\) ions will it give in solution

        Example, basicity of \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) = 2
        \(\therefore\) for \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\), valency factor = 2

        But, bear in mind that valency factor also depends on the reaction.

        For example, in the below reaction
        \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) + \(\text{NaOH}\) \(\rightarrow\) \(\text{NaH}{SO}_4\) + \(\text{H}_2{O}\), basicity = 1

      • For bases, valency Factor = acidity
        = how many \(\text{OH}^-\) ions will it give in solution

      • For salts, valency Factor = total +ve charge on the cation (or total -ve charge on anion).
        = charge on the cation X number of atoms of the cation

        Example, total +ve charge on the cation \(\text{Al}^{3+}\) of \(\text{Al}_2{({SO}_4)}_3\) = (2+) X 3
        \(\therefore\) For \(\text{Al}_2{({SO}_4)}_3\), valency factor = 6

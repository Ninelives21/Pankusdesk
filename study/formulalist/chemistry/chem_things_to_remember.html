    1. Law of conservation of mass : Sum of mass of reactants = sum of mass of products.
    2. Law of Definite Proportions : A given chemical compound always contains the same elements in the exact same proportions by mass.
    3. Law of Multiple Proportions : When two elements combine with each other to form more than one compound, the weights of one element that combine with a fixed weight of the other are in a ratio of small whole numbers.
    4. Gay Lussac's Law : When gases react, they do so in volume which bears a simple whole number ratio to one another and to the volume of products formed, if gaseous (under the same temperature and pressure conditions).
    5. Avogadro's Hypothesis : Equal volumes of all gases, at the same temperature and pressure, have the same number of molecules.
    6. Law of Reciprocal Proportions : when two elements combine with a fixed amount of a third element, the ratio of their masses is either the same or a whole number multiple.

    1. Isoneutronic nuclides have the same number of neutrons.
    2. The speed of light (\(c\)) is \(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\).
    3. The wavelength (\(\lambda\)) is often given in nanometers (nm) and must be converted to meters (m) for calculations.
    4. The frequency (\(\nu\)) is calculated using the formula: \(\nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda}\).
    5. The wave number (\(\tilde{\nu}\)) is calculated using the formula: \(\tilde{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda}\).
    6. Radiowaves have the longest wavelengths in the electromagnetic spectrum, ranging from 1 mm to 100 km.
    7. To find the fraction of an atom that is occupied by the nucleus, compare their volumes using the formula for the volume of a sphere: \(\dfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3\).
    8. Wavelength (\(\lambda\)) can be converted from angstroms (Å) to meters (m) by multiplying by \(10^{-10}\).
    9. The energy of a photon (\(E_{\text{photon}}\)) is calculated using the formula \(E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).
    10. The photoelectric equation is \(E_{\text{photon}} = \text{Work function} + \text{Kinetic energy}\).
    11. The number of photons (\(N\)) can be calculated by dividing the total energy by the energy of one photon: \(N = \dfrac{\text{Total Energy}}{\text{Energy per photon}}\).
    12. To calculate the number of photons emitted, use the formula for the energy of a photon: \(E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\) and then calculate the total number of photons emitted based on the total energy emitted by the light source.
    13. The threshold frequency (\(\nu_0\)) for a metal in a photoelectric experiment can be calculated using the formula \(h\nu_0 = E_{\text{photon}} - \text{Kinetic energy}\).

    1. Average molar mass of an element = \(\dfrac{{n_1m_1 + n_2m_2}} {{n_1 + n_2}}\)
      • \(\text{m}_1\), \(\text{m}_2\) are the molar masses of the two isotopes of the element.
      • \(\text{n}_1\), \(\text{n}_2\) are the fractional abundances of the two isotopes.
      • Example : Average molar mass of Chlorine with isotopes \(\text{Cl}_{35}\) and \(\text{Cl}_{37}\)
        • Molar mass of \(\text{Cl}_{35}\) = 35 gm/mol
        • Molar mass of \(\text{Cl}_{37}\) = 37 gm/mol
        • Average abundance of \(\text{Cl}_{35}\) = 75 %
        • Average abundance of \(\text{Cl}_{37}\) = 25 %
        • Average molar mass of Cl = \(\dfrac{{75 * 35 + 25 * 37}} {{75 + 25}}\) = 35.5 gm/mol
    2. Weight by weight % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}} * 100 \)
    3. Volume by volume % = \(\dfrac{\text{Volume of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} * 100 \)
    4. Weight by volume % = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} * 100 \)
    5. No pf ppm = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}}\) * \(\text{10}^6\)
    6. No pf ppb = \(\dfrac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}}\) * \(\text{10}^9\)
    7. Number of moles = \(\dfrac{\text{Given mass}}{\text{Molecular mass}}\)
    8. Molarity = M = \(\dfrac{\text{Number of moles of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution in L}}\) moles/L
    9. Let a solution of molarity \(\text{M}_1\) and volume \(\text{V}_1\) be mixed with another solution (of same solute) of molarity \(\text{M}_2\) and volume \(\text{V}_2\).
      Molarity of the new mixture of solutions, M = \(\dfrac{{M_1V_1 + M_1V_2}}{V_1 + V_2}\)
    10. Molality m = \(\dfrac{\text{Number of moles of solute}}{\text{Mass of solvent in Kg}}\) moles/Kg
    11. Equivalent weight = \(\dfrac{\text{Molecular Weight}} {\text{Valency Factor}}\)
    12. Valency Factor :

      • For acids, valency Factor = basicity
        = how many \(\text{H}^+\) ions will it give in solution

        Example, basicity of \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) = 2
        \(\therefore\) for \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\), valency factor = 2

        But, bear in mind that valency factor also depends on the reaction.

        For example, in the below reaction
        \(\text{H}_2{SO}_4\) + \(\text{NaOH}\) \(\rightarrow\) \(\text{NaH}{SO}_4\) + \(\text{H}_2{O}\), basicity = 1

      • For bases, valency Factor = acidity
        = how many \(\text{OH}^-\) ions will it give in solution

        Example, acidity of \(\text{Al(OH)}_3\) = 3
        \(\therefore\) For \(\text{Al(OH)}_3\), valency factor = 3

      • For salts, valency Factor = total +ve charge on the cation (or total -ve charge on anion).
        = charge on the cation * number of atoms of the cation

        Example, total +ve charge on the cation \(\text{Al}^{3+}\) of \(\text{Al}_2{({SO}_4)}_3\) = (2+) * 3
        \(\therefore\) For \(\text{Al}_2{({SO}_4)}_3\), valency factor = 6

    13. Gram equivalent weight (GEW) = \(\dfrac{\text{Given mass}} {\text{Equivalent weight}}\)
    14. Gram equivalent = Num of moles X Valency factor
    15. Normality N = \(\dfrac{\text{Num of gm eq of solute}} {\text{Volume of solution in L}}\) \(\dfrac{\text{Gm eq}} {\text{L}}\)
    16. \(\dfrac{N}{M}\) = \(\dfrac{\text{mol mass}} {\text{eq mass}}\) = Valency factor (X)
    17. N = M * X
    18. Normality of mixtures:
      1. Acid/Acid or Base/Base
        Let a solution of normality \(\text{N}_1\) and volume \(\text{V}_1\) be mixed with another solution (acid/acid or base/base) of normality \(\text{N}_2\) and volume \(\text{V}_2\).
        Normality of the new mixture of solutions, N = \(\dfrac{{N_1V_1 + N_2V_2}}{V_1 + V_2}\)

      2. Acid + Base :
        • Let an acid solution of normality \(\text{N}_a\) and volume \(\text{V}_a\) be mixed with a basic solution of normality \(\text{N}_b\) and volume \(\text{V}_b\).
        • \(\text{N}_a{V}_a\) > \(\text{N}_b{V}_b\), resultant mixture is acidic;
          N (\(\text{H}^+\)) of the resultant mixture = \(\dfrac{{N_aV_a - N_bV_b}}{V_a + V_b}\)
        • \(\text{N}_a{V}_a\) < \(\text{N}_b{V}_b\), resultant mixture is basic;
          N (\(\text{OH}^-\)) of the resultant mixture = \(\dfrac{{ N_bV_b - N_aV_a}}{V_a + V_b}\)
        • \(\text{N}_a{V}_a\) = \(\text{N}_b{V}_b\), resultant mixture is completely neutralized.

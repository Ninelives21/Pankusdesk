Atomic Structure

    1. The mass of a single electron is approximately \(9.109 \times 10^{-31}\) kg.
    2. Avogadro's number, which is the number of particles in one mole of a substance, is approximately \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\).
    3. Isoneutronic nuclides have the same number of neutrons.
    4. The speed of light (\(c\)) is \(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\).
    5. The wavelength (\(\lambda\)) is often given in nanometers (nm) and must be converted to meters (m) for calculations.
    6. The frequency (\(\nu\)) is calculated using the formula: \(\nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda}\).
    7. The wave number (\(\tilde{\nu}\)) is calculated using the formula: \(\tilde{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda}\).
    8. Radiowaves have the longest wavelengths in the electromagnetic spectrum, ranging from 1 mm to 100 km.
    9. To find the fraction of an atom that is occupied by the nucleus, compare their volumes using the formula for the volume of a sphere: \(\dfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3\).
    10. Planck's constant (\(h\)) is \(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) JÂ·s.
    11. Wavelength (\(\lambda\)) can be converted from angstroms (\(\mathring{A}\)) to meters (m) by multiplying by \(10^{-10}\).
    12. The energy of a photon (\(E_{\text{photon}}\)) is calculated using the formula \(E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\).

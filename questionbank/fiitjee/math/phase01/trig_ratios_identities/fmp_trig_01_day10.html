Given the equation: \( 2 \sin x + 3 \cos y = 5 \)

We need to find: \( 4 \tan \left( \dfrac{x}{2} \right) + 7 \tan y \)

First, let's express \( \sin x \) and \( \cos y \) in terms of \( \tan \left( \dfrac{x}{2} \right) \) and \( \tan y \) respectively.

Using the double angle formulas: \[ \sin x = \dfrac{2 \tan \left( \dfrac{x}{2} \right)}{1 + \tan^2 \left( \dfrac{x}{2} \right)} \] \[ \cos y = \dfrac{1 - \tan^2 y}{1 + \tan^2 y} \]

We notice from the problem statement, we might need to use some standard trigonometric identities or simplifications. Let's explore the direct substitution if any values can simplify this equation.

If \( \sin x = 1 \) and \( \cos y = 1 \) (assuming special angles), this makes: \[ 2 \sin x = 2 \cdot 1 = 2 \] \[ 3 \cos y = 3 \cdot 1 = 3 \] \[ 2 + 3 = 5 \]

Thus, \[ x = \dfrac{\pi}{2} \Rightarrow \tan \left( \dfrac{\pi}{4} \right) = 1 \] \[ y = 0 \Rightarrow \tan (0) = 0 \]

Substitute back to the requirement: \[ 4 \cdot 1 + 7 \cdot 0 = 4 \]

Therefore, the answer is: \( 4 \)

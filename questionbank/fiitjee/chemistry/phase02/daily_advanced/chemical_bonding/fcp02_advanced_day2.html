Explanation:
  • Statement-1: Polarization power of cations decreases down the group for alkali metals. This is true because, as you move down the group in the periodic table, the size of the cations increases. Larger cations have lower charge density, which means their ability to polarize (or distort) the electron cloud of nearby anions is reduced. Therefore, the polarization power decreases down the group.
  • Statement-2: Alkali metals show a decreasing ionization energy trend from Li > Na > K > Rb > Cs. This is also true. Ionization energy decreases down the group because the outermost electrons are further from the nucleus and are more easily removed due to the increased atomic radius and electron shielding effect.
  • However, Statement-2 does not directly explain Statement-1. The decrease in polarization power down the group is primarily due to the increase in cation size, not the trend in ionization energy. Therefore, while both statements are true, Statement-2 is not the correct explanation for Statement-1.

Answer:

  • (a) CuCl > NaCl:
    CuCl has a higher covalent character than NaCl.
    Reasoning: Cu²⁺ is smaller and has a higher charge density compared to Na⁺, which increases the covalent character in CuCl. The chloride ion (Cl⁻) is the same in both cases, so the difference in covalent character is due to the cation.
  • (b) Na₃N > Na₂O > NaF:
    Na₃N has the highest covalent character followed by Na₂O and then NaF.
    Reasoning: Nitride ion (N³⁻) has the largest size and highest polarizability, leading to more covalent character. Oxide ion (O²⁻) is smaller and less polarizable than nitride, so it has less covalent character than Na₃N. Fluoride ion (F⁻) is the smallest and least polarizable, resulting in the least covalent character.
  • (c) NaI > NaBr > NaCl > NaF:
    NaI has the highest covalent character, followed by NaBr, NaCl, and NaF.
    Reasoning: Iodide ion (I⁻) is the largest and most polarizable, leading to the highest covalent character. As you move up the halogens (from I⁻ to F⁻), the size decreases, and polarizability decreases, reducing the covalent character.

The nature of bonding in SnCl₂ and SnCl₄ can be explained by Fajan's Rule:

Fajan's Rule: More the charge on the cation, more is the covalent character of the compound. SnCl₄ is more covalent in nature because the Sn⁴⁺ ion has a higher charge compared to the Sn²⁺ ion in SnCl₂.

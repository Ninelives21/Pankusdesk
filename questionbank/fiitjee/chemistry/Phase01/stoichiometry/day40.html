To determine how many grams of NaOH are needed to completely neutralize the HCl produced when 1 gram of H2 gas reacts with 2 grams of Cl2 gas, we follow these steps:

1. First, write the balanced chemical equation:

H2 + Cl2 → 2HCl

2. Then, calculate the moles of H2 and Cl2:

- Molar mass of H2: 2 g/mol
- Molar mass of Cl2: 71 g/mol

Given:
- Mass of H2 = 1 g
- Mass of Cl2 = 2 g

Moles of H2 = 1 g / 2 g/mol = 0.5 mol

Moles of Cl2 = 2 g / 71 g/mol ≈ 0.028 mol

Since Cl2 is the limiting reactant:

Moles of HCl produced = 0.028 mol × 2 = 0.056 mol

The neutralization reaction is:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O

The moles of NaOH needed to neutralize 0.056 moles of HCl are also 0.056 moles.

3. Calculate the mass of NaOH required:

- Molar mass of NaOH: 40 g/mol

Mass of NaOH = 0.056 mol × 40 g/mol = 2.24 g

Therefore, 2.24 grams of NaOH are needed to completely neutralize the HCl produced.

When a mixture of 10 moles of SO2 and 15 moles of O2 was passed over a catalyst, 8 moles of SO3 were formed. To find out how many moles of SO2 and O2 did not enter into combination, follow these steps:

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

2SO2 + O2 → 2SO3

2. Determine the moles of reactants used:

- Moles of SO2 used to form 8 moles of SO3:
Moles of SO2 used = 8 moles

- Moles of O2 used to form 8 moles of SO3:
Moles of O2 used = 4 moles

3. Calculate the moles of reactants left:

- Moles of SO2 left = 10 moles - 8 moles = 2 moles

- Moles of O2 left = 15 moles - 4 moles = 11 moles

Therefore,

- Moles of SO2 that did not enter into combination: 2 moles
- Moles of O2 that did not enter into combination: 11 moles

To find the composition of products when 1 litre of CO2 is passed over hot coke and the volume becomes 1.4 litres, follow these steps:

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

C(s) + CO2(g) → 2CO(g)

- Initial volume of CO2 = 1 litre
- Final volume of gas mixture = 1.4 litres

- Change in volume = Final volume - Initial volume
- Change in volume = 1.4 litres - 1 litre = 0.4 litres

4. Using the balanced equation, determine the moles of CO2 and CO:

- 1 mole of CO2 produces 2 moles of CO
- Therefore, 1 litre of CO2 produces 2 litres of CO at STP

5. Calculate the composition of the gas mixture:

- Initial CO2 volume = 1 litre
- CO produced = 0.4 litres

- CO2 = 0.6 litres

Sodium carbonate of 92% purity is used in the reaction: Na2CO3 + CaCl2 → CaCO3 + 2NaCl. To determine how many grams of Na2CO3 would yield 1 gram of CaCO3, follow these steps:

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

Na2CO3 + CaCl2 → CaCO3 + 2NaCl

2. Determine the molar masses of the relevant compounds:

- Molar mass of Na2CO3: 106 g/mol
- Molar mass of CaCO3: 100 g/mol

3. Calculate the moles of CaCO3 for 1 gram:

Moles of CaCO3 = 1 g / 100 g/mol = 0.01 mol

4. Using stoichiometry, determine that 0.01 moles of Na2CO3 are required.

5. Calculate the mass of Na2CO3 required:

Mass of Na2CO3 = 0.01 mol × 106 g/mol = 1.06 g

6. Adjust for purity, since the sodium carbonate is 92% pure, the actual amount needed is:

Mass of Na2CO3 required = 1.06 g / 0.92 ≈ 1.152 g

Therefore, approximately 1.152 grams of Na2CO3 (92% pure) are needed to yield 1 gram of CaCO3.

A commercial sample of H2SO4 (96% of H2SO4, density = 1.81 g/cm³) was treated with 20.8 g of BaCl2 solution. To calculate the volume of acid used to react completely with BaCl2 to form BaSO4 precipitate, follow these steps:

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

BaCl2 + H2SO4 → BaSO4 + 2HCl

2. Determine the molar masses of the relevant compounds:

- Molar mass of BaCl2: 208 g/mol
- Molar mass of H2SO4: 98 g/mol

3. Calculate the moles of BaCl2 used:

Moles of BaCl2 = 20.8 g / 208 g/mol = 0.1 mol

4. Determine the moles of H2SO4 required:

Moles of H2SO4 required = 0.1 mol

5. Calculate the mass of H2SO4 required:

Mass of H2SO4 = 0.1 mol × 98 g/mol = 9.8 g

6. Adjust for purity (96% H2SO4):

Mass of commercial H2SO4 solution required = 9.8 g / 0.96 = 10.208 g

7. Calculate the volume of H2SO4 solution:

Volume = Mass / Density = 10.208 g / 1.81 g/cm³ ≈ 5.64 cm³

Therefore, approximately 5.64 cm³ of the commercial H2SO4 solution is required to react completely with 20.8 g of BaCl2.

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

AgNO3 + KCl → AgCl + KNO3

2. Calculate the moles of reactants:

When you have a solution, the number of moles of a solute (the substance being dissolved) can be calculated using the formula: Moles=Molarity×Volume

Moles of AgNO3 = Molarity × Volume = 0.1 M × 0.5 L = 0.05 moles
Moles of KCl = Molarity × Volume = 0.1 M × 0.5 L = 0.05 moles

3. Determine the reaction products:

Moles of KNO3 formed = 0.05 moles

4. Calculate the total volume of the mixture:

Total Volume = 500 mL + 500 mL = 1000 mL = 1 L

5. Determine the concentration of nitrate in the resulting mixture:

Concentration of Nitrate = Moles of NO3- / Total Volume = 0.05 moles / 1 L = 0.05 M

0.7 g of Na2CO3·nH2O is dissolved in 100 mL. 20 mL of this solution required 19.8 mL of 0.1 M HCl. To determine the value of n, follow these steps:

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

Na2CO3 + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2

2. Calculate the moles of HCl used:

- Molarity of HCl = 0.1 M
- Volume of HCl = 19.8 mL = 0.0198 L
- Moles of HCl = Molarity × Volume = 0.1 M × 0.0198 L = 0.00198 moles

3. Determine the moles of Na2CO3 in 20 mL solution:

- Moles of Na2CO3 = 0.00198 moles of HCl / 2 = 0.00099 moles

4. Calculate the moles of Na2CO3·nH2O in 100 mL solution:

- Moles of Na2CO3 in 100 mL = 0.00099 moles × 5 = 0.00495 moles

5. Calculate the molar mass of Na2CO3·nH2O:

- Given mass = 0.7 g
- Molar Mass = Given Mass / Moles = 0.7 g / 0.00495 moles = 141.41 g/mol

6. Determine the value of n:

- Molar mass of Na2CO3 = 106 g/mol
- Molar mass of H2O = 18 g/mol
- 141.41 = 106 + 18n
- 18n = 35.41
- n = 35.41 / 18 ≈ 2

Therefore, the value of n is 2.

If 0.5 mol of BaCl2 is mixed with 0.2 mol of Na3PO4, the maximum number of moles of Ba3(PO4)2 that can be formed can be determined as follows:

1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

3BaCl2 + 2Na3PO4 → Ba3(PO4)2 + 6NaCl

2. Determine the limiting reactant:

- Moles of BaCl2 = 0.5 mol
- Moles of Na3PO4 = 0.2 mol

3. Calculate the reactant ratios:

- Ratio for BaCl2 = 0.5 mol / 3 ≈ 0.167
- Ratio for Na3PO4 = 0.2 mol / 2 = 0.1

Since the ratio for Na3PO4 is smaller, Na3PO4 is the limiting reactant.

4. Determine the moles of Ba3(PO4)2 formed:

- From the balanced equation, 2 moles of Na3PO4 produce 1 mole of Ba3(PO4)2
- Moles of Ba3(PO4)2 formed = 0.2 mol / 2 = 0.1 mol

Therefore, the maximum number of moles of Ba3(PO4)2 that can be formed is 0.1 mol.

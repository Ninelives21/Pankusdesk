1. Answer for Question 1

    To determine how many grams of NaOH are needed to completely neutralize the HCl produced when 1 gram of H2 gas reacts with 2 grams of Cl2 gas, we follow these steps:

    1. First, write the balanced chemical equation:

    H2 + Cl2 → 2HCl

    2. Then, calculate the moles of H2 and Cl2:

    - Molar mass of H2: 2 g/mol
    - Molar mass of Cl2: 71 g/mol

    Given:
    - Mass of H2 = 1 g
    - Mass of Cl2 = 2 g

    Moles of H2 = 1 g / 2 g/mol = 0.5 mol

    Moles of Cl2 = 2 g / 71 g/mol ≈ 0.028 mol

    Since Cl2 is the limiting reactant:

    Moles of HCl produced = 0.028 mol × 2 = 0.056 mol

    The neutralization reaction is:

    HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O

    The moles of NaOH needed to neutralize 0.056 moles of HCl are also 0.056 moles.

    3. Calculate the mass of NaOH required:

    - Molar mass of NaOH: 40 g/mol

    Mass of NaOH = 0.056 mol × 40 g/mol = 2.24 g

    Therefore, 2.24 grams of NaOH are needed to completely neutralize the HCl produced.

    - Moles of SO2 that did not enter into combination: 2 moles
    - Moles of O2 that did not enter into combination: 11 moles

  3. Answer for Question 3

    To determine how many grams of Na2CO3 (sodium carbonate) are needed to yield 1 gram of CaCO3 (calcium carbonate), we follow these steps:
    1. First, write the balanced chemical equation:

    Na2CO3 + CaCl2 → CaCO3 + 2NaCl

    2. Then, determine the molar masses of the relevant compounds:

    - Molar mass of Na2CO3: 106 g/mol
    - Molar mass of CaCO3: 100 g/mol

    3. Calculate the moles of CaCO3 for 1 gram:

    Moles of CaCO3 = 1 g / 100 g/mol = 0.01 mol

    4. Using stoichiometry, determine that 0.01 moles of Na2CO3 are required.

    5. Calculate the mass of Na2CO3 required:

    Mass of Na2CO3 = 0.01 mol × 106 g/mol = 1.06 g

    6. Adjust for purity, since the sodium carbonate is 92% pure, the actual amount needed is:

    Mass of Na2CO3 required = 1.06 g / 0.92 ≈ 1.152 g

    Therefore, approximately 1.152 grams of Na2CO3 (92% pure) are needed to yield 1 gram of CaCO3.

  7. Answer for Question 7

    0.7 g of Na2CO3·nH2O is dissolved in 100 mL. 20 mL of this solution required 19.8 mL of 0.1 M HCl. To determine the value of n, follow these steps:

    1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

    Na2CO3 + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2

    2. Calculate the moles of HCl used:

    - Molarity of HCl = 0.1 M
    - Volume of HCl = 19.8 mL = 0.0198 L
    - Moles of HCl = Molarity × Volume = 0.1 M × 0.0198 L = 0.00198 moles

    3. Determine the moles of Na2CO3 in 20 mL solution:

    - Moles of Na2CO3 = 0.00198 moles of HCl / 2 = 0.00099 moles

    4. Calculate the moles of Na2CO3·nH2O in 100 mL solution:

    - Moles of Na2CO3 in 100 mL = 0.00099 moles × 5 = 0.00495 moles

    5. Calculate the molar mass of Na2CO3·nH2O:

    - Given mass = 0.7 g
    - Molar Mass = Given Mass / Moles = 0.7 g / 0.00495 moles = 141.41 g/mol

    6. Determine the value of n:

    - Molar mass of Na2CO3 = 106 g/mol
    - Molar mass of H2O = 18 g/mol
    - 141.41 = 106 + 18n
    - 18n = 35.41
    - n = 35.41 / 18 ≈ 2

    Therefore, the value of n is 2.

    Ans = B

  9. Answer for Question 9

    To prepare a solution that is 0.2 M NaCl, starting with 100 mL of 0.1 M NaCl solution, the correct options are:

    1. Calculate the initial moles of NaCl:

    - Molarity (M) = 0.1 M
    - Volume (V) = 100 mL = 0.1 L
    - Moles of NaCl = Molarity × Volume = 0.1 M × 0.1 L = 0.01 moles

    2. Determine the final volume needed for 0.2 M solution:

    - Desired Molarity (M) = 0.2 M
    - Initial moles of NaCl = 0.01 moles
    - Final Volume = Initial Moles of NaCl / Desired Molarity = 0.01 moles / 0.2 M = 0.05 L = 50 mL
    Therefore, to make the solution 0.2 M, the final volume needs to be 50 mL. Since the initial volume is 100 mL, 50 mL of water should be evaporated.

    3. Calculate the additional moles of NaCl needed:

    - Desired Molarity (M) = 0.2 M
    - Initial Volume (V) = 0.1 L
    - Desired Moles of NaCl for 0.2 M = 0.2 M × 0.1 L = 0.02 moles
    - Additional moles of NaCl needed = 0.02 moles - 0.01 moles = 0.01 moles

    4. Convert moles of NaCl to grams:

    - Molar mass of NaCl = 58.5 g/mol
    - Mass of NaCl = Moles × Molar mass = 0.01 moles × 58.5 g/mol = 0.585 g

    5. Verify the given options:

    - (A) add 0.585 g NaCl: Correct
    - (B) add 1.17 g NaCl: Incorrect
    - (C) add 0.019 mol NaCl: Incorrect
    - (D) evaporate 50 mL of water: Correct

    Therefore, the correct options are (A) and (D).

  10. Answer for Question 10

    The reaction 2C + O2 → 2CO is carried out by taking 24 g of carbon and 96 g of O2. To determine which of the given options are correct, follow these steps:

    1. Write the balanced chemical equation:

    2C + O2 → 2CO

    2. Calculate the moles of reactants:

    - Molar mass of carbon (C) = 12 g/mol
    - Molar mass of oxygen (O2) = 32 g/mol
    - Moles of carbon (C) = 24 g / 12 g/mol = 2 moles
    - Moles of oxygen (O2) = 96 g / 32 g/mol = 3 moles

    3. Determine the limiting reactant:

    - According to the balanced equation, 2 moles of carbon react with 1 mole of O2.
    - Therefore, 2 moles of carbon would require 1 mole of O2.
    - Given 3 moles of O2, oxygen is in excess.

    4. Calculate the amount of CO formed:

    - Since 2 moles of carbon produce 2 moles of CO:
    - Moles of CO formed = 2 moles
    - Molar mass of CO = 28 g/mol
    - Mass of CO formed = 2 moles × 28 g/mol = 56 g

    5. Calculate the amount of O2 left:

    - 1 mole of O2 is needed for the reaction:
    - Moles of O2 left = 3 moles - 1 mole = 2 moles

    6. Calculate the amount of carbon needed to use O2 completely:

    - To use 3 moles of O2 completely:
    - Moles of C required = 6 moles
    - Mass of C required = 6 moles × 12 g/mol = 72 g

    Therefore, the correct options are:

    - (A) O2 is left in excess
    - (B) 56 g of CO is formed
    - (C) 2 moles of O2 is left
    - (D) To use O2 completely, 72 g carbon is needed

    Ans = A, B, C, D

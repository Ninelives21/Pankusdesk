To calculate the critical frequency (\(\nu_0\)) of the metal, we use the photoelectric equation:

1. The photoelectric equation is given by:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = h\nu \]

where \( E_{\text{photon}} \) is the energy of the incident photon, \( h \) is Planck's constant, and \( \nu \) is the frequency of the incident light.

2. The kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons is given by:

\[ \text{Kinetic energy} = E_{\text{photon}} - h\nu_0 \]

3. For the first frequency \( \nu_1 = 4.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1} \), the kinetic energy is:

\[ \text{K.E.}_1 = h\nu_1 - h\nu_0 \]

4. For the second frequency \( \nu_2 = 2.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1} \), the kinetic energy is:

\[ \text{K.E.}_2 = h\nu_2 - h\nu_0 \]

5. According to the problem, the kinetic energy for the first frequency is three times the kinetic energy for the second frequency:

\[ \text{K.E.}_1 = 3 \times \text{K.E.}_2 \]

6. Substituting the kinetic energy equations, we get:

\[ h\nu_1 - h\nu_0 = 3(h\nu_2 - h\nu_0) \]

7. Simplifying, we get:

\[ \nu_1 - \nu_0 = 3(\nu_2 - \nu_0) \]

\[ \nu_1 - \nu_0 = 3\nu_2 - 3\nu_0 \]

\[ \nu_1 = 3\nu_2 - 2\nu_0 \]

8. Solving for \( \nu_0 \), we get:

\[ 2\nu_0 = 3\nu_2 - \nu_1 \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{3\nu_2 - \nu_1}{2} \]

9. Substituting the given frequencies, we get:

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{3 \times 2.0 \times 10^{16} - 4.0 \times 10^{16}}{2} \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{6.0 \times 10^{16} - 4.0 \times 10^{16}}{2} \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{2.0 \times 10^{16}}{2} \]

\[ \nu_0 = 1.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1} \]

Therefore, the critical frequency (\(\nu_0\)) of the metal is \(1.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1}\).

To solve this problem, we use the concept of the electrostatic potential energy of the \(\alpha\)-particle in the electric field of the copper nucleus.

The electrostatic potential energy \(U\) at a distance \(r\) from the nucleus is given by:

\[ U = \dfrac{k \cdot Q_1 \cdot Q_2}{r} \]

where:

\(k\) is the Coulomb's constant \(8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{Nm}^2/\text{C}^2\)

\(Q_1\) is the charge of the \(\alpha\)-particle (which is \(2e\), where \(e\) is the elementary charge)

\(Q_2\) is the charge of the copper nucleus (which is \(29e\))

\(r\) is the distance from the nucleus \(10^{-13} \, \text{m}\)

The potential energy is converted into kinetic energy as the \(\alpha\)-particle approaches the nucleus.

The kinetic energy \(K\) of the \(\alpha\)-particle is given by:

\[ K = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

where:

\(m\) is the mass of the \(\alpha\)-particle

\(v\) is the velocity of the \(\alpha\)-particle

Equating the potential energy and kinetic energy, we get:

\[ \dfrac{k \cdot Q_1 \cdot Q_2}{r} = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Substituting the values, we get:

\[ \dfrac{8.99 \times 10^9 \times 2 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 29 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}{10^{-13}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \times 6.68 \times 10^{-27} \times v^2 \]

\[ v^2 = \dfrac{8.99 \times 10^9 \times 2 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 29 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}{10^{-13} \times 0.5 \times 6.68 \times 10^{-27}} \]

\[ v^2 = \dfrac{1.326 \times 10^{-18}}{3.34 \times 10^{-14}} \]

\[ v = \sqrt{3.97 \times 10^{6}} \, \text{m/s} \]

Therefore, the velocity of the \(\alpha\)-particle is approximately \(6.34 \times 10^{6} \, \text{m/s}\).

Ans = \(6.34 \times 10^{6} \, \text{m/s}\)

To calculate the velocity of the ejected electron, follow these steps:

1. Calculate the energy of the incident photon:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Where \( h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s} \) and \( c = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s} \).

Substitute the values:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{200 \times 10^{-9}} = 9.939 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

2. Convert the work function to joules:

\[ \phi = 5 \, \text{eV} = 5 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} = 8 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

3. Calculate the kinetic energy of the ejected electron:

\[ E_{\text{kinetic}} = E_{\text{photon}} - \phi \]

\[ E_{\text{kinetic}} = 9.939 \times 10^{-19} - 8 \times 10^{-19} = 1.939 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

4. Calculate the velocity of the ejected electron:

\[ E_{\text{kinetic}} = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Where \( m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg} \).

Rearranging to solve for \( v \):

\[ v = \sqrt{\dfrac{2E_{\text{kinetic}}}{m}} \]

\[ v = \sqrt{\dfrac{2 \times 1.939 \times 10^{-19}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}} = \sqrt{4.262 \times 10^{11}} = 6.52 \times 10^5 \, \text{m/s} \]

Therefore, the velocity of the ejected electron is approximately \(6.52 \times 10^5 \, \text{m/s}\).

Ans = \(6.52 \times 10^5 \, \text{m/s}\)

To find the fraction of the radiant energy converted into kinetic energy, we use the following steps:

1. Calculate the energy of the incident photon:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Substitute \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s}\), \(c = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\), and \(\lambda = 253.7 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m}\):

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{253.7 \times 10^{-9}} \]

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = 7.83 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

2. Convert the dissociation energy from kJ/mol to J/photon:

\[ E_{\text{dissociation}} = 430.53 \, \text{kJ/mol} \times \dfrac{1000 \, \text{J}}{1 \, \text{kJ}} \times \dfrac{1 \, \text{mol}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{molecules}} \]

\[ E_{\text{dissociation}} = 7.15 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

3. The remaining energy is converted into kinetic energy:

\[ E_{\text{kinetic}} = E_{\text{photon}} - E_{\text{dissociation}} \]

\[ E_{\text{kinetic}} = 7.83 \times 10^{-19} - 7.15 \times 10^{-19} = 0.68 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

4. The fraction of the radiant energy converted into kinetic energy is:

\[ \text{Fraction} = \dfrac{E_{\text{kinetic}}}{E_{\text{photon}}} \times 100 \%\]

\[ \text{Fraction} = \dfrac{0.68 \times 10^{-19}}{7.83 \times 10^{-19}} \times 100 \% \]

\[ \text{Fraction} \approx 8.68 \% \]

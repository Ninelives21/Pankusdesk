To calculate the wavelength of the first line in the Balmer series (n=3 to n=2 transition) of the hydrogen spectrum, we use the Rydberg formula:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

Where:

\( R_H \) is the Rydberg constant (\( 1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1} \))

\( n_1 = 2 \)

\( n_2 = 3 \)

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{3^2} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{1}{4} - \dfrac{1}{9} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{9 - 4}{36} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{5}{36} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.523 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}^{-1} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{1}{1.523 \times 10^6} \approx 656.3 \, \text{nm} \]

Ans = 656.3 nm

The shortest wavelength in the Lyman series corresponds to the transition from \( n = \infty \) to \( n = 1 \).

Using the Rydberg formula:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( 1 - \dfrac{1}{n^2} \right) \]

For \( n = \infty \):

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \]

Given \( R_H = 109,677 \, \text{cm}^{-1} \), convert it to meters:

\[ R_H = 1.09677 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{1}{1.09677 \times 10^7} \approx 91.18 \, \text{nm} \]

Convert to angstroms:

\[ 91.18 \, \text{nm} = 911.8 \, \text{Å} \]

Ans = 911.8 Å

To find the transition in the hydrogen spectrum that has the same wavelength as the Balmer transition \( n = 4 \) to \( n = 2 \) of \( \text{He}^+ \), we use the Rydberg formula for hydrogen-like ions:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_Z \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

For \( \text{He}^+ \) (Z=2):

\[ R_{\text{He}^+} = 4 R_H \]

Transition for \( \text{He}^+ \):

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 4 R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{4^2} \right) = 4 R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{4} - \dfrac{1}{16} \right) = 4 R_H \left( \dfrac{4-1}{16} \right) = 4 R_H \left( \dfrac{3}{16} \right) = \dfrac{3 R_H}{4} \]

For the hydrogen spectrum:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

To match the wavelength:

\[ R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) = \dfrac{3 R_H}{4} \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} = \dfrac{3}{4} \]

Solving for \( n_1 \) and \( n_2 \):

For \( n_1 = 1 \) and \( n_2 = 2 \):

\[ \dfrac{1}{1^2} - \dfrac{1}{2^2} = 1 - \dfrac{1}{4} = \dfrac{4-1}{4} = \dfrac{3}{4} \]

Therefore, the transition \( n_1 = 1 \) and \( n_2 = 2 \) in the hydrogen spectrum would have the same wavelength as the Balmer transition \( n = 4 \) to \( n = 2 \) of \( \text{He}^+ \).

Ans = \( n_1 = 1 \) and \( n_2 = 2 \)

To find the ionisation energy of \( \text{Li}^{2+} \) ion, we use the formula for ionisation energy of hydrogen-like ions:

\[ E_n = 13.6 \, \text{eV} \times Z^2 \]

For \( \text{Li}^{2+} \) (Z=3):

\[ E_n = 13.6 \, \text{eV} \times 3^2 \]

\[ E_n = 13.6 \, \text{eV} \times 9 \]

\[ E_n = 122.4 \, \text{eV} \]

Therefore, the ionisation energy of \( \text{Li}^{2+} \) ion is 122.4 eV.

To calculate the wavelength of the second line and the limiting line in the Balmer series, we use the following steps:

1. The wavelength (\(\lambda_1\)) of the first line (n = 3 to n = 2) is given as 656 nm. To find the wavelength of the second line (n = 4 to n = 2), we use the Rydberg formula:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

where \( R_H \) is the Rydberg constant (\(1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1}\)), \( n_1 = 2 \), and \( n_2 = 4 \).

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda_2} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{4^2} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda_2} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{1}{4} - \dfrac{1}{16} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda_2} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \dfrac{4 - 1}{16} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda_2} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times \dfrac{3}{16} \]

\[ \lambda_2 = \dfrac{16}{1.097 \times 10^7 \times 3} \]

\[ \lambda_2 \approx 486.1 \, \text{nm} \approx 4861 \, \text{Å} \]

2. The limiting line (n = ∞ to n = 2) can be calculated using the same formula:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda_\text{limit}} = R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{2^2} - \dfrac{1}{\infty^2} \right) \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda_\text{limit}} = R_H \left( \dfrac{1}{4} - 0 \right) \]

\[ \lambda_\text{limit} = \dfrac{4}{R_H} \]

\[ \lambda_\text{limit} = \dfrac{4}{1.097 \times 10^7} \]

\[ \lambda_\text{limit} \approx 364.7 \, \text{nm} \approx 3647 \, \text{Å} \]

Therefore, the wavelength of the second line in the Balmer series is approximately 4861 Å, and the wavelength of the limiting line is approximately 3647 Å.

Ans = 3647 Å

To calculate the wavelength of the first line and the series limit for the Lyman series, follow these steps:

First Line in the Lyman Series:

Use the formula for the wavelength of the Lyman series: \[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left(1 - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \] where \( R_H \) is the Rydberg constant (\( 109,677 \text{ cm}^{-1} \)), and for the first line, \( n_2 = 2 \).

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 109,677 \left(1 - \dfrac{1}{4}\right) \] \[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 109,677 \left(\dfrac{3}{4}\right) = 82,258.25 \text{ cm}^{-1} \] \[ \lambda = \dfrac{1}{82,258.25} \text{ cm} = 1.215 \times 10^{-5} \text{ cm} \]

Series Limit in the Lyman Series:

Use the formula for the series limit of the Lyman series: \[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left(1 - \dfrac{1}{\infty}\right) = R_H = 109,677 \text{ cm}^{-1} \] \[ \lambda = \dfrac{1}{109,677} \text{ cm} = 9.1176 \times 10^{-6} \text{ cm} \]

Therefore, the wavelength of the first line in the Lyman series is \(1.215 \times 10^{-5}\) cm, and the series limit is \(9.1176 \times 10^{-6}\) cm.

Ans = \(9.1176 \times 10^{-6}\) cm

To calculate the wavelength of the other photon, follow these steps:

1. Calculate the energy of the absorbed photon with a wavelength of 3000 Å.

\( E_{3000} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{3000 \times 10^{-10}} = 6.626 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \)

2. Calculate the energy of the re-emitted red photon with a wavelength of 7600 Å.

\( E_{7600} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{7600 \times 10^{-10}} = 2.61 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \)

3. The energy of the other re-emitted photon is the difference in energy.

\( E_{\text{other}} = E_{3000} - E_{7600} = 6.626 \times 10^{-19} - 2.61 \times 10^{-19} = 4.016 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \)

4. Calculate the wavelength of the other photon using its energy.

\( \lambda_{\text{other}} = \dfrac{hc}{E_{\text{other}}} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{4.016 \times 10^{-19}} = 4956.5 \, \text{Å} \)

Therefore, the wavelength of the other photon is 4956.5 Å.

To calculate the wavelength emitted during the transition, we first determine the initial and final energy levels. The sum is 4 and the difference is 2, so the levels are n=3 and n=1.

Using the Rydberg formula for hydrogen-like ions:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

Where:

\( R_H \) is the Rydberg constant (\( 1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1} \)) and \( Z \) is the atomic number (for \( \text{Li}^{2+} \), \( Z = 3 \)).

For the transition from n=3 to n=1:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times 3^2 \left( \dfrac{1}{1^2} - \dfrac{1}{3^2} \right) = 9.873 \times 10^7 \left( 1 - \dfrac{1}{9} \right) = 9.873 \times 10^7 \times \dfrac{8}{9} \]

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 8.776 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1} \Rightarrow \lambda = \dfrac{1}{8.776 \times 10^7} \approx 1.14 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{m} \approx 1.14 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{cm} \]

Ans = 1.14 × 10⁻⁶ cm

To find the wavelength of the series limit for the Lyman series for \( \text{He}^{+} \) ion, we use the Rydberg formula for hydrogen-like ions:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left( \dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \]

Where:

\( R_H \) is the Rydberg constant (\( 1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1} \)), \( Z \) is the atomic number (for \( \text{He}^{+} \), \( Z = 2 \)), \( n_1 = 1 \), and \( n_2 = \infty \) (for the series limit).

Substituting the values, we get:

\[ \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times 2^2 \left( 1 - 0 \right) = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times 4 = 4.388 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1} \]

Therefore, \[ \lambda = \dfrac{1}{4.388 \times 10^7} = 2.278 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m} \]

Converting to Ångströms (1 m = \( 10^{10} \) Å):

\[ \lambda = 2.278 \times 10^{-8} \times 10^{10} = 227.8 \, \text{Å} \]

Therefore, the correct answer is choice (B).

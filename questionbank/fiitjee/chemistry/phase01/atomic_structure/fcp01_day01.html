To determine the number of neutrons and electrons present in the given nuclei, follow these steps:

1. Identify the atomic number (Z) and mass number (A) for each element:

- \({}^{13}_{6}C\): Z = 6, A = 13

- \({}^{16}_{8}O\): Z = 8, A = 16

- \({}^{24}_{12}Mg\): Z = 12, A = 24

- \({}^{56}_{26}Fe\): Z = 26, A = 56

- \({}^{88}_{38}Sr\): Z = 38, A = 88

2. Calculate the number of neutrons (N) using the formula \(N = A - Z\):

- \({}^{13}_{6}C\): \(N = 13 - 6 = 7\)

- \({}^{16}_{8}O\): \(N = 16 - 8 = 8\)

- \({}^{24}_{12}Mg\): \(N = 24 - 12 = 12\)

- \({}^{56}_{26}Fe\): \(N = 56 - 26 = 30\)

- \({}^{88}_{38}Sr\): \(N = 88 - 38 = 50\)

3. The number of electrons in a neutral atom is equal to the atomic number (Z):

- \({}^{13}_{6}C\): Electrons = 6

- \({}^{16}_{8}O\): Electrons = 8

- \({}^{24}_{12}Mg\): Electrons = 12

- \({}^{56}_{26}Fe\): Electrons = 26

- \({}^{88}_{38}Sr\): Electrons = 38

Therefore, the number of neutrons and electrons in the given nuclei are:

- \({}^{13}_{6}C\): Neutrons = 7, Electrons = 6

- \({}^{16}_{8}O\): Neutrons = 8, Electrons = 8

- \({}^{24}_{12}Mg\): Neutrons = 12, Electrons = 12

- \({}^{56}_{26}Fe\): Neutrons = 30, Electrons = 26

- \({}^{88}_{38}Sr\): Neutrons = 50, Electrons = 38

To determine the charge, mass, and charge-to-mass ratio of an electron, follow these steps:

1. The charge of an electron is:

\( -1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} \)

2. The mass of an electron is:

\( 9.108 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg} \)

3. The charge-to-mass ratio of an electron is:

\( \dfrac{-1.602 \times 10^{-19}}{9.108 \times 10^{-31}} \text{ C/kg} = 1.758 \times 10^{11} \text{ C/kg} \)

To calculate the charge of one mole of electrons, follow these steps:

1. Determine the charge of a single electron:

- Charge of one electron: \(-1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}\)

2. Use Avogadro's number to find the number of electrons in one mole:

- Avogadro's number: \(6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{electrons/mol}\)

3. Calculate the total charge of one mole of electrons:

Total charge = Charge of one electron \(\times\) Number of electrons in one mole

\[ = (-1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}) \times (6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{electrons/mol}) \]

\[ = -9.65 \times 10^{4} \, \text{C/mol} \]

Therefore, the charge of one mole of electrons is approximately \(96,500 \, \text{C}\).

To calculate the mass of one mole of electrons, follow these steps:

1. Determine the mass of one electron:

- Mass of one electron: \(9.108 \times 10^{-31}\) kg

2. Calculate the number of electrons in one mole:

- Number of electrons in one mole (Avogadro's number): \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\)

3. Calculate the mass of one mole of electrons:

- Mass of one mole of electrons = Mass of one electron \(\times\) Number of electrons in one mole

\(= 9.108 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}\)

\(= 5.485 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{kg}\)

4. Convert the mass to milligrams:

- Mass = \(5.485 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{kg} \times 10^6 \, \text{mg/kg} = 0.5485 \, \text{mg}\)

Therefore, the mass of one mole of electrons is approximately \(0.55 \, \text{mg}\).

Ans = 0.55 mg

To calculate the mass of one mole of protons, follow these steps:

1. Determine the mass of a single proton:

- Mass of a proton: \(1.6726219 \times 10^{-27}\) kg

2. Calculate the mass of one mole of protons using Avogadro's number:

- Avogadro's number: \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) protons/mole

Mass of one mole of protons = Mass of a single proton \(\times\) Avogadro's number

\(= 1.6726219 \times 10^{-27}\) kg \(\times 6.022 \times 10^{23}\)

\(= 1.00727\) g

Therefore, the mass of one mole of protons is approximately \(1.00727\) g.

To calculate the mass of one mole of neutrons, follow these steps:

1. Determine the mass of a single neutron:

- Mass of a neutron: \(1.008668 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg}\)

2. Use Avogadro's number to find the mass of one mole of neutrons:

- Avogadro's number: \(6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{particles/mol}\)

3. Calculate the mass of one mole of neutrons:

\(\text{Mass of one mole of neutrons} = 1.008668 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{particles/mol}\)

4. Simplify the calculation:

\(\text{Mass of one mole of neutrons} \approx 1.008668 \, \text{g/mol}\)

Therefore, the mass of one mole of neutrons is approximately \(1.008668 \, \text{g/mol}\).

Ans = 1.008668 g

To determine the number of electrons present on the oil drop with a charge of \(-3.2044 \times 10^{-19}\) C, follow these steps:

1. The charge of one electron is \(-1.602 \times 10^{-19}\) C.

2. Calculate the number of electrons:

\[ \text{Number of electrons} = \dfrac{\text{Total charge}}{\text{Charge of one electron}} \]

\[ \text{Number of electrons} = \dfrac{-3.2044 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}}{-1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}} \]

\[ \text{Number of electrons} = 2 \]

Therefore, there are 2 electrons present on the oil drop.

Ans = 2

To determine the increasing order of e/m (charge to mass ratio) for electron (e), proton (p), neutron (n) and alpha particle (\(\alpha\)), consider the following:

1. Neutron (n) has zero charge, so its charge to mass ratio (e/m) is zero.

2. Alpha particle (\(\alpha\)) has a charge of \(+2e\) and a mass of 4 times that of a proton. Thus, its e/m ratio is \(\dfrac{2e}{4m_p} = \dfrac{e}{2m_p}\).

3. Proton (p) has a charge of \(+e\) and its mass is \(m_p\). Thus, its e/m ratio is \(\dfrac{e}{m_p}\).

4. Electron (e) has a charge of \(-e\) and its mass is \(m_e\). Thus, its e/m ratio is \(\dfrac{-e}{m_e}\). The absolute value of this ratio is significantly larger than that of a proton or alpha particle because the mass of an electron is much smaller.

5. Comparing the values, we get the following order: \(n < \alpha < p < e\).

Therefore, the correct answer is choice (D).

Ans = D

SUBJECTIVE

To calculate the kinetic energy of an electron emitted when radiation of frequency \( \nu = 1.0 \times 10^{15} \, \text{s}^{-1} \) hits the metal, follow these steps:

1. Use the photoelectric equation: \( E_k = h\nu - h\nu_0 \), where:

- \( E_k \) is the kinetic energy

- \( h \) is Planck's constant (\( 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s} \))

- \( \nu \) is the frequency of the incident radiation (\( 1.0 \times 10^{15} \, \text{s}^{-1} \))

- \( \nu_0 \) is the threshold frequency (\( 7.0 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1} \))

2. Substitute the given values into the equation:

\[ E_k = h\nu - h\nu_0 = (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s})(1.0 \times 10^{15} \, \text{s}^{-1}) - (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s})(7.0 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1}) \]

3. Simplify the equation to find \( E_k \):

\[ E_k = (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s})(1.0 \times 10^{15} - 7.0 \times 10^{14}) \, \text{s}^{-1} \]

\[ E_k = (6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.0 \times 10^{14}) \, \text{J} \]

\[ E_k = 1.988 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

Therefore, the kinetic energy of the emitted electron is \( 1.988 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \).

To calculate the threshold frequency \((\nu_0)\), follow these steps:

1. Use the equation that relates the speed of light (\(c\)), wavelength (\(\lambda\)), and frequency (\(\nu\)):

\[ c = \lambda \nu \]

2. Rearrange the equation to solve for \(\nu\):

\[ \nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda} \]

3. Substitute the given values into the equation. The speed of light, \(c\), is \(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\), and the wavelength, \(\lambda\), is \(4000 \, \text{Å}\) or \(4000 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}\):

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}}{4000 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}} \]

4. Calculate the threshold frequency:

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{3 \times 10^8}{4 \times 10^{-7}} = 7.5 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \]

Therefore, the threshold frequency \((\nu_0)\) is \(7.5 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz}\).

To determine the threshold frequency (\(\nu_0\)), we use the photoelectric equation for two different frequencies \(\nu_1\) and \(\nu_2\):

\[ \text{K.E.}_1 = h (\nu_1 - \nu_0) \]

\[ \text{K.E.}_2 = h (\nu_2 - \nu_0) \]

Given the ratio of the kinetic energies is 1 : K, we can write:

\[ \dfrac{\text{K.E.}_2}{\text{K.E.}_1} = K \]

Substituting the expressions for kinetic energy, we get:

\[ \dfrac{h (\nu_2 - \nu_0)}{h (\nu_1 - \nu_0)} = K \]

Canceling \(h\) from both sides, we get:

\[ \dfrac{\nu_2 - \nu_0}{\nu_1 - \nu_0} = K \]

Cross-multiplying and simplifying for \(\nu_0\), we get:

\[ \nu_2 - \nu_0 = K (\nu_1 - \nu_0) \]

\[ \nu_2 - \nu_0 = K \nu_1 - K \nu_0 \]

\[ \nu_2 - \nu_0 + K \nu_0 = K \nu_1 \]

\[ \nu_2 + (K-1) \nu_0 = K \nu_1 \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{K \nu_1 - \nu_2}{K-1} \]

Therefore, the correct answer is choice (B).

Comparison of the First and Second Ionization Potentials of Copper and Zinc:

Copper (Cu): The electronic configuration of copper is [Ar] 3d10 4s1. The first ionization potential (IP₁) involves removing the 4s electron, which requires a moderate amount of energy. After the removal of the 4s electron, copper attains a stable 3d10 configuration. The second ionization potential (IP₂) involves removing an electron from this stable 3d10 configuration, which requires significantly more energy, making IP₂ much higher than IP₁.

Zinc (Zn): The electronic configuration of zinc is [Ar] 3d10 4s2. The first ionization potential (IP₁) involves removing one of the 4s electrons, which requires a moderate amount of energy. The second ionization potential (IP₂) involves removing the remaining 4s electron. This also requires more energy than the first, but the increase is less drastic compared to copper because both electrons removed are from the 4s orbital.

Conclusion: In copper, the second ionization potential (IP₂) is significantly higher than the first (IP₁) due to the removal of an electron from the stable 3d10 configuration. In zinc, the difference between IP₁ and IP₂ is less drastic, as both electrons are removed from the 4s orbital, with the larger increase in ionization potential occurring after reaching the stable 3d10 configuration.

Answer: 32.65% of the Mg atoms are ionized to Mg²⁺.

Explanation:

Step 1: Calculate the moles of Mg atoms in 1 gram of Mg
The atomic mass of Mg (Magnesium) is approximately 24.3 g/mol.

Moles of Mg = (Mass) / (Molar Mass) = 1 g / 24.3 g/mol = 0.0412 mol

Step 2: Calculate the energy required for ionization
The first ionization energy (IE₁) = 740 kJ/mol.
The second ionization energy (IE₂) = 1450 kJ/mol.

For 0.0412 mol of Mg:
Energy for Mg → Mg⁺ = 0.0412 mol × 740 kJ/mol = 30.488 kJ
Energy for Mg⁺ → Mg²⁺ = 0.0412 mol × 1450 kJ/mol = 59.74 kJ

Step 3: Distribute the absorbed energy
The total energy absorbed by the Mg atoms = 50 kJ.

First, all the Mg atoms will be ionized to Mg⁺:
Energy left after forming Mg⁺ = 50 kJ - 30.488 kJ = 19.512 kJ

This remaining energy will further ionize some of the Mg⁺ ions to Mg²⁺.

Step 4: Calculate the fraction of Mg²⁺ formed
The energy required to convert all Mg⁺ to Mg²⁺ is 59.74 kJ. Since only 19.512 kJ is available, only a fraction of the Mg⁺ will be ionized to Mg²⁺:

Fraction of Mg⁺ converting to Mg²⁺ = 19.512 kJ / 59.74 kJ = 0.3265 or 32.65%

Conclusion: 32.65% of the Mg atoms are ionized to Mg²⁺.

Answer: The ionization enthalpy of atomic hydrogen is \(1.31 \times 10^{6}\) J/mol.

Explanation:

Step 1: Understanding the Problem
The energy given, \(-2.18 \times 10^{-18}\) J, is the energy required to remove an electron from the ground state of a single hydrogen atom. The ionization enthalpy is the energy required to ionize 1 mole of hydrogen atoms.

Step 2: Convert the Energy of a Single Atom to 1 Mole
Use Avogadro's number, \(N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}\) mol\(^{-1}\), to convert the energy per atom to energy per mole.

Ionization enthalpy = \((-2.18 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J}) \times (6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{mol}^{-1})\)

Step 3: Perform the Calculation
Ionization enthalpy = \((-2.18 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J}) \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{mol}^{-1} = -1.31 \times 10^{6} \, \text{J/mol}\)

Step 4: Consider the Sign
Ionization enthalpy is typically expressed as a positive value because it represents the energy required to remove the electron. So, we take the absolute value:

Ionization enthalpy = \(1.31 \times 10^{6}\) J/mol

Conclusion: The ionization enthalpy of atomic hydrogen is \(1.31 \times 10^{6}\) J/mol.

Ionization potential is the energy required to remove an electron from a neutral atom in its gaseous state.

General Trend:
- Across a Period: Ionization potential increases as you move from left to right across a period due to increasing nuclear charge, which holds the electrons more tightly.
- Down a Group: Ionization potential decreases as you move down a group because the outermost electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded by inner electrons, making them easier to remove.

Given Elements:
- Sodium (Na) and Potassium (K) are alkali metals in Group 1, with K below Na.
- Beryllium (Be) is an alkaline earth metal in Group 2.

Analysis:
- Potassium (K): Lower ionization potential than sodium because it is below Na in Group 1.
- Sodium (Na): Higher ionization potential than K, but lower than Be.
- Beryllium (Be): Higher ionization potential than both Na and K because it is in Group 2 and to the right of Na and K in Period 2.

Conclusion: The correct order of increasing ionization potential is K < Na < Be.

To calculate the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in \({}^{80}_{35}\text{Br}\), follow these steps:

1. The atomic number (Z) of bromine (Br) is 35. This means the number of protons (\(p\)) is 35.

2. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons (\(e\)) equals the number of protons. Therefore, the number of electrons (\(e\)) is also 35.

3. The mass number (A) of \({}^{80}_{35}\text{Br}\) is 80. The number of neutrons (\(n\)) can be calculated using the formula:

\(\text{Number of neutrons} = \text{Mass number} - \text{Atomic number} = 80 - 35 = 45\)

Therefore, the number of protons is 35, the number of electrons is 35, and the number of neutrons is 45.

To determine the proper symbol of the species, follow these steps:

2. The mass number (A) is equal to the sum of the number of protons and neutrons, which is \(16 + 16 = 32\).

4. Therefore, the symbol for the species is \( \mathbf{^{32}_{16}S^{2-}} \).

To calculate the power of the bulb, we need to determine the energy of the photons being emitted and then multiply by the number of photons emitted per second.

1. Calculate the energy of a single photon using the equation:

\[ E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Where:

\( h \) is Planck's constant \( (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{Js}) \)

\( c \) is the speed of light \( (3 \times 10^{8} \, \text{m/s}) \)

\( \lambda \) is the wavelength \( (5000 \, \text{Å} = 5000 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}) \)

\[ E = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^{8}}{5000 \times 10^{-10}} \]

\[ E = \dfrac{6.626 \times 3}{5000} \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

\[ E \approx 3.975 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

2. Calculate the total energy emitted per second by multiplying the energy of a single photon by the number of photons emitted per second:

\[ \text{Total Energy} = E \times \text{Number of photons} \]

\[ \text{Total Energy} = 3.975 \times 10^{-19} \times 1.515 \times 10^{20} \]

\[ \text{Total Energy} \approx 60.2 \, \text{J/s} \]

Therefore, the power of the bulb is 60.2 W.

To calculate the ratio of energies of two radiation, follow these steps:

1. Use the energy-wavelength relationship formula \( E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \).

2. For the first radiation with \( \lambda_1 = 10,000\mathring{A} \):

\( E_1 = \dfrac{hc}{10,000\mathring{A}} \)

3. For the second radiation with \( \lambda_2 = 5000\mathring{A} \):

\( E_2 = \dfrac{hc}{5000\mathring{A}} \)

4. Calculate the ratio \( \dfrac{E_1}{E_2} \):

\( \dfrac{E_1}{E_2} = \dfrac{\dfrac{hc}{10,000\mathring{A}}}{\dfrac{hc}{5000\mathring{A}}} = \dfrac{5000}{10,000} = \dfrac{1}{2} \)

5. Therefore, the ratio of energies is \( 1:2 \).

To calculate the wave number and frequency of yellow radiation with a wavelength of \( 5800 \, \text{Å} \), follow these steps:

(a) Wave Number

1. The wave number (\( \tilde{\nu} \)) is the reciprocal of the wavelength (\( \lambda \)).

2. Convert the wavelength from angstroms (\( \text{Å} \)) to centimeters (cm):

\[ \lambda = 5800 \, \text{Å} = 5800 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{cm} = 5.8 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{cm} \]

3. Calculate the wave number:

\[ \tilde{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\lambda} = \dfrac{1}{5.8 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{cm}} = 1.724 \times 10^{4} \, \text{cm}^{-1} \]

(b) Frequency

4. Use the speed of light (\( c \)) to find the frequency (\( \nu \)). The speed of light is \( 3 \times 10^{10} \, \text{cm/s} \).

5. Calculate the frequency:

\[ \nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda} = \dfrac{3 \times 10^{10} \, \text{cm/s}}{5.8 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{cm}} = 5.172 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1} \]

Therefore, the wave number is \( 1.724 \times 10^{4} \, \text{cm}^{-1} \) and the frequency is \( 5.172 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1} \).

To calculate the energy of one mole of photons, we can follow these steps:

1. Determine the energy of a single photon using the formula:

\[ E = h \nu \]

where:

\( h \) is Planck's constant (\(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) J·s)

\( \nu \) is the frequency (\(5 \times 10^{14}\) Hz)

2. Substitute the values into the formula:

\[ E = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 5 \times 10^{14} \]

\[ E = 3.313 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/photon} \]

3. To find the energy of one mole of photons, multiply the energy of a single photon by Avogadro's number (\(6.022 \times 10^{23}\)):

\[ E_{\text{mole}} = 3.313 \times 10^{-19} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \]

\[ E_{\text{mole}} = 199.51 \times 10^{3} \text{ J/mol} \]

4. Convert the energy from J/mol to kJ/mol:

\[ E_{\text{mole}} = 199.51 \text{ kJ/mol} \]

Therefore, the energy of one mole of photons is \(199.51 \text{ kJ/mol}\).

To calculate the frequency of radiation with a wavelength of 600 nm, follow these steps:

1. Use the speed of light equation: \( c = \lambda \nu \), where:

- \( c \) is the speed of light (\(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\))

- \( \lambda \) is the wavelength (\(600 \, \text{nm} = 600 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m}\))

- \( \nu \) is the frequency

2. Rearrange the equation to solve for \( \nu \):

\[ \nu = \dfrac{c}{\lambda} = \dfrac{3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}}{600 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m}} \]

3. Calculate the frequency:

\[ \nu = 5 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \]

Therefore, the frequency of radiation with a wavelength of 600 nm is \( 5 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \).

To calculate the number of photons needed, follow these steps:

1. Calculate the energy of one photon using the formula \(E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\):

Given:
\(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) Js (Planck's constant)
\(c = 3 \times 10^8\) m/s (speed of light)
\(\lambda = 660\) nm = \(660 \times 10^{-9}\) m

\[ E = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{660 \times 10^{-9}} = \dfrac{19.878 \times 10^{-26}}{660 \times 10^{-9}} = 3.012 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \]

2. Calculate the number of photons (\(n\)) needed to produce \(10^{-18}\) J:

Therefore, the number of photons needed is 3.

To calculate the number of photons emitted in 10 hours by a 60 W sodium lamp with a wavelength of 5893Å, follow these steps:

1. Calculate the energy of one photon using the formula \(E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\):

Given:
\(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) Js (Planck's constant)
\(c = 3 \times 10^8\) m/s (speed of light)
\(\lambda = 5893\) Å = \(5893 \times 10^{-10}\) m

\[ E = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{5893 \times 10^{-10}} = \dfrac{19.878 \times 10^{-26}}{5893 \times 10^{-10}} = 3.37 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \]

2. Calculate the total energy emitted by the lamp in 10 hours:

\[ \text{Power} = 60 \text{ W} \implies \text{Energy} = 60 \text{ W} \times 10 \text{ hours} \times 3600 \text{ s/hour} = 60 \times 36000 = 2.16 \times 10^6 \text{ J} \]

3. Calculate the number of photons (\(n\)) emitted:

\[ n = \dfrac{\text{Total energy emitted}}{\text{Energy of one photon}} = \dfrac{2.16 \times 10^6}{3.37 \times 10^{-19}} = 6.41 \times 10^{24} \]

Therefore, the number of photons emitted in 10 hours is \(6.4 \times 10^{24}\).

To find the ratio of the energies of photons with wavelengths 2000 Å and 4000 Å, follow these steps:

1. The energy of a photon is given by \(E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\), where \(h\) is Planck's constant, \(c\) is the speed of light, and \(\lambda\) is the wavelength.

2. Let \(E_1\) be the energy of the photon with \(\lambda_1 = 2000\) Å and \(E_2\) be the energy of the photon with \(\lambda_2 = 4000\) Å.

3. The ratio of the energies is given by: \[ \dfrac{E_1}{E_2} = \dfrac{\dfrac{hc}{\lambda_1}}{\dfrac{hc}{\lambda_2}} = \dfrac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1} \]

4. Substituting the given wavelengths: \[ \dfrac{E_1}{E_2} = \dfrac{4000}{2000} = 2 \]

Therefore, the ratio of the energies of photons of 2000 Å to that of 4000 Å is 2.

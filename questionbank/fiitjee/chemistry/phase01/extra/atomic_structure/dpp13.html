To calculate the critical frequency (\(\nu_0\)) of the metal, we use the photoelectric equation:

1. The photoelectric equation is given by:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = h\nu \]

where \( E_{\text{photon}} \) is the energy of the incident photon, \( h \) is Planck's constant, and \( \nu \) is the frequency of the incident light.

2. The kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons is given by:

\[ \text{Kinetic energy} = E_{\text{photon}} - h\nu_0 \]

3. For the first frequency \( \nu_1 = 4.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1} \), the kinetic energy is:

\[ \text{K.E.}_1 = h\nu_1 - h\nu_0 \]

4. For the second frequency \( \nu_2 = 2.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1} \), the kinetic energy is:

\[ \text{K.E.}_2 = h\nu_2 - h\nu_0 \]

5. According to the problem, the kinetic energy for the first frequency is three times the kinetic energy for the second frequency:

\[ \text{K.E.}_1 = 3 \times \text{K.E.}_2 \]

6. Substituting the kinetic energy equations, we get:

\[ h\nu_1 - h\nu_0 = 3(h\nu_2 - h\nu_0) \]

7. Simplifying, we get:

\[ \nu_1 - \nu_0 = 3(\nu_2 - \nu_0) \]

\[ \nu_1 - \nu_0 = 3\nu_2 - 3\nu_0 \]

\[ \nu_1 = 3\nu_2 - 2\nu_0 \]

8. Solving for \( \nu_0 \), we get:

\[ 2\nu_0 = 3\nu_2 - \nu_1 \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{3\nu_2 - \nu_1}{2} \]

9. Substituting the given frequencies, we get:

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{3 \times 2.0 \times 10^{16} - 4.0 \times 10^{16}}{2} \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{6.0 \times 10^{16} - 4.0 \times 10^{16}}{2} \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{2.0 \times 10^{16}}{2} \]

\[ \nu_0 = 1.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1} \]

Therefore, the critical frequency (\(\nu_0\)) of the metal is \(1.0 \times 10^{16} \, \text{s}^{-1}\).

The electrostatic potential energy \(U\) at a distance \(r\) from the nucleus is given by:

\[ U = \dfrac{k \cdot Q_1 \cdot Q_2}{r} \]

where:

\(Q_1\) is the charge of the \(\alpha\)-particle (which is \(2e\), where \(e\) is the elementary charge)

The kinetic energy \(K\) of the \(\alpha\)-particle is given by:

\[ K = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

where:

\(m\) is the mass of the \(\alpha\)-particle

\(v\) is the velocity of the \(\alpha\)-particle

Equating the potential energy and kinetic energy, we get:

\[ \dfrac{k \cdot Q_1 \cdot Q_2}{r} = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

To solve this problem, we'll follow these steps:

1. Calculate the energy of one photon:
The energy of a photon is given by the equation:
\( E = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \)
where:
\( h \) is Planck's constant (\( 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J} \cdot \text{s} \)),
\( c \) is the speed of light (\( 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s} \)),
\( \lambda \) is the wavelength in meters (\( 4500 \text{ Å} = 4500 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m} \)).
Plugging in the values:
\( E = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{4500 \times 10^{-10}} \)
\( E = \dfrac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{4500 \times 10^{-10}} \)
\( E \approx 4.42 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \)

2. Determine the energy absorbed by one mole of iodine molecules:
The energy of one mole of photons (Avogadro's number \( N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \)) is:
\( E_{\text{mole}} = E \times N_A \)
\( E_{\text{mole}} = 4.42 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \)
\( E_{\text{mole}} \approx 266.01 \text{ kJ/mol} \)

3. Bond dissociation energy for one iodine molecule:
The bond energy of \( I_2 \) is given as 240 kJ/mol. This is the energy required to break one mole of \( I_2 \) molecules into atoms.

4. Calculate the kinetic energy of the iodine atoms:
The kinetic energy of the iodine atoms after dissociation is the difference between the energy absorbed and the bond dissociation energy:
\( \text{Kinetic Energy} = E_{\text{mole}} - \text{Bond Energy} \)
\( \text{Kinetic Energy} = 266.01 \text{ kJ/mol} - 240 \text{ kJ/mol} \)
\( \text{Kinetic Energy} = 26.01 \text{ kJ/mol} \)

5. Convert the kinetic energy from kJ/mol to J per molecule:
First, convert the energy from kJ/mol to J/mol:
\( 26.01 \text{ kJ/mol} = 26010 \text{ J/mol} \)
Then, divide by Avogadro's number to get the energy per molecule:
\( \text{Energy per molecule} = \dfrac{26010 \text{ J/mol}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ molecules/mol}} \)
\( \text{Energy per molecule} \approx 4.32 \times 10^{-20} \text{ J} \)

Given the bond energy per molecule:
\( \text{Bond Energy per molecule} = \dfrac{240 \times 10^3 \text{ J/mol}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ molecules/mol}} \approx 3.98 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/molecule} \)
The kinetic energy difference is:
\( 4.42 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} - 3.98 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \approx 0.44 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \)
Dividing by two atoms, we get:
\( \dfrac{0.44 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}}{2} \approx 0.216 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \)

Therefore, the kinetic energy of iodine atoms is \( 0.216 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \).

To find the wavelength of the proton, we use the de Broglie wavelength formula:

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{h}{p} \]

Where:

- \( \lambda \) is the wavelength
- \( h \) is Planck's constant (\( 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \) J·s)
- \( p \) is the momentum of the proton

The momentum \( p \) can be found from the kinetic energy using the relationship:

\[ KE = \dfrac{1}{2} mv^2 \]

Solving for \( v \):

\[ v = \sqrt{\dfrac{2 KE}{m}} \]

Then, the momentum \( p \) is:

\[ p = mv \]

Substituting \( v \):

\[ p = m \sqrt{\dfrac{2 KE}{m}} = \sqrt{2mKE} \]

Now substituting \( p \) back into the de Broglie wavelength formula:

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{h}{\sqrt{2mKE}} \]

Substituting the given values:

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{2 \times 1.66 \times 10^{-27} \times 5 \times 10^{-27}}} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{1.66 \times 10^{-26} \times 10^{-26}}} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{1.66 \times 10^{-52}}} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{1.66} \times 10^{-26}} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{1.29 \times 10^{-26}} \]

\[ \lambda = \dfrac{6.626}{1.29} \times 10^{-8} \]

\[ \lambda \approx 5.14 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m} \]

Therefore, the wavelength of the proton is approximately \( 5.14 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m} \).

Ans = \( 5.14 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{m} \)

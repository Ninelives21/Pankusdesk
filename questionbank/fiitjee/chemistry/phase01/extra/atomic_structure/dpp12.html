To calculate the number of photons emitted, we need to follow these steps:

1. Determine the energy emitted by the lamp in 10 hours.

2. Convert this energy into the number of photons using the energy of one photon.

Step 1: Energy emitted by the lamp

Power of the lamp = 60 W

Time = 10 hours = 10 × 3600 seconds = 36,000 seconds

Energy emitted (E) = Power × Time

E = 60 W × 36,000 s = 2,160,000 J (Joules)

Step 2: Energy of one photon

Wavelength (\(\lambda\)) = 2893 Å = 2893 × 10-10 m

Energy of one photon (Ephoton) = \(\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}\)

Where:

h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10-34 J·s

c = speed of light = 3 × 108 m/s

Substituting the values, we get:

Ephoton = \(\dfrac{6.626 × 10^{-34} \times 3 × 10^8}{2893 × 10^{-10}}\)

Ephoton ≈ 6.88 × 10-19 J

Step 3: Number of photons

Number of photons (N) = \(\dfrac{\text{Total Energy}}{\text{Energy per photon}}\)

N = \(\dfrac{2,160,000}{6.88 × 10^{-19}}\)

N ≈ 3.14 × 1024 photons

Therefore, the number of photons emitted in 10 hours by a 60 W sodium lamp is approximately 3.14 × 1024 photons.

Ans = 3.14 × 1024 photons

To calculate the ionisation energy of sodium in kJ mol\(^{-1}\), we need to use the following steps:

1. The wavelength (\(\lambda\)) is given as 242 nm. First, we convert this to meters:

\(242 \, \text{nm} = 242 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m} = 2.42 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m}\).

2. The energy of a photon (\(E_{\text{photon}}\)) is calculated using the formula:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Where:

\(h\) is Planck's constant (\(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s}\))

\(c\) is the speed of light (\(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\))

Substituting the values, we get:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{2.42 \times 10^{-7}} \]

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = 8.21 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

3. To convert this energy to kJ/mol, we multiply by Avogadro's number (\(6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{mol}^{-1}\)):

\[ E_{\text{ionisation}} = 8.21 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{mol}^{-1} \]

\[ E_{\text{ionisation}} = 494.47 \, \text{kJ/mol} \]

Therefore, the ionisation energy of sodium is 494.47 kJ mol\(^{-1}\).

Ans = 494.47 kJ mol\(^{-1}\)

To calculate the number of photons emitted by the bulb per second, follow these steps:

1. The wavelength (\(\lambda\)) is given as 4500 Å. First, convert this to meters:

\(4500 \, \text{Å} = 4500 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m} = 4.5 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m}\).

2. The energy of a photon (\(E_{\text{photon}}\)) is calculated using the formula:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Where:

\(h\) is Planck's constant (\(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s}\))

\(c\) is the speed of light (\(3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}\))

Substituting the values, we get:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{4.5 \times 10^{-7}} \]

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = 4.42 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

3. The power of the bulb is 150 W, and 8% of this power is emitted as light:

\[ \text{Power emitted as light} = 0.08 \times 150 \, \text{W} = 12 \, \text{W} \]

4. The number of photons emitted per second (\(N\)) is calculated by dividing the power emitted as light by the energy of one photon:

\[ N = \dfrac{\text{Power emitted as light}}{E_{\text{photon}}} \]

Substituting the values, we get:

\[ N = \dfrac{12}{4.42 \times 10^{-19}} \]

\[ N = 2.71 \times 10^{19} \, \text{photons/second} \]

Therefore, the number of photons emitted by the bulb per second is \(2.71 \times 10^{19}\).

Ans = \(2.71 \times 10^{19}\)

To calculate the threshold frequency (\(\nu_0\)) for the metal, we use the photoelectric equation:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = h\nu \]

where \( E_{\text{photon}} \) is the energy of the incident photon, \( h \) is Planck's constant, and \( \nu \) is the frequency of the incident light.

The photoelectric equation also states:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = \text{Work function} + \text{Kinetic energy} \]

where the Work function (\(\phi\)) is given by:

\[ \phi = h\nu_0 \]

Given:

\( \nu = 5.2 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1} \)

\( \text{Kinetic energy} = 1.3 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \)

We know that:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = h\nu \]

Using Planck's constant (\( h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s} \)), we calculate the energy of the incident photon:

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 5.2 \times 10^{14} \]

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = 3.446 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

Now, using the photoelectric equation:

\[ 3.446 \times 10^{-19} = h\nu_0 + 1.3 \times 10^{-19} \]

Solving for \( \nu_0 \):

\[ h\nu_0 = 3.446 \times 10^{-19} - 1.3 \times 10^{-19} \]

\[ h\nu_0 = 2.146 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

\[ \nu_0 = \dfrac{2.146 \times 10^{-19}}{6.626 \times 10^{-34}} \]

\[ \nu_0 = 3.24 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1} \]

Therefore, the threshold frequency (\(\nu_0\)) for the metal is \(3.24 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1}\).

Ans = \(3.24 \times 10^{14} \, \text{s}^{-1}\)

To find the energy of photons and the kinetic energy of photoelectrons, we use the following steps:

Part a: The energy of photons

1. Convert the wavelength from Ångströms to meters: \[ 5000 \, \text{Å} = 5000 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m} = 5 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m} \]

2. Use the formula for the energy of a photon: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} \] where \( h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s} \) (Planck's constant) and \( c = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s} \) (speed of light).

3. Substitute the values into the formula: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{5 \times 10^{-7}} \] \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{5 \times 10^{-7}} \] \[ E_{\text{photon}} = 3.9756 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

4. Convert the energy from joules to electron volts (1 eV = \( 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \)): \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{3.9756 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \] \[ E_{\text{photon}} \approx 2.48 \, \text{eV} \]

Therefore, the energy of photons is approximately 2.48 eV.

Part b: The kinetic energy of photoelectrons

1. Use the photoelectric equation: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \text{Work function} + \text{Kinetic energy} \]

2. Rearrange the equation to solve for the kinetic energy: \[ \text{Kinetic energy} = E_{\text{photon}} - \text{Work function} \]

3. Substitute the given values: \[ \text{Kinetic energy} = 2.48 \, \text{eV} - 1.9 \, \text{eV} \] \[ \text{Kinetic energy} = 0.58 \, \text{eV} \]

Therefore, the kinetic energy of photoelectrons is 0.58 eV.

To calculate the energy of the other photon, we use the following steps:

1. Calculate the energy of the absorbed photon with wavelength 300 nm.

\[ E_{300} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{300 \times 10^{-9}} = 6.626 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

2. Calculate the energy of the re-emitted photon with wavelength 400 nm.

\[ E_{400} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{400 \times 10^{-9}} = 4.969 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

3. The energy of the other re-emitted photon is the difference in energy.

\[ E_{\text{other}} = E_{300} - E_{400} = 6.626 \times 10^{-19} - 4.969 \times 10^{-19} = 1.657 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

Ans = \(1.657 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}\)
